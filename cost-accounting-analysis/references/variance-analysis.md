# Variance Analysis

## Introduction

Variance analysis is a systematic process of comparing actual performance against predetermined standards or budgets to identify deviations, understand their causes, and take corrective action. It is a cornerstone of management control systems, enabling organizations to monitor performance, control costs, and drive continuous improvement.

This reference provides comprehensive coverage of variance analysis techniques, interpretation methods, and best practices for effective cost control.

## Fundamentals of Variance Analysis

### Definition and Purpose

**Variance**: The difference between an actual result and a budgeted, planned, or standard amount.

**Purposes**:
- Identify areas of strong and weak performance
- Support cost control and reduction initiatives
- Evaluate managerial and operational efficiency
- Provide early warning of problems
- Support continuous improvement efforts
- Inform future planning and budgeting
- Assign accountability for results

### Types of Variances

**Favorable Variance (F)**:
- Actual results are better than expected
- Actual costs < Standard/Budgeted costs
- Actual revenues > Standard/Budgeted revenues
- Generally positive, but requires investigation to understand causes

**Unfavorable Variance (U)**:
- Actual results are worse than expected
- Actual costs > Standard/Budgeted costs
- Actual revenues < Standard/Budgeted revenues
- Requires investigation and corrective action

### Variance Analysis Framework

1. **Calculate Variances**: Determine the difference between actual and standard/budget
2. **Decompose Variances**: Break down total variances into component parts (price, quantity, volume, etc.)
3. **Investigate Causes**: Identify root causes of significant variances
4. **Assign Responsibility**: Determine who is accountable for each variance
5. **Take Action**: Implement corrective measures or capture best practices
6. **Follow Up**: Monitor whether actions are effective
7. **Update Standards**: Revise standards if conditions have permanently changed

## Direct Materials Variances

### Materials Price Variance (MPV)

**Definition**: The difference between the actual price paid for materials and the standard price, multiplied by the actual quantity purchased.

**Formula**:
```
MPV = (Standard Price - Actual Price) × Actual Quantity Purchased
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
MPV = (SP × AQ) - (AP × AQ)
MPV = (SP - AP) × AQ
```

Where:
- SP = Standard Price per unit
- AP = Actual Price per unit
- AQ = Actual Quantity purchased

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual price < Standard price (paid less than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual price > Standard price (paid more than expected)

**Timing**: Typically calculated at the time of purchase.

**Responsibility**: Usually the purchasing department.

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Effective negotiation with suppliers
- Bulk purchase discounts
- Favorable market conditions
- Finding alternative suppliers
- Purchasing lower-quality materials (may lead to quality issues)

*Unfavorable*:
- Market price increases
- Rush orders requiring premium prices
- Purchasing higher-quality materials
- Ineffective negotiation
- Supplier price increases
- Freight and handling cost increases

### Materials Quantity Variance (MQV)

**Definition**: The difference between the actual quantity of materials used and the standard quantity allowed for actual production, multiplied by the standard price.

**Formula**:
```
MQV = (Standard Quantity - Actual Quantity) × Standard Price
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
MQV = (SQ × SP) - (AQ × SP)
MQV = (SQ - AQ) × SP
```

Where:
- SQ = Standard Quantity allowed for actual output
- AQ = Actual Quantity used
- SP = Standard Price per unit

**Standard Quantity Calculation**:
```
SQ = Standard Quantity per Unit × Actual Units Produced
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual quantity < Standard quantity (used less than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual quantity > Standard quantity (used more than expected)

**Timing**: Calculated when materials are used in production.

**Responsibility**: Usually the production department.

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Efficient use of materials
- Improved production processes
- Better-quality materials (less waste)
- Skilled workforce
- Effective supervision
- Improved equipment

*Unfavorable*:
- Excessive waste or scrap
- Poor-quality materials
- Inadequate training
- Equipment malfunctions
- Theft or loss
- Inaccurate standards
- Design changes

### Total Materials Variance

**Formula**:
```
Total Materials Variance = MPV + MQV
```

Or directly:
```
Total Materials Variance = (SQ × SP) - (AQ × AP)
```

## Direct Labor Variances

### Labor Rate Variance (LRV)

**Definition**: The difference between the actual labor rate paid and the standard labor rate, multiplied by the actual hours worked.

**Formula**:
```
LRV = (Standard Rate - Actual Rate) × Actual Hours
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
LRV = (SR × AH) - (AR × AH)
LRV = (SR - AR) × AH
```

Where:
- SR = Standard Rate per hour
- AR = Actual Rate per hour
- AH = Actual Hours worked

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual rate < Standard rate (paid less per hour than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual rate > Standard rate (paid more per hour than expected)

**Responsibility**: Usually the production or human resources department.

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Using lower-skilled (lower-paid) workers
- Effective wage negotiations
- Less overtime than expected
- Favorable changes in labor market

*Unfavorable*:
- Using higher-skilled (higher-paid) workers
- Wage rate increases
- Excessive overtime
- Shift premiums
- Unexpected bonuses or incentives

### Labor Efficiency Variance (LEV)

**Definition**: The difference between the actual hours worked and the standard hours allowed for actual production, multiplied by the standard labor rate.

**Formula**:
```
LEV = (Standard Hours - Actual Hours) × Standard Rate
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
LEV = (SH × SR) - (AH × SR)
LEV = (SH - AH) × SR
```

Where:
- SH = Standard Hours allowed for actual output
- AH = Actual Hours worked
- SR = Standard Rate per hour

**Standard Hours Calculation**:
```
SH = Standard Hours per Unit × Actual Units Produced
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual hours < Standard hours (took less time than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual hours > Standard hours (took more time than expected)

**Responsibility**: Usually the production department.

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Efficient workforce
- Improved processes or methods
- Better equipment or technology
- Effective training
- High employee motivation
- Better-quality materials (easier to work with)

*Unfavorable*:
- Inefficient workers or inadequate training
- Equipment breakdowns or malfunctions
- Poor-quality materials (difficult to work with)
- Poor supervision
- Low employee morale
- Excessive breaks or idle time
- Rework due to quality problems

### Total Labor Variance

**Formula**:
```
Total Labor Variance = LRV + LEV
```

Or directly:
```
Total Labor Variance = (SH × SR) - (AH × AR)
```

## Variable Manufacturing Overhead Variances

### Variable Overhead Spending Variance

**Definition**: The difference between actual variable overhead costs incurred and the variable overhead costs that should have been incurred for the actual hours worked.

**Formula**:
```
Variable Overhead Spending Variance = (Standard Rate × Actual Hours) - Actual Variable Overhead
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
VOSV = (SR - AR) × AH
```

Where:
- SR = Standard Variable Overhead Rate per hour
- AR = Actual Variable Overhead Rate per hour (Actual VOH / Actual Hours)
- AH = Actual Hours

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Spent less on variable overhead per hour than expected
- **Unfavorable**: Spent more on variable overhead per hour than expected

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Effective cost control
- Lower prices for overhead items
- Efficient use of overhead resources

*Unfavorable*:
- Price increases for overhead items (utilities, supplies)
- Waste or inefficiency in overhead usage
- Unexpected overhead costs

### Variable Overhead Efficiency Variance

**Definition**: The difference between the actual hours worked and the standard hours allowed for actual production, multiplied by the standard variable overhead rate.

**Formula**:
```
Variable Overhead Efficiency Variance = (Standard Hours - Actual Hours) × Standard Variable Overhead Rate
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
VOEV = (SH - AH) × SR
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual hours < Standard hours (efficient use of the overhead base)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual hours > Standard hours (inefficient use of the overhead base)

**Note**: This variance is driven by the same factors that affect labor efficiency variance when labor hours are the allocation base.

### Total Variable Overhead Variance

**Formula**:
```
Total Variable Overhead Variance = Variable Overhead Spending Variance + Variable Overhead Efficiency Variance
```

Or directly:
```
Total Variable Overhead Variance = (SH × SR) - Actual Variable Overhead
```

## Fixed Manufacturing Overhead Variances

### Fixed Overhead Budget Variance (Spending Variance)

**Definition**: The difference between actual fixed overhead costs incurred and budgeted fixed overhead costs.

**Formula**:
```
Fixed Overhead Budget Variance = Budgeted Fixed Overhead - Actual Fixed Overhead
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual fixed overhead < Budgeted fixed overhead
- **Unfavorable**: Actual fixed overhead > Budgeted fixed overhead

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Effective cost control
- Lower-than-expected fixed costs (rent, insurance, salaries)
- Delayed expenditures

*Unfavorable*:
- Unexpected increases in fixed costs
- Poor cost control
- Unplanned expenditures
- Salary increases

### Fixed Overhead Volume Variance

**Definition**: The difference between budgeted fixed overhead and the fixed overhead applied to production based on standard hours allowed for actual output.

**Formula**:
```
Fixed Overhead Volume Variance = Budgeted Fixed Overhead - Applied Fixed Overhead
```

Where:
```
Applied Fixed Overhead = Standard Hours Allowed × Standard Fixed Overhead Rate
```

**Alternative Formula**:
```
FOVV = (Budgeted Hours - Standard Hours Allowed) × Standard Fixed Overhead Rate
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Produced more than budgeted capacity (over-applied overhead)
- **Unfavorable**: Produced less than budgeted capacity (under-applied overhead)

**Note**: This variance measures capacity utilization, not spending efficiency.

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Higher-than-expected production volume
- Efficient use of capacity
- Increased demand

*Unfavorable*:
- Lower-than-expected production volume
- Idle capacity
- Reduced demand
- Production delays or interruptions

### Total Fixed Overhead Variance

**Formula**:
```
Total Fixed Overhead Variance = Fixed Overhead Budget Variance + Fixed Overhead Volume Variance
```

Or directly:
```
Total Fixed Overhead Variance = Applied Fixed Overhead - Actual Fixed Overhead
```

## Sales Variances

### Sales Price Variance

**Definition**: The difference between the actual selling price and the budgeted selling price, multiplied by the actual quantity sold.

**Formula**:
```
Sales Price Variance = (Actual Price - Budgeted Price) × Actual Quantity Sold
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual price > Budgeted price (sold at higher price than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual price < Budgeted price (sold at lower price than expected)

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Effective pricing strategies
- Strong market demand
- Premium product positioning
- Reduced discounting

*Unfavorable*:
- Competitive pressure
- Excessive discounting
- Weak market demand
- Price concessions to customers

### Sales Volume Variance

**Definition**: The difference between the actual quantity sold and the budgeted quantity, multiplied by the budgeted contribution margin per unit.

**Formula**:
```
Sales Volume Variance = (Actual Quantity - Budgeted Quantity) × Budgeted Contribution Margin per Unit
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Favorable**: Actual quantity > Budgeted quantity (sold more than expected)
- **Unfavorable**: Actual quantity < Budgeted quantity (sold less than expected)

**Possible Causes**:

*Favorable*:
- Effective marketing and sales efforts
- Strong market demand
- Competitive advantages
- Market share gains

*Unfavorable*:
- Weak market demand
- Competitive pressure
- Product quality issues
- Ineffective marketing
- Supply chain disruptions

## Flexible Budget Variance Analysis

### Static Budget vs. Flexible Budget

**Static Budget**:
- Prepared for a single planned level of activity
- Does not adjust for actual activity levels
- Comparisons can be misleading when actual volume differs from planned

**Flexible Budget**:
- Adjusts for actual activity levels
- Shows what costs should have been for actual output
- Enables meaningful performance comparisons

### Flexible Budget Formula

```
Flexible Budget Amount = Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost per Unit × Actual Activity Level)
```

### Three-Way Variance Analysis

**Total Variance** = Actual Results - Static Budget

This can be decomposed into:

1. **Flexible Budget Variance** = Actual Results - Flexible Budget
   - Measures efficiency and spending control
   - Isolates the impact of price and efficiency differences

2. **Sales Volume Variance** = Flexible Budget - Static Budget
   - Measures the impact of actual volume differing from planned volume
   - Shows the profit impact of selling more or fewer units than budgeted

### Variance Analysis Hierarchy

```
Total Variance
├── Flexible Budget Variance
│   ├── Price/Rate Variances
│   └── Efficiency/Quantity Variances
└── Sales Volume Variance
    ├── Sales Mix Variance (for multiple products)
    └── Market Size Variance (for multiple products)
```

## Investigating and Interpreting Variances

### Materiality and Significance

Not all variances warrant investigation. Focus on:

**Quantitative Criteria**:
- Absolute dollar amount exceeds threshold
- Percentage deviation exceeds threshold (e.g., >5% or >10%)
- Variance is statistically significant

**Qualitative Criteria**:
- Variance is controllable
- Variance is recurring or shows a trend
- Variance relates to strategic priorities
- Variance has potential for corrective action

### Root Cause Analysis

When investigating significant variances:

1. **Gather Data**: Collect detailed information about the variance
2. **Interview Stakeholders**: Talk to managers and employees involved
3. **Analyze Processes**: Examine the processes that generated the variance
4. **Identify Root Causes**: Use techniques like:
   - Five Whys
   - Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams
   - Pareto analysis
5. **Distinguish Causes**:
   - Controllable vs. uncontrollable
   - Internal vs. external
   - Temporary vs. permanent
6. **Develop Action Plans**: Create specific, actionable responses

### Interrelated Variances

Variances often interact and should be analyzed together:

**Materials Price and Quantity**:
- Favorable price variance from buying cheaper materials may cause unfavorable quantity variance due to higher waste

**Labor Rate and Efficiency**:
- Favorable rate variance from using less-skilled workers may cause unfavorable efficiency variance due to slower work

**Labor Efficiency and Variable Overhead Efficiency**:
- These move together when labor hours are the overhead allocation base

**Sales Price and Volume**:
- Lowering prices (unfavorable price variance) may increase volume (favorable volume variance)

### Behavioral Considerations

**Positive Uses**:
- Identify improvement opportunities
- Recognize and reward good performance
- Support learning and development
- Guide resource allocation

**Potential Dysfunctions**:
- Gaming the system (manipulating results to look good)
- Short-term focus at expense of long-term goals
- Blame culture rather than learning culture
- Suboptimization (optimizing one area at expense of overall performance)

**Best Practices**:
- Use variances for learning, not punishment
- Consider controllability when evaluating performance
- Balance financial and non-financial measures
- Focus on trends, not single-period results
- Encourage open communication about variances

## Reporting Variance Analysis

### Effective Variance Reports

**Key Elements**:
1. **Clear Presentation**: Use tables, charts, and visual aids
2. **Hierarchy**: Show total variance broken down into components
3. **Favorable/Unfavorable Indicators**: Use clear notation (F/U, colors, symbols)
4. **Materiality Focus**: Highlight significant variances
5. **Trend Information**: Show current and prior period variances
6. **Explanations**: Provide brief explanations of major variances
7. **Action Plans**: Include corrective actions or recommendations
8. **Responsibility Assignment**: Indicate who is accountable

### Variance Report Format Example

```
| Cost Element | Actual | Budget | Variance | % | F/U | Explanation |
|--------------|--------|--------|----------|---|-----|-------------|
| Direct Materials | $X | $Y | $Z | n% | U | Higher material prices |
| Direct Labor | $X | $Y | $Z | n% | F | Improved efficiency |
| Variable OH | $X | $Y | $Z | n% | U | Increased utility costs |
| Fixed OH | $X | $Y | $Z | n% | F | Lower depreciation |
| Total | $X | $Y | $Z | n% | U | |
```

### Dashboard and Visualization

Modern variance reporting often uses:
- **Traffic Light Indicators**: Green (favorable), yellow (caution), red (unfavorable)
- **Variance Charts**: Bar charts showing favorable and unfavorable variances
- **Trend Lines**: Showing variance patterns over time
- **Heat Maps**: Highlighting areas of concern
- **Drill-Down Capability**: Allowing users to explore details

## Advanced Variance Analysis Topics

### Mix and Yield Variances

For processes using multiple inputs:

**Mix Variance**: Impact of using a different proportion of inputs than standard

**Yield Variance**: Impact of getting a different output quantity from a given input quantity than standard

### Sales Mix and Quantity Variances

For companies with multiple products:

**Sales Mix Variance**: Impact of selling a different product mix than budgeted

**Sales Quantity Variance**: Impact of selling a different total quantity than budgeted

### Market Size and Market Share Variances

**Market Size Variance**: Impact of the total market being larger or smaller than expected

**Market Share Variance**: Impact of the company's market share being higher or lower than expected

### Revenue Variances in Service Industries

For service organizations:
- **Rate Variance**: Difference in billing rates
- **Efficiency Variance**: Difference in hours billed vs. standard
- **Capacity Variance**: Difference in capacity utilization

## Conclusion

Variance analysis is a powerful tool for cost control, performance evaluation, and continuous improvement. Effective variance analysis requires:

- Accurate and timely data
- Appropriate standards and budgets
- Focus on material and controllable variances
- Thorough investigation of root causes
- Consideration of interrelated variances
- Constructive use of information for learning and improvement
- Clear communication through effective reporting

When properly implemented, variance analysis provides actionable insights that drive operational excellence and financial performance.
