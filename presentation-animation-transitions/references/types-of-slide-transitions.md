## Types of Slide Transitions



### Cut/None

The simplest transition—immediate switch from one slide to the next.

**Best For**:
- Fast-paced presentations
- Technical or data-heavy content
- Professional/corporate contexts
- When content flow is more important than visual effect

**When to Use**: Default choice when transitions shouldn't distract from content.



### Fade

Gradual opacity change where one slide fades out while the next fades in.

**Variations**:
- Fade Through Black: Current slide fades to black, then new slide fades in
- Cross-Fade: Simultaneous fade out/in creates blend
- Fade Duration: Short (0.3s) for subtle, longer (1s+) for dramatic

**Best For**:
- Topic or section changes
- Emotional or contemplative moments
- Professional presentations
- Universal compatibility across platforms



### Push/Slide

The new slide pushes the current slide off-screen in a specified direction.

**Variations**:
- Push Left/Right: Horizontal progression
- Push Up/Down: Vertical movement
- Cover: New slide covers old (slides over)
- Uncover: Old slide reveals new underneath

**Best For**:
- Sequential processes
- Timeline presentations
- Narrative progression
- Implying forward movement



### Wipe

A line moves across the screen, revealing the new slide behind it.

**Variations**:
- Horizontal/Vertical Wipe
- Diagonal Wipe
- Clock Wipe: Circular reveal
- Wedge Wipe: Expanding triangular reveal

**Best For**:
- Before/after comparisons
- Time progression
- Revealing hidden information
- Creative presentations



### Zoom

Scaling effect that zooms into or out of content.

**Variations**:
- Zoom In: Camera appears to move closer
- Zoom Out: Camera appears to pull back
- Zoom to Object: Focuses on specific element
- Prezi-Style Zoom: Canvas-based navigation

**Best For**:
- Focusing on details within larger context
- Map or diagram navigation
- Hierarchy exploration
- Modern, dynamic presentations



### Rotate

The slide rotates during transition.

**Variations**:
- Flip: Card-turning effect
- Revolve: Full rotation
- Swing: Pivot from edge
- Cube: 3D box rotation

**Best For**:
- Playful presentations
- Revealing "other side" of topic
- Creative industries
- Use sparingly in professional contexts



### 3D Transitions

Three-dimensional effects creating depth and spatial movement.

**Examples**:
- Cube: Sides of rotating cube
- Flip: 3D card flip
- Gallery: Image gallery rotation
- Origami: Paper-folding effect

**Best For**:
- High-impact moments
- Creative presentations
- Technology showcases
- Limited use to avoid gimmickry



### Morph/Magic Move

The most sophisticated transition type—intelligently animating matching objects between slides.

**How It Works**: Objects with the same name or similar characteristics on consecutive slides are transformed smoothly, creating the illusion of continuous animation.

**Capabilities**:
- Object position changes (movement)
- Size transformations (scaling)
- Rotation animations
- Color transitions
- Shape morphing
- Text transformations

**Best For**:
- Seamless visual storytelling
- Complex animations without manual setup
- Professional presentations
- Demonstrating change over time
