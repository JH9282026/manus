# Creative Writing & Storytelling

Craft compelling narratives with strong characters, effective structure, and polished prose across fiction, non-fiction, and content storytelling.

## Overview

Storytelling is the most powerful communication tool — whether writing a novel, crafting a brand narrative, or structuring a presentation. Strong stories share universal elements: clear structure, compelling characters, meaningful conflict, and resonant themes. This skill covers story structure frameworks, character development, prose technique, dialogue craft, and genre-specific conventions for both creative and commercial storytelling.

## Story Structure Selection Guide

| Structure | Form | Best For | Complexity | Reference |
|-----------|------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Three-Act Structure | Setup → Confrontation → Resolution | Most stories, screenplays, novels | Low–Medium | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| Hero's Journey (12 stages) | Ordinary World → Call → Ordeal → Return | Epic adventures, transformation arcs | Medium | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| Save the Cat (15 beats) | Specific beat sheet with page targets | Screenplays, commercial fiction | Medium | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| Kishōtenketsu (4-act) | Introduction → Development → Twist → Conclusion | Stories without conflict-driven plots | Medium | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| In Medias Res | Start in the middle of action | Thrillers, action, grabbing attention fast | Low | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| Frame narrative | Story within a story | Literary fiction, unreliable narrator | High | `/references/story-structures.md` |
| Nonlinear/fragmented | Non-chronological events | Literary fiction, mystery reveals | High | `/references/story-structures.md` |

### Three-Act Structure Quick Reference

| Act | Proportion | Key Events | Purpose |
|-----|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Act 1 (Setup) | 25% | Hook, inciting incident, first plot point | Establish world, character, stakes |
| Act 2 (Confrontation) | 50% | Rising action, midpoint shift, dark moment | Escalate conflict, develop character |
| Act 3 (Resolution) | 25% | Climax, falling action, resolution | Pay off promises, resolve conflict |

### Key Story Beats

| Beat | Position | Function | Example |
|------|----------|----------|---------|
| Opening image | Page 1 | Set tone and world | Before-state snapshot |
| Inciting incident | 10–15% | Disrupt the status quo | "You're fired" / "There's been a murder" |
| First plot point | 25% | Lock protagonist into the story | Point of no return |
| Midpoint | 50% | Shift from reactive to proactive | New information changes everything |
| All is lost | 75% | Lowest point, seems impossible | Mentor dies, plan fails, betrayal |
| Climax | 85–90% | Protagonist faces ultimate challenge | Final confrontation |
| Resolution | 95–100% | New normal established | Changed world or character |

## Character Development Framework

### Character Dimensions

| Dimension | What to Define | Why It Matters | Reference |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| Want (external goal) | What the character pursues | Drives plot forward | `/references/character-development.md` |
| Need (internal growth) | What they must learn/overcome | Creates character arc | `/references/character-development.md` |
| Flaw | Psychological or moral weakness | Makes character human and interesting | `/references/character-development.md` |
| Ghost/wound | Past event shaping current behavior | Explains the flaw, creates empathy | `/references/character-development.md` |
| Voice | How they speak, think, perceive | Distinguishes from other characters | `/references/character-development.md` |
| Stakes | What they lose if they fail | Creates tension and urgency | `/references/character-development.md` |

### Character Arc Types

| Arc Type | Pattern | Example | Tone |
|----------|---------|---------|------|
| Positive change | Flaw → Growth → Overcomes flaw | Scrooge (A Christmas Carol) | Hopeful, redemptive |
| Negative change | Good → Corrupted → Falls | Walter White (Breaking Bad) | Tragic, cautionary |
| Flat/testing | Stays true despite pressure | James Bond, Atticus Finch | Reassuring, heroic |
| Disillusionment | Belief → Challenge → Abandons belief | Coming-of-age narratives | Bittersweet, realistic |

## Dialogue Craft

### Dialogue Functions

| Function | Example | Guideline |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Reveal character | "I don't do apologies" — shows stubborn pride | Every line should sound like only THIS character would say it |
| Advance plot | "They found the body" — new information | Don't use dialogue for pure information delivery |
| Create conflict | "That's not what you said yesterday" — tension | Subtext is more powerful than direct confrontation |
| Control pacing | Short exchanges speed up; monologues slow down | Match dialogue rhythm to scene tension |
| Establish relationship | Formal = distance, casual = closeness | How characters address each other reveals dynamics |

### Dialogue Rules
- **Cut greetings and pleasantries** — start scenes mid-conversation
- **Each character needs a distinct voice** — cover the names; can you tell who's speaking?
- **Use subtext** — what's NOT said is often more powerful than what is said
- **Read dialogue aloud** — if it sounds unnatural spoken, it reads unnatural
- **Keep dialogue tags simple** — "said" is invisible to readers; "exclaimed", "opined" draw attention to themselves
- **Avoid exposition in dialogue** — characters shouldn't explain things they both already know

## Prose Technique

### Show vs. Tell Decision Guide

| Situation | Show (scene) | Tell (summary) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Emotionally important moment | ✅ Always show | ❌ Never tell |
| Character-defining action | ✅ Show through behavior | ❌ Don't state traits directly |
| Transition between scenes | ❌ Don't dramatize | ✅ Summarize briefly |
| Passage of time | ❌ Don't show every day | ✅ "Three weeks passed" |
| Backstory | ⚠️ Show in small doses | ✅ Brief summary or dialogue |

### Prose Style Elements

| Element | Technique | Effect | Reference |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Sentence rhythm | Vary length: long for flow, short for impact | Controls pacing and emphasis | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |
| Sensory detail | Engage all five senses, not just sight | Immersive experience | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |
| Specificity | "1987 Toyota Corolla" not "old car" | Creates vivid mental image | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |
| Active voice | Subject → verb → object | Stronger, more direct prose | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |
| Metaphor/simile | Compare unlike things for insight | Creates resonance and beauty | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |
| White space | Short paragraphs, scene breaks | Improves readability, creates rhythm | `/references/prose-techniques.md` |

## Genre Conventions

| Genre | Reader Expectation | Must Include | Avoid |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| Literary fiction | Beautiful prose, complex themes | Character depth, moral ambiguity | Cliché, predictable endings |
| Thriller/suspense | Page-turning tension, high stakes | Ticking clock, twists, physical danger | Slow pacing in Act 2 |
| Romance | Emotional connection, satisfying ending | Meet-cute, obstacles, HEA/HFN ending | Killing the love interest |
| Mystery | Puzzle, clues, satisfying solution | Fair clues, red herrings, surprise reveal | Deus ex machina solutions |
| Fantasy | World-building, magic systems | Consistent rules, stakes despite magic | Info-dumping world details |
| Science fiction | Ideas, extrapolation, wonder | Internal consistency, "what if?" premise | Breaking own established rules |

## Best Practices

- **Write the first draft fast, edit slow** — perfectionism during drafting kills momentum
- **Make the protagonist active** — things shouldn't just happen TO them; they should drive action
- **Enter scenes late, leave early** — cut setup and aftermath; start at the interesting part
- **Every scene needs conflict** — even quiet scenes need tension, disagreement, or unresolved desire
- **Kill your darlings** — if a beautiful sentence doesn't serve the story, cut it
- **Read widely in your genre** — you must know the conventions to use (or subvert) them effectively
