# DOOH Creative Best Practices

## Introduction

Creating effective Digital Out-of-Home (DOOH) advertising requires a different approach than other digital formats. DOOH creative must work in challenging conditions—viewed from a distance, often while audiences are in motion, competing with environmental distractions, and displayed in varying lighting conditions. Success requires understanding the unique constraints and opportunities of the medium and designing accordingly.

This guide provides comprehensive best practices for creating DOOH creative that captures attention, communicates clearly, and drives results.

## Fundamental Design Principles

### Simplicity is Essential

**The Challenge**: DOOH audiences have limited time to process messages—often just 2-10 seconds. Complex designs or lengthy copy will not be absorbed.

**Best Practices**:

**Minimal Text**: Use 7 words or fewer for high-speed locations (highways, busy streets). Place-based media with longer dwell times can accommodate more text, but brevity is still crucial.

**Single Message**: Communicate one clear idea per creative. Avoid trying to convey multiple messages or complex concepts.

**Clear Hierarchy**: Use size, color, and placement to guide the eye to the most important elements in order of priority.

**Eliminate Clutter**: Remove unnecessary elements. Every component should serve a purpose.

**Examples**:
- ✅ "Summer Sale. 50% Off. ShopNow.com"
- ❌ "Visit our website to learn about our extensive summer clearance event featuring discounts up to 50% off on selected items from our spring and summer collections including..."

### Bold and Large

**The Challenge**: DOOH is viewed from significant distances—often 50-500+ feet away. Small or subtle elements will not be visible.

**Best Practices**:

**Large Typography**: Use oversized, bold fonts that are readable from a distance. Minimum recommended font size varies by viewing distance:
- Highway billboards: 5-6 feet tall letters for primary message
- Urban panels: 12-18 inches for primary message
- Place-based screens: 6-12 inches depending on viewing distance

**High Contrast**: Ensure strong contrast between text and background for readability in all lighting conditions. Black on white, white on black, or bold colors on contrasting backgrounds work best.

**Bold Imagery**: Use large, clear images that are instantly recognizable. Avoid small details or intricate patterns that won't be visible.

**Thick Strokes**: Use fonts with thick strokes rather than thin, delicate typefaces. Sans-serif fonts generally work better than serif for DOOH.

### Strong Branding

**The Challenge**: Audiences need to instantly recognize who the advertiser is, even if they don't read the entire message.

**Best Practices**:

**Prominent Logo**: Include a large, clearly visible logo. Don't relegate branding to a small corner.

**Brand Colors**: Use distinctive brand colors consistently to aid recognition.

**Brand Voice**: Ensure messaging aligns with brand personality and tone.

**Consistent Style**: Maintain visual consistency with other brand touchpoints.

**Placement**: Position logo where it will be seen—typically top or bottom, large enough to be instantly recognizable.

### Motion and Animation

**The Challenge**: Motion attracts attention in static environments, but excessive or complex animation can be distracting or difficult to follow.

**Best Practices**:

**Purposeful Motion**: Use animation to attract attention, guide the eye, or reveal information sequentially. Avoid motion for motion's sake.

**Simple Animations**: Keep animations simple and easy to follow. Avoid rapid, complex sequences.

**Appropriate Speed**: Match animation speed to viewing context—slower for high-speed locations where viewers have brief exposure, faster for place-based media with longer dwell times.

**Clear Transitions**: Use smooth, clear transitions between elements. Avoid jarring cuts or rapid flashing.

**Static Moments**: Include moments of stillness to allow message absorption. Don't keep everything in constant motion.

**Looping**: Ensure animations loop smoothly for continuous display.

**Examples of Effective Motion**:
- Text sliding in to reveal key message
- Product rotating to show different angles
- Sequential reveal of benefits or features
- Subtle background movement to add energy

**Avoid**:
- Rapid flashing or strobing (can cause discomfort, may violate regulations)
- Complex multi-element animations
- Text that moves too quickly to read
- Excessive motion that distracts from message

## Format-Specific Considerations

### Large Format Billboards

**Context**: Viewed from vehicles traveling 30-70+ mph, viewing time 2-10 seconds, viewing distance 100-500+ feet.

**Creative Guidelines**:

**Ultra-Simple Messaging**: Maximum 5-7 words

**Huge Typography**: Letters 4-6 feet tall for primary message

**Bold Imagery**: Large, instantly recognizable images

**High Contrast**: Ensure visibility in all lighting and weather

**Minimal Elements**: Logo, headline, image—that's it

**No Fine Print**: Avoid disclaimers, URLs with complex paths, or detailed information

**Directional Messaging**: Consider including directional cues ("Next Exit", "2 Miles Ahead") if relevant

**Example Structure**:
- Large product image or brand visual (60% of space)
- Short, bold headline (30% of space)
- Logo (10% of space)

### Transit Advertising

**Context**: Viewed by waiting passengers or commuters, dwell time 30 seconds to several minutes, viewing distance 10-50 feet.

**Creative Guidelines**:

**Moderate Complexity**: Can include more information than billboards but still keep it concise

**Readable Typography**: Large enough to read from platform or across train car

**Engaging Visuals**: Capture attention in busy environments

**Clear CTA**: Include specific action (visit website, download app, use promo code)

**Contextual Relevance**: Consider commuter mindset and needs

**Sequential Messaging**: For multiple screens, consider telling a story across them

**Example Structure**:
- Attention-grabbing visual or headline
- 1-2 sentences of supporting copy
- Clear call-to-action
- Logo and branding

### Place-Based Media

**Context**: Viewed in specific venues (gyms, malls, offices), extended dwell time (1-10+ minutes), viewing distance 5-20 feet.

**Creative Guidelines**:

**More Information**: Can include more detailed messaging given longer viewing time

**Contextual Relevance**: Tailor messaging to venue and audience mindset

**Engaging Content**: Hold attention over longer periods

**Interactive Elements**: Consider QR codes, social media prompts, or other engagement mechanisms

**Video Content**: Can use longer-form video (30-60 seconds) if appropriate

**Demonstration**: Show product in use or demonstrate benefits

**Example Structure**:
- Engaging visual or video
- Headline and supporting copy (2-3 sentences)
- Product benefits or features
- Clear call-to-action with QR code or URL
- Logo and branding

### Urban Panels and Street Furniture

**Context**: Viewed by pedestrians, viewing time 5-30 seconds, viewing distance 5-30 feet.

**Creative Guidelines**:

**Pedestrian-Friendly**: Design for walking speed and eye-level viewing

**Readable Text**: Large enough to read while walking

**Engaging Visuals**: Capture attention in busy urban environments

**Interactive Potential**: Consider QR codes, NFC, or other mobile interactions

**Neighborhood Relevance**: Tailor to local context and audience

**Portrait Orientation**: Many urban panels are portrait format—design accordingly

**Example Structure**:
- Bold visual or headline
- 1-2 lines of supporting copy
- Call-to-action with QR code
- Logo

### Point-of-Purchase

**Context**: Viewed by shoppers in retail environments, brief exposure while moving through store, viewing distance 3-15 feet.

**Creative Guidelines**:

**Product Focus**: Showcase the product clearly

**Impulse Trigger**: Create desire and urgency

**Promotional Messaging**: Highlight offers, discounts, or limited-time deals

**Simple CTA**: "Buy Now", "Try Today", "Limited Time"

**Brand Recognition**: Ensure product and brand are instantly identifiable

**Appetite Appeal**: For food/beverage, use high-quality, appetizing imagery

**Example Structure**:
- Large product image
- Promotional offer ("$2 Off Today")
- Brand logo
- Simple CTA

## Technical Specifications

### File Formats

**Video**: MP4 (H.264 codec) is the most widely supported format
- Recommended for animated or video content
- Ensure compatibility with target platforms

**Static Images**: JPG or PNG
- JPG for photographic content
- PNG for graphics with transparency or sharp edges

**Animated Graphics**: Animated GIF or MP4
- GIF for simple animations
- MP4 for more complex or longer animations

**Check Platform Requirements**: Always verify specific format requirements for your target platforms

### Resolution and Aspect Ratios

**Common Resolutions**:
- Full HD: 1920x1080 (minimum for most DOOH)
- 4K: 3840x2160 (for premium placements)
- Custom resolutions for specific screen sizes

**Common Aspect Ratios**:
- 16:9 (landscape): Most common for large format and transit
- 9:16 (portrait): Increasingly common for urban panels and street furniture
- 1:1 (square): Some urban and place-based screens
- Custom ratios: Various non-standard ratios exist

**Best Practice**: Create flexible design systems that can adapt to multiple aspect ratios rather than creating unique designs for each

### File Size

**Typical Limits**: 50-200MB depending on platform

**Optimization**:
- Balance quality with file size
- Compress video efficiently without visible quality loss
- Use appropriate bitrates (typically 5-10 Mbps for Full HD)
- Test files on target platforms before full deployment

**Considerations**: Larger files may take longer to load or update, potentially causing delays or technical issues

### Duration

**Standard Duration**: 10-15 seconds for most DOOH placements

**Variations**:
- 6-8 seconds: Some high-rotation environments
- 15-30 seconds: Premium placements or place-based media
- 30-60 seconds: Long-form content for captive audiences

**Design for Duration**: Ensure your message is communicated within the allotted time

### Safe Zones

**Account for Bezels**: Screen edges may be obscured by frames or bezels

**Safe Zone Guidelines**: Keep critical elements (text, logos, CTAs) within 90% of frame

**Test on Actual Screens**: If possible, preview creative on actual screens to ensure nothing is cut off

## Dynamic Creative Optimization (DCO)

### What is DCO?

Dynamic Creative Optimization enables real-time adaptation of creative content based on data signals such as weather, time of day, location, audience, or performance.

### Weather-Triggered Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Promote umbrellas, raincoats, or indoor activities when raining
- Advertise cold beverages, sunscreen, or outdoor activities on hot days
- Showcase winter products during cold weather
- Adjust messaging based on temperature, precipitation, or conditions

**Implementation**: Set up creative variants for different weather conditions and rules for when each displays

**Example**: Coffee brand shows hot coffee creative when temperature is below 50°F, iced coffee when above 70°F

### Time-Based Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Breakfast items in morning, lunch midday, dinner in evening
- Happy hour promotions in late afternoon
- Different messaging for weekdays vs. weekends
- Seasonal campaigns that automatically update

**Implementation**: Create creative variants for different times and set dayparting rules

**Example**: Restaurant shows breakfast menu 6-10am, lunch 11am-2pm, dinner 5-9pm

### Location-Based Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Directional messaging based on proximity ("2 blocks away", "Next exit")
- Neighborhood-specific offers or messaging
- Language adaptation for different markets
- Local event or sports team references

**Implementation**: Create location-specific creative variants and target to appropriate screens

**Example**: Retail chain shows different products based on local inventory or preferences

### Event-Triggered Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Celebrate local sports team wins
- Respond to local events or festivals
- Adjust for traffic or transit delays
- React to breaking news (with appropriate brand safety)

**Implementation**: Set up triggers based on event data feeds and corresponding creative

**Example**: Sports bar shows celebratory message when local team wins, consolation offer when they lose

### Performance-Based Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Automatically shift to better-performing creative variants
- Test multiple approaches and scale winners
- Optimize messaging based on conversion data

**Implementation**: Set up A/B tests with multiple creative variants and enable automatic optimization

**Example**: E-commerce brand tests different product images and automatically shows top performers

### Inventory-Based Creative

**Use Cases**:
- Promote products currently in stock
- Highlight sale items
- Adjust based on inventory levels

**Implementation**: Integrate creative system with inventory management and set rules for what to display

**Example**: Auto dealer shows specific vehicles currently on lot

## Creative Testing and Optimization

### Elements to Test

**Messaging**:
- Different headlines or value propositions
- Benefit-focused vs. feature-focused
- Emotional vs. rational appeals
- Urgency and scarcity language

**Visuals**:
- Product images vs. lifestyle imagery
- Different color schemes
- Photography vs. illustration
- With or without people

**Calls-to-Action**:
- Different CTAs ("Shop Now", "Learn More", "Visit Today")
- With or without incentives
- QR codes vs. URLs vs. no specific CTA

**Layout and Design**:
- Different compositions and hierarchies
- Text placement and sizing
- Logo prominence and placement

**Animation**:
- Static vs. animated
- Different animation styles or speeds
- Sequential reveal vs. all-at-once

### Testing Methodology

**A/B Testing**: Run two creative variants simultaneously and compare performance

**Multivariate Testing**: Test multiple elements simultaneously to identify optimal combinations

**Sequential Testing**: Test variants one after another to compare performance over time

**Geographic Testing**: Run different creative in different markets to compare

**Holdout Testing**: Run creative in some locations, not others, to measure incremental impact

### Metrics for Creative Performance

**Attention Metrics**: Dwell time, gaze tracking (if available)

**Engagement Metrics**: QR code scans, website visits, app downloads

**Conversion Metrics**: Store visits, purchases, sign-ups

**Brand Metrics**: Recall, awareness, perception (from brand lift studies)

**Comparative Metrics**: Performance relative to other creative variants

### Optimization Process

1. **Launch Multiple Variants**: Start with 2-4 creative variants

2. **Collect Data**: Run for sufficient time to gather meaningful data (typically 1-2 weeks minimum)

3. **Analyze Performance**: Compare variants across key metrics

4. **Identify Winners**: Determine which creative performs best

5. **Scale Winners**: Shift more budget/impressions to top performers

6. **Iterate**: Create new variants based on learnings and continue testing

7. **Document Insights**: Capture learnings to inform future creative

## Common Creative Mistakes to Avoid

### Too Much Text

**Problem**: Lengthy copy that can't be read in available viewing time

**Solution**: Ruthlessly edit to essential message only. Use 7 words or fewer for high-speed locations.

### Small or Thin Typography

**Problem**: Text that's not readable from viewing distance

**Solution**: Use large, bold fonts with thick strokes. Test readability from actual viewing distance.

### Low Contrast

**Problem**: Text or elements that don't stand out from background

**Solution**: Ensure strong contrast between all elements. Test in various lighting conditions.

### Cluttered Design

**Problem**: Too many elements competing for attention

**Solution**: Simplify ruthlessly. Remove anything that doesn't directly support the core message.

### Weak Branding

**Problem**: Logo too small or brand not clearly identifiable

**Solution**: Make logo prominent. Use distinctive brand colors and style.

### Unclear Message

**Problem**: Audience doesn't understand what's being advertised or what action to take

**Solution**: Lead with clear benefit or offer. Include obvious call-to-action.

### Excessive Animation

**Problem**: Complex or rapid animation that's distracting or hard to follow

**Solution**: Use simple, purposeful motion. Include static moments for message absorption.

### Ignoring Context

**Problem**: Creative that doesn't fit the viewing environment or audience mindset

**Solution**: Design specifically for the format, location, and audience context.

### Poor Image Quality

**Problem**: Low-resolution or pixelated images on large screens

**Solution**: Use high-resolution assets appropriate for screen size. Test on actual screens.

### Adapting from Other Channels

**Problem**: Simply repurposing creative from other channels without DOOH optimization

**Solution**: Create DOOH-specific creative designed for the medium's unique requirements.

## Creative Production Workflow

### 1. Strategy and Briefing

- Define campaign objectives and KPIs
- Identify target audience and contexts
- Determine key message and value proposition
- Establish brand guidelines and requirements
- Specify formats, sizes, and technical requirements

### 2. Concept Development

- Brainstorm creative approaches
- Develop multiple concepts
- Create rough sketches or mockups
- Review and select direction

### 3. Design and Production

- Create detailed designs
- Develop copy and messaging
- Source or create imagery
- Design animations or video
- Ensure technical compliance

### 4. Review and Approval

- Internal review
- Client or stakeholder approval
- Legal and compliance review if needed
- Revisions as necessary

### 5. Technical Preparation

- Export in required formats and specifications
- Optimize file sizes
- Test on platforms
- Prepare multiple versions for different formats

### 6. Deployment

- Upload to platforms
- Assign to campaigns and placements
- Verify correct display
- Monitor for technical issues

### 7. Performance Monitoring

- Track creative performance metrics
- Compare variants
- Identify optimization opportunities

### 8. Optimization and Iteration

- Update or replace underperforming creative
- Scale top performers
- Create new variants based on learnings
- Continuous improvement

## Conclusion

Effective DOOH creative requires understanding the unique constraints and opportunities of the medium—brief viewing times, viewing distances, environmental context, and the ability to deliver dynamic, contextually relevant messages. By following these best practices—keeping designs simple and bold, optimizing for viewing conditions, leveraging dynamic creative capabilities, and continuously testing and optimizing—advertisers can create DOOH campaigns that capture attention, communicate clearly, and drive results. As DOOH technology continues to evolve with AI, AR, and advanced interactivity, the creative possibilities will only expand, offering ever more innovative ways to engage audiences in the physical world.
