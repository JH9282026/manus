# Strategic Planning Frameworks

Detailed guidance on major strategic planning frameworks and their application.

---

## SWOT Analysis

### Overview
SWOT Analysis examines internal Strengths and Weaknesses, and external Opportunities and Threats to provide a comprehensive view of an organization's strategic position.

### When to Use
- Initial strategic assessments
- Market entry decisions
- Evaluating major strategic decisions
- Annual strategic planning sessions
- Competitive positioning analysis

### Methodology

**1. Identify Strengths (Internal, Positive)**
- Core competencies and capabilities
- Unique resources and assets
- Strong brand reputation
- Skilled personnel and expertise
- Proprietary technology or intellectual property
- Efficient processes and systems
- Financial resources and stability
- Strong customer relationships

**2. Identify Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)**
- Skill gaps and capability limitations
- Outdated systems or technology
- Limited financial resources
- Poor location or facilities
- Weak brand recognition
- Inefficient processes
- High employee turnover
- Limited market presence

**3. Identify Opportunities (External, Positive)**
- Emerging markets and customer segments
- Technological advancements
- Regulatory changes favoring the business
- Competitor weaknesses
- Strategic partnerships potential
- Changing customer preferences
- Economic growth trends
- Industry consolidation opportunities

**4. Identify Threats (External, Negative)**
- New competitors entering market
- Changing regulations
- Economic downturns
- Technological disruption
- Changing customer preferences
- Supply chain vulnerabilities
- Political instability
- Environmental challenges

### Analysis and Strategy Development

**SO Strategies (Strength-Opportunity)**
- Use strengths to capitalize on opportunities
- Example: Leverage strong R&D to enter emerging technology markets

**WO Strategies (Weakness-Opportunity)**
- Overcome weaknesses to pursue opportunities
- Example: Partner with technology firms to address capability gaps

**ST Strategies (Strength-Threat)**
- Use strengths to mitigate threats
- Example: Use brand strength to defend against new competitors

**WT Strategies (Weakness-Threat)**
- Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats
- Example: Divest weak business units in declining markets

### Best Practices
- Involve diverse stakeholders for comprehensive perspectives
- Use data and evidence to support assessments
- Prioritize factors by impact and urgency
- Update SWOT analysis regularly as conditions change
- Link SWOT findings directly to strategic initiatives

## Balanced Scorecard

### Overview
The Balanced Scorecard tracks performance across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth, ensuring a comprehensive view beyond financial results.

### Four Perspectives

**1. Financial Perspective**
- Revenue growth and profitability
- Cost management and efficiency
- Return on investment (ROI)
- Cash flow and liquidity
- Shareholder value creation

**2. Customer Perspective**
- Customer satisfaction and loyalty
- Market share and penetration
- Customer retention and acquisition
- Brand perception and reputation
- Customer lifetime value

**3. Internal Process Perspective**
- Operational efficiency and productivity
- Quality metrics and defect rates
- Innovation and new product development
- Cycle times and throughput
- Process compliance and control

**4. Learning and Growth Perspective**
- Employee skills and competencies
- Employee engagement and satisfaction
- Information systems capabilities
- Organizational culture and climate
- Innovation capacity

### Implementation Process

**Step 1: Define Strategic Objectives**
- Identify 3-5 objectives for each perspective
- Ensure objectives support overall strategy
- Create cause-and-effect linkages between perspectives

**Step 2: Develop Measures**
- Define specific metrics for each objective
- Balance leading and lagging indicators
- Ensure measures are quantifiable and trackable

**Step 3: Set Targets**
- Establish baseline performance levels
- Define ambitious but achievable targets
- Set short-term and long-term goals

**Step 4: Identify Initiatives**
- Determine actions needed to achieve targets
- Allocate resources to strategic initiatives
- Assign ownership and accountability

**Step 5: Monitor and Review**
- Track performance regularly (monthly/quarterly)
- Review strategy maps and dashboards
- Adjust strategies based on performance

### Strategy Mapping
Create visual strategy maps showing:
- Cause-and-effect relationships between objectives
- How learning and growth enables internal processes
- How internal processes drive customer outcomes
- How customer outcomes lead to financial results

## Objectives and Key Results (OKRs)

### Overview
OKRs link ambitious objectives to measurable results, creating focus by limiting priorities and demanding specific, measurable outcomes.

### Structure

**Objectives**
- Qualitative, aspirational goals
- Inspirational and motivating
- Time-bound (typically quarterly)
- Limited to 3-5 per level (company, team, individual)

**Key Results**
- Quantitative, measurable outcomes
- Specific numeric targets
- Verifiable and objective
- 3-5 key results per objective

### OKR Characteristics
- **Ambitious**: Stretch goals that push boundaries (aim for 70-80% achievement)
- **Transparent**: Visible across the organization
- **Aligned**: Cascaded from company to team to individual
- **Frequent**: Reviewed weekly, set quarterly
- **Graded**: Scored objectively at end of period

### Implementation Process

**1. Set Company-Level OKRs**
- Leadership defines 3-5 company objectives
- Align with annual strategic plan
- Communicate widely across organization

**2. Cascade to Teams**
- Teams create OKRs that support company objectives
- Ensure alignment while allowing autonomy
- Review for conflicts and dependencies

**3. Individual OKRs**
- Individuals set OKRs aligned with team objectives
- Include both role-specific and developmental goals
- Discuss with managers for alignment

**4. Weekly Check-ins**
- Review progress on key results
- Identify blockers and needed support
- Adjust tactics as needed

**5. Quarterly Reviews**
- Score key results objectively (0.0-1.0 scale)
- Reflect on what worked and what didn't
- Set new OKRs for next quarter

### Example OKR

**Objective**: Become the market leader in customer satisfaction

**Key Results**:
1. Increase Net Promoter Score from 45 to 65
2. Reduce average response time from 24 hours to 4 hours
3. Achieve 95% customer retention rate
4. Increase customer satisfaction score from 4.2 to 4.7 out of 5.0

## Porter's Five Forces

### Overview
Analyzes competitive dynamics within an industry by examining five forces that determine industry attractiveness and profitability.

### The Five Forces

**1. Threat of New Entrants**
Factors to assess:
- Capital requirements for entry
- Economies of scale advantages
- Brand loyalty and switching costs
- Access to distribution channels
- Regulatory barriers
- Technology and know-how requirements

**High threat**: Low barriers, easy entry, many potential entrants
**Low threat**: High barriers, difficult entry, few potential entrants

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
Factors to assess:
- Number and concentration of suppliers
- Uniqueness of supplier products/services
- Switching costs to alternative suppliers
- Supplier forward integration potential
- Importance of volume to suppliers

**High power**: Few suppliers, unique inputs, high switching costs
**Low power**: Many suppliers, commodity inputs, low switching costs

**3. Bargaining Power of Buyers**
Factors to assess:
- Number and concentration of customers
- Volume of purchases
- Switching costs for buyers
- Buyer backward integration potential
- Price sensitivity
- Product differentiation

**High power**: Few buyers, large volumes, low switching costs
**Low power**: Many buyers, small volumes, high switching costs

**4. Threat of Substitute Products/Services**
Factors to assess:
- Availability of substitute products
- Relative price-performance of substitutes
- Switching costs to substitutes
- Buyer propensity to substitute
- Emerging technologies

**High threat**: Many substitutes, better value, low switching costs
**Low threat**: Few substitutes, inferior value, high switching costs

**5. Competitive Rivalry**
Factors to assess:
- Number and diversity of competitors
- Industry growth rate
- Fixed costs and exit barriers
- Product differentiation
- Brand loyalty
- Capacity additions

**High rivalry**: Many competitors, slow growth, low differentiation
**Low rivalry**: Few competitors, fast growth, high differentiation

### Strategic Implications

**Favorable Industry Structure**
- Low threat of new entrants
- Low supplier power
- Low buyer power
- Low threat of substitutes
- Moderate rivalry

**Unfavorable Industry Structure**
- High threat of new entrants
- High supplier power
- High buyer power
- High threat of substitutes
- Intense rivalry

### Application
1. Assess each force systematically
2. Identify which forces are strongest
3. Develop strategies to:
   - Reduce negative forces
   - Exploit favorable forces
   - Position company advantageously
4. Monitor forces over time for changes

## Blue Ocean Strategy

### Overview
Focuses on creating new market spaces (blue oceans) rather than competing in crowded markets (red oceans), making competition irrelevant through value innovation.

### Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

**Red Ocean Strategy**
- Compete in existing market space
- Beat the competition
- Exploit existing demand
- Make value-cost trade-off
- Align activities with strategic choice (differentiation OR low cost)

**Blue Ocean Strategy**
- Create uncontested market space
- Make competition irrelevant
- Create and capture new demand
- Break value-cost trade-off
- Align activities in pursuit of differentiation AND low cost

### Four Actions Framework

Use this framework to reconstruct buyer value elements:

**1. Eliminate**
- Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
- What can be removed that customers don't value?

**2. Reduce**
- Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- What is over-designed or over-delivered?

**3. Raise**
- Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- What should be enhanced to create more value?

**4. Create**
- Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
- What new value elements can be introduced?

### Strategy Canvas

Visualize current competitive landscape:
- X-axis: Key competing factors in the industry
- Y-axis: Offering level (low to high)
- Plot your company and competitors
- Identify opportunities to diverge from competition

### Six Paths Framework

Explore alternative market spaces:

**Path 1: Look Across Alternative Industries**
- What alternatives do customers consider?
- Example: Movie theaters vs. restaurants for entertainment

**Path 2: Look Across Strategic Groups**
- What factors determine buyer choice between groups?
- Example: Luxury vs. economy segments

**Path 3: Look Across Chain of Buyers**
- Who are all the buyers in the chain?
- Example: Purchasers, users, influencers

**Path 4: Look Across Complementary Products/Services**
- What happens before, during, and after product use?
- Example: iTunes creating ecosystem around iPod

**Path 5: Look Across Functional-Emotional Orientation**
- Can you add emotional appeal to functional products?
- Can you strip emotional elements for simplicity?

**Path 6: Look Across Time**
- What trends are shaping your industry?
- How can you capitalize on irreversible trends?

### Implementation Process

1. **Select the right scope**: Choose the right starting point (industry, strategic group, buyer group)
2. **Get the strategic sequence right**:
   - Buyer utility: Is there exceptional utility?
   - Price: Is the price easily accessible to mass of buyers?
   - Cost: Can you produce at target cost and profit?
   - Adoption: What are adoption hurdles?
3. **Execute blue ocean strategy**: Align value, profit, and people propositions

## PESTLE Analysis

### Overview
Examines external macro-environmental factors: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental.

### The Six Factors

**Political Factors**
- Government stability and policy
- Tax policies and trade regulations
- Political ideology and priorities
- Government spending and priorities
- Lobbying and political influence
- International relations

**Economic Factors**
- Economic growth rates and trends
- Interest rates and inflation
- Exchange rates and currency stability
- Unemployment levels
- Disposable income and spending patterns
- Credit availability

**Social Factors**
- Demographics and population trends
- Cultural attitudes and values
- Lifestyle changes and preferences
- Education levels
- Health consciousness
- Social mobility

**Technological Factors**
- Rate of technological change
- Automation and AI adoption
- R&D activity and innovation
- Technology infrastructure
- Digital transformation trends
- Emerging technologies

**Legal Factors**
- Employment laws and regulations
- Consumer protection laws
- Health and safety regulations
- Data protection and privacy laws
- Antitrust and competition laws
- Industry-specific regulations

**Environmental Factors**
- Climate change and weather patterns
- Environmental regulations
- Sustainability concerns
- Carbon footprint and emissions
- Waste management requirements
- Resource scarcity

### Application Process

**Step 1: Identify Relevant Factors**
- Brainstorm factors in each category
- Focus on factors most relevant to your industry and organization
- Consider both current and emerging factors

**Step 2: Assess Impact**
- Rate each factor's potential impact (high, medium, low)
- Determine whether impact is positive or negative
- Assess likelihood of occurrence

**Step 3: Prioritize**
- Focus on high-impact, high-likelihood factors
- Create a priority matrix
- Identify factors requiring immediate attention

**Step 4: Develop Responses**
- For opportunities: Strategies to capitalize
- For threats: Mitigation or contingency plans
- For uncertainties: Monitoring and scenario planning

**Step 5: Monitor and Update**
- Regularly review PESTLE factors (quarterly or semi-annually)
- Update assessments as conditions change
- Integrate insights into strategic planning

### Integration with Strategy
- Use PESTLE to inform SWOT analysis (external factors)
- Incorporate findings into scenario planning
- Align strategic initiatives with macro trends
- Build flexibility to respond to environmental changes

## Additional Frameworks

### Gap Planning Framework

**Purpose**: Identify the difference between current state and desired future state, then build plans to close the gap.

**Process**:
1. Define desired future state (vision, goals, targets)
2. Assess current state (performance, capabilities, resources)
3. Identify gaps (performance gaps, capability gaps, resource gaps)
4. Prioritize gaps by impact and feasibility
5. Develop action plans to close each gap
6. Implement and monitor progress

### Ansoff Growth Matrix

**Purpose**: Evaluate growth options based on products and markets.

**Four Strategies**:
1. **Market Penetration** (existing products, existing markets)
   - Increase market share
   - Increase usage frequency
   - Lowest risk

2. **Product Development** (new products, existing markets)
   - Develop new products for current customers
   - Enhance existing products
   - Medium risk

3. **Market Development** (existing products, new markets)
   - Enter new geographic markets
   - Target new customer segments
   - Medium risk

4. **Diversification** (new products, new markets)
   - Enter completely new businesses
   - Highest risk, highest potential reward
   - Requires significant resources

### BCG Growth-Share Matrix

**Purpose**: Categorize business units or products to guide investment decisions.

**Four Categories**:
1. **Stars** (high growth, high market share)
   - Invest to maintain leadership
   - Future cash cows

2. **Cash Cows** (low growth, high market share)
   - Harvest cash to fund other units
   - Maintain position efficiently

3. **Question Marks** (high growth, low market share)
   - Invest selectively to build share
   - Or divest if prospects are poor

4. **Dogs** (low growth, low market share)
   - Divest or harvest
   - Minimal investment

### McKinsey 7S Model

**Purpose**: Align seven organizational elements for maximum effectiveness.

**Seven Elements**:
1. **Strategy**: Plan to achieve competitive advantage
2. **Structure**: Organizational hierarchy and reporting lines
3. **Systems**: Processes and procedures
4. **Shared Values**: Core beliefs and culture
5. **Skills**: Capabilities and competencies
6. **Style**: Leadership approach and management style
7. **Staff**: People and their development

**Application**: Ensure all seven elements are aligned and mutually reinforcing.

### Value Chain Analysis

**Purpose**: Examine how each business activity creates value.

**Primary Activities**:
1. Inbound logistics
2. Operations
3. Outbound logistics
4. Marketing and sales
5. Service

**Support Activities**:
1. Firm infrastructure
2. Human resource management
3. Technology development
4. Procurement

**Analysis**: Identify opportunities to reduce costs or differentiate offerings in each activity.
