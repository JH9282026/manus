# Video Accessibility & Inclusive Design

## Introduction to Video Accessibility

Video accessibility ensures that video content is perceivable, understandable, and usable by all people, including those with disabilities. With over one billion people worldwide living with some form of disability, accessible video is not only an ethical imperative but often a legal requirement. Beyond compliance, accessible video reaches wider audiences, improves SEO, and creates better experiences for everyone.

### Why Video Accessibility Matters

**Legal Requirements**: Laws in many jurisdictions require accessible video content, including the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Section 508, the European Accessibility Act, and others.

**Audience Reach**: Accessible content reaches the 15-20% of the population with disabilities, plus many others who benefit from accessibility features.

**Better User Experience**: Accessibility improvements often benefit all users—captions help in noisy environments, clear audio helps non-native speakers.

**SEO Benefits**: Captions and transcripts provide searchable text content, improving discoverability.

**Corporate Responsibility**: Accessible content demonstrates commitment to inclusion and diversity.

### Understanding Disabilities Relevant to Video

**Visual Disabilities**:
- Blindness (complete or significant vision loss)
- Low vision (partial sight, clarity issues)
- Color blindness (difficulty distinguishing colors)
- Light sensitivity

**Hearing Disabilities**:
- Deafness (complete or significant hearing loss)
- Hard of hearing (partial hearing loss)
- Auditory processing disorders
- Tinnitus

**Motor Disabilities**:
- Limited fine motor control
- Paralysis or limited mobility
- Tremors
- Repetitive strain injuries

**Cognitive Disabilities**:
- Learning disabilities (dyslexia, dyscalculia)
- Attention disorders (ADHD)
- Memory impairments
- Intellectual disabilities

**Seizure Disorders**:
- Photosensitive epilepsy
- Vestibular disorders
- Motion sensitivity

## WCAG Guidelines for Video

### Understanding WCAG

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide the international standard for web accessibility, including video content. WCAG is organized around four principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust (POUR).

### WCAG Conformance Levels

**Level A (Minimum)**:
- Basic accessibility features
- Prevents major barriers
- Minimum legal compliance
- Examples: Captions for pre-recorded video, audio descriptions available

**Level AA (Recommended)**:
- Standard compliance target
- Most legal requirements reference this level
- Good accessibility for most users
- Examples: Captions for live video, audio description for pre-recorded

**Level AAA (Enhanced)**:
- Highest level of accessibility
- Not always achievable for all content
- Best practice target
- Examples: Sign language interpretation, extended audio descriptions

### Key WCAG Success Criteria for Video

**1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded)** - Level A:
- Provide transcript for audio-only content
- Provide audio track or text alternative for video-only

**1.2.2 Captions (Prerecorded)** - Level A:
- Captions for all prerecorded audio content in video

**1.2.3 Audio Description or Media Alternative** - Level A:
- Audio description or text alternative for video content

**1.2.4 Captions (Live)** - Level AA:
- Captions for live audio content in synchronized media

**1.2.5 Audio Description (Prerecorded)** - Level AA:
- Audio description for all prerecorded video content

**1.2.6 Sign Language (Prerecorded)** - Level AAA:
- Sign language interpretation provided for audio content

**1.2.7 Extended Audio Description** - Level AAA:
- Extended audio description when pauses are insufficient

**1.2.8 Media Alternative (Prerecorded)** - Level AAA:
- Full text alternative for synchronized media

**1.2.9 Audio-only (Live)** - Level AAA:
- Text alternative for live audio-only content

**2.3.1 Three Flashes or Below Threshold** - Level A:
- Content doesn't flash more than 3 times per second

**2.3.2 Three Flashes** - Level AAA:
- Content doesn't contain any flashing

## Captions and Subtitles

### Understanding Captions vs. Subtitles

**Captions**:
- Designed for deaf and hard-of-hearing viewers
- Include all audio information (dialogue, sound effects, music)
- Same language as the audio
- Include speaker identification
- Note relevant non-speech audio

**Subtitles**:
- Designed for hearing viewers who don't understand the language
- Primarily dialogue translation
- Assume viewer can hear audio
- May not include non-speech information

**SDH (Subtitles for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing)**:
- Hybrid of captions and subtitles
- Translated with accessibility features
- Include sound effects and speaker identification

### Types of Captions

**Closed Captions (CC)**:
- Can be turned on/off by viewer
- Separate text file or embedded data
- Industry standard for distribution
- Platform-dependent display

**Open Captions**:
- Burned into video image
- Always visible
- No viewer control
- Consistent across platforms

**Real-Time Captions (CART)**:
- Generated live during events
- Communication Access Realtime Translation
- Professional stenographers or AI
- Used for live streaming

### Caption Quality Standards

**Accuracy**:
- 99%+ accuracy for professional captions
- Verbatim or meaning-for-meaning translation
- Correct spelling, grammar, punctuation
- Appropriate treatment of accents/dialects

**Synchronization**:
- Captions appear with corresponding audio
- Maximum 3-second delay acceptable
- Shot change alignment when possible
- Appropriate duration (minimum 1 second, maximum 7 seconds)

**Completeness**:
- All dialogue included
- Sound effects described (e.g., [door slams])
- Music described (e.g., ♪ upbeat jazz music ♪)
- Speaker identification (e.g., SARAH:)

**Readability**:
- Maximum 2 lines per caption frame
- Maximum 32 characters per line (broadcast standard)
- Appropriate reading speed (130-160 WPM)
- Clear segmentation at natural pauses

### Caption File Formats

**SRT (SubRip Text)**:
- Most widely supported format
- Simple text-based
- Sequential numbering
- Timestamp and text only

**VTT (Web Video Text Tracks)**:
- HTML5 native format
- Supports styling
- Metadata capabilities
- Good for web delivery

**SCC (Scenarist Closed Caption)**:
- Broadcast standard
- CEA-608 compliant
- Two-channel support
- Professional broadcast use

**TTML (Timed Text Markup Language)**:
- XML-based format
- Rich styling options
- Used by streaming services
- Complex but powerful

**STL (EBU Subtitling)**:
- European broadcast standard
- Fixed-width format
- Color and positioning support
- Television distribution

### Creating Quality Captions

**Manual Captioning Workflow**:
1. Transcribe audio accurately
2. Add speaker identification
3. Include sound effects and music cues
4. Segment into appropriate caption frames
5. Time synchronize with audio
6. Review for accuracy and timing
7. Export to required format

**Caption Editing Tools**:
- Subtitle Edit (free, powerful)
- Aegisub (free, feature-rich)
- Rev.com Caption Editor
- Adobe Premiere Pro Captions Panel
- YouTube Studio Caption Editor

**AI-Assisted Captioning**:
- Auto-generate initial captions
- Review and correct errors
- Adjust timing as needed
- Add missing elements (sound effects, music)
- Always human-verify for accuracy
