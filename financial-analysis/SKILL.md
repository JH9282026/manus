# Financial Analysis

Analyze financial statements, evaluate company performance, and make informed decisions using ratio analysis, cash flow evaluation, and forecasting techniques.

## Overview

Financial analysis is the process of evaluating businesses, projects, budgets, and other finance-related entities to determine their performance and suitability. This skill provides frameworks for analyzing financial statements, calculating and interpreting key ratios, assessing cash flow quality, comparing performance across companies and time periods, and developing financial forecasts. It enables data-driven decision-making for investors, creditors, managers, and other stakeholders.

## Financial Analysis Framework

Financial analysis follows a structured approach:

| Analysis Stage | Key Questions | Primary Tools |
|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Strategic Analysis | What is the business model? What are competitive advantages? | Industry analysis, Porter's Five Forces |
| Accounting Analysis | Are financial statements reliable? What adjustments are needed? | Quality of earnings, accounting policy review |
| Financial Analysis | How is the company performing? What are the trends? | Ratio analysis, common-size statements |
| Prospective Analysis | What is the company worth? What are future prospects? | Forecasting, valuation models |

## Financial Statement Analysis

### The Three Core Statements

**Income Statement**: Measures profitability over a period
- Revenue (top line)
- Expenses (operating and non-operating)
- Net Income (bottom line)
- Key insight: How profitable is the company?

**Balance Sheet**: Snapshot of financial position at a point in time
- Assets (what company owns)
- Liabilities (what company owes)
- Equity (residual ownership)
- Key insight: What does the company own and owe?

**Cash Flow Statement**: Tracks actual cash movements
- Operating activities (core business)
- Investing activities (capital expenditures, acquisitions)
- Financing activities (debt, equity, dividends)
- Key insight: Where does cash come from and go?

### Statement Relationships

The three statements are interconnected:

```
Net Income (Income Statement)
  → Retained Earnings (Balance Sheet)
  → Starting point for Operating Cash Flow (Cash Flow Statement)

Capital Expenditures (Cash Flow Statement)
  → Property, Plant & Equipment (Balance Sheet)
  → Depreciation Expense (Income Statement)

Debt Issuance/Repayment (Cash Flow Statement)
  → Debt Balance (Balance Sheet)
  → Interest Expense (Income Statement)
```

### Common-Size Analysis

**Vertical Analysis** (Common-Size Statements):
- Express each line item as % of base figure
- Income Statement: % of Revenue
- Balance Sheet: % of Total Assets
- Enables comparison across companies of different sizes

**Horizontal Analysis** (Trend Analysis):
- Compare line items across time periods
- Calculate year-over-year growth rates
- Identify trends and inflection points

## Financial Ratio Analysis

### Five Categories of Ratios

| Category | Purpose | Key Question |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Liquidity | Ability to meet short-term obligations | Can the company pay its bills? |
| Solvency | Ability to meet long-term obligations | Can the company survive long-term? |
| Profitability | Ability to generate profits | How profitable is the company? |
| Efficiency | Effectiveness of asset utilization | How well does the company use its assets? |
| Market Value | Market perception and valuation | How does the market value the company? |

### Liquidity Ratios

Measure ability to meet short-term obligations.

| Ratio | Formula | Healthy Range | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Current Ratio | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | 1.5 - 3.0 | Overall short-term liquidity |
| Quick Ratio | (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities | 1.0 - 2.0 | Liquidity without selling inventory |
| Cash Ratio | Cash / Current Liabilities | 0.5 - 1.0 | Most conservative liquidity measure |

**Red Flags**:
- Current ratio < 1.0: May struggle to pay bills
- Declining liquidity ratios: Deteriorating financial health
- Very high ratios (>3.0): Inefficient use of capital

### Solvency Ratios

Measure ability to meet long-term obligations and financial leverage.

| Ratio | Formula | Healthy Range | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Debt-to-Equity | Total Debt / Total Equity | 0.3 - 1.0 (varies by industry) | Financial leverage |
| Debt-to-Assets | Total Debt / Total Assets | < 0.6 | Proportion of assets financed by debt |
| Interest Coverage | EBIT / Interest Expense | > 3.0 | Ability to service debt |
| Debt Service Coverage | Operating Cash Flow / Total Debt Service | > 1.5 | Cash available for debt payments |

**Red Flags**:
- Interest coverage < 2.0: Difficulty servicing debt
- Debt-to-equity > 2.0: High financial risk (industry-dependent)
- Declining coverage ratios: Increasing financial distress risk

### Profitability Ratios

Measure ability to generate profits relative to revenue, assets, or equity.

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Gross Margin | Gross Profit / Revenue | Profitability before operating expenses |
| Operating Margin | Operating Income / Revenue | Profitability from core operations |
| Net Margin | Net Income / Revenue | Bottom-line profitability |
| Return on Assets (ROA) | Net Income / Total Assets | Profit generated per dollar of assets |
| Return on Equity (ROE) | Net Income / Shareholders' Equity | Return to shareholders |
| Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) | NOPAT / Invested Capital | Return on all capital (debt + equity) |

**Margin Analysis**:
- Compare margins across time (trend)
- Compare to industry peers (relative performance)
- Analyze margin drivers (pricing, costs, efficiency)

**ROE Decomposition (DuPont Analysis)**:
```
ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
    = (Net Income/Revenue) × (Revenue/Assets) × (Assets/Equity)
```

This breaks ROE into:
- **Profitability** (Net Margin)
- **Efficiency** (Asset Turnover)
- **Leverage** (Equity Multiplier)

### Efficiency Ratios

Measure how effectively company uses its assets.

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Asset Turnover | Revenue / Total Assets | Revenue generated per dollar of assets |
| Inventory Turnover | COGS / Average Inventory | How quickly inventory is sold |
| Receivables Turnover | Revenue / Average Accounts Receivable | How quickly receivables are collected |
| Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) | 365 / Receivables Turnover | Average collection period |
| Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) | 365 / Inventory Turnover | Average time inventory held |
| Cash Conversion Cycle | DIO + DSO - DPO | Time cash is tied up in operations |

**Efficiency Insights**:
- Higher turnover ratios generally better (faster conversion to cash)
- Compare to industry norms (varies significantly by sector)
- Declining turnover may indicate operational problems

### Market Value Ratios

Measure market perception and valuation.

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Price-to-Earnings (P/E) | Stock Price / EPS | How much investors pay per dollar of earnings |
| Price-to-Book (P/B) | Stock Price / Book Value per Share | Market value vs. accounting value |
| Price-to-Sales (P/S) | Market Cap / Revenue | Valuation relative to sales |
| Dividend Yield | Annual Dividend / Stock Price | Income return to shareholders |
| Earnings Yield | EPS / Stock Price | Inverse of P/E, comparable to bond yields |

**Valuation Context**:
- Compare to historical averages
- Compare to peer companies
- Consider growth prospects (PEG ratio = P/E / Growth Rate)
- High multiples may indicate growth expectations or overvaluation
- Low multiples may indicate value opportunity or fundamental problems

## Comparative Analysis

### Time-Series Analysis

Compare company's performance over time.

**Trend Analysis**:
- Calculate 3-5 year trends for key metrics
- Identify improving or deteriorating performance
- Look for inflection points

**Growth Rates**:
```
CAGR = [(Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/Years)] - 1
```

### Cross-Sectional Analysis

Compare company to peers and industry.

**Peer Selection Criteria**:
- Similar business model
- Comparable size
- Same industry/sector
- Similar geographic markets

**Benchmarking Process**:
1. Identify 3-5 comparable companies
2. Calculate key ratios for all companies
3. Compare target company to peer median/average
4. Identify relative strengths and weaknesses

### Industry Analysis

Understand industry context and norms.

| Industry Characteristic | Impact on Financial Analysis |
|------------------------|-----------------------------|
| Capital Intensity | High fixed assets, lower asset turnover |
| Inventory Requirements | Affects working capital, turnover ratios |
| Competitive Dynamics | Impacts margins, pricing power |
| Regulatory Environment | Affects costs, compliance requirements |
| Cyclicality | Revenue and earnings volatility |
| Growth Stage | Mature vs. growth affects profitability, cash flow |

## Quality of Earnings Analysis

### Earnings Quality Indicators

**High-Quality Earnings**:
- Cash-based (not just accruals)
- Sustainable (from core operations)
- Conservative accounting policies
- Consistent with cash flow

**Red Flags for Low-Quality Earnings**:
- Net income > Operating cash flow (consistently)
- Aggressive revenue recognition
- Frequent one-time charges
- Declining cash flow despite rising earnings
- Complex off-balance-sheet arrangements
- Frequent accounting policy changes

### Accruals Analysis

**Total Accruals**:
```
Total Accruals = Net Income - Operating Cash Flow
```

**Interpretation**:
- High accruals relative to earnings: Potential earnings management
- Negative accruals (OCF > Net Income): Conservative, high-quality earnings
- Persistent high accruals: Investigate accounting policies

### Cash Flow vs. Earnings

**Healthy Pattern**:
- Operating cash flow ≥ Net income (over time)
- Free cash flow positive and growing
- Cash flow supports earnings

**Warning Signs**:
- Operating cash flow < Net income (persistently)
- Negative free cash flow despite profitability
- Diverging trends (earnings up, cash flow down)

## Financial Health Assessment

### Altman Z-Score (Bankruptcy Prediction)

Predicts probability of bankruptcy within 2 years.

```
Z-Score = 1.2×(WC/TA) + 1.4×(RE/TA) + 3.3×(EBIT/TA) + 0.6×(MVE/TL) + 1.0×(Sales/TA)

Where:
WC = Working Capital
TA = Total Assets
RE = Retained Earnings
EBIT = Earnings Before Interest and Taxes
MVE = Market Value of Equity
TL = Total Liabilities
```

**Interpretation**:
- Z > 2.99: Safe zone (low bankruptcy risk)
- 1.81 < Z < 2.99: Gray zone (moderate risk)
- Z < 1.81: Distress zone (high bankruptcy risk)

### Piotroski F-Score (Financial Strength)

Scores company on 9 criteria (0-9 scale).

**Profitability Signals** (4 points):
1. Positive net income
2. Positive operating cash flow
3. Increasing ROA
4. Operating cash flow > Net income

**Leverage, Liquidity, Source of Funds** (3 points):
5. Decreasing long-term debt
6. Increasing current ratio
7. No new shares issued

**Operating Efficiency** (2 points):
8. Increasing gross margin
9. Increasing asset turnover

**Interpretation**:
- F-Score 8-9: Strong financial position
- F-Score 0-2: Weak financial position
- Used to identify value stocks with improving fundamentals
