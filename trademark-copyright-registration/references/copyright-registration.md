# Copyright Registration

## Introduction

Copyright registration with the U.S. Copyright Office provides formal recognition of copyright ownership and offers significant legal advantages for protecting original works of authorship. While copyright protection arises automatically upon creation and fixation of a work, registration is a prerequisite for filing infringement lawsuits for U.S. works and enables copyright owners to claim statutory damages and attorney's fees.

This reference provides comprehensive guidance on the copyright registration process, including application preparation, deposit requirements, examination procedures, and post-registration considerations.

## Benefits of Copyright Registration

### Legal Benefits

**1. Prerequisite for Infringement Lawsuit**:
- U.S. works must be registered before filing copyright infringement lawsuit
- Registration establishes jurisdiction in federal court
- Without registration, cannot enforce rights through litigation

**2. Prima Facie Evidence of Validity**:
- Registration within 5 years of publication creates legal presumption:
  - Copyright is valid
  - Facts stated in certificate are true
  - Copyright owner is the person named in certificate
- Shifts burden of proof to defendant to challenge validity

**3. Statutory Damages and Attorney's Fees**:
- Available only if work registered:
  - Before infringement commenced, OR
  - Within 3 months of first publication
- Statutory damages: $750-$30,000 per work (up to $150,000 for willful infringement)
- Actual damages often difficult to prove; statutory damages provide alternative
- Attorney's fees can be substantial; availability encourages enforcement

**4. Public Record of Copyright Claim**:
- Establishes public notice of copyright ownership
- Provides information to potential licensees
- Creates searchable record in Copyright Office database
- Helps establish chain of title

**5. Customs Protection**:
- Registered works can be recorded with U.S. Customs and Border Protection
- Enables Customs to seize infringing copies at the border
- Prevents importation of pirated goods

**6. Basis for Foreign Registration**:
- U.S. registration can facilitate registration in certain foreign countries
- Some countries require or prefer evidence of U.S. registration

### Practical Benefits

**Deterrent Effect**:
- Copyright notice with registration symbol (®) warns potential infringers
- Demonstrates seriousness about protecting rights
- May discourage infringement

**Licensing and Commercialization**:
- Registration certificate provides proof of ownership for licensing negotiations
- Facilitates due diligence in transactions
- Enhances value of copyright assets

**Dispute Resolution**:
- Registration certificate provides evidence in disputes
- Simplifies proof of ownership and validity
- Strengthens negotiating position

## Pre-Registration Considerations

### Timing of Registration

**Optimal Timing**:

**Before Publication**:
- Preserves eligibility for statutory damages for all infringement
- Establishes early public record
- Recommended for high-value works

**Within 3 Months of Publication**:
- Preserves eligibility for statutory damages and attorney's fees
- Balances cost with protection
- Recommended for commercially published works

**Before Infringement**:
- Required to file lawsuit
- Expedited processing available if litigation pending

**Strategic Considerations**:
- Register valuable works early
- Consider group registrations to reduce costs
- Balance registration costs with value and risk

### Determining Copyrightability

**Requirements for Copyright Protection**:

**1. Original Work of Authorship**:
- Independently created by author
- Possesses at least minimal creativity
- Not copied from another source

**2. Fixed in Tangible Medium**:
- Embodied in physical or digital form
- Sufficiently permanent or stable
- Can be perceived, reproduced, or communicated

**3. Human Authorship**:
- Created by human author
- AI-generated works not copyrightable
- Works must involve human creative input

**Copyrightable Subject Matter**:
- Literary works
- Musical works (including lyrics)
- Dramatic works (including music)
- Pantomimes and choreographic works
- Pictorial, graphic, and sculptural works
- Motion pictures and audiovisual works
- Sound recordings
- Architectural works

**Not Copyrightable**:
- Ideas, concepts, principles, discoveries
- Procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation
- Facts and data
- Titles, names, short phrases, slogans (may be trademarks)
- Familiar symbols or designs
- Mere variations of typographic ornamentation
- Listings of ingredients or contents
- Works by U.S. Government employees in official capacity
- Works lacking human authorship

### Identifying Authorship and Ownership

**Individual Authors**:
- Person who created the work
- Owns copyright automatically upon creation

**Joint Authors**:
- Two or more authors who:
  - Create inseparable or interdependent contributions
  - Intend to merge contributions into unitary whole
  - Intend to be co-authors at time of creation
- Each co-author owns undivided interest in entire work
- Each can license work non-exclusively without others' consent
- Must account to co-authors for profits

**Works Made for Hire**:

**Employee Works**:
- Work created by employee within scope of employment
- Employer is considered author and owns copyright
- Factors: employer's right to control work, provision of tools, location of work, duration of relationship, employer's discretion over work assignments

**Commissioned Works**:
- Work specially ordered or commissioned
- Falls into one of nine statutory categories:
  1. Contribution to collective work
  2. Part of motion picture or audiovisual work
  3. Translation
  4. Supplementary work
  5. Compilation
  6. Instructional text
  7. Test
  8. Answer material for test
  9. Atlas
- Parties sign written agreement designating work as "made for hire"
- Commissioning party is considered author and owns copyright

**Transfers of Ownership**:
- Copyright ownership can be transferred by written agreement
- Exclusive licenses must be in writing
- Non-exclusive licenses can be oral
- Transfers should be recorded with Copyright Office

### Group Registration Options

**Purpose**: Register multiple works with single application and fee

**Available Group Registrations**:

**1. Group of Unpublished Works**:
- Up to 10 unpublished works
- All works by same author
- Author must be individual (not employer for hire)
- All works owned by same claimant
- Single title for group
- Lower cost than individual registrations

**2. Group of Published Photographs**:
- Up to 750 photographs
- All published within same calendar year
- All by same photographer (or employer for hire)
- All owned by same copyright claimant
- Must be published works

**3. Group of Contributions to Periodicals**:
- Multiple contributions to periodicals
- All by same individual author
- All published within 12-month period
- Author must be named on each contribution

**4. Group of Short Online Literary Works**:
- Multiple short literary works (up to 17,500 words each)
- All published on same website
- All published within 3-month period
- All by same author
- All owned by same claimant

**5. Group of Serials**:
- Multiple issues of same serial publication
- Published within same calendar year
- Same title for all issues

**6. Group of Newspapers**:
- Multiple issues of same newspaper
- Published within same calendar month

**Benefits**:
- Reduced filing fees
- Simplified administration
- Single registration covers multiple works

**Limitations**:
- Specific eligibility requirements
- All works must meet group criteria
- May complicate infringement litigation (need to identify specific work)

## Application Preparation

### Three Required Elements

Every copyright registration requires:

1. **Completed Application Form**
2. **Nonrefundable Filing Fee**
3. **Nonreturnable Deposit Copy(ies)**

### Application Methods

**Electronic Filing (eCO System)** - Strongly Recommended:

**Advantages**:
- Lower filing fees ($45-$65 vs. $125 for paper)
- Faster processing (typically 3-10 months vs. 8-16 months)
- Online status tracking
- Ability to upload electronic deposits
- Immediate confirmation of receipt
- Ability to save drafts

**Process**:
1. Create account at copyright.gov
2. Select type of work
3. Complete online application
4. Pay fee electronically
5. Upload deposit (if electronic) or mail physical deposit with shipping slip

**Paper Filing**:

**When Required**:
- Mask works
- Vessel hull designs
- GATT registrations
- Renewal registrations
- Certain group registrations (e.g., non-photographic databases)

**Forms**:
- Form TX: Literary works
- Form VA: Visual arts works
- Form PA: Performing arts works (including motion pictures)
- Form SR: Sound recordings
- Form SE: Single serial issue

**Process**:
1. Download and complete form
2. Mail form, fee, and deposit together in same package
3. Address to: U.S. Copyright Office, Library of Congress, 101 Independence Avenue SE, Washington, DC 20559

### Completing the Application

#### Type of Work

Select category that best describes work:
- Literary Work
- Work of the Visual Arts
- Work of the Performing Arts
- Sound Recording
- Motion Picture/Audiovisual Work
- Single Serial Issue

**Importance**: Determines questions asked and deposit requirements

#### Title of Work

**Requirements**:
- Specific, not generic
- Use Roman letters and Arabic numerals
- Avoid special characters

**Types of Titles**:
- **Title of Work Being Registered**: Primary title
- **Title of Larger Work**: If registering contribution to larger work
- **Previous or Alternative Titles**: If work known by other titles
- **Contents Titles**: For collections, list individual titles

**Best Practices**:
- Use title by which work is known
- Be consistent with title on work itself
- For untitled works, provide brief description

#### Author Information

**Required Information**:
- Author's full legal name
- Author's citizenship or domicile
- Year of birth (optional but recommended)
- Year of death (if deceased)

**Author's Contribution**:
- Describe what author created
- Select from categories or provide description
- Examples: "text," "photograph," "music," "lyrics," "entire work"

**Anonymous or Pseudonymous Works**:
- **Anonymous**: Author's identity not revealed on copies
- **Pseudonymous**: Author identified by fictitious name
- Can check appropriate box
- Can provide author's name or leave blank
- Affects copyright duration calculation

**Multiple Authors**:
- List all authors
- Describe each author's contribution
- Indicate if joint work (inseparable/interdependent contributions)

**Work Made for Hire**:
- Check "work made for hire" box
- Employer/hiring party is considered author
- Provide employer's name as author
- Indicate individual who created work (optional)

#### Claimant Information

**Copyright Claimant**: Person or entity that owns copyright

**If Claimant Same as Author**:
- Claimant is author
- No transfer statement needed

**If Claimant Different from Author**:
- Provide claimant's name and address
- Explain how claimant obtained ownership:
  - "By written agreement"
  - "Transfer of all rights by author"
  - "By will"
  - "By inheritance"
  - "Assignment"

**Multiple Claimants**:
- List all copyright owners
- Each owns undivided interest unless otherwise specified

#### Creation and Publication

**Year of Creation**:
- Year work was fixed in tangible form
- Year work was completed
- For derivative works or compilations, year new material was created

**Publication**:

**Definition**: Distribution of copies to the public by sale, rental, lease, or lending; offering to distribute copies to a group for further distribution or public display

**Not Publication**:
- Public performance or display
- Distribution of limited number of copies for review or approval
- Offering work for sale without actual distribution

**If Published**:
- Provide date of first publication (month, day, year)
- Provide nation of first publication
- If published simultaneously in multiple countries, list all

**If Unpublished**:
- Leave publication fields blank
- Work is unpublished

**Importance**:
- Affects copyright duration
- Affects deposit requirements
- Affects eligibility for statutory damages

#### Limitation of Claim

**Purpose**: Exclude material not owned by claimant or not copyrightable

**Previous Registration**:
- If work previously registered, provide registration number
- Explain what is different in this version

**Preexisting Material**:
- Identify material created by others or previously published
- Examples: "text," "photograph," "music"
- Describe what is being excluded from claim

**Material Not Copyrightable**:
- Exclude uncopyrightable elements
- Examples: "typeface," "ideas," "titles"

**New Material**:
- Describe new copyrightable authorship in this version
- Examples: "text," "compilation," "editorial revisions," "additional artwork"

**Derivative Work or Compilation**:
- Identify preexisting work(s)
- Describe new material added
- Claim covers only new material, not preexisting work

#### Rights and Permissions

**Optional Information**:
- Name and contact information for licensing inquiries
- Helps potential licensees contact copyright owner
- Not required but recommended for commercial works

#### Correspondent and Certification

**Correspondent**:
- Person Copyright Office should contact with questions
- Provide name, email, phone, address
- Receives all correspondence about application

**Certification**:
- Person certifying application is accurate
- Must be:
  - Author
  - Copyright claimant
  - Owner of exclusive rights
  - Authorized agent of above
- Provides name and date
- Electronic signature for online applications
- Handwritten signature for paper applications

**False Certification**: Providing false information can result in fines and criminal penalties

## Deposit Requirements

### General Deposit Rules

**Purpose**: Provide copy of work for Library of Congress collections and Copyright Office examination

**Nonreturnable**: Deposits are not returned to applicant

**Basic Rules**:

**Unpublished Works**:
- One complete copy or phonorecord
- Electronic deposit preferred

**Published Works**:
- Two complete copies of best edition (if first published in U.S.)
- One complete copy (if first published outside U.S.)
- Physical deposit required for works published in physical format in U.S.

### Best Edition

**Definition**: Edition of highest quality published in the United States

**Best Edition Guidelines**: Copyright Office maintains detailed list of preferences

**General Preferences**:
- Hardcover over paperback
- Larger size over smaller
- Color over black and white
- Higher quality paper
- Special editions over trade editions

**Examples**:
- Book: Hardcover library binding preferred over paperback
- Photograph: Archival quality print preferred over newsprint
- Music: Printed score preferred over lead sheet

### Electronic Deposits

**Strongly Encouraged For**:
- Unpublished works
- Works published only electronically
- Works where identifying material is permitted
- Works first published abroad in electronic format

**Required For**:
- Certain group registrations (e.g., up to 10 unpublished works, up to 750 photographs)

**Acceptable File Formats**:
- PDF (preferred for text and images)
- JPEG, TIFF, PNG (images)
- MP3, WAV (audio)
- MP4, MOV (video)
- See Copyright Office website for complete list

**File Size Limit**: 500 MB per upload

**Multiple Files**: Can upload multiple files if needed

### Physical Deposits

**When Required**:
- Works first published in U.S. in physical format
- Works published both physically and electronically
- Works first published abroad in physical format (if physical edition exists)

**Shipping**:
- Mail in boxes (not envelopes) to prevent damage
- Include shipping slip from eCO system (if filing online)
- Address to: Library of Congress, U.S. Copyright Office, 101 Independence Avenue SE, Washington, DC 20559-6000

**Packaging**:
- Protect fragile items
- Clearly label package
- Include shipping slip inside package

### Special Deposit Requirements

**Computer Programs**:
- First and last 25 pages of source code
- If fewer than 50 pages, entire source code
- For revised programs, 25 pages with revisions
- Trade secret concerns: Can submit first and last 10 pages with portions blocked out, or first and last 25 pages of object code

**Websites**:
- Representative pages showing copyrightable content
- PDF or screenshots
- Include home page and key content pages

**Multimedia Works**:
- Complete copy of work
- Operating software if necessary to view work
- Instructions for accessing content

**Three-Dimensional Works**:
- Photographs or drawings showing all sides
- Multiple views if necessary

**Architectural Works**:
- Architectural drawings or blueprints
- Photographs of constructed building (if built)

**Motion Pictures**:
- Complete copy of motion picture
- DVD or digital file
- For published works, two copies of best edition

**Sound Recordings**:
- Complete phonorecord
- CD or digital file
- If claiming both sound recording and musical work, deposit both

**Identifying Material**:
- For certain works, can submit identifying material instead of complete copy
- Photographs, drawings, or printouts
- Must clearly show copyrightable content

## Examination and Registration

### Processing Timeline

**Standard Processing**:
- Electronic filing: 3-10 months
- Paper filing: 8-16 months
- Timelines vary based on workload

**Expedited Processing**:
- Special handling available for additional fee ($800)
- Processing within 5-10 business days
- Available when:
  - Litigation pending or anticipated
  - Customs matter
  - Contract or publishing deadline
  - Other urgent need

### Examination Process

**Registration Specialist Review**:

**Examines**:
- Whether work contains copyrightable authorship
- Whether legal and formal requirements met
- Whether application complete and accurate
- Whether deposit adequate

**Does Not Examine**:
- Artistic merit or quality
- Originality in detail (assumes author's representation)
- Ownership disputes (registers claim as stated)

**Correspondence**:
- If issues identified, specialist contacts applicant
- Requests clarification or additional information
- Applicant must respond within 45 days
- Failure to respond results in administrative closure

**Common Issues**:
- Insufficient authorship (work not copyrightable)
- Incomplete application
- Inadequate deposit
- Unclear authorship or ownership
- Work appears to be work made for hire but not indicated

### Registration or Refusal

**If Approved**:
- Certificate of Registration issued
- Mailed to address provided
- Effective date is date Copyright Office received all required elements
- Certificate includes:
  - Registration number
  - Date of registration
  - Title of work
  - Author and claimant information
  - Copyright Office seal

**If Refused**:
- Written refusal explaining reasons
- Common reasons:
  - Work lacks copyrightable authorship
  - Work is not original
  - Work is not fixed
  - Application incomplete or incorrect

**Appeal Process**:

**First Request for Reconsideration**:
- Submit written request explaining why refusal is incorrect
- Provide additional evidence or arguments
- Reviewed by Copyright Office attorney
- Fee required

**Second Request for Reconsideration**:
- If first request denied, can submit second request
- Reviewed by Copyright Office Review Board
- Fee required
- Board decision is final agency action

**Judicial Review**:
- Can file lawsuit in federal district court
- Court reviews Copyright Office decision
- Can present additional evidence

## Post-Registration Considerations

### No Maintenance Requirements

**Automatic Duration**:
- Copyright lasts for statutory term automatically
- No renewal or maintenance filings required
- No fees after initial registration

**Contrast with Trademarks**: Trademarks require periodic maintenance filings; copyrights do not

### Recording Transfers

**Purpose**: Establish public record of copyright ownership transfers

**Benefits**:
- Provides constructive notice of transfer
- Establishes priority among conflicting transfers
- Prerequisite for infringement suit by transferee
- Protects against subsequent transfers

**What Can Be Recorded**:
- Assignments (transfers of ownership)
- Exclusive licenses
- Security interests
- Wills and inheritance documents
- Court orders affecting copyright

**Process**:
- Submit copy of transfer document
- Complete Document Cover Sheet
- Pay recording fee
- Can record online or by mail

**Timing**:
- Record within 1 month of execution (if executed in U.S.)
- Record within 2 months of execution (if executed abroad)
- Earlier recording takes priority

### Supplementary Registration

**Purpose**: Correct or amplify information in original registration

**When to Use**:
- Correct errors in original application
- Add information omitted from original
- Clarify ambiguous information

**Cannot Change**:
- Basic facts of authorship or ownership (if incorrect, may need new registration)
- Deposit copy

**Process**:
- File Form CA (Supplementary Registration)
- Provide original registration number
- Explain error or omission
- Provide correct information
- Pay fee

**Effect**: Creates new certificate supplementing original; does not replace original

### Renewal (Pre-1978 Works Only)

**Not Required for Works Created After 1977**: Copyright lasts for full term automatically

**Required for Works Published 1964-1977**: Must renew to extend copyright for second term

**Works Published Before 1964**: If not renewed, now in public domain

**Renewal Process**:
- File renewal application during last year of initial term
- Form RE
- Extends copyright for additional term

## Enforcement and Litigation

### Prerequisites for Lawsuit

**Registration Required**:
- Must register U.S. works before filing infringement lawsuit
- Can file application and then sue once registration issued or refused
- Non-U.S. works: Registration not required but beneficial

**Expedited Processing**:
- Special handling available if litigation pending
- Faster registration to enable lawsuit

### Statutory Damages and Attorney's Fees

**Eligibility**:
- Work must be registered:
  - Before infringement commenced, OR
  - Within 3 months of first publication

**Statutory Damages**:
- $750 to $30,000 per work infringed
- Up to $150,000 per work for willful infringement
- As low as $200 for innocent infringement
- Court has discretion within statutory range

**Attorney's Fees**:
- Court may award attorney's fees to prevailing party
- Significant incentive for enforcement
- Also available to defendants in some cases

**Actual Damages Alternative**:
- Can elect actual damages and infringer's profits instead
- Must prove actual harm and profits
- Often difficult to quantify

### DMCA Takedown Notices

**Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA)**:
- Provides process for removing infringing content from online platforms
- Safe harbor for online service providers who comply

**Takedown Notice Requirements**:
- Identify copyrighted work
- Identify infringing material and its location
- Provide contact information
- Include good faith statement
- Include statement of accuracy under penalty of perjury
- Physical or electronic signature

**Process**:
1. Copyright owner sends takedown notice to service provider
2. Service provider removes or disables access to material
3. Service provider notifies user
4. User can file counter-notice
5. If counter-notice filed, material may be restored unless copyright owner files lawsuit

**Benefits**:
- Faster than litigation
- No court filing required
- Effective for online infringement

### Customs Recordation

**Purpose**: Prevent importation of infringing copies

**Process**:
- Record copyright with U.S. Customs and Border Protection
- Provide copy of registration certificate
- Pay fee
- Valid for 20 years (renewable)

**Benefits**:
- Customs can seize infringing goods at border
- Prevents pirated copies from entering U.S.
- Particularly useful for physical goods

## Conclusion

Copyright registration is a straightforward process that provides substantial legal benefits for protecting creative works. By understanding the requirements, preparing complete applications, providing appropriate deposits, and registering works in a timely manner, copyright owners can maximize their legal protections and enforcement options.

While copyright protection is automatic, registration transforms that protection into a powerful legal tool, enabling lawsuits, statutory damages, and enhanced enforcement. For creators and businesses that depend on copyright-protected works, registration is an essential component of a comprehensive intellectual property strategy.
