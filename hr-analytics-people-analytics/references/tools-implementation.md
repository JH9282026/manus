## Building HR Analytics Workflow

### Step 1: Start with Business Problem

**Approach:**
- Focus on business outcomes, not just HR processes
- Engage business leaders to understand priorities
- Identify pain points and opportunities
- Align with organizational strategy

**Example Questions:**
- "How can we reduce turnover in critical roles to maintain project continuity?"
- "What factors predict successful hires in our sales organization?"
- "How can we identify and develop future leaders?"
- "What drives employee engagement in our organization?"

**Avoid:**
- Starting with available data rather than business questions
- Focusing on HR metrics without business context
- Analyzing for the sake of analyzing

### Step 2: Define Success and Key Metrics

**Establish Clear Success Criteria:**
- What does success look like?
- How will we measure impact?
- What are the target outcomes?
- What is the timeline?

**Identify Specific Metrics:**
- Primary metrics (main outcomes)
- Secondary metrics (supporting indicators)
- Leading indicators (early signals)
- Lagging indicators (final results)

**Example:**
"Success means reducing regrettable attrition in engineering by 30% over 12 months. Primary metric: Regrettable attrition rate. Secondary metrics: Engagement scores, promotion rates, compensation competitiveness. Leading indicators: Turnover risk scores, manager effectiveness ratings."

### Step 3: Set Workflow Boundaries

**Define Scope:**
- Which departments or roles?
- What time period?
- What level of detail?
- What resources are available?

**Identify Stakeholders:**
- Who needs to be involved?
- Who will use the insights?
- Who has decision-making authority?
- Who can provide context and expertise?

**Establish Constraints:**
- Budget limitations
- Data availability
- Timeline requirements
- Technical capabilities

### Step 4: Map and Access Data Sources

**Identify Internal Data Sources:**
- HRIS/HCM system
- Performance management platform
- Talent acquisition system
- Learning management system
- Payroll system
- Employee surveys
- Time and attendance system

**Determine External Data Needs:**
- Industry benchmarks
- Labor market data
- Compensation surveys
- Economic indicators

**Ensure Data Access:**
- Obtain necessary permissions
- Understand data extraction methods
- Identify data owners and stewards
- Establish data refresh frequency

**Assess Data Quality:**
- Completeness
- Accuracy
- Consistency
- Timeliness
- Relevance

### Step 5: Standardize and Clean Data

**Data Cleaning Activities:**

**Remove Duplicates:**
- Identify and eliminate duplicate records
- Establish unique identifiers
- Merge duplicate entries

**Fix Formatting Issues:**
- Standardize date formats
- Consistent naming conventions
- Uniform categorizations
- Standardize text fields (e.g., job titles)

**Address Missing Data:**
- Identify patterns in missing data
- Determine if missing data is random or systematic
- Decide on handling approach:
  - Delete records with missing data (if minimal)
  - Impute missing values (mean, median, mode)
  - Create "missing" category
  - Collect missing data if critical

**Ensure Consistency:**
- Standardize across data sources
- Reconcile discrepancies
- Establish data definitions
- Document transformations

**Validate Accuracy:**
- Cross-check against source systems
- Identify and investigate outliers
- Validate calculations
- Spot-check samples

### Step 6: Analyze "What" and "Why"

**Descriptive Analysis (What):**
- Calculate summary statistics
- Create frequency distributions
- Identify trends over time
- Segment by relevant dimensions
- Compare to benchmarks

**Diagnostic Analysis (Why):**
- Correlation analysis
- Segmentation and cohort analysis
- Root cause analysis
- Regression analysis
- Statistical significance testing

**Tools:**
- Excel for basic analysis
- R or Python for advanced analysis
- Tableau or Power BI for visualization
- Statistical software (SPSS, SAS) for specialized tests

**Example:**
"Descriptive analysis shows 25% turnover in engineering, 10 percentage points above company average. Diagnostic analysis reveals turnover is highest among employees with 2-3 years tenure (35%), concentrated in specific teams. Correlation analysis indicates manager effectiveness (r=-0.62) and compensation competitiveness (r=-0.48) are strongest predictors. Regression model explains 67% of variance in turnover."

### Step 7: Package Insights for Decision-Making

**Create Clear Visualizations:**
- Use appropriate chart types
- Highlight key findings
- Minimize clutter
- Use consistent formatting
- Include context (benchmarks, trends)

**Develop Compelling Narratives:**
- Tell a story with data
- Start with key insights
- Provide supporting evidence
- Explain implications
- Recommend actions

**Link to Business Impact:**
- Quantify costs or benefits
- Connect to business outcomes
- Show ROI of interventions
- Demonstrate urgency

**Provide Actionable Recommendations:**
- Specific actions to take
- Prioritization
- Implementation approach
- Expected outcomes
- Success metrics

**Example Insight Package:**
"Engineering turnover is costing us $4.2M annually in replacement costs and lost productivity. Analysis identifies three key drivers: (1) Manager effectiveness - teams with low-rated managers have 2.5x higher turnover; (2) Compensation - employees below market rate are 3x more likely to leave; (3) Career development - lack of promotion opportunities drives 40% of departures. Recommended actions: (1) Manager training and coaching for bottom quartile; (2) Compensation adjustment for below-market employees; (3) Career pathing and promotion process improvements. Expected impact: 30% reduction in regrettable attrition, $1.3M annual savings, ROI of 4:1."

### Step 8: Turn Insights into Action and Measure Outcomes

**Implement Interventions:**
- Develop detailed action plans
- Assign ownership and accountability
- Establish timelines
- Allocate resources
- Communicate plans

**Track Results:**
- Monitor key metrics
- Track intervention implementation
- Measure outcomes
- Compare to baseline
- Assess progress toward goals

**Measure ROI:**
- Calculate costs of interventions
- Quantify benefits
- Compute return on investment
- Compare to projections

**Iterate and Refine:**
- Analyze what worked and what didn't
- Gather feedback
- Adjust approach
- Continuous improvement
- Scale successful interventions

**Example:**
"After 6 months of implementation: (1) Manager training completed for 15 managers, their teams' turnover decreased from 32% to 19%; (2) Compensation adjustments for 45 employees, retention rate improved from 65% to 88%; (3) Career pathing program launched, early indicators positive. Overall engineering turnover decreased from 25% to 18%, on track to meet 30% reduction goal. Estimated savings: $850K in first 6 months. Continuing to monitor and refine interventions."

## Conclusion

Successful HR analytics implementation requires the right combination of tools, skills, processes, and culture.
