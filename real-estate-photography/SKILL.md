# Real Estate Photography

Create compelling property images that showcase spaces accurately and attractively for real estate marketing.

## Overview

Real estate photography requires technical precision to overcome challenging lighting conditions, spatial constraints, and perspective distortions. Success involves mastering HDR techniques, flash photography, wide-angle composition, and post-processing workflows that balance realism with visual appeal. This skill covers equipment selection, shooting techniques for interiors and exteriors, lighting strategies, and efficient editing workflows for high-volume property photography.

## Essential Equipment

### Camera Requirements

**Key Features**:
- **Auto Exposure Bracketing (AEB)**: Essential for HDR, captures 3-7 exposures automatically
- **Manual Mode**: Full control over exposure settings
- **RAW Format**: Maximum editing flexibility
- **Minimum 24MP**: Sufficient resolution for web and print marketing
- **Remote Trigger Compatibility**: Prevents camera shake during bracketed exposures

**Camera Type**:
- Full-frame or APS-C DSLR/mirrorless
- High-megapixel cameras not required (final images typically 2000px wide)
- Modern entry-level cameras (Canon R50, Sony A6400, Nikon Z5) are sufficient

### Lens Selection

**Wide-Angle Lenses (Primary)**:
- **Full-Frame**: 16-35mm range
- **APS-C**: 10-24mm range (equivalent to 15-36mm)
- **Recommended Aperture**: f/2.8-f/4
- **Features**: Image stabilization, minimal distortion, weather sealing

**Focal Length Strategy**:
- **16-17mm**: Large rooms, tight spaces
- **24mm**: Standard room coverage, natural perspective
- **35mm**: Detail shots, avoiding excessive distortion

**Avoid Ultra-Wide (10-14mm)**: Creates unnatural distortion and exaggerated perspectives.

### Tripod

**Essential Features**:
- **Sturdy Construction**: Supports 2-3x camera/lens weight
- **Height Range**: 25-65 inches for various shooting angles
- **Built-in Bubble Level**: Ensures straight horizons and verticals
- **Quick-Release Plate**: Fast camera mounting
- **Quality Head**: Ball head or geared head for precise adjustments

**Why Critical**:
- Prevents camera movement between bracketed exposures
- Enables consistent framing across properties
- Allows slower shutter speeds for deep depth of field
- Professional standard for real estate photography

### Lighting Equipment

**Flash Options**:
- **Off-Camera Speedlights**: Wireless triggers, portable, versatile
- **Strobe Flashes**: More powerful, faster recycle times, professional results
- **Hot Shoe Flash**: Compact, budget-friendly option

**Light Modifiers**:
- **Softboxes**: Produce soft, even light for controlled interiors
- **Umbrellas**: Disperse light over wide area, quick setup
- **Diffusers**: Reduce harshness from direct flash
- **Grids**: Focus light beam for targeted illumination
- **MagMod MagSphere**: Popular diffuser for real estate flash

**Continuous Lighting**:
- **LED Panels**: Adjustable color temperature, see lighting in real-time
- **High CRI Rating**: Accurate color rendering
- **Portable and Lightweight**: Easy to position

**Support Equipment**:
- **Light Stands**: Position off-camera flashes
- **Radio Triggers**: Wireless flash activation
- **Reflectors**: Bounce natural light into shadowy areas

### Accessories

- **Remote Trigger**: Minimize camera shake during exposures
- **Extra Batteries**: Long shooting days, multiple properties
- **Memory Cards**: Fast write speeds (UHS-II or CFexpress), 64-128GB
- **Lens Cleaning Kit**: Remove dust and smudges
- **Tablet or Laptop**: On-site image review and client approval

## Camera Settings

### Optimal Settings for Real Estate

**Aperture**: f/8 to f/11
- Maximizes sharpness across frame
- Provides deep depth of field (entire room in focus)
- Avoids diffraction from very small apertures (f/16+)
- Consistent across all bracketed shots

**ISO**: 100 or Lowest Native ISO
- Minimizes noise for clean, professional images
- Tripod allows low ISO regardless of shutter speed

**Shutter Speed**: Variable (Adjusted for Exposure)
- Ranges from 1/20s to several seconds
- Varies between bracketed exposures
- Below flash sync speed (typically 1/200s) when using flash

**White Balance**:
- **Auto White Balance (AWB)**: Modern cameras handle well
- **Manual/Custom**: Use gray card for precise color
- **Shoot RAW**: Allows white balance adjustment in post

**Focus Mode**:
- **Manual Focus**: Set to hyperfocal distance for maximum depth of field
- **Focus Point**: 1/3 into the scene from camera

**File Format**: RAW
- Maximum editing flexibility
- Essential for HDR processing and color correction

### Exposure Bracketing Setup

**Standard 3-Shot Bracket**:
- **-2 EV**: Highlight exposure (windows, bright areas)
- **0 EV**: Mid-tone exposure (walls, furniture)
- **+2 EV**: Shadow exposure (dark corners, under furniture)

**Extended 5-Shot Bracket** (for extreme contrast):
- **-4 EV, -2 EV, 0 EV, +2 EV, +4 EV**
- Provides maximum control in post-processing
- Useful for rooms with very bright windows and dark interiors

**Bracket Interval**: 1-2 stops (EV)

**Timer Delay**: 2-second delay prevents camera shake from pressing shutter

## HDR Photography Technique

### What is HDR?

High Dynamic Range photography combines multiple exposures to capture detail in both bright and dark areas, overcoming camera sensor limitations. Real estate scenes often have 16-20 stops of dynamic range (bright windows + dark corners), while cameras capture 12-14 stops.

### HDR Shooting Process

**1. Compose and Frame**:
- Set up tripod at chest height (4-5 feet)
- Level camera (horizontal and vertical)
- Compose shot showing room's best features
- Ensure verticals are straight

**2. Lock Settings**:
- Set aperture (f/8-f/11)
- Set ISO (100)
- Enable AEB (3 or 5 shots)
- Set bracket interval (2 EV)
- Use 2-second timer or remote trigger

**3. Capture Bracket**:
- Press shutter once
- Camera automatically captures all bracketed exposures
- Remain still, avoid touching tripod
- Review images for proper exposure range

**4. Verify Coverage**:
- Check histogram on brightest exposure (no blown highlights in windows)
- Check histogram on darkest exposure (no crushed blacks in corners)
- Re-shoot if exposure range insufficient

### When to Use HDR

**Ideal Scenarios**:
- Interiors with large windows and dramatic views
- Rooms with high contrast (bright and dark areas)
- Exteriors with bright sky and shadowed architecture
- Consistent, professional look across all images

**Limitations**:
- Moving subjects (curtains, trees) can create ghosting
- Requires tripod and more time per shot
- Can look artificial if over-processed

## Flash Photography Technique

### Flash vs. HDR

**Flash Advantages**:
- Excellent control over interior lighting
- Consistent color accuracy
- Sharp detail retention
- Controlled shadows
- Faster shooting (no bracketing)
- Superior for darker interiors

**HDR Advantages**:
- Preserves natural light appearance
- Better window view preservation
- No flash equipment to carry
- Consistent results

**Flambient Technique**: Combines both (flash + ambient HDR) for best of both worlds.

### Single Flash Technique

**Bounced Flash Setup**:
- Position flash on stand, aim at ceiling or wall
- Creates large, soft light source
- Requires neutral-colored bounce surface
- Fills room with even illumination

**Settings**:
- **Camera**: Manual mode, f/8, ISO 100, 1/160s (below sync speed)
- **Flash**: Manual power, start at 1/4 power, adjust as needed
- **White Balance**: Flash or 5500K

**Process**:
1. Position flash in corner or doorway (out of frame)
2. Aim at ceiling for overhead bounce
3. Take test shot, adjust flash power
4. Ensure even illumination, no harsh shadows

### Multiple Flash System

**Advanced Setup**:
- 2-4 flashes positioned around room
- Each flash illuminates specific area
- Wireless triggers control all flashes
- Zoned lighting for independent power adjustment

**Benefits**:
- Complete control over lighting
- Consistent illumination across large spaces
- Balance interior/exterior exposure
- Professional, magazine-quality results

**Considerations**:
- Significant equipment investment
- Complex setup (10-15 minutes per room)
- Steeper learning curve
- Best for high-end properties and commercial work

## Flambient Technique

### Process Overview

Flambient blends flash exposure (even interior lighting) with ambient exposure (natural window views) for balanced, polished results.

**Step 1: Flash Exposure**:
- Set up flash(es) to evenly light interior
- Camera settings: f/8, ISO 100, 1/160s
- Capture image with flash fired
- Interior properly exposed, windows blown out

**Step 2: Ambient Exposure**:
- Turn off flash
- Adjust shutter speed to expose for windows (e.g., 1/10s)
- Capture image with natural light only
- Windows and exterior properly exposed, interior dark

**Step 3: Blend in Post**:
- Import both images into Photoshop
- Stack as layers (ambient on top)
- Create layer mask on ambient layer
- Paint in window areas from ambient exposure
- Result: Evenly lit interior with visible window views

### Flambient Benefits

- Natural yet polished appearance
- Superior window view preservation
- Excellent control over lighting
- Less equipment than multi-flash setup
- Increasingly popular technique among professionals

## Composition and Framing

### Room Photography Guidelines

**Camera Height**:
- **Standard**: Chest height (4-5 feet) for natural perspective
- **Avoid**: Eye level (too high) or waist level (too low)
- **Consistency**: Same height throughout property

**Shooting Angles**:
- **Corner Shots**: Capture two walls, show room depth and dimension
- **Straight-On**: Emphasize symmetry, architectural features
- **Doorway Shots**: Frame room through doorway, create depth

**Vertical Lines**:
- Keep walls perfectly vertical (no converging lines)
- Use tripod level and camera level indicators
- Correct any distortion in post-processing

**Horizontal Lines**:
- Keep horizons and countertops level
- Avoid tilted perspectives

### What to Include

**Show Room Function**:
- Capture enough space to understand room purpose
- Include key features (fireplace, built-ins, windows)
- Show flow between spaces

**Highlight Best Features**:
- Architectural details (crown molding, coffered ceilings)
- High-end finishes (granite counters, hardwood floors)
- Views and natural light
- Unique selling points

**Create Depth**:
- Include foreground, middle ground, background elements
- Shoot through doorways to show multiple rooms
- Use leading lines (hallways, counters) to draw eye

### What to Avoid

**Clutter and Distractions**:
- Remove personal items, clutter, trash
- Hide cords, remotes, toiletries
- Close toilet lids
- Straighten furniture, pillows, rugs

**Reflections**:
- Avoid photographer reflection in mirrors, windows, appliances
- Position carefully or remove reflective items
- Use polarizing filter to reduce glare

**Distortion**:
- Avoid extreme wide-angle (creates unnatural look)
- Don't tilt camera up or down (causes converging lines)
- Maintain natural proportions

## Exterior Photography

### Timing and Lighting

**Golden Hour** (Best):
- First hour after sunrise or last hour before sunset
- Soft, warm, flattering light
- Long shadows add dimension
- Blue sky with warm highlights

**Twilight/Blue Hour** (Dramatic):
- 20-30 minutes after sunset
- Deep blue sky, interior lights glowing
- Creates luxury, high-end appearance
- Requires tripod and longer exposures

**Overcast Days** (Acceptable):
- Soft, even light without harsh shadows
- Good for showing property details
- Less dramatic than golden hour

**Avoid Midday Sun**:
- Harsh shadows, high contrast
- Washed-out sky
- Unflattering light on architecture

### Exterior Composition

**Showcase Curb Appeal**:
- Front facade, landscaping, driveway
- Capture from street perspective (buyer's first view)
- Include context (neighborhood, surroundings)

**Multiple Angles**:
- Front, side, rear views
- Elevated perspective (drone or ladder) for large properties
- Detail shots (entryway, architectural features)

**Sky Replacement**:
- If shooting on overcast day, consider sky replacement in post
- Use natural-looking skies, avoid over-dramatic
- Ensure lighting direction matches replaced sky

## Post-Processing Workflow

### HDR Merging

**Software Options**:
- **Adobe Lightroom**: Built-in HDR merge, seamless workflow
- **Aurora HDR**: Dedicated HDR software, advanced controls
- **Photomatix Pro**: Industry standard, powerful tone mapping
- **SNS-HDR**: Professional-grade, natural results

**Merging Process**:
1. **Import** bracketed exposures
2. **Select** all images from bracket
3. **Merge** using HDR function
4. **Alignment**: Ensure perfect alignment (software auto-aligns)
5. **Deghosting**: Remove artifacts from movement (curtains, trees)
6. **Tone Mapping**: Start with conservative settings for natural look

### Essential Adjustments

**Perspective Correction**:
- Straighten vertical lines (walls, doorframes)
- Correct horizontal lines (counters, horizons)
- Use lens correction profiles to remove distortion
- Lightroom: Transform tool, Auto or Guided mode
- Photoshop: Adaptive Wide Angle or Perspective Warp

**Exposure and Tone**:
- **Global Contrast**: Enhance overall punch
- **Shadow Recovery**: Lift dark areas without losing depth
- **Highlight Protection**: Prevent blown windows
- **Whites/Blacks**: Set white and black points for full tonal range

**Color Correction**:
- **White Balance**: Accurate, neutral colors (avoid color casts)
- **Vibrance**: Subtle color enhancement (more natural than saturation)
- **HSL Adjustments**: Fine-tune specific colors (grass, sky, wood tones)

**Sharpening and Clarity**:
- **Sharpening**: Moderate amount for crisp details
- **Clarity**: Adds mid-tone contrast, enhances texture
- **Avoid Over-Sharpening**: Creates halos and unnatural look

**Noise Reduction**:
- Minimal noise at ISO 100, but check shadow areas
- Luminance noise reduction if needed

### Avoiding Over-Processing

**Common Mistakes**:
- **Over-HDR Look**: Excessive tone mapping, halos, unnatural colors
- **Too Much Clarity**: Gritty, over-sharpened appearance
- **Unrealistic Colors**: Overly saturated, neon-like hues
- **Blown Highlights**: Lost detail in windows or bright areas

**Aim for Balanced Realism**:
- Images should look natural and inviting
- Enhance reality, don't create fantasy
- Consistent style across all property images
- Professional, clean, and polished

## Efficient Workflow for Multiple Properties

### Shooting Checklist Per Property

**Exterior** (5-10 shots):
- Front facade
- Side and rear views
- Driveway and garage
- Backyard and outdoor spaces
- Architectural details

**Interior** (15-25 shots):
- Living room (2-3 angles)
- Kitchen (3-4 angles)
- Dining room
- Master bedroom and bath
- Additional bedrooms
- Additional bathrooms
- Special features (office, gym, wine cellar)

**Time Allocation**:
- Small property (1000-1500 sq ft): 30-45 minutes
- Medium property (1500-3000 sq ft): 45-75 minutes
- Large property (3000+ sq ft): 90-120 minutes

### Batch Processing

**Lightroom Workflow**:
1. **Import** all properties
2. **Organize** into collections by property
3. **Merge HDR** for all bracketed sets
4. **Apply Preset** to all images (starting point)
5. **Batch Adjustments**: Perspective correction, lens correction
6. **Individual Tweaks**: Fine-tune each image
7. **Export** with consistent settings

**Presets for Speed**:
- Create presets for common scenarios (bright interiors, dark rooms, exteriors)
- Apply on import or during editing
- Reduces editing time per image
