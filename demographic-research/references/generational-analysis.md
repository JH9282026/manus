# Generational Cohort Analysis

## Generational Profiles

### Gen Alpha (2013–present)
- First generation entirely born in 21st century
- Growing up with AI, voice assistants, and smart homes
- Most racially diverse generation in history
- Parented by millennials with high engagement styles
- Estimated 2 billion globally by 2025
- Key insight: Products must be intuitive, visual, and technology-integrated

### Gen Z (1997–2012)
- Digital natives who never knew a pre-smartphone world
- Value authenticity, diversity, and social responsibility
- Pragmatic about finances (witnessed 2008 recession as children)
- Mental health awareness significantly higher than prior generations
- Entering workforce and household formation stage
- Key insight: Reach through social media, short-form video, peer influence

### Millennials (1981–1996)
- Largest living generation in many countries
- Delayed traditional milestones (marriage, homeownership, children)
- Experience-driven spending preferences
- Now entering peak earning and family formation years
- Driving suburban migration and family housing demand
- Key insight: Value convenience, digital experiences, purpose-driven brands

### Gen X (1965–1980)
- Often called the "forgotten generation" between larger cohorts
- Peak earning years, significant purchasing power
- Sandwich generation (caring for aging parents + raising children)
- Pragmatic, self-reliant, brand-loyal when earned
- Highest homeownership rates currently
- Key insight: Value efficiency, quality, and straightforward communication

### Baby Boomers (1946–1964)
- Largest wealth holders in most developed economies
- Mass retirement wave creating workforce transitions
- Increasing healthcare and aging services demand
- Active lifestyle expectations unlike prior retirees
- Downsizing housing, driving senior living demand
- Key insight: Growing market for health, travel, financial services, senior living

## Age-Period-Cohort Analysis

### Separating Effects

When analyzing any outcome across demographics, distinguish:

- **Age effects**: Changes attributable to aging (e.g., healthcare use increases with age regardless of generation)
- **Period effects**: Changes affecting all age groups simultaneously (e.g., recession impacts all generations)
- **Cohort effects**: Unique experiences of a birth cohort that persist (e.g., Great Depression shaped Silent Generation savings behavior)

### Application Framework

1. Identify the outcome variable (spending, health behavior, technology adoption)
2. Plot by age, period, and cohort separately
3. Look for patterns unique to one dimension
4. Use APC models to decompose effects statistically
5. Apply cohort-specific insights to forward-looking strategy

## Generational Sizing and Spending Power

### Market Sizing Steps

1. Count cohort population in target geography
2. Adjust for labor force participation and income levels
3. Estimate disposable income by cohort
4. Apply category-specific spending propensities
5. Project forward based on cohort aging and income trajectories

### Life Stage Transitions

Track when cohorts hit key spending milestones:
- First job / career start
- Household formation / first apartment
- Marriage / partnership
- First child
- Peak earning years
- Children leaving home (empty nest)
- Retirement
- Healthcare intensification
