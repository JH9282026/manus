# Food Photography

Create appetizing, professional food images that showcase culinary creations for commercial and editorial use.

## Overview

Food photography combines technical skill with artistic styling to make dishes look irresistible. Success requires understanding lighting (natural and artificial), composition principles, food styling techniques, and camera angles that best showcase different types of cuisine. This skill covers equipment selection, lighting setups, styling strategies, composition techniques, and post-processing workflows for restaurant menus, cookbooks, social media, and advertising.

## Essential Equipment

### Camera and Lenses

**Camera Requirements**:
- DSLR or mirrorless with manual controls
- Good color accuracy and dynamic range
- Live view capability for precise composition
- Tethering support for client approval (professional work)

**Recommended Lenses**:
- **35-50mm Prime** (f/1.4-f/2.8): Sharpness, wide aperture, minimal distortion
- **50mm f/1.8**: Affordable, excellent for food, natural perspective
- **85-105mm**: Compression, beautiful bokeh, detail shots
- **24-70mm Zoom**: Versatility for various compositions

**Avoid**: Ultra-wide lenses (distort food), extreme telephoto (impractical for close work)

### Tripod and Support

**Overhead Rig or Tripod**:
- Essential for flat lay photography (90° overhead shots)
- Tripod with horizontal center column OR dedicated overhead rig
- Allows hands-free shooting for styling adjustments
- Ensures consistent framing across multiple shots

### Lighting Equipment

**Natural Light Tools**:
- **Diffusers**: White fabric, sheer curtains, diffusion panels (soften harsh sunlight)
- **Reflectors**: White foam boards, silver/gold reflectors (bounce light, fill shadows)
- **Black Cards**: Absorb light, deepen shadows, add contrast

**Artificial Light**:
- **Softboxes**: Large (24"x36"+) for soft, window-like light
- **LED Panels**: Continuous light, adjustable color temperature (3000-5600K)
- **Speedlights**: Portable flash with diffusers
- **Light Stands**: Position lights at various angles

### Styling Props

**Backgrounds**:
- Wooden boards (rustic, natural)
- Marble tiles (elegant, high-end)
- Textured fabrics (linen, burlap)
- Seamless paper (clean, modern)
- Non-reflective surfaces (prevent glare)

**Props and Utensils**:
- Plates, bowls, cutting boards (various styles)
- Cutlery and serving utensils
- Napkins and linens
- Ingredients and garnishes
- Glassware and drinkware

**Styling Tools**:
- Tweezers (precise garnish placement)
- Brushes (apply oil for shine)
- Spray bottles (water droplets, freshness)
- Cotton swabs (clean edges)
- Toothpicks (position elements)

## Lighting Techniques

### Natural Light Photography

**Window Light Setup**:
- Position food near large window (north-facing ideal for consistent light)
- Shoot during daytime when light is soft and even
- Avoid direct sunlight (creates harsh shadows)

**Side Lighting** (Most Common):
- Light source 90° to side of subject
- Accentuates textures and colors
- Creates gentle shadows for dimension
- Use reflector opposite light to fill shadows

**Backlighting**:
- Light source behind subject
- Highlights shine and translucency (drinks, lettuce, sauces)
- Creates rim light effect
- Requires reflector or fill light in front to prevent silhouette
- Dramatic and artistic

**Diffusion**:
- Place sheer white curtain or diffusion panel between window and food
- Softens harsh sunlight
- Creates even, flattering illumination
- Cloudy days provide natural diffusion

**Reflectors**:
- White foam board: Soft, subtle fill light
- Silver reflector: Brighter, more dramatic fill
- Gold reflector: Warm, sunset-like glow
- Position opposite main light source to bounce light back

**Black Cards**:
- Opposite reflector, absorbs light
- Deepens shadows for contrast and drama
- Creates moodier, more dimensional look

### Artificial Light Setup

**Mimicking Window Light**:
- Large softbox (36"x48") positioned to side
- Simulates natural window light
- Consistent, controllable results
- Independent of weather and time of day

**Key + Fill Setup**:
- **Key Light**: Main softbox at 45° angle, slightly above subject
- **Fill Light**: Smaller softbox or reflector opposite key, lower power
- Ratio: Key at 100%, fill at 30-50%

**Overhead Lighting**:
- Softbox directly above subject
- Even illumination for flat lays
- Minimal shadows
- Clean, modern look

**Color Temperature**:
- Match light temperature to desired mood
- **5000-5600K**: Daylight, neutral, fresh
- **3200-4000K**: Warm, cozy, inviting
- **Avoid Fluorescent**: Green tint, unflattering

**Continuous vs. Flash**:
- **Continuous (LED)**: See lighting in real-time, easier for beginners
- **Flash**: More powerful, freezes motion, professional standard

## Composition and Camera Angles

### The Three Primary Angles

**0-45° Angle** (Most Versatile):
- Camera at table height, slight downward angle
- Shows depth, layers, and height of food
- Mimics diner's perspective
- Ideal for: Burgers, cakes, layered dishes, stacked items
- Aperture: f/2.8-f/5.6 for shallow depth of field

**90° Overhead (Flat Lay)**:
- Camera directly above subject
- Graphic, eye-catching perspective
- Shows patterns, arrangements, multiple items
- Ideal for: Salads, pizzas, charcuterie boards, table settings
- Aperture: f/8-f/16 for everything in focus

**Straight-On (Eye Level)**:
- Camera at same height as food
- Emphasizes height and vertical elements
- Ideal for: Tall drinks, sandwiches, burgers, ice cream cones
- Creates intimate, engaging perspective

### Composition Principles

**Rule of Thirds**:
- Divide frame into 9 equal rectangles (3x3 grid)
- Place main subject at intersection points
- Creates balanced, dynamic composition
- Avoid centering subject (static, boring)

**Negative Space**:
- Empty areas around subject
- Allows subject to breathe
- Provides space for text (menus, ads)
- Creates clean, uncluttered look
- Typically 30-50% of frame

**Leading Lines**:
- Use cutlery, napkins, ingredients to guide eye
- Direct viewer's attention to main dish
- Create movement and flow
- Add visual interest

**Layering and Depth**:
- Foreground, middle ground, background elements
- Shallow depth of field (f/2.8-f/4) blurs background
- Main dish sharp, surroundings soft
- Creates three-dimensional feel

**Framing**:
- Use props or ingredients to frame main subject
- Draws attention to focal point
- Adds context and story

**Patterns and Repetition**:
- Multiple similar items (cookies, cupcakes)
- Creates visual harmony
- One item different (breaking pattern) adds interest

**Color Harmony**:
- Complementary colors (opposite on color wheel)
- Analogous colors (adjacent on color wheel)
- Props and backgrounds that enhance food colors

### Styling to the Camera

**Critical Technique**:
- Set up camera and lock position FIRST
- Build scene while looking through viewfinder
- What looks good to eye may not translate to camera
- Adjust styling based on camera's perspective

**Hero Element**:
- Identify most appealing part of dish
- Position prominently in frame
- Ensure it's in sharp focus
- Example: Perfect strawberry, glistening sauce, crispy edge

## Food Styling Techniques

### Making Food Look Appetizing

**Freshness**:
- Shoot quickly after plating (food wilts, melts, dries)
- Keep backup dishes ready
- Refresh garnishes between shots
- Mist with water for dewy, fresh look

**Shine and Gloss**:
- Brush oil on meats, vegetables for appetizing sheen
- Glycerin for longer-lasting shine
- Avoid over-oiling (looks greasy)

**Height and Dimension**:
- Stack ingredients for visual interest
- Use hidden supports (toothpicks, foam) to elevate elements
- Avoid flat, one-dimensional plating

**Garnishes**:
- Fresh herbs add color and freshness
- Sprinkle finishing touches (salt, pepper, seeds)
- Drizzle sauces artfully
- Less is more (avoid clutter)

**Color Contrast**:
- Colorful ingredients pop against neutral backgrounds
- Green herbs on brown meat
- Red tomatoes on white plate
- Variety of colors creates visual interest

### Common Styling Tricks

**Ice Cream**:
- Use mashed potatoes or shortening (doesn't melt)
- Real ice cream for hero shots (work fast)
- Pre-freeze bowls and props

**Beverages**:
- Fake ice cubes (acrylic, reusable)
- Glycerin for condensation droplets
- Backlight for translucency and glow

**Steam and Smoke**:
- Wet cotton balls in microwave (creates steam)
- Incense or smoke machine (background)
- Adds warmth and freshness cues

**Grill Marks**:
- Heated metal skewer creates perfect marks
- More controlled than actual grilling

**Imperfections**:
- Slight irregularities add authenticity
- Crumbs, drips, and casual placement
- Overly perfect looks artificial

## Camera Settings

**Aperture**:
- **f/2.8-f/5.6**: Shallow depth of field, blurred background, subject isolation
- **f/8-f/16**: Deep depth of field, everything in focus (flat lays)
- Wider aperture = more bokeh, narrower = more sharpness throughout

**Shutter Speed**:
- **1/80-1/125s**: General food photography with tripod
- **1/200s+**: Handheld or action shots (pouring, sprinkling)
- Slower speeds acceptable on tripod with still subjects

**ISO**:
- **100-400**: Minimize noise, maximum quality
- Increase only if light is insufficient
- Use tripod to keep ISO low

**White Balance**:
- **Daylight (5200K)**: For natural window light
- **Custom**: Use gray card for accurate colors
- **Shoot RAW**: Allows white balance adjustment in post

**Focus**:
- Manual focus for precision
- Focus on hero element (closest part of main dish)
- Use live view and magnification for accuracy

## Post-Processing

**Minimal Adjustments**:
- Professional food photographers do most work in-camera
- Post-processing enhances, doesn't fix

**Essential Edits**:
- **Exposure**: Brighten slightly if needed
- **Contrast**: Add punch without crushing blacks
- **Vibrance**: Subtle color enhancement (more natural than saturation)
- **Sharpening**: Moderate, emphasize textures
- **White Balance**: Ensure accurate, appetizing colors

**Color Grading**:
- Warm tones (orange/yellow) = comfort, appetite
- Cool tones (blue) = fresh, modern (use sparingly)
- Consistent color palette across series

**Cleanup**:
- Remove distracting crumbs or spills
- Clone out imperfections
- Clean edges of plates

**Avoid**:
- Over-saturation (unnatural, garish)
- Excessive sharpening (gritty, harsh)
- Heavy filters (distract from food)
