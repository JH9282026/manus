# Advanced Music Theory Techniques

Professional composition and analysis methods.

## Advanced Harmony

### Modal Interchange (Borrowed Chords)

**Concept** — Borrowing chords from parallel major/minor keys

**Common Borrowed Chords in Major:**
- bVI (from minor) — Adds darkness and color
- bVII (from minor) — Provides alternative to V
- iv (from minor) — Subdominant with minor quality
- bIII (from minor) — Mediant with major quality

**Application** — Use for emotional contrast and harmonic color

### Secondary Dominants

**Definition** — Dominant chord that resolves to chord other than tonic

**Notation** — V/V (dominant of dominant), V/ii, V/iii, V/IV, V/vi

**Function** — Creates temporary tonicization of target chord

**Example in C Major:**
- V/V = D7 (resolves to G, the V chord)
- V/ii = A7 (resolves to Dm, the ii chord)

### Diminished Seventh Chords

**Symmetrical Structure** — Built entirely of minor thirds

**Enharmonic Equivalence** — Each dim7 chord has four possible roots

**Function** — Leading tone diminished seventh (vii°7)

**Resolution** — Typically resolves to tonic or acts as passing chord

### Augmented Sixth Chords

**Italian Sixth (It+6)** — Root, M3, A6

**French Sixth (Fr+6)** — Root, M3, A4, A6

**German Sixth (Ger+6)** — Root, M3, P5, A6

**Function** — Predominant chords resolving to dominant

## Advanced Modulation Techniques

### Pivot Chord Modulation

**Process:**
1. Establish original key
2. Introduce pivot chord (common to both keys)
3. Reinterpret pivot chord in new key
4. Confirm new key with cadence

**Example:** C major to G major using Am (vi in C, ii in G)

### Common-Tone Modulation

**Technique** — Hold single note between keys as connection

**Application** — Useful for distant key relationships

**Effect** — Smooth transition despite harmonic distance

### Chromatic Modulation

**Method** — Use chromatic voice leading to move between keys

**Characteristics** — Gradual harmonic shift through chromaticism

**Common in** — Romantic era music and film scores

### Direct (Phrase) Modulation

**Approach** — Abrupt key change without preparation

**Effect** — Dramatic, attention-grabbing

**Usage** — Final chorus key changes, sectional contrasts

## Counterpoint

### Species Counterpoint

**First Species** — Note against note (1:1 ratio)
- Focus on consonant intervals
- Contrary and oblique motion preferred
- Perfect consonances on strong beats

**Second Species** — Two notes against one (2:1 ratio)
- Introduces passing tones
- Dissonance allowed on weak beats
- Maintains smooth voice leading

**Third Species** — Four notes against one (4:1 ratio)
- Greater melodic freedom
- Neighbor tones and passing tones
- Dissonance on weak beats only

**Fourth Species** — Syncopation (suspensions)
- Tied notes creating suspensions
- Preparation, suspension, resolution
- Creates expressive dissonance

**Fifth Species** — Florid counterpoint (free combination)
- Combines all previous species
- Maximum melodic and rhythmic freedom
- Maintains contrapuntal principles

### Fugue Construction

**Subject** — Main melodic theme

**Answer** — Subject transposed to dominant (real or tonal)

**Countersubject** — Counterpoint accompanying answer

**Episodes** — Transitional passages between subject entries

**Stretto** — Overlapping subject entries

**Exposition** — Initial presentation of subject in all voices

### Invertible Counterpoint

**Definition** — Counterpoint that works when voices are inverted

**Double Counterpoint** — Two voices can be inverted

**Triple Counterpoint** — Three voices can be inverted in any order

**Application** — Creates variation and development opportunities

## Advanced Chord Progressions

### Jazz Harmony

**ii-V-I Progression** — Fundamental jazz cadence

**Tritone Substitution** — Replace V7 with bII7 (shares tritone)

**Turnaround** — I-vi-ii-V progression returning to I

**Rhythm Changes** — I-vi-ii-V / I-vi-ii-V / III-VI-II-V / I-vi-ii-V

### Extended Harmony

**Quartal Harmony** — Chords built in fourths instead of thirds

**Cluster Chords** — Adjacent notes creating dense harmonies

**Polychords** — Two chords sounded simultaneously

**Upper Structure Triads** — Triad built on extensions of seventh chord

### Neo-Riemannian Theory

**Parallel (P)** — Change third quality (C major ↔ C minor)

**Leading-Tone Exchange (L)** — Move by minor third, change mode

**Relative (R)** — Move to relative major/minor

**Application** — Analyze chromatic harmony in Romantic music

## Form and Structure

### Binary Form (AB)

**Simple Binary** — Two contrasting sections

**Rounded Binary** — Return of A material at end of B

**Balanced Binary** — Both sections end with similar material

### Ternary Form (ABA)

**Structure** — Statement, contrast, restatement

**Da Capo Aria** — ABA form with ornamentation on return

**Minuet and Trio** — Classic ternary form in dance movements

### Sonata Form

**Exposition** — Primary theme (tonic), secondary theme (dominant/relative major)

**Development** — Thematic manipulation and modulation

**Recapitulation** — Return of themes in tonic key

**Coda** — Concluding section reinforcing tonic

### Rondo Form

**Structure** — ABACA or ABACABA

**Refrain** — Recurring A section in tonic

**Episodes** — Contrasting B and C sections

**Usage** — Final movements of classical works

## Orchestration and Voicing

### Four-Part Harmony

**SATB** — Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass

**Spacing** — Keep upper three voices within octave

**Doubling** — Double root in root position, avoid doubling leading tone

**Voice Leading** — Minimize motion, resolve tendency tones

### Open vs. Closed Voicing

**Closed Voicing** — Chord tones as close as possible

**Open Voicing** — Wider spacing between chord tones

**Drop 2** — Lower second-highest note by octave

**Drop 3** — Lower third-highest note by octave

### Orchestral Ranges and Timbres

**String Section** — Violin, viola, cello, double bass

**Woodwind Section** — Flute, oboe, clarinet, bassoon

**Brass Section** — Trumpet, horn, trombone, tuba

**Percussion** — Timpani, snare, cymbals, auxiliary

**Doubling** — Combine instruments for blend and power

## Contemporary Techniques

### Serialism and Twelve-Tone

**Tone Row** — Ordered arrangement of all 12 chromatic notes

**Prime Form** — Original row

**Retrograde** — Row in reverse

**Inversion** — Intervals inverted

**Retrograde Inversion** — Inverted row in reverse

### Minimalism

**Repetition** — Extensive use of repeated patterns

**Phase Shifting** — Gradual tempo changes creating phase relationships

**Additive Process** — Gradually adding notes to pattern

**Subtractive Process** — Gradually removing notes from pattern

### Modal Composition

**Static Harmony** — Emphasis on single mode without functional progression

**Pedal Points** — Sustained or repeated bass note

**Quartal/Quintal Harmony** — Chords built in fourths or fifths

**Melodic Focus** — Melody drives harmony rather than vice versa

### Polytonality and Atonality

**Polytonality** — Multiple keys simultaneously

**Atonality** — Absence of tonal center

**Pandiatonicism** — Free use of diatonic scale without functional harmony

**Chromaticism** — Extensive use of chromatic notes
