# Journey Mapping & Service Blueprinting

## Journey Mapping Deep Dive

### Journey Map Components

Every journey map should include these layers:

**Customer Actions** — What the customer does at each stage (searches, clicks, calls, visits)

**Touchpoints** — Where interactions happen (website, app, store, phone, email)

**Thoughts** — What the customer is thinking ("Is this the right choice?" "Why is this so complicated?")

**Emotions** — How the customer feels (frustrated, confident, anxious, delighted)

**Pain Points** — Specific friction or failure points

**Opportunities** — Areas for improvement or differentiation

### Journey Stages Framework

| Stage | Customer Goal | Key Questions |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| Awareness | Recognize need/problem | "Do I have a problem?" |
| Consideration | Research options | "What solutions exist?" |
| Decision | Choose solution | "Which option is best?" |
| Purchase | Complete transaction | "Is this easy to buy?" |
| Onboarding | Start using | "How do I get started?" |
| Usage | Get value | "Is this meeting my needs?" |
| Support | Resolve issues | "Can I get help quickly?" |
| Renewal/Expansion | Continue/grow | "Should I stay/buy more?" |
| Advocacy | Recommend | "Would I tell others?" |

### Emotion Mapping Techniques

Rate emotions on a -5 to +5 scale at each touchpoint:
- **+5 to +3**: Delight moments (exceed expectations)
- **+2 to +1**: Positive (meets expectations)
- **0**: Neutral
- **-1 to -2**: Mild frustration (minor friction)
- **-3 to -5**: Pain points (significant negative experience)

Plot these as an emotion curve across the journey to identify peaks and valleys.

### Moments of Truth

Identify critical interactions that disproportionately affect overall perception:

**First Moment of Truth** — First impression with product/brand
**Second Moment of Truth** — Actual usage experience
**Zero Moment of Truth** — Pre-purchase research (Google's concept)
**Ultimate Moment of Truth** — When customer shares experience with others

## Service Blueprinting

### Blueprint Layers

1. **Physical Evidence** — Tangible elements customers see (website, store, packaging)
2. **Customer Actions** — Steps customers take
3. **Frontstage Actions** — Visible employee/system actions
4. **Line of Visibility** — Boundary between visible and invisible
5. **Backstage Actions** — Invisible employee/system actions
6. **Line of Internal Interaction** — Boundary between departments
7. **Support Processes** — Internal systems and processes enabling delivery

### Creating a Service Blueprint

1. Identify the service process to blueprint
2. Map customer actions (journey map becomes top layer)
3. Map frontstage employee/system actions aligned to customer steps
4. Draw the line of visibility
5. Map backstage actions that support frontstage delivery
6. Draw the line of internal interaction
7. Map support processes (IT systems, training, policies)
8. Identify fail points, bottlenecks, and wait times
9. Note areas for automation or improvement

### Blueprint Symbols

- **F** — Fail point (where things commonly go wrong)
- **W** — Wait point (where customers experience delays)
- **D** — Decision point (where process branches)
- **B** — Bottleneck (where capacity constrains flow)

### Applying Blueprints to Improvement

Use blueprints to:
- Identify redundant steps that add no customer value
- Find disconnects between frontstage promises and backstage capabilities
- Spot single points of failure with no backup
- Design technology interventions for manual bottlenecks
- Align cross-department handoffs that cause customer friction
- Plan capacity for high-demand touchpoints
