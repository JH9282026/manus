# Green Screen Lighting Setups

Professional lighting configurations for optimal chroma key results.

---

## Fundamental Lighting Principles

### Even Illumination Requirements

**Why Even Lighting Matters**
- Consistent color across entire screen
- Uniform brightness for clean keying
- Eliminates hotspots that cause transparency issues
- Reduces post-production correction time

**Measuring Even Lighting**
- Use waveform monitor to check brightness consistency
- Target 40-45 IRE for green screens
- Target 25-30 IRE for blue screens
- Variation should be less than 5 IRE across screen

### Light Quality Considerations

**Soft vs. Hard Light**
- **Soft light**: Diffused, even, minimal shadows (preferred for green screen)
- **Hard light**: Direct, creates defined shadows (avoid for background)
- Use softboxes, diffusion panels, or bounce for soft light

**Color Temperature**
- Match all lights to same color temperature (typically 5600K daylight)
- Use color meter to verify consistency
- Avoid mixing tungsten and daylight sources
- Correct any variations in post-production

## Basic Two-Light Setup

### Configuration

**Light Placement**
- Position two lights on either side of green screen
- Angle lights at 45 degrees to screen surface
- Place lights slightly behind subject plane
- Height: Aim lights slightly downward toward screen center

**Distance and Power**
- Position lights 6-8 feet from screen
- Adjust power to achieve target IRE levels
- Balance both lights for even coverage
- Use barn doors to control spill onto subject

**Best For**
- Small to medium green screens (8x10 feet or less)
- Single subject shoots
- Budget-conscious setups
- Portable/location work

### Limitations

- May have slight falloff at screen edges
- Limited coverage for larger screens
- Requires careful positioning for even results

## Four-Light Background Setup

### Configuration

**Light Positions**
- Two lights upper left and right (angled down)
- Two lights lower left and right (angled up)
- Create overlapping coverage zones
- Eliminate dark spots and hotspots

**Overlap Strategy**
- Each light covers approximately 60% of screen width
- Overlap zones blend for even illumination
- Adjust individual light power for consistency
- Use waveform monitor to verify even coverage

**Best For**
- Medium to large green screens (10x20 feet)
- Multiple subject shoots
- Professional studio installations
- Situations requiring consistent results

### Advantages

- Superior even coverage across large areas
- Eliminates virtually all hotspots and shadows
- Provides redundancy if one light fails
- Professional-grade results

## Subject Lighting Integration

### Three-Point Lighting for Green Screen

**Key Light**
- Primary light source for subject
- Position 45 degrees from camera-subject axis
- Elevated 30-45 degrees above subject eye level
- Provides main illumination and modeling

**Fill Light**
- Softens shadows created by key light
- Position opposite key light, closer to camera axis
- Power: 50-75% of key light intensity
- Reduces contrast for cleaner keying

**Back Light (Critical for Green Screen)**
- Positioned behind subject, aimed at back of head/shoulders
- Creates separation from green screen
- Reduces green spill on subject edges
- Essential for clean edge keying

### Hair Light Importance

**Purpose**
- Illuminates hair from above and behind
- Creates definition and separation
- Reduces green spill in hair
- Preserves fine detail for keying

**Positioning**
- Directly above and behind subject
- Angled to graze hair without hitting face
- Power: Similar to or slightly brighter than key light
- Use barn doors to control spill

## Advanced Lighting Techniques

### Graduated Lighting

**Concept**
- Intentionally vary screen brightness top to bottom
- Matches natural lighting falloff in backgrounds
- Creates more realistic integration
- Requires careful planning with background plate

**Implementation**
- Reduce lower light power by 10-20%
- Creates subtle gradient from top to bottom
- Adjust subject lighting to match gradient
- Coordinate with background plate lighting

### Edge Lighting Strategy

**Rim Lighting**
- Additional lights specifically for subject edges
- Positioned to graze subject outline
- Reduces green spill on edges
- Creates clean separation for keying

**Kicker Lights**
- Accent lights from behind and to side
- Adds dimension and depth to subject
- Helps separate subject from background
- Adjustable intensity for different looks

## Lighting for Specific Scenarios

### Full-Body Shots

**Challenges**
- Larger screen area to light evenly
- Subject movement creates varying distances
- Floor/ground plane requires attention
- Shadows on screen from subject

**Solutions**
- Use four or more background lights
- Increase subject-to-screen distance (6-8 feet minimum)
- Light floor separately if visible
- Elevate subject on platform if possible

### Close-Up/Talking Head Shots

**Advantages**
- Smaller screen area required
- Easier to achieve even lighting
- Less subject movement
- Reduced spill concerns

**Optimization**
- Focus lighting on screen area behind subject only
- Use flags to prevent light spill beyond needed area
- Increase subject-to-screen distance for softer shadows
- Emphasize hair/edge lighting for separation

### Product/Object Lighting

**Considerations**
- Reflective surfaces require special attention
- No "back light" for separation
- May need overhead lighting
- Shadows more critical for realism

**Techniques**
- Use polarizing filters for reflective surfaces
- Create artificial shadows in post-production
- Light from multiple angles to eliminate unwanted reflections
- Consider using blue screen for metallic objects

## Lighting Equipment Guide

### Light Types

| Type | Advantages | Disadvantages | Best For |
|------|------------|---------------|----------|
| LED Panels | Cool operation, adjustable color temp, energy efficient | Lower output than tungsten | Studio, continuous shooting |
| Tungsten | High output, inexpensive | Hot, fixed color temp, high power draw | Budget setups, short shoots |
| Fluorescent | Soft light, cool, energy efficient | Bulky, potential flicker | Studio installations |
| HMI | Very high output, daylight balanced | Expensive, requires ballast | Large screens, film production |

### Modifiers and Accessories

**Softboxes**
- Create soft, even light
- Various sizes for different applications
- Reduce hotspots on green screen
- Essential for professional results

**Diffusion Panels**
- Placed between light and screen
- Spreads light for even coverage
- Reduces intensity, requires more powerful lights
- Excellent for eliminating hotspots

**Barn Doors**
- Control light spill and direction
- Prevent background lights from hitting subject
- Shape light for specific coverage areas
- Essential for clean lighting separation

**Flags and Cutters**
- Block light from specific areas
- Prevent lens flare
- Control spill onto unwanted surfaces
- Create negative fill when needed

## Troubleshooting Lighting Issues

### Hotspots

**Causes**
- Lights too close to screen
- Lights aimed directly at screen center
- Reflective screen material
- Insufficient diffusion

**Solutions**
- Increase light-to-screen distance
- Angle lights more obliquely
- Add diffusion to lights
- Use more lights at lower power

### Uneven Coverage

**Causes**
- Insufficient number of lights
- Poor light positioning
- Lights at different power levels
- Screen not flat or evenly colored

**Solutions**
- Add more lights for overlap
- Reposition lights for better coverage
- Balance light output using waveform monitor
- Repair or replace screen if damaged

### Green Spill on Subject

**Causes**
- Subject too close to screen
- Excessive background light power
- Insufficient subject backlighting
- Reflective subject materials

**Solutions**
- Increase subject-to-screen distance
- Reduce background light intensity
- Add/increase hair and edge lighting
- Change wardrobe or use dulling spray on reflective items

### Shadows on Screen

**Causes**
- Subject too close to screen
- Hard light sources
- Insufficient background lighting
- Subject blocking background lights

**Solutions**
- Move subject further from screen (minimum 3-5 feet)
- Use softer, more diffused light sources
- Add more background lights
- Reposition lights to avoid subject blocking

## Lighting Workflow Checklist

### Pre-Shoot Setup

- [ ] Position green screen flat and wrinkle-free
- [ ] Set up background lights first
- [ ] Measure and balance background light levels
- [ ] Verify even coverage with waveform monitor
- [ ] Position subject at appropriate distance
- [ ] Set up subject lighting (key, fill, back, hair)
- [ ] Check for green spill on subject
- [ ] Verify no shadows on screen
- [ ] Test key with camera and keying software
- [ ] Make final adjustments based on test

### During Shoot

- [ ] Monitor waveform for consistent background levels
- [ ] Check for shadows as subject moves
- [ ] Verify lighting consistency across takes
- [ ] Adjust for any wardrobe or prop changes
- [ ] Maintain consistent camera settings

### Post-Shoot

- [ ] Document lighting setup for future reference
- [ ] Note any issues encountered
- [ ] Record light positions and power settings
- [ ] Save setup photos for recreation
