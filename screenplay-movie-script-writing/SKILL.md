# Screenplay & Movie Script Writing

## Introduction to Screenplay Writing

Screenplay writing is the art and craft of writing scripts for film, television, and other visual media. Unlike novels or stage plays, screenplays are blueprints for visual storytelling—they describe what audiences will see and hear on screen while leaving room for directors, actors, and cinematographers to bring the story to life.

A screenplay is a highly structured document that follows strict formatting conventions established by the film industry over decades. These standards exist for practical reasons: they help producers estimate runtime, allow crew members to break down scenes for production, and enable actors to learn their dialogue efficiently.

Professional screenwriters understand that their work serves as the foundation for collaborative filmmaking. The screenplay must communicate story, character, and emotion clearly while remaining technically precise enough for production planning.

---

## Screenplay vs. Other Forms of Writing

### Key Differences from Prose Fiction

Unlike novels, screenplays cannot delve into characters' internal thoughts or provide extensive backstory through narration. Everything must be externalized—shown through action, dialogue, or visual cues. Where a novelist might write "Sarah felt a deep sense of betrayal," a screenwriter must convey this through what Sarah does and says.

**Novel approach:**
> Sarah felt her heart sink as she realized the truth. Years of trust, shattered in an instant. She thought about all the times he had looked her in the eyes and lied.

**Screenplay approach:**
> Sarah's smile fades. She stares at the photograph, then slowly crumples it in her fist. A single tear rolls down her cheek.

### Differences from Stage Plays

While both screenplays and stage plays use dialogue and action, screenplays have far greater flexibility in setting and scope. Stage plays typically occur in limited locations with continuous scenes, while screenplays can jump between locations, time periods, and perspectives instantly through editing.

Stage plays rely heavily on dialogue to convey information, while screenplays can show rather than tell through visual storytelling, montages, and the power of the camera to capture subtle details.

### The Visual Medium

Screenwriting is fundamentally visual writing. Every scene should be conceived cinematically—what does the camera see? What sounds fill the environment? The screenwriter thinks in images, sounds, and movements that translate to the screen.

## Types of Screenplays

### Feature Film Screenplays

Feature films typically run 90-120 minutes, translating to screenplays of 90-120 pages (following the industry standard of approximately one page per minute of screen time). Feature screenplays tell complete, self-contained stories with clear beginnings, middles, and endings.

**Characteristics:**
- Complete story arc
- 90-120 pages standard length
- Three-act structure most common
- Single protagonist journey
- Clear resolution

### Short Film Screenplays

Short films range from 1-40 minutes, with most festival shorts running 10-20 minutes. Short screenplays demand economy of storytelling—every scene must serve multiple purposes.

**Characteristics:**
- 5-30 pages typical length
- Single concept or theme focus
- Limited character count
- Often experimental or artistic
- Budget-conscious storytelling

### TV Pilot Scripts

Pilot scripts introduce a television series, establishing characters, setting, tone, and the central premise. They must tell a compelling story while demonstrating the show's potential for multiple episodes.

**Characteristics:**
- 30 pages (half-hour comedy) or 60 pages (hour drama)
- Establishes series concept
- Introduces main characters
- Sets up ongoing conflicts
- Demonstrates episode potential

### TV Episode Scripts

Episode scripts continue established series, maintaining consistency with previous episodes while advancing seasonal and series arcs.

**Characteristics:**
- Follows established format and style
- Balances episodic and serialized elements
- Maintains character voices
- Advances ongoing storylines
- Typically includes A, B, and sometimes C stories

### Web Series Scripts

Web series scripts accommodate shorter runtimes and digital viewing habits. Episodes typically run 5-15 minutes with punchy, fast-paced storytelling.

**Characteristics:**
- 5-15 pages per episode
- Hook-heavy opening
- Quick scene transitions
- Cliffhanger endings
- Mobile-friendly pacing

## Screenplay Fundamentals

### Three-Act Structure

The three-act structure remains the dominant framework for feature film storytelling:

**Act One (Setup) - Pages 1-30:**
- Introduces protagonist and their ordinary world
- Establishes stakes and central conflict
- Presents the inciting incident
- Ends with the first major plot point

**Act Two (Confrontation) - Pages 30-90:**
- Protagonist pursues their goal
- Escalating obstacles and complications
- Midpoint reversal shifts the story
- All seems lost moment before Act Three

**Act Three (Resolution) - Pages 90-120:**
- Climactic confrontation
- Final battle or ultimate test
- Resolution of central conflict
- New equilibrium established

### Story Structure and Plot

Plot is the sequence of events that comprises the story. Strong plots feature:

- **Causality:** Each event causes the next
- **Escalation:** Stakes and tension increase
- **Inevitability:** The ending feels both surprising and inevitable
- **Unity:** All elements serve the central story

### Character Development

Characters drive stories. Audiences connect with protagonists who:

- Have clear, compelling goals
- Face meaningful obstacles
- Change through their experiences
- Make difficult choices
- Reveal depth through action

### Conflict and Stakes

Without conflict, there is no drama. Every scene requires conflict—whether external (character vs. character, character vs. nature) or internal (character vs. self). Stakes define what characters stand to gain or lose, creating emotional investment.

**Types of stakes:**
- **Personal:** Love, relationships, identity
- **Professional:** Career, reputation, achievement
- **Physical:** Life, safety, health
- **Societal:** Community, justice, world

### Theme and Message

Theme is the underlying meaning or central idea of your screenplay. While not explicitly stated, theme emerges through character choices, plot outcomes, and visual motifs. Strong themes provide coherence and resonance.

## Screenplay Format and Industry Standards

### Standard Screenplay Format

Professional screenplays follow strict formatting conventions:

**Font:** Courier 12-point (monospaced font ensures consistent page timing)

**Margins:**
- Left margin: 1.5 inches
- Right margin: 1 inch
- Top margin: 1 inch
- Bottom margin: 1 inch

**Page count:** Approximately 1 page = 1 minute of screen time

### Scene Headings (Sluglines)

Scene headings establish location and time. Format:

```
INT. COFFEE SHOP - DAY
```

**Components:**
- **INT./EXT.:** Interior or Exterior
- **LOCATION:** Specific place name
- **TIME:** DAY, NIGHT, DAWN, DUSK, CONTINUOUS, LATER

**Examples:**
```
EXT. CENTRAL PARK - DAWN
INT. SARAH'S APARTMENT - KITCHEN - NIGHT
INT. MOVING CAR - DAY
EXT. BEACH - SUNSET
```

### Action Lines

Action lines describe what happens on screen—visual action, character movement, and setting details. They are written in present tense, third person.

```
Sarah enters the coffee shop, scanning the crowded room. She spots MICHAEL (30s, disheveled, nursing a cold coffee) in the corner booth and freezes.
```

**Guidelines:**
- Present tense only
- Visual description only (no internal thoughts)
- Brief, punchy sentences
- Active voice
- New paragraph for new actions

### Character Names

Character names appear in ALL CAPS when first introduced in action lines, then normal case thereafter. Above dialogue, names are always centered and in ALL CAPS.

**First introduction:**
```
DETECTIVE RACHEL CHEN (40s, sharp eyes, rumpled suit) steps out of the unmarked car.
```

**Subsequent mentions:**
```
Rachel approaches the crime scene tape.
```

### Dialogue

Dialogue is centered on the page, beneath the character name:

```
                         SARAH
          I thought you said you'd be on time.

                         MICHAEL
          Traffic. You know how it is.

                         SARAH
          For three hours?
```

### Parentheticals

Parentheticals provide brief direction for how dialogue is delivered. Use sparingly—only when the delivery isn't clear from context.

```
                         MICHAEL
                   (defensive)
          I got held up at work.

                         SARAH
                   (sotto voce)
          Sure you did.
```

**Common parentheticals:**
- (beat)
- (continuing)
- (O.S.) - off screen
- (V.O.) - voice over
- (CONT'D) - continued

### Transitions

Transitions indicate how scenes connect. In modern screenwriting, transitions are used sparingly.

**Common transitions:**
```
                                              CUT TO:

                                              DISSOLVE TO:

                                              SMASH CUT TO:

                                              FADE OUT.

                                              FADE IN:
```

Most scene changes are implied and don't require explicit transitions.

## Scene Headings Deep Dive

### INT. vs. EXT.

**INT. (Interior):** Scenes occurring inside buildings, vehicles, or enclosed spaces.
```
INT. HOSPITAL ROOM - NIGHT
INT. SUBWAY CAR - DAY
INT. CAVE - DAY
```

**EXT. (Exterior):** Scenes occurring outdoors.
```
EXT. CITY STREET - NIGHT
EXT. MOUNTAIN SUMMIT - DAY
EXT. BACKYARD - DUSK
```

**Combination scenes:**
```
INT./EXT. CAR - MOVING - DAY
```

### Location Specificity

Be specific enough for production planning but not overly detailed:

**Too vague:**
```
INT. HOUSE - DAY
```

**Appropriate:**
```
INT. SARAH'S HOUSE - LIVING ROOM - DAY
```

**Too specific:**
```
INT. SARAH'S HOUSE - LIVING ROOM - BY THE FIREPLACE NEAR THE WINDOW - DAY
```

### Time of Day

Standard time designations:
- **DAY:** Normal daylight
- **NIGHT:** After dark
- **DAWN:** Early morning light
- **DUSK:** Evening light
- **CONTINUOUS:** Scene continues from previous location
- **LATER:** Same location, time has passed
- **MOMENTS LATER:** Brief time passage

### Continuous Scenes

When action moves between locations without time passage:

```
INT. OFFICE BUILDING - HALLWAY - DAY

Sarah races down the corridor.

INT. OFFICE BUILDING - STAIRWELL - CONTINUOUS

She bursts through the door and leaps down the stairs.

EXT. OFFICE BUILDING - PARKING LOT - CONTINUOUS

Sarah explodes out the exit, searching frantically.
```

## Writing Action Lines

### Present Tense Imperative

Always write in present tense—screenplays describe what is happening now:

**Incorrect:**
```
Sarah walked to the window and looked outside.
```

**Correct:**
```
Sarah walks to the window. Looks outside.
```

### Visual Description

Describe only what the camera can capture:

**Incorrect:**
```
Sarah thinks about her failed marriage while making coffee.
```

**Correct:**
```
Sarah stares at the wedding photo on the counter. She pours coffee, her hand trembling. The ring on her finger catches the light.
```

### Brevity and Clarity

Keep action lines concise. Use short paragraphs with white space:

**Too dense:**
```
The door flies open and Detective Chen rushes in with her gun drawn, scanning the room from left to right, taking in the overturned furniture, the broken window, the blood on the carpet, before her eyes finally settle on the body sprawled in the corner near the fireplace where a fire still burns despite the chaos.
```

**Better:**
```
The door FLIES OPEN.

Detective Chen enters, gun drawn. Scans the room.

Overturned furniture. Broken window. Blood on the carpet.

Her eyes find the body in the corner. The fire still crackles.
```

### White Space

Use white space strategically. Dense pages read slowly; pages with more white space read faster. Action sequences should have more white space to convey urgency.

### Avoiding Camera Directions

Unless you're also directing, avoid explicit camera directions:

**Avoid:**
```
CLOSE UP on Sarah's face.
The camera PANS across the room.
We DOLLY IN on the photograph.
```

**Instead, imply the shot:**
```
Sarah's eyes narrow.
The room reveals its secrets: empty bottles, unpaid bills, a loaded gun.
The photograph dominates the frame—a happy family, now shattered.
```

## Writing Dialogue

### Character Voice

Each character should have a distinct voice—vocabulary, rhythm, and speech patterns that reflect their background, personality, and emotional state.

**Generic dialogue:**
```
                         CHARACTER A
          I'm really angry about what happened.

                         CHARACTER B
          I understand why you feel that way.
```

**Distinctive voices:**
```
                         TONY
          You think you can pull that garbage 
          with me? After everything I done for 
          you?

                         MARGARET
          What you've done for me? Is that what 
          we're calling it now?
```

### Subtext

Strong dialogue operates on multiple levels—what characters say and what they mean are often different. Subtext creates depth and intrigue.

**On-the-nose dialogue:**
```
                         JAMES
          I love you and I'm afraid you'll leave me.
```

**Dialogue with subtext:**
```
                         JAMES
          You're wearing that perfume again.

                         ELENA
          You always notice.

                         JAMES
          Hard not to.
               (beat)
          When will you be back?

                         ELENA
          When I'm back.
```

### Natural Speech Patterns

Real people interrupt, trail off, and speak in fragments:

```
                         DAVID
          I thought we could maybe—

                         LISA
          Don't.

                         DAVID
          You don't even know what I—

                         LISA
          I know exactly what you were going to 
          say. And the answer is no.

                         DAVID
          Fine. Whatever. I just thought...
               (trails off)
          Forget it.
```

### Avoiding Exposition Dumps

Don't use dialogue to convey information the characters already know:

**Clunky exposition:**
```
                         JOHN
          Well, Dr. Smith, as you know, we've 
          been working on this cancer cure for 
          fifteen years, ever since your wife 
          died of the disease.
```

**Better approach:**
```
                         JOHN
          Fifteen years.
               (beat)
          Think she'd be proud of us?

          Dr. Smith looks at his wife's photo on the desk.

                         DR. SMITH
          She'd say we took too damn long.
```

## Character Development

### Protagonist and Antagonist

**Protagonist:** The main character whose journey drives the story. Audiences experience the story through their perspective.

- Clear external goal
- Internal flaw or wound
- Compelling reason to pursue the goal
- Capacity for change

**Antagonist:** The force opposing the protagonist. May be a person, institution, nature, or internal conflict.

- Meaningful opposition to protagonist's goal
- Own logic and motivation
- Formidable challenge
- Often mirrors protagonist's shadow self

### Character Arcs

Characters should change through the story. The arc typically involves:

1. **Starting point:** Flaw, wound, or false belief
2. **Catalyst:** Event that forces change
3. **Struggle:** Resistance to change
4. **Transformation:** Embracing new truth
5. **Resolution:** Changed character in new equilibrium

### Backstory

Backstory informs character but should rarely be stated directly. Reveal through:

- Objects and environment
- Reactions to situations
- Fragments in dialogue
- Behavior under pressure
- Relationship dynamics

### Motivation and Goals

Every character needs a clear reason to act. Goals should be:

- **Concrete:** Specific and achievable
- **Urgent:** Time-sensitive
- **Personal:** Meaningful to the character
- **Opposed:** Facing real obstacles

### Character Introductions

First appearances should reveal character essence:

```
MARCUS COLE (50s) sits alone at the bar, nursing bourbon. His 
expensive suit is rumpled, tie loosened. A wedding ring tan 
line on his finger—no ring in sight.
```

This introduction tells us: middle-aged, once prosperous, currently struggling, recently separated or divorced, drinks alone.

## Story Structure

### Three-Act Structure Expanded

**Act One (Setup) - Approximately 25% of screenplay:**

- **Opening Image:** Sets tone and theme
- **Ordinary World:** Protagonist's normal life
- **Inciting Incident:** Event that disrupts status quo (page 10-15)
- **Debate:** Protagonist resists call to action
- **First Plot Point:** Protagonist commits to the journey (page 25-30)

**Act Two (Confrontation) - Approximately 50% of screenplay:**

- **B-Story begins:** Subplot introduced, often romantic or thematic
- **Fun and Games:** Promise of premise delivered
- **Midpoint:** Major revelation or reversal (page 55-60)
- **Bad Guys Close In:** Opposition intensifies
- **All Is Lost:** Lowest moment for protagonist (page 75-85)
- **Dark Night of the Soul:** Protagonist faces their flaw
- **Second Plot Point:** Final piece needed for climax (page 85-90)

**Act Three (Resolution) - Approximately 25% of screenplay:**

- **Break into Three:** Protagonist gains new insight
- **Finale:** Climactic confrontation
- **Final Image:** Mirror/contrast to opening, shows change

### Plot Points and Turning Points

Plot points are major events that change the story's direction:

- **Inciting Incident:** Disrupts protagonist's world
- **First Plot Point:** Locks protagonist into the story
- **Midpoint:** Raises stakes or reveals new information
- **Second Plot Point:** All seems lost
- **Climax:** Final confrontation

### Alternative Structures

**The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell/Christopher Vogler):**
1. Ordinary World
2. Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the First Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. Ordeal
9. Reward
10. The Road Back
11. Resurrection
12. Return with the Elixir

**Save the Cat! (Blake Snyder):**
Fifteen beats including Opening Image, Theme Stated, Setup, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image.

**Seven-Point Structure:**
Hook, Plot Turn 1, Pinch Point 1, Midpoint, Pinch Point 2, Plot Turn 2, Resolution.

## Opening Your Screenplay

### Cold Open vs. Establishing Scenes

**Cold Open:** Plunges audience into action immediately, often a sequence that hooks before the title card.

```
INT. BANK VAULT - NIGHT

DARKNESS. Then—a FLASHLIGHT beam cuts through.

Two figures in ski masks work silently. One drills into a 
safe deposit box. The other watches the door.

                         THIEF #1
                   (whispered)
          Thirty seconds.

SIRENS in the distance. Getting closer.
```

**Establishing Scene:** Introduces the world and protagonist before the conflict begins.

```
EXT. SUBURBAN NEIGHBORHOOD - DAWN

Sprinklers water perfect lawns. A JOGGER passes.

INT. WILLIAMS HOUSE - KITCHEN - CONTINUOUS

CAROL WILLIAMS (40s, polished, tense) prepares breakfast 
with military precision. Everything in its place.
```

### Hooking the Reader

The first ten pages determine whether your script gets read. Hook readers with:

- Compelling opening image
- Immediate conflict or tension
- Intriguing character introduction
- Unanswered questions
- Distinctive voice and style

### The Inciting Incident

The inciting incident disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and sets the story in motion. It should occur within the first 15 pages.

**Examples:**
- A murder in a mystery
- A love interest appearing in a romance
- A disaster striking in a survival story
- A secret revealed in a thriller

## Building Tension and Conflict

### Rising Action

Tension should escalate throughout the script. Each scene should raise questions, increase stakes, or complicate the situation.

**Escalation techniques:**
- Time pressure (ticking clock)
- Increasing personal stakes
- Removing safety nets
- Adding complications
- Raising the price of failure

### Obstacles and Complications

Protagonists need meaningful obstacles:

**Types of obstacles:**
- **External:** Physical barriers, antagonists, circumstances
- **Internal:** Fear, doubt, moral conflicts
- **Relational:** Allies who hinder, enemies who help
- **Institutional:** Systems and structures opposing the goal

### Stakes Escalation

Stakes should grow throughout the story:

**Example progression:**
1. Job at stake
2. Career at stake
3. Marriage at stake
4. Family safety at stake
5. Life at stake

### Ticking Clocks

Deadlines create urgency:

```
                         CAPTAIN
          The bomb detonates at midnight. That 
          gives you six hours.

                         DETECTIVE
          I'll need ten.

                         CAPTAIN
          Then three thousand people die. Your 
          choice.
```

## Writing Compelling Scenes

### Scene Purpose

Every scene must serve at least one of these functions:

1. Advance the plot
2. Reveal character
3. Provide essential information
4. Create emotional impact

Ideally, scenes accomplish multiple purposes simultaneously.

### Conflict in Every Scene

Every scene needs conflict—two characters wanting different things, or a character struggling against circumstance.

**Scene without conflict:**
```
Sarah enters. Michael hands her the report.

                         MICHAEL
          Here's the report.

                         SARAH
          Thanks.

She leaves.
```

**Scene with conflict:**
```
Sarah enters. Michael doesn't look up from his computer.

                         SARAH
          The Henderson report?

                         MICHAEL
          Still working on it.

                         SARAH
          It was due yesterday.

                         MICHAEL
          And you're only asking now?
```

### Scene Structure

Scenes follow a mini-structure:

1. **Goal:** What does the character want in this scene?
2. **Conflict:** What opposes them?
3. **Outcome:** Disaster, partial success, or success with complications

### Transitions Between Scenes

Connect scenes for smooth flow:

- **Visual match:** Similar images linking scenes
- **Audio bridge:** Sound from one scene continuing into next
- **Thematic link:** Scenes connected by idea or emotion
- **Contrast:** Juxtaposing opposite tones or situations

## Subplots and B-Stories

### Integrating Subplots

Subplots enrich your screenplay by:

- Revealing different facets of the protagonist
- Providing contrast or parallel to main plot
- Adding depth and complexity
- Offering breathing room from main tension

### Thematic Resonance

The best subplots reinforce or comment on the main theme:

**Main plot:** Detective hunts serial killer (theme: obsession's cost)
**Subplot:** Detective's marriage fails due to work obsession

### Balancing Multiple Storylines

- A-story: Main plot (60-70% of screen time)
- B-story: Primary subplot (20-30%)
- C-story: Minor subplot (10% or less)

Interweave storylines so they converge at key moments, particularly the climax.

## Genre-Specific Considerations

### Action
- Fast-paced action lines
- Clear geography in action sequences
- Character revealed through choices under pressure
- Spectacular set pieces
- Physical stakes

### Comedy
- Rhythm and timing crucial
- Setup and payoff structure
- Character-based humor more enduring
- Visual comedy opportunities
- Subverted expectations

### Drama
- Deep character exploration
- Emotional truth
- Meaningful stakes
- Subtext-rich dialogue
- Moral complexity

### Horror
- Atmosphere and dread
- Controlled information release
- Vulnerability established
- Rules of the threat clear
- Escalating fear

### Thriller
- Suspense over surprise
- Audience knows more than characters
- Constant tension
- Red herrings and misdirection
- High personal stakes

### Romance
- Chemistry on the page
- Obstacles to love
- Emotional vulnerability
- Meet-cute and key romantic moments
- Satisfying resolution

### Sci-Fi and Fantasy
- World-building integrated naturally
- Rules established and followed
- Human story within fantastical setting
- Visual imagination
- Theme amplified by genre elements

## Rewriting and Revision

### First Draft to Final Draft

Professional screenplays typically go through multiple drafts:

1. **Vomit draft:** Get the story out
2. **Structure pass:** Fix plot and pacing
3. **Character pass:** Deepen characters and arcs
4. **Dialogue polish:** Refine every line
5. **Final polish:** Cut everything unnecessary

### Story Notes and Feedback

Seek feedback from:
- Trusted readers (not family/friends who can't be objective)
- Writers groups
- Professional coverage services
- Script consultants

### Script Coverage

Professional coverage includes:
- Logline
- Synopsis
- Comments on premise, structure, character, dialogue
- Pass/Consider/Recommend rating

### Polish and Refinement

Final polish checklist:
- Cut 10% of dialogue
- Eliminate redundant scenes
- Strengthen opening and closing images
- Ensure every scene has conflict
- Verify consistent character voices
- Check formatting

## TV Script Writing Specifics

### TV Pilot Structure

**Hour Drama Pilot:**
- Cold open (teaser)
- Act One: Establish world and protagonist
- Act Two: Complicate
- Act Three: Raise stakes
- Act Four: Crisis point
- Act Five: Cliffhanger or resolution with hook

**Half-Hour Comedy Pilot:**
- Cold open (optional)
- Act One: Establish situation
- Act Two: Complicate and resolve

### Act Breaks and Commercial Breaks

Traditional network TV requires act breaks for commercials. Each act should end on a question, revelation, or moment of tension that keeps viewers through the break.

### Teaser and Tag

- **Teaser:** Pre-credits sequence, often a hook or cold open
- **Tag:** Post-credits scene, usually comedic or setting up next episode

### Series Bible

A series bible accompanies pilot scripts and includes:
- Logline and synopsis
- Character breakdowns
- World/setting details
- Tone and style guide
- Episode ideas for first season
- Long-term series vision

## Short Film Scripts

### Condensed Storytelling

Short films demand efficiency:
- Enter scenes late, leave early
- Single clear conflict
- Limited character count
- One location if possible
- Visual storytelling over dialogue

### Single Concept Focus

Short films work best with one strong idea executed well rather than multiple competing elements.

### Budget Considerations

Short films often have limited budgets. Write for:
- Minimal locations
- Small cast
- Available resources
- Practical effects

### Feature Films
- **Standard:** 90-120 pages
- **Comedy:** Often 90-100 pages
- **Drama:** 100-120 pages
- **Epic:** Up to 150 pages (established writers only)

### TV Pilots
- **Hour drama:** 55-65 pages
- **Half-hour comedy (multi-cam):** 45-52 pages
- **Half-hour comedy (single-cam):** 28-35 pages

### TV Episodes
- Follow established show format
- Hour dramas: 50-60 pages
- Half-hour: 22-32 pages

### Short Films
- **Micro short:** 1-5 pages (1-5 minutes)
- **Short:** 5-20 pages (5-20 minutes)
- **Medium short:** 20-40 pages (20-40 minutes)

## Industry Standards and Submission

### Title Page Format

Centered on page:
```
                    TITLE OF SCREENPLAY

                           by

                      Writer's Name




Contact information                        Registration number
in lower left corner                       in lower right (optional)
```

### Registration and Copyright

Protect your work:
- WGA (Writers Guild of America) registration
- U.S. Copyright Office registration
- Keep dated drafts as evidence of creation

### Query Letters

Query letters to agents/managers/producers should include:
- Brief, compelling logline
- One-paragraph synopsis
- Your relevant credentials
- Professional, concise tone

### Loglines

A logline is a one-sentence summary:

**Formula:** When [inciting incident] happens to [protagonist], they must [goal] before [stakes].

**Example:** When a retired CIA operative's daughter is kidnapped in Paris, he has 96 hours to use his lethal skills to save her before she disappears forever.

### Synopses

- Short synopsis: 1 page
- Long synopsis: 3-5 pages
- Tell the whole story including ending
- Focus on main plot, minimal subplot detail
- Convey tone and style

### Pitch Decks

Visual presentations for pitching include:
- Logline and synopsis
- Character breakdowns with potential casting
- Tone images and visual references
- Comparable films
- Market positioning
- Your unique angle

## Common Screenplay Mistakes

1. **Over-writing action lines:** Keep them brief and visual
2. **On-the-nose dialogue:** Subtext is essential
3. **Starting too early:** Enter the story as late as possible
4. **Passive protagonist:** Characters must drive action
5. **Weak antagonist:** Opposition must be formidable
6. **Telling instead of showing:** Film is a visual medium
7. **Too many characters:** Focus on essential roles
8. **Unmotivated actions:** Every choice needs reason
9. **Predictable plot:** Surprise within inevitability
10. **Weak ending:** The climax must satisfy
11. **Improper formatting:** Industry standard is non-negotiable
12. **First scene in a car:** Avoid clichéd openings
13. **Dream sequences that cheat:** Audiences feel manipulated
14. **Voice-over as crutch:** Use sparingly and purposefully
15. **Unproduceable scripts:** Consider budget and logistics

---

## Best Practices for Screenplay Writing

1. **Read screenplays:** Study produced scripts in your genre
2. **Write regularly:** Consistency beats inspiration
3. **Outline first:** Structure before drafting
4. **Get feedback early:** Don't polish first drafts
5. **Cut ruthlessly:** Every word must earn its place
6. **Know your ending:** Write toward a destination
7. **Rewrite extensively:** First drafts are never final
8. **Study film:** Watch movies as a writer
9. **Learn the market:** Know what's selling and why
10. **Network:** Relationships drive the industry
11. **Stay current:** Industry standards evolve
12. **Be professional:** Presentation matters
13. **Develop multiple projects:** Don't stake everything on one script
14. **Accept rejection:** It's part of the process
15. **Keep improving:** Craft develops over time

## Advanced Screenplay Techniques

### Non-Linear Storytelling

Non-linear narratives present events out of chronological order:

**Flashback structure:** Present story interrupted by past events
**Frame narrative:** Story within a story
**Reverse chronology:** Events shown backward (e.g., *Memento*)
**Parallel timelines:** Multiple time periods interwoven
**Fractured narrative:** Scrambled sequence reassembled by viewer

**Formatting flashbacks:**
```
INT. COURTROOM - DAY (PRESENT)

Sarah takes the witness stand.

                         PROSECUTOR
          Tell us what happened that night.

Sarah closes her eyes.

                                              DISSOLVE TO:

INT. WAREHOUSE - NIGHT (FLASHBACK)

A younger Sarah crouches behind crates. GUNFIRE echoes.
```

### Montage Sequences

Montages compress time and show progression:

```
MONTAGE - SARAH'S TRAINING

-- Sarah struggles through pushups, collapses after ten.

-- Sarah runs, gasping, falls behind the group.

-- Sarah at the shooting range, misses every target.

-- WEEKS LATER: Sarah knocks out fifty pushups, barely winded.

-- Sarah leads the running pack.

-- Sarah's grouping: perfect center mass.

END MONTAGE
```

### Voice-Over Narration

Use voice-over purposefully, not as exposition crutch:

**Effective voice-over:**
```
                         MARCUS (V.O.)
          They say you can't choose your family.
          But you can choose how hard you fight
          for them.

EXT. HOSPITAL - NIGHT

Marcus sits on a bench outside the ER, head in hands.
Rain soaks through his jacket. He doesn't move.
```

### Dual Dialogue

When two characters speak simultaneously:

```
                   SARAH                          MICHAEL
          I can't believe you—           I didn't mean to—
```

### Intercut Sequences

Cutting between two simultaneous scenes:

```
INT. INTERROGATION ROOM - NIGHT

Detective Chen leans across the table.

                         DETECTIVE CHEN
          Where were you at midnight?

INTERCUT WITH:

INT. BASEMENT - NIGHT

Marcus works frantically, dismantling equipment.

                         SUSPECT (V.O.)
          Home. Sleeping.

Back to the interrogation room:

                         DETECTIVE CHEN
          Funny. Your car says otherwise.
```

## The Business of Screenwriting

### Breaking In

Entry paths to professional screenwriting:

**Spec scripts:** Write original screenplays to demonstrate your voice
**Competitions:** Win or place in prestigious contests
**Coverage services:** Use Black List or similar for exposure
**Networking:** Industry connections through events, classes, organizations
**Short films:** Write produceable shorts to build portfolio
**Web series:** Create original content showcasing abilities
**Assistants:** Work as assistant to learn industry from inside

### Representation

**Agents:** Submit your work to buyers, negotiate deals
**Managers:** Develop your career, help with creative decisions
**Entertainment lawyers:** Handle contracts, some can submit material

Getting representation requires:
- Strong writing samples (2-3 polished specs)
- Competition wins or placement
- Industry referrals
- Query letters (rarely successful but possible)

### The Development Process

After selling a script:

1. **Option/Purchase:** Studio acquires rights
2. **Development notes:** Executives provide feedback
3. **Rewrites:** Writer revises per notes
4. **Polish pass:** Final refinements
5. **Production draft:** Script locked for shooting
6. **Production rewrites:** Changes during filming

### WGA and Union Considerations

The Writers Guild of America:
- Sets minimum compensation rates
- Provides health and pension benefits
- Arbitrates credit disputes
- Protects writers' rights

Joining WGA requires:
- Employment on a WGA-covered project
- Accumulating sufficient units
- Paying initiation fees

## Writing for Different Formats

### Writing for Streaming

Streaming platforms have different needs:

- **Episode length flexibility:** Not bound to broadcast slots
- **Binge-watching consideration:** End episodes with hooks
- **Serialized storytelling:** More complex seasonal arcs
- **Content freedom:** Less censorship than broadcast
- **International audience:** Global viewer considerations

### Limited Series/Miniseries

Self-contained stories over 4-10 episodes:

- Complete story arc
- No need for ongoing series potential
- Often based on novels or true stories
- Prestige format attracting talent
- Each episode serves the whole

### Anthology Series

Different stories each episode or season:

- Standalone episodes (e.g., *Black Mirror*)
- Seasonal anthologies (e.g., *True Detective*)
- Thematic connections
- Fresh cast each installment
- Genre flexibility

### Adaptation

Adapting existing material:

**From novels:**
- Identify core story and theme
- Cut subplots that don't serve screen story
- Externalize internal monologue
- Condense timeline
- Combine characters when necessary

**From true stories:**
- Research extensively
- Composite characters often necessary
- Dramatic license within truth
- Legal considerations
- Living subjects' concerns

## Scene Analysis Framework

### Breaking Down Great Scenes

Analyze produced scenes for:

1. **Objective:** What does each character want?
2. **Obstacle:** What prevents them getting it?
3. **Tactic:** How do they try to overcome obstacles?
4. **Stakes:** What happens if they fail?
5. **Turn:** How does the scene shift power or information?
6. **Button:** What's the final moment that punctuates the scene?

### Example Analysis

*The Silence of the Lambs* - Quid Pro Quo Scene:

- **Clarice's objective:** Get information about Buffalo Bill
- **Lecter's objective:** Gain personal information about Clarice
- **Obstacle:** Each wants what the other won't freely give
- **Tactics:** Negotiation, manipulation, vulnerability
- **Stakes:** Lives hang in balance; Clarice's trauma exposed
- **Turn:** Power shifts as Clarice reveals childhood trauma
- **Button:** "Quid pro quo" established as ongoing dynamic

## Visual Storytelling Techniques

### Show, Don't Tell

Replace dialogue exposition with visual information:

**Telling:**
```
                         SARAH
          My father was abusive. That's why 
          I have trust issues.
```

**Showing:**
```
Sarah flinches when Michael raises his hand to wave.

                         MICHAEL
          You okay?

                         SARAH
          Fine. Just... startled.

She forces a smile. Her hand unconsciously touches 
an old scar on her wrist.
```

### Environmental Storytelling

Let settings reveal character and history:

```
INT. DAVID'S APARTMENT - DAY

Sparse. Mattress on the floor. Moving boxes still 
packed—labels dated two years ago. 

Empty pizza boxes stacked by the door. A single 
photograph, face-down on the windowsill.
```

### Object Significance

Give meaning to props:

```
Sarah's hand finds her grandmother's ring on the chain 
around her neck. She grips it tight.

                         SARAH
          I'm ready.
```

When the ring appears later, audiences understand its significance without explanation.

## Collaboration and Production Awareness

### Working with Directors

Writers and directors collaborate:

- Directors may request changes
- Be open to interpretation
- Defend what matters, release what doesn't
- Understand visual storytelling partner
- Provide flexibility in action descriptions

### Writing for Budget

Consider practical limitations:

**Budget-conscious choices:**
- Limited locations
- Contemporary settings
- Small cast
- Contained action
- Practical effects over CGI

**Example:** Instead of a chase through city streets, contain the sequence in a parking garage.

### Production Script Considerations

Production-ready scripts include:

- Scene numbers
- Revision colors (white, blue, pink, yellow, green, goldenrod)
- Locked pages
- Asterisks marking changes
- Day breaks for scheduling
