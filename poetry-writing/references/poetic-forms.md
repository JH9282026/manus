# Poetic Forms Reference

This reference provides detailed information on traditional and contemporary poetic forms, including structural requirements, historical context, and examples.

## Traditional Fixed Forms

### Sonnet

**Structure**: 14 lines, typically in iambic pentameter

**Types**:

1. **Shakespearean (English) Sonnet**
   - Rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
   - Structure: Three quatrains + closing couplet
   - Volta (turn): Usually occurs at line 13 (the couplet)
   - Thematic development: Problem/exploration in quatrains, resolution in couplet
   - Example poets: William Shakespeare, Edna St. Vincent Millay

2. **Petrarchan (Italian) Sonnet**
   - Rhyme scheme: ABBAABBA CDECDE (or CDCDCD)
   - Structure: Octave (8 lines) + sestet (6 lines)
   - Volta: Occurs between octave and sestet (after line 8)
   - Thematic development: Problem/question in octave, resolution/answer in sestet
   - Example poets: Petrarch, John Milton, Elizabeth Barrett Browning

3. **Spenserian Sonnet**
   - Rhyme scheme: ABAB BCBC CDCD EE
   - Interlocking rhymes connect quatrains
   - Named after Edmund Spenser
   - Less common than Shakespearean or Petrarchan

**Writing Tips**:
- The volta is crucial—it should shift perspective or deepen understanding
- Iambic pentameter creates natural English speech rhythm
- Modern sonnets may vary meter and rhyme while maintaining 14-line structure
- Focus on a single theme or argument
- Use the form's constraints to generate creative solutions

### Villanelle

**Structure**: 19 lines—five tercets followed by one quatrain

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABA ABA ABA ABA ABA ABAA

**Refrains**:
- Line 1 (A1) repeats as lines 6, 12, 18
- Line 3 (A2) repeats as lines 9, 15, 19
- Both refrains appear in final quatrain

**Characteristics**:
- Only two rhymes throughout entire poem
- Circular, obsessive quality from repetition
- Refrains should be strong enough to bear repetition
- Each repetition can gain new meaning from context

**Famous Examples**:
- "Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night" by Dylan Thomas
- "One Art" by Elizabeth Bishop
- "The Waking" by Theodore Roethke

**Writing Tips**:
- Choose refrains that are flexible in meaning
- Vary punctuation around refrains to shift emphasis
- Use the obsessive repetition to reinforce theme
- Consider how context changes refrain meaning
- The form suits themes of obsession, loss, or circularity

### Sestina

**Structure**: 39 lines—six sestets (6-line stanzas) + three-line envoi

**End-Word Pattern**:
- Choose six end-words (A, B, C, D, E, F)
- Rotate them through stanzas in this pattern:
  - Stanza 1: ABCDEF
  - Stanza 2: FAEBDC
  - Stanza 3: CFDABE
  - Stanza 4: ECBFAD
  - Stanza 5: DEACFB
  - Stanza 6: BDFECA
- Envoi: All six words appear (two per line): BE, DC, FA

**Characteristics**:
- Complex mathematical structure
- No rhyme requirement
- End-words build thematic resonance through repetition
- Creates web of associations
- Challenging but rewarding form

**Famous Examples**:
- "Sestina" by Elizabeth Bishop
- "Sestina: Altaforte" by Ezra Pound
- "A Miracle for Breakfast" by Elizabeth Bishop

**Writing Tips**:
- Choose end-words with multiple meanings or uses (noun/verb)
- Select words central to your theme
- Allow the form to generate unexpected connections
- Don't force meaning—let the pattern guide discovery
- The envoi should feel like a resolution or summation

### Haiku

**Structure**: Three lines with 5-7-5 syllable pattern

**Traditional Elements**:
- **Kigo**: Seasonal reference
- **Kireji**: Cutting word (creates juxtaposition)
- Focus on nature and natural phenomena
- Present tense, immediate observation
- Objective rather than subjective
- Moment of insight or awareness

**Modern Variations**:
- May not strictly follow 5-7-5 in English
- Can address urban or contemporary subjects
- May be more personal or emotional
- Focus on brevity and compression remains

**Famous Examples**:
- Matsuo Bashō: "An old silent pond / A frog jumps into the pond— / Splash! Silence again."
- Kobayashi Issa: "O snail / Climb Mount Fuji, / But slowly, slowly!"

**Writing Tips**:
- Capture a single moment or image
- Use concrete, sensory details
- Avoid explanation—show, don't tell
- Create juxtaposition between images
- Less is more—every word must count
- Consider the pause between lines

### Ghazal

**Structure**: Series of couplets (typically 5-15), each autonomous

**Rhyme and Refrain**:
- First couplet establishes rhyme and refrain: AA
- Subsequent couplets: BA, CA, DA, etc.
- Refrain (radif) repeats at end of each couplet
- Rhyme (qafia) precedes refrain

**Characteristics**:
- Each couplet stands alone thematically
- Final couplet often includes poet's name (takhallus)
- Themes of love, loss, longing, mysticism
- Originated in Arabic and Persian poetry
- Popularized in English by Agha Shahid Ali

**Example Structure**:
```
Line 1: [text] [rhyme] [refrain]
Line 2: [text] [rhyme] [refrain]

Line 3: [text] [different rhyme] [refrain]
Line 4: [text] [different rhyme] [refrain]
```

**Writing Tips**:
- Choose a refrain that's evocative and flexible
- Each couplet should be complete in itself
- Embrace the form's associative logic
- Themes can shift between couplets
- The repetition creates musical, incantatory effect

### Pantoum

**Structure**: Quatrains with repeating lines

**Pattern**:
- Lines 2 and 4 of each stanza become lines 1 and 3 of the next
- Final stanza's lines 2 and 4 are lines 3 and 1 of first stanza
- Creates circular structure

**Example Pattern**:
```
Stanza 1: A B C D
Stanza 2: B E D F
Stanza 3: E G F H
Stanza 4: G C H A
```

**Characteristics**:
- Dreamlike, recursive quality
- Lines gain new meaning through repetition
- No set rhyme scheme
- Originated in Malay poetry

**Writing Tips**:
- Write lines that can work in multiple contexts
- Use repetition to deepen or shift meaning
- Vary punctuation to change line interpretation
- Suits themes of memory, obsession, cycles

### Terza Rima

**Structure**: Tercets with interlocking rhyme scheme

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABA BCB CDC DED, etc.

**Characteristics**:
- Each stanza's middle line rhymes with first and third lines of next stanza
- Creates forward momentum
- Traditionally in iambic pentameter
- Often ends with single line or couplet
- Used by Dante in "Divine Comedy"

**Writing Tips**:
- The interlocking rhymes propel the poem forward
- Suits narrative or journey themes
- Can be challenging to sustain in English
- Consider slant rhymes for more flexibility

### Rondeau

**Structure**: 15 lines in three stanzas (5-4-6 lines)

**Rhyme Scheme**: AABBA AABR AABBAR

**Refrain**: Opening words repeat as R (unrhymed)

**Characteristics**:
- Only two rhymes throughout
- Refrain is partial line (rentrement)
- Light, musical quality
- French origin

**Writing Tips**:
- Choose a memorable opening phrase
- The refrain should work in all three positions
- Suits playful or lyrical subjects

## Contemporary and Open Forms

### Free Verse

**Characteristics**:
- No regular meter or rhyme scheme
- Relies on natural speech rhythms
- Uses line breaks for emphasis and pacing
- Freedom in structure and form
- Popularized by Walt Whitman, modernist poets

**Techniques**:
- **Line breaks**: Create pauses, emphasis, multiple meanings
- **Stanza breaks**: Mark shifts in thought or tone
- **White space**: Visual element, pacing control
- **Repetition**: Creates rhythm without meter
- **Syntax**: Sentence structure creates movement

**Writing Tips**:
- Freedom doesn't mean formlessness
- Every line break should be intentional
- Create rhythm through repetition and variation
- Use visual arrangement meaningfully
- Read aloud to test natural flow

### Prose Poetry

**Characteristics**:
- Written in prose format (no line breaks)
- Maintains poetic intensity and compression
- Uses poetic devices (imagery, metaphor, sound)
- Blurs boundaries between poetry and prose
- Often brief (paragraph to page length)

**Techniques**:
- Dense imagery and metaphor
- Rhythmic sentences
- Compression and precision
- Narrative or lyrical focus
- Attention to sonic qualities

**Famous Practitioners**:
- Charles Baudelaire (pioneer)
- Arthur Rimbaud
- Russell Edson
- Claudia Rankine

**Writing Tips**:
- Every sentence should be essential
- Maintain poetic compression
- Use rhythm within sentences
- Balance narrative and lyrical elements
- Consider the prose block as visual unit

### Concrete/Visual Poetry

**Characteristics**:
- Visual arrangement is integral to meaning
- Text forms shapes or images
- Engages both visual and linguistic interpretation
- Explores typography and spatial relationships
- Can be read multiple ways

**Types**:
- **Shape poems**: Text forms recognizable image
- **Pattern poems**: Geometric or abstract arrangements
- **Typographic poems**: Font and size create meaning
- **Spatial poems**: White space is active element

**Famous Examples**:
- George Herbert's "Easter Wings"
- Guillaume Apollinaire's calligrammes
- e.e. cummings' experimental typography

**Writing Tips**:
- Visual form should enhance meaning
- Consider how poem is read (order, direction)
- Balance visual and linguistic elements
- Experiment with digital tools
- Think about page as canvas

### List Poem

**Characteristics**:
- Structured as list or catalog
- Items can be related or disparate
- Accumulation creates meaning
- Can be humorous or serious
- Often uses anaphora (repeated opening)

**Techniques**:
- Parallel structure
- Repetition and variation
- Building toward climax or revelation
- Juxtaposition of items

**Writing Tips**:
- Vary length and rhythm of items
- Consider order carefully
- Use repetition strategically
- Build toward something (insight, emotion, surprise)

### Erasure/Blackout Poetry

**Characteristics**:
- Created by removing words from existing text
- Remaining words form new poem
- Visual element from redaction
- Found poetry technique

**Process**:
- Select source text (newspaper, book page, etc.)
- Identify words to keep
- Remove or obscure other words
- Remaining text creates new meaning

**Writing Tips**:
- Choose source text with rich language
- Look for unexpected connections
- Consider visual impact of erasure
- Balance readability and abstraction

## Hybrid and Experimental Forms

### Abecedarian

- Each line or stanza begins with successive letter of alphabet
- Can be strict (A-Z) or loose
- Ancient form with contemporary practitioners

### Acrostic

- First letter of each line spells word or phrase
- Can be obvious or subtle
- Vertical message adds layer of meaning

### Anaphora Poem

- Repeated phrase at beginning of lines or stanzas
- Creates rhythm and emphasis
- Example: "I Have a Dream" speech by MLK

### Golden Shovel

- Invented by Terrance Hayes
- End words of each line form line(s) from another poem
- Homage to source poem while creating new work

## Choosing a Form

**Consider**:
- **Subject matter**: Does form suit content?
- **Skill level**: Start with simpler forms, progress to complex
- **Purpose**: What effect do you want to create?
- **Constraints**: Can limitations spark creativity?
- **Tradition**: Are you honoring or subverting form?

**Experimentation**:
- Try same content in multiple forms
- Combine elements from different forms
- Invent your own constraints
- Break rules intentionally and purposefully

## Conclusion

Poetic forms range from ancient fixed structures to contemporary innovations. Mastering traditional forms provides technical foundation, while exploring open and experimental forms encourages creative freedom. The best form for any poem is the one that serves its content and purpose most effectively.
