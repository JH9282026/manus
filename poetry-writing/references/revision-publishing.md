# Revision and Publishing Strategies

This reference covers comprehensive strategies for revising poetry and navigating the publication process, from initial drafts to seeing your work in print.

## The Revision Process

### Why Revision Matters

Revision is where good poems become great. First drafts capture raw material and inspiration, but revision:

- Clarifies meaning and intent
- Strengthens language and imagery
- Eliminates unnecessary elements
- Enhances rhythm and sound
- Ensures consistency of tone and voice
- Discovers deeper layers of meaning

**Key Principle**: "Writing is rewriting" — most published poems go through multiple drafts.

### Stages of Revision

#### Stage 1: Cooling Period

**Wait Before Revising**
- Set draft aside for days or weeks
- Gain emotional distance
- Return with fresh perspective
- See what you actually wrote vs. what you intended

**Benefits**:
- Spot weaknesses more easily
- Less attached to specific phrases
- More objective evaluation
- Notice patterns and repetitions

#### Stage 2: Macro Revision (Big Picture)

**Focus on Overall Structure**

**Questions to Ask**:
- What is this poem really about?
- Does it achieve its purpose?
- Is the structure appropriate for the content?
- Where does the poem truly begin? (Often not the first line)
- Where should it end? (Sometimes earlier than current ending)
- Are all sections necessary?
- Is the progression logical or emotionally coherent?
- Does the tone remain consistent (or shift purposefully)?

**Common Macro Issues**:

**Weak Opening**
- Poem takes too long to get started
- Opening is too abstract or explanatory
- Solution: Cut preliminary lines; start with strongest image or moment

**Weak Ending**
- Poem continues past natural conclusion
- Ending explains or summarizes unnecessarily
- Solution: End at moment of insight or strongest image; trust reader

**Structural Problems**
- Sections in wrong order
- Missing transitions
- Unbalanced proportions
- Solution: Outline poem's movement; rearrange; add or cut sections

**Unclear Focus**
- Poem tries to do too much
- Multiple unrelated themes
- Solution: Identify core idea; cut tangential material; consider splitting into multiple poems

#### Stage 3: Micro Revision (Line Level)

**Focus on Language and Craft**

**Word Choice**
- Replace vague words with specific ones
- Eliminate clichés
- Choose words for sound as well as meaning
- Consider connotations, not just denotations
- Remove unnecessary adjectives and adverbs
- Strengthen verbs (avoid "is," "was," "has")

**Before**: "The bird flew quickly through the sky"
**After**: "The hawk sliced through clouds"

**Imagery**
- Make abstract concrete
- Engage multiple senses
- Ensure images are fresh and specific
- Check for mixed or inconsistent metaphors
- Develop key images more fully

**Sound and Rhythm**
- Read aloud to test flow
- Adjust line breaks for emphasis and pacing
- Check for unintentional rhymes or awkward sounds
- Enhance sonic patterns (alliteration, assonance)
- Vary sentence length and structure

**Compression**
- Eliminate redundancy
- Cut unnecessary words ("very," "really," "just")
- Combine weak lines
- Make every word count

**Before**: "In the early morning, the sun was rising slowly"
**After**: "Dawn crept across the hills"

#### Stage 4: Fine-Tuning

**Polish and Perfect**

**Grammar and Punctuation**
- Check for errors
- Ensure punctuation serves the poem
- Consider unconventional punctuation purposefully
- Verify line breaks don't create confusion

**Consistency**
- Verb tense (unless shifts are intentional)
- Point of view
- Tone and diction level
- Metaphor or imagery systems

**Title**
- Does it enhance the poem?
- Is it too obvious or too obscure?
- Does it add information or create expectation?
- Consider alternatives

**Format**
- Check spacing and indentation
- Ensure visual presentation supports content
- Verify stanza breaks are intentional

### Revision Techniques

#### Reading Aloud

**Why It Works**:
- Reveals awkward rhythms
- Highlights unclear passages
- Tests sonic qualities
- Engages different part of brain
- Simulates reader experience

**How to Do It**:
- Read slowly and clearly
- Note where you stumble
- Listen for unintentional rhymes or repetitions
- Feel the emotional impact
- Record yourself and listen back

#### Reverse Outlining

**Process**:
1. Summarize each line or stanza in one phrase
2. Look at the outline of summaries
3. Evaluate logical or emotional progression
4. Identify gaps, redundancies, or misplaced sections
5. Rearrange or revise as needed

#### Constraint Revision

**Exercises**:
- Cut poem by 25% (forces compression)
- Rewrite in different form (sonnet to free verse, etc.)
- Change point of view (I to you, etc.)
- Eliminate all adjectives or adverbs
- Replace every abstract word with concrete image

**Purpose**: Forces new perspectives and solutions

#### Line Break Experiments

**Process**:
1. Write poem as prose paragraph
2. Break into lines multiple ways
3. Compare effects of different breaks
4. Choose version that best serves content

**Considerations**:
- Where do you want emphasis?
- Where should reader pause?
- What words should stand alone?
- How does visual appearance affect meaning?

#### Word-by-Word Analysis

**Process**:
1. Examine every single word
2. Ask: Is this the best word? Is it necessary?
3. Consider alternatives
4. Test removing it
5. Keep only essential words

**Particularly Scrutinize**:
- Adjectives and adverbs
- "Very," "really," "just," "quite"
- "Is," "was," "has" (weak verbs)
- Articles ("a," "an," "the") — sometimes removable

### Getting Feedback

#### Workshop Groups

**Benefits**:
- Multiple perspectives
- Identify patterns in responses
- Learn from others' work
- Build community
- Develop critical skills

**Finding Workshops**:
- Local writing centers or libraries
- Community colleges or universities
- Online poetry communities
- MFA programs
- Private workshops or classes

**Workshop Etiquette**:
- Give constructive, specific feedback
- Focus on the poem, not the poet
- Balance criticism with appreciation
- Ask questions rather than prescribing solutions
- Be open to feedback on your own work

#### Beta Readers

**Choosing Readers**:
- Fellow poets who understand craft
- Trusted readers with good literary taste
- Mix of perspectives (poets and general readers)
- People who will be honest

**Asking for Feedback**:
- Be specific about what you want
- "Does the ending work?"
- "Is the metaphor clear?"
- "Where do you lose interest?"
- Don't explain or defend during feedback
- Take notes; consider later

#### Evaluating Feedback

**Not All Feedback Is Equal**:
- Consider the source's expertise
- Look for patterns (multiple people noting same issue)
- Trust your instinct about your vision
- Distinguish between "this isn't for me" and "this doesn't work"

**Red Flags in Feedback**:
- "I would have written it differently" (not helpful)
- Prescriptive solutions without explanation
- Feedback that misunderstands your intent
- Purely subjective taste without craft basis

**Green Flags**:
- Specific, concrete observations
- Questions that help you think deeper
- Identification of patterns or inconsistencies
- Recognition of what works well

### Self-Editing Checklist

**Content**:
- [ ] Poem has clear focus or purpose
- [ ] Opening engages immediately
- [ ] Ending feels complete without over-explaining
- [ ] All sections are necessary
- [ ] Progression is logical or emotionally coherent

**Language**:
- [ ] Word choice is specific and precise
- [ ] Clichés eliminated
- [ ] Imagery is fresh and vivid
- [ ] Abstractions made concrete
- [ ] Unnecessary words removed

**Sound and Rhythm**:
- [ ] Reads smoothly aloud
- [ ] Line breaks are purposeful
- [ ] Rhythm serves content
- [ ] Sound patterns enhance meaning
- [ ] No unintentional awkward sounds

**Craft**:
- [ ] Metaphors are consistent and apt
- [ ] Point of view is consistent
- [ ] Verb tense is consistent (or shifts purposefully)
- [ ] Tone is appropriate and consistent
- [ ] Form serves content

**Polish**:
- [ ] Grammar and punctuation are correct (or purposefully unconventional)
- [ ] Title enhances poem
- [ ] Format is clean and intentional
- [ ] No typos or errors

## The Publication Process

### Why Publish?

**Reasons to Seek Publication**:
- Share your work with wider audience
- Gain validation and feedback
- Build literary resume
- Connect with poetry community
- Contribute to literary conversation
- Professional development (for academic poets)

**Remember**: Publication isn't the only measure of success. Writing itself is valuable.

### Types of Publication Venues

#### Literary Journals

**Print Journals**:
- Traditional prestige
- Physical artifact
- Often university-affiliated
- Longer production timeline
- Examples: *Poetry*, *The Paris Review*, *Ploughshares*

**Online Journals**:
- Faster publication
- Wider accessibility
- Often more experimental
- Growing prestige
- Examples: *Rattle*, *Verse Daily*, *The Offing*

**Hybrid Journals**:
- Both print and online presence
- Best of both worlds

**Tiers**:
- **Top-tier**: Highly competitive, prestigious (*Poetry*, *The New Yorker*)
- **Mid-tier**: Respected, competitive, good readership
- **Emerging**: Newer journals, often more open to new voices
- **All tiers have value**: Publication anywhere is an achievement

#### Contests

**Benefits**:
- Prize money
- Publication
- Recognition
- Resume builder

**Considerations**:
- Entry fees (typically $15-30)
- Competition is fierce
- Often includes publication in journal
- Judge's taste matters

**Types**:
- Single poem contests
- Chapbook contests
- Full-length manuscript contests

#### Anthologies

**Themed Collections**:
- Poems on specific topics
- Often open calls
- Good for targeted work

**Best-of Anthologies**:
- Curated from previous publications
- *Best American Poetry*, *Pushcart Prize*
- Editors nominate; guest editor selects

#### Chapbooks

**Definition**: Short poetry collection (typically 20-30 pages)

**Publication Routes**:
- Chapbook contests
- Small press submissions
- Self-publication

**Purpose**:
- Stepping stone to full-length collection
- Thematically unified work
- Building readership

#### Full-Length Collections

**Definition**: 48+ pages of poetry

**Publication Routes**:
- Contest (most common for first book)
- Open submission to publishers
- Agent representation (rare for poetry)

**Timeline**: Often years from submission to publication

#### Self-Publishing

**Platforms**:
- Print-on-demand (Amazon KDP, IngramSpark)
- E-books
- Personal website or blog

**Pros**:
- Complete control
- Keep all profits
- Faster timeline
- No gatekeepers

**Cons**:
- No editorial input
- Limited distribution
- Less prestige in literary community
- Marketing is your responsibility

**Best For**:
- Building audience
- Experimental work
- Local/community distribution
- Supplementing traditional publication

### Submission Process

#### Researching Venues

**How to Find Journals**:
- *Poets & Writers* magazine and website
- Duotrope (subscription database)
- Submittable (submission platform)
- NewPages (free database)
- Read journals you admire
- Ask other poets for recommendations

**Evaluating Fit**:
- Read recent issues
- Check aesthetic and style
- Note themes or preferences
- Review submission guidelines
- Consider prestige vs. acceptance rate

**Red Flags**:
- Charges reading fees (except contests)
- Promises publication for payment (vanity press)
- No clear editorial standards
- Unprofessional website or communication

#### Preparing Submissions

**Manuscript Preparation**:
- Follow guidelines exactly
- Standard format: 12pt font, single-spaced poems, double-space between stanzas
- Include name and contact info (unless blind submission)
- Number pages
- Proofread carefully
- Save as PDF (unless otherwise specified)

**Cover Letter**:
- Keep brief (3-4 sentences)
- Mention if you've read the journal
- Include relevant publications (if any)
- No need to explain poems
- Professional but not stiff

**Example**:
```
Dear Editors,

Please consider the enclosed poems for publication in [Journal Name]. I've admired your recent work by [Author], particularly [specific detail].

My poems have appeared in [Journal 1], [Journal 2], and [Journal 3].

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
```

**First Submission** (no publications yet):
```
Dear Editors,

Please consider the enclosed poems for publication in [Journal Name].

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
```

#### Submission Strategy

**Simultaneous Submissions**:
- Sending same poems to multiple journals at once
- Most journals allow this
- Must withdraw if accepted elsewhere
- Note in cover letter if required

**Tracking Submissions**:
- Use spreadsheet or Submittable
- Record: journal, date sent, poems submitted, response date, outcome
- Note response times
- Track acceptances for resume

**How Many Poems**:
- Follow guidelines (typically 3-5)
- Submit your best work
- Consider grouping thematically
- Show range if possible

**Timing**:
- Check reading periods (many journals close seasonally)
- Note response times (can be 3-6 months)
- Be patient
- Keep submitting new work while waiting

#### Handling Responses

**Acceptances**:
- Respond promptly and professionally
- Withdraw poems from other journals immediately
- Provide any requested information
- Thank editors
- Promote publication when it appears

**Rejections**:
- Don't take personally (competition is fierce)
- Standard rejections are normal
- Personal rejections (with comments) are encouraging
- Invitations to submit again are positive
- Revise and resubmit elsewhere

**No Response**:
- Check guidelines for response time
- Query after stated time + 2 weeks
- Consider withdrawing if excessive delay

### Building a Publication Record

**Start Small**:
- Submit to emerging journals
- Enter contests selectively
- Build confidence and experience
- Gradually aim higher

**Be Prolific**:
- Keep writing new poems
- Always have work in circulation
- Don't wait for responses to submit more
- Aim for 50-100 submissions per year

**Develop Thick Skin**:
- Rejection is normal (even for established poets)
- Acceptance rates are often 1-3%
- Persistence is key
- Every rejection brings you closer to acceptance

**Network**:
- Attend readings
- Join poetry organizations
- Connect on social media
- Support other poets
- Build community

### Book Publication

#### Preparing a Manuscript

**Organization**:
- Arrange poems thoughtfully (not chronologically)
- Create sections if appropriate
- Consider narrative or thematic arc
- Strong opening and closing poems
- Vary tone, length, subject

**Manuscript Length**:
- Chapbook: 20-30 pages
- Full-length: 48-80 pages (typically 50-60)

**Title**:
- Memorable and evocative
- Reflects collection's themes
- Not too generic or too obscure

**Getting Feedback**:
- Workshop full manuscript
- Hire manuscript consultant (optional)
- Get input from published poets
- Revise based on feedback

#### Submission Process

**Contests**:
- Most common route for first book
- Entry fee ($25-30 typically)
- Winner gets publication + prize money
- Often finalists also published
- Research judge's aesthetic

**Open Submissions**:
- Some presses accept unsolicited manuscripts
- Often during specific reading periods
- No fee
- Longer odds than contests

**Agents**:
- Rare for poetry
- More common for poet-celebrities
- Not necessary for most poets

**Timeline**:
- Contest results: 3-6 months
- Publication after acceptance: 12-18 months
- Total: 2-3 years from submission to book

### After Publication

**Promotion**:
- Share on social media
- Update website/bio
- Send to friends and family
- Thank editors publicly
- Add to CV/resume

**Readings**:
- Bookstore readings
- Library events
- Open mics
- Literary festivals
- Virtual readings

**Reviews**:
- Send to review venues
- Request reviews from poet friends
- Share positive reviews

**Keep Writing**:
- Don't rest on laurels
- Start next project
- Publication is milestone, not destination

## Conclusion

Revision and publication are essential parts of the poetry journey. Revision transforms raw drafts into polished, powerful poems through patient, thoughtful work. Publication shares your voice with the world and connects you to the larger poetry community. Both require persistence, thick skin, and commitment to craft. Remember: the writing itself is the reward, and publication is a bonus. Keep writing, keep revising, keep submitting, and keep growing as a poet.
