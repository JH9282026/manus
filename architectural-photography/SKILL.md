# Architectural Photography

Capture buildings and structures with technical precision, correcting perspective distortion and showcasing architectural design.

## Overview

Architectural photography requires technical expertise to overcome perspective distortion, challenging lighting, and spatial constraints. Success involves mastering tilt-shift lenses or software correction, understanding architectural composition, and balancing technical accuracy with artistic vision. This skill covers equipment selection, perspective correction techniques, lighting strategies, and workflows for professional architectural documentation.

## Essential Equipment

### Tilt-Shift Lenses
**What They Do**:
- **Shift**: Move lens axis perpendicular to sensor, correcting converging lines
- **Tilt**: Change focal plane angle, control depth of field

**Why Essential**:
- Keep vertical lines parallel when photographing tall buildings
- Maintain accurate proportions
- Correct perspective optically (superior to software correction)

**Common Focal Lengths**:
- **17mm**: Ultra-wide, interiors and tight spaces
- **24mm**: Standard architectural focal length
- **45mm**: Detail shots, less distortion
- **90mm**: Architectural details, exteriors from distance

**Recommended**: Canon TS-E 24mm f/3.5L II, Nikon PC-E 24mm f/3.5D

### Alternative: Wide-Angle Lenses
**If No Tilt-Shift**:
- 16-35mm zoom or 24mm prime
- Correct perspective in post-processing (Lightroom, Photoshop)
- Loss of resolution and image quality from correction

### Tripod
**Essential for**:
- Precise composition and leveling
- Long exposures (interiors, twilight)
- Consistent framing
- Tilt-shift lens use (sensitive to movement)

## Perspective Correction

### The Problem
**Converging Lines**:
- Tilting camera upward → vertical lines lean backward
- Tilting downward → vertical lines lean forward
- Creates distorted, unnatural appearance

**Cause**: Top of building farther from camera than base, appears smaller

### Tilt-Shift Solution
**Shift Function**:
1. Keep camera level (sensor perpendicular to ground)
2. Shift lens upward to include top of building
3. Vertical lines remain parallel
4. Accurate, undistorted representation

**Advantages Over Software**:
- Preserves image quality (no stretching or cropping)
- Real-time visualization in viewfinder
- Professional standard for architectural work
- Faster workflow (correct in-camera)

### Software Correction
**Lightroom/Photoshop**:
- Transform tool (Auto, Guided, or Manual)
- Straighten verticals and horizontals
- Crop to remove empty areas

**Limitations**:
- Reduces resolution (stretching and cropping)
- Can introduce softness
- Time-consuming for large projects

## Shooting Techniques

### Exterior Photography
**Timing**:
- **Golden Hour**: Warm, soft light, long shadows
- **Blue Hour**: 20-30 min after sunset, deep blue sky, interior lights glowing
- **Overcast**: Even light, good for details

**Composition**:
- Include context (surroundings, landscaping)
- Emphasize architectural features
- Multiple angles (front, side, corner views)

**Camera Settings**:
- **Aperture**: f/8-f/11 (sharpness, depth of field)
- **ISO**: 100-400
- **Tripod**: Essential for sharp images

### Interior Photography
**Challenges**:
- Mixed lighting (windows + artificial)
- High contrast (bright windows, dark corners)
- Tight spaces

**Solutions**:
- HDR bracketing (like real estate photography)
- Flash or continuous lighting
- Wide-angle or tilt-shift lens

**Composition**:
- Show room function and flow
- Emphasize architectural details
- Straight verticals and horizontals

## Lighting Strategies

### Natural Light
- Position to show building in best light
- Avoid harsh midday sun (flat, washed out)
- Use shadows to add dimension

### Twilight Exteriors
- Dramatic, high-end look
- Interior lights create warm glow
- Deep blue sky provides contrast
- Requires tripod, longer exposures (1-10 seconds)

### Artificial Light (Interiors)
- Flash or continuous lights to balance window light
- Even illumination throughout space
- Flambient technique (flash + ambient)

## Post-Processing

### Perspective Correction
- Straighten verticals and horizontals
- Lens correction (remove distortion)
- Crop to final composition

### Exposure and Tone
- Balance highlights and shadows
- Enhance architectural details
- Natural, accurate colors

### HDR Processing
- Merge bracketed exposures
- Preserve window views
- Avoid over-processed look
