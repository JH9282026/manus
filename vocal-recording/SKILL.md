# Vocal Recording

Professional vocal recording techniques.

## Overview

Vocal recording is a critical skill in music production, requiring careful attention to microphone selection, placement, acoustic environment, and signal chain. Professional vocal recording involves understanding microphone types and polar patterns, optimizing room acoustics, proper gain staging, and addressing common issues like plosives, sibilance, and room reflections.

This skill covers techniques from close-miking for intimacy to distant placement for natural room tone, microphone selection based on voice characteristics, acoustic treatment strategies, and best practices for capturing clean, professional vocal performances across genres.

## Core Concepts

### Microphone Placement Techniques

**Frontal Close Mic (2-6 inches)** — Intimate sound, good consonant articulation, proximity warmth; requires pop filter

**Frontal Loose Mic (12 inches)** — Natural room tone, reduced de-essing needs, unique depth

**Top Head Placement** — Reduces plosives and sibilance for breathy performers; requires clear off-axis response

**Lower Chest Placement** — Natural diaphragmatic warmth, full-bodied sound, reduces nasal tones

**Off-Axis Recording (15-30 degrees)** — Reduces sibilance while maintaining clarity

### Microphone Types

**Dynamic Microphones** — Durable, high SPL tolerance, midrange emphasis, less sensitive to room acoustics (Shure SM57/SM58)

**Condenser Microphones** — Greater sensitivity, wider frequency response, requires phantom power; large-diaphragm for silky highs, small-diaphragm for transients

**Ribbon Microphones** — Warm, natural, flat sound; delicate; excellent for bright sources

### Polar Patterns

**Cardioid** — Captures front, minimizes sides/rear; susceptible to proximity effect and plosives

**Omnidirectional** — Captures all directions; immune to proximity effect, resistant to plosives

**Figure 8 (Bidirectional)** — Captures front and rear, rejects sides

## Professional Workflows

### Studio Setup and Acoustic Treatment

**Room Selection** — Choose quiet room with soft furnishings, carpets, curtains; avoid hard reflective surfaces

**Absorption** — Control primary reflections, especially wall behind microphone; use absorptive surfaces, open closets with clothes

**Noise Reduction** — Turn off appliances, air conditioners; close windows/doors; use airplane mode on phones

**Gain Staging** — Maintain peaks between -12 and -6 dBFS for sufficient resolution and headroom

### Recording Workflow

1. **Microphone Selection** — Choose based on voice characteristics and desired sound
2. **Placement** — Position microphone at appropriate distance and angle
3. **Pop Filter Setup** — Place between singer and microphone
4. **Headphone Mix** — Create comfortable monitor mix for performer
5. **Gain Setting** — Set levels based on loudest passages with 3-6 dB headroom
6. **Test Recording** — Record short section to check levels and sound
7. **Multiple Takes** — Record several complete takes for comping
8. **Comping** — Combine best parts of multiple takes
