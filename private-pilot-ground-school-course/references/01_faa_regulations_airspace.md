# FAA Regulations and Airspace

## Introduction

Understanding Federal Aviation Regulations (FARs) and airspace classification is fundamental to safe and legal flight operations. This comprehensive guide covers the regulatory framework governing private pilots and the complex airspace system in the United States.

## Federal Aviation Regulations (FARs)

### Regulatory Structure

**Title 14 Code of Federal Regulations (14 CFR)**:
- Part 61: Certification of Pilots, Flight Instructors, and Ground Instructors
- Part 91: General Operating and Flight Rules
- Part 43: Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance, Rebuilding, and Alteration
- Part 39: Airworthiness Directives
- NTSB 830: Accident and Incident Reporting

### Part 61: Pilot Certification

**Private Pilot Certificate Requirements (61.103-61.109)**:

**Eligibility (61.103)**:
- At least 17 years old (16 for glider or balloon)
- Read, speak, write, and understand English
- Hold at least a third-class medical certificate (or BasicMed)
- Receive required ground and flight training
- Pass knowledge and practical tests

**Aeronautical Knowledge (61.105)**:
Must receive and log ground training in:
- Applicable FARs
- Accident reporting requirements
- Use of aeronautical charts
- Radio communication procedures
- Recognition of critical weather situations
- Safe and efficient operation of aircraft
- Effects of density altitude
- Weight and balance computations
- Principles of aerodynamics, powerplants, and aircraft systems
- Stall awareness, spin entry, spins, and spin recovery
- Aeronautical decision making and judgment

**Flight Proficiency (61.107)**:
Must receive and log flight training in:
- Preflight preparation and procedures
- Airport and seaplane base operations
- Takeoffs, landings, and go-arounds
- Performance maneuvers
- Ground reference maneuvers
- Navigation
- Slow flight and stalls
- Basic instrument maneuvers
- Emergency operations
- Night operations (if applicable)
- Postflight procedures

**Aeronautical Experience (61.109)**:
Minimum 40 hours total flight time (35 hours under Part 141):
- 20 hours flight training with instructor
- 10 hours solo flight time
- 3 hours cross-country flight training
- 3 hours night flight training (including one cross-country over 100 nm and 10 takeoffs/landings)
- 3 hours instrument training
- 3 hours within 2 months of practical test
- 10 hours solo (including 5 hours cross-country, one flight of 150+ nm with landings at 3 points)

**Privileges and Limitations (61.113)**:

**Privileges**:
- Act as pilot in command (PIC) of aircraft for which rated
- Carry passengers
- Share operating expenses with passengers (pro-rata share)
- Demonstrate aircraft to prospective buyers (if 200+ hours)
- Act as PIC for charitable, nonprofit, or community events (with limitations)

**Limitations**:
- Cannot act as PIC for compensation or hire
- Cannot pay less than pro-rata share of operating expenses
- Cannot act as PIC carrying passengers or property for compensation or hire

### Part 91: General Operating and Flight Rules

**Subpart A: General**

**Pilot in Command Authority (91.3)**:
- PIC is directly responsible for and final authority for operation of aircraft
- In emergency, may deviate from any rule to extent required to meet emergency
- Must send written report if requested by Administrator

**Preflight Action (91.103)**:
Before every flight, PIC must become familiar with all available information concerning that flight, including:
- Weather reports and forecasts
- Fuel requirements
- Alternatives if flight cannot be completed as planned
- Known traffic delays
- Runway lengths at airports of intended use
- Takeoff and landing distance data

For flights not in vicinity of airport:
- Weather reports and forecasts
- Fuel requirements
- Alternatives available if planned flight cannot be completed
- Known ATC traffic delays

**Alcohol and Drugs (91.17)**:
No person may act as crewmember:
- Within 8 hours of consuming alcohol
- While under the influence of alcohol
- With blood alcohol content of 0.04% or greater
- While using any drug that affects faculties contrary to safety

**Medical Certificate Requirements (91.23, 61.23)**:

**Class 3 Medical**:
- Required for private pilot operations
- Valid for 60 months (under age 40)
- Valid for 24 months (age 40 and over)

**BasicMed (61.113(i))**:
Alternative to medical certificate:
- Valid U.S. driver's license
- Medical examination by state-licensed physician every 48 months
- Complete online medical education course every 24 months
- Limitations: Aircraft up to 6,000 lbs, max 6 occupants, max 250 KIAS, max 18,000 ft MSL, within U.S.

**Flight Crewmember Duties (91.105)**:
- No person may manipulate controls during critical phases of flight unless qualified
- No person may engage in activities that could distract from flight duties during critical phases

**Seatbelts (91.107)**:
- Each person on board must occupy approved seat with safety belt fastened during takeoff and landing
- Each person under 2 years may be held by adult

**Subpart B: Flight Rules**

**General (91.111-91.126)**:

**Operating Near Other Aircraft (91.111)**:
- No person may operate aircraft so close to another as to create collision hazard
- No formation flight with passengers for hire
- No formation flight carrying passengers without prior arrangement with PIC of each aircraft

**Right-of-Way Rules (91.113)**:
When aircraft are approaching head-on or nearly so, each shall alter course to right.

Aircraft on right has right-of-way when converging at approximately same altitude (except balloons have right-of-way over any other category, gliders over airships/airplanes/rotorcraft, airships over airplanes/rotorcraft).

Aircraft being overtaken has right-of-way; overtaking aircraft shall alter course to right.

Aircraft in distress has right-of-way over all other aircraft.

**Minimum Safe Altitudes (91.119)**:
Except for takeoff or landing:
- **Anywhere**: Altitude allowing emergency landing without undue hazard to persons or property on surface
- **Over congested areas**: 1,000 feet above highest obstacle within 2,000-foot radius
- **Over other than congested areas**: 500 feet above surface, except over open water or sparsely populated areas (500 feet from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure)
- **Helicopters**: May operate at less than minimums if operation is conducted without hazard

**Cruising Altitudes (91.159)**:
VFR cruising altitudes (more than 3,000 feet AGL):
- Magnetic course 0-179°: Odd thousands + 500 feet (3,500, 5,500, 7,500, etc.)
- Magnetic course 180-359°: Even thousands + 500 feet (4,500, 6,500, 8,500, etc.)

**Visual Flight Rules (91.155)**:

**Basic VFR Weather Minimums**:

| Airspace | Visibility | Cloud Clearance |
|----------|------------|-----------------|
| Class A | Not applicable (IFR only) | Not applicable |
| Class B | 3 SM | Clear of clouds |
| Class C | 3 SM | 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal |
| Class D | 3 SM | 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal |
| Class E (at or above 10,000' MSL) | 5 SM | 1,000' below, 1,000' above, 1 SM horizontal |
| Class E (below 10,000' MSL) | 3 SM | 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal |
| Class G (1,200' AGL or less, day) | 1 SM | Clear of clouds |
| Class G (1,200' AGL or less, night) | 3 SM | 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal |
| Class G (more than 1,200' AGL, less than 10,000' MSL) | 1 SM (day), 3 SM (night) | 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal |
| Class G (more than 1,200' AGL, at or above 10,000' MSL) | 5 SM | 1,000' below, 1,000' above, 1 SM horizontal |

**Special VFR (91.157)**:
- May be authorized by ATC in Class B, C, D, or E surface areas
- Requires clear of clouds, 1 SM visibility
- At night, pilot must be instrument rated and aircraft must be IFR equipped

**Subpart C: Equipment, Instrument, and Certificate Requirements**

**Required Equipment (91.205)**:

**VFR Day (ATOMATOFLAMES)**:
- **A**ltimeter
- **T**achometer for each engine
- **O**il pressure gauge for each engine
- **M**anifold pressure gauge (for each altitude engine)
- **A**irspeed indicator
- **T**emperature gauge for each liquid-cooled engine
- **O**il temperature gauge for each air-cooled engine
- **F**uel gauge for each tank
- **L**anding gear position indicator (if retractable)
- **A**nti-collision lights (aircraft certified after 3/11/96)
- **M**agnetic compass
- **E**LT (Emergency Locator Transmitter)
- **S**afety belts

**VFR Night (add FLAPS)**:
- **F**uses (one spare set)
- **L**anding light (if for hire)
- **A**nti-collision lights
- **P**osition lights (navigation lights)
- **S**ource of electrical power

**Transponder Requirements (91.215)**:
Mode C transponder required:
- Class A, B, and C airspace
- Class E above 10,000' MSL (excluding airspace at and below 2,500' AGL)
- Within 30 nm of Class B primary airport (Mode C veil)
- Above Class C airspace ceiling

**ELT Requirements (91.207)**:
- Required for most aircraft
- Must be inspected within 12 calendar months
- Batteries must be replaced (or recharged) when:
  - Transmitter has been in use for more than 1 cumulative hour
  - 50% of useful life has expired

**Airworthiness Certificate (91.203)**:
Must be displayed in aircraft at all times.

**Aircraft Maintenance (91.405-91.417)**:
- Owner/operator responsible for maintaining aircraft in airworthy condition
- Annual inspection required every 12 calendar months
- 100-hour inspection required if aircraft used for hire or flight instruction
- VOR check required every 30 days for IFR operations
- Altimeter/static system inspection every 24 calendar months for IFR
- Transponder inspection every 24 calendar months
- ELT inspection every 12 calendar months

**Mnemonic: AV1ATE**:
- **A**nnual inspection (12 calendar months)
- **V**OR check (30 days, IFR only)
- **1**00-hour inspection (if for hire)
- **A**ltimeter/static system (24 calendar months, IFR)
- **T**ransponder (24 calendar months)
- **E**LT (12 calendar months)

### NTSB 830: Accident and Incident Reporting

**Definitions**:

**Aircraft Accident**: Occurrence associated with operation of aircraft where:
- Any person suffers death or serious injury, OR
- Aircraft receives substantial damage

**Serious Injury**: Injury requiring hospitalization for more than 48 hours (within 7 days of injury), fracture (except simple fractures of fingers, toes, or nose), severe hemorrhages, nerve/muscle/tendon damage, second or third-degree burns, or any injury to internal organs.

**Substantial Damage**: Damage or failure that adversely affects structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics, requiring major repair or replacement (excluding engine failure, damage limited to engine, bent fairings, dented skin, small punctures, ground damage to propeller, landing gear, flaps, or wingtips).

**Incident**: Occurrence other than accident that affects or could affect safety of operations.

**Reporting Requirements (830.15)**:

**Immediate Notification** (most expeditious means available):
Required for:
- Aircraft accidents
- Serious incidents (flight control malfunction, in-flight fire, collision in flight, damage to property other than aircraft exceeding $25,000, overdue aircraft believed to be in accident)

**Written Report**:
- Accident: Within 10 days
- Overdue aircraft (if not located): Within 7 days

## U.S. Airspace System

### Airspace Classification

**Class A Airspace**:
- **Altitude**: 18,000' MSL to FL600
- **Requirements**: IFR clearance, IFR equipped, instrument rated pilot
- **ATC Services**: Separation of all aircraft
- **Speed Limit**: None
- **Entry**: IFR clearance required
- **VFR**: Not permitted

**Class B Airspace**:
- **Location**: Around busiest airports (e.g., LAX, JFK, ORD)
- **Shape**: Upside-down wedding cake (typically surface to 10,000' MSL)
- **Requirements**: 
  - ATC clearance required before entering
  - Two-way radio communication
  - Mode C transponder
  - Private pilot certificate (or student with endorsement)
- **VFR Minimums**: 3 SM visibility, clear of clouds
- **Speed Limit**: 250 KIAS below 10,000' MSL; 200 KIAS in underlying Class B or VFR corridor
- **ATC Services**: Separation of all aircraft

**Class C Airspace**:
- **Location**: Around moderately busy airports
- **Shape**: Two-tier upside-down wedding cake (typically surface to 4,000' AGL)
- **Requirements**:
  - Two-way radio communication before entering
  - Mode C transponder
- **VFR Minimums**: 3 SM visibility, 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal from clouds
- **Speed Limit**: 250 KIAS below 10,000' MSL; 200 KIAS within 4 nm of primary airport at or below 2,500' AGL
- **ATC Services**: Separation between IFR and VFR, traffic advisories for VFR

**Class D Airspace**:
- **Location**: Around airports with control tower
- **Shape**: Cylinder (typically surface to 2,500' AGL)
- **Requirements**: Two-way radio communication before entering
- **VFR Minimums**: 3 SM visibility, 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal from clouds
- **Speed Limit**: 200 KIAS at or below 2,500' AGL within 4 nm of primary airport
- **ATC Services**: Separation for IFR, traffic advisories for VFR (workload permitting)

**Class E Airspace**:
- **Location**: Controlled airspace not A, B, C, or D
- **Types**:
  - Surface area (designated airports)
  - Extension to surface area
  - 700' AGL (magenta vignette on sectional)
  - 1,200' AGL (everywhere else unless Class G)
  - 14,500' MSL to 17,999' MSL (excluding airspace less than 1,500' AGL)
- **Requirements**: None for VFR
- **VFR Minimums**: 
  - Below 10,000' MSL: 3 SM visibility, 500' below, 1,000' above, 2,000' horizontal
  - At or above 10,000' MSL: 5 SM visibility, 1,000' below, 1,000' above, 1 SM horizontal
- **Speed Limit**: 250 KIAS below 10,000' MSL
- **ATC Services**: Separation for IFR only

**Class G Airspace**:
- **Location**: Uncontrolled airspace
- **Altitude**: Surface to base of overlying controlled airspace (typically 700' or 1,200' AGL)
- **Requirements**: None
- **VFR Minimums**: Vary by altitude and day/night (see table above)
- **Speed Limit**: 250 KIAS below 10,000' MSL
- **ATC Services**: None

### Special Use Airspace

**Prohibited Areas**:
- Flight prohibited at all times
- Example: P-56 (Camp David), P-49 (White House)
- Depicted on sectional charts with blue hatching

**Restricted Areas**:
- Contains unusual, often invisible hazards (artillery firing, aerial gunnery, guided missiles)
- Penetration without authorization could be extremely hazardous
- May be penetrated when not active with ATC permission
- Depicted on sectional charts with blue hatching

**Warning Areas**:
- Similar to restricted but in international airspace (beyond 3 nm offshore)
- No permission required but caution advised
- Depicted on sectional charts with blue hatching

**Military Operation Areas (MOAs)**:
- Military training activities
- VFR traffic not prohibited but should exercise caution
- IFR traffic separated by ATC
- Depicted on sectional charts with magenta hatching

**Alert Areas**:
- High volume of pilot training or unusual aerial activity
- No special permission required
- Depicted on sectional charts with magenta hatching

**Controlled Firing Areas (CFAs)**:
- Activities suspended when aircraft approach
- Not depicted on charts

### Other Airspace

**Mode C Veil**:
- 30 nm radius around Class B primary airport
- Mode C transponder required (with exceptions)

**Terminal Radar Service Area (TRSA)**:
- Voluntary participation
- ATC provides separation and traffic advisories
- Depicted on sectional charts with solid black lines

**National Security Areas (NSAs)**:
- Temporary or permanent areas where flight is discouraged (not prohibited)
- Depicted on sectional charts

**Temporary Flight Restrictions (TFRs)**:
- Established for:
  - Disaster/hazard areas
  - Presidential movements (30 nm radius)
  - Space operations
  - Major sporting events (3 nm radius, up to 3,000' AGL)
- Check NOTAMs before every flight

**Wildlife Refuge Areas**:
- Requested to maintain 2,000' AGL
- Depicted on sectional charts

## Best Practices

1. **Know Before You Go**: Review airspace along route before every flight
2. **Use Current Charts**: Ensure sectional charts are current
3. **Check NOTAMs**: TFRs and airspace changes
4. **Communicate**: Establish radio contact before entering controlled airspace
5. **Altitude Awareness**: Maintain appropriate VFR cruising altitudes
6. **Weather Minimums**: Never compromise on VFR weather minimums
7. **Regulatory Compliance**: Stay current on FAR changes
8. **Continuous Learning**: Regulations and airspace evolve

## Conclusion

Mastery of FAA regulations and airspace classification is essential for safe, legal flight operations. Private pilots must not only memorize these rules but understand their purpose and apply them consistently. Regular review, staying current with changes, and conservative decision-making ensure compliance and safety throughout a pilot's flying career.
