# Advanced User Research Methods

Detailed protocols and execution guidance for ethnography, diary studies, and advanced interviewing techniques.

---

## Ethnographic Research

Ethnography involves observing users in their natural environments to understand behaviors, needs, and contexts that traditional research methods might miss.

### Planning Ethnographic Studies

**Step 1: Define Research Focus**

Be specific about what you want to observe:
- ❌ "Understand how people work"
- ✓ "Understand how remote teams coordinate across time zones"

**Research Questions Examples:**
- How do users integrate our product into their daily workflow?
- What environmental factors influence product usage?
- What workarounds have users created?
- How do users collaborate with others using our product?

**Step 2: Select Observation Sites**

Choose locations that provide rich context:
- User's workplace (office, home office)
- Public spaces (coffee shops, co-working spaces)
- User's home (for consumer products)
- Online environments (for digital products)

**Access Considerations:**
- Obtain necessary permissions (workplace, public spaces)
- Build rapport before observation
- Respect privacy and boundaries
- Consider observer effect (Hawthorne effect)

**Step 3: Prepare Observation Protocol**

Create structured framework for note-taking:

**Observation Template:**
```
Date/Time: [timestamp]
Participant: [ID]
Location: [description]
Activity: [what they're doing]

Observations:
- Physical environment: [layout, tools, artifacts]
- Behaviors: [actions, sequences, patterns]
- Interactions: [with people, tools, systems]
- Verbal: [what they say, tone]
- Non-verbal: [body language, expressions]
- Interruptions: [what disrupts flow]
- Workarounds: [creative solutions, hacks]

Questions to follow up:
- [note anything unclear or interesting]

Initial insights:
- [preliminary patterns or hypotheses]
```

### Conducting Field Observations

**Passive Observation**

Watch without interfering:

**Best Practices:**
- Position yourself unobtrusively
- Take detailed notes (or record with permission)
- Note timestamps for key events
- Capture environmental context (photos)
- Observe for extended periods (2-4 hours minimum)
- Look for patterns across multiple sessions

**What to Observe:**
- **Sequences:** Order of actions
- **Frequency:** How often behaviors occur
- **Duration:** How long activities take
- **Triggers:** What initiates behaviors
- **Outcomes:** Results of actions
- **Emotions:** Frustration, delight, confusion

**Participant Observation**

Join the activity to gain insider perspective:

**When to Use:**
- Understanding complex workflows
- Learning specialized tasks
- Building deep empathy
- Accessing restricted environments

**Approach:**
- Start as observer, gradually participate
- Learn the "language" and norms
- Balance participation with observation
- Reflect on your own experience

**Example: Observing Restaurant Staff**
- Shadow servers during shifts
- Help with simple tasks (bussing tables)
- Experience the pace and pressure
- Note pain points from insider view

**Contextual Inquiry**

Combine observation with real-time interviewing:

**Structure:**
1. **Introduction:** Explain study, build rapport
2. **Observation:** Watch user perform tasks
3. **Inquiry:** Ask questions during or after tasks
4. **Interpretation:** Confirm understanding with user

**Sample Questions During Tasks:**
- "What are you doing now?"
- "Why did you choose that approach?"
- "What are you thinking about?"
- "What would you do if [scenario]?"

**Think-Aloud Protocol:**
Ask users to verbalize their thoughts while working:
- "Please say out loud what you're thinking as you do this."
- Useful for understanding decision-making
- Can feel unnatural; practice helps

### Digital Ethnography (Netnography)

Study online communities and digital behaviors.

**Research Contexts:**
- Social media platforms (Twitter, Reddit, LinkedIn)
- Online forums and communities
- Product review sites
- Discord/Slack communities
- YouTube comments and discussions

**Approach:**

**1. Lurking Phase (1-2 weeks)**
- Observe without participating
- Understand community norms and culture
- Identify key members and topics
- Note language and terminology

**2. Active Participation (2-4 weeks)**
- Introduce yourself (if appropriate)
- Engage authentically
- Ask questions and contribute
- Build relationships

**3. Data Collection**
- Screenshot relevant discussions
- Save posts and comments
- Note patterns and themes
- Track sentiment and tone

**Ethical Considerations:**
- Respect community rules
- Disclose research intent when appropriate
- Anonymize quotes and usernames
- Don't exploit vulnerable communities

**Analysis:**
- Identify common pain points and needs
- Understand user language and mental models
- Discover unmet needs and workarounds
- Track sentiment toward products/features

**Tools:**
- Social listening: Brandwatch, Sprout Social, Mention
- Community analytics: Orbit, Common Room
- Manual: Spreadsheets, note-taking apps

### Field Notes Best Practices

**During Observation:**
- Write continuously (capture everything)
- Use shorthand and abbreviations
- Note exact quotes (use quotation marks)
- Sketch diagrams and layouts
- Timestamp key events

**After Observation:**
- Expand notes within 24 hours (while fresh)
- Add reflections and interpretations
- Identify emerging themes
- Note questions for follow-up

**Field Note Structure:**
```
Descriptive Notes:
- Objective observations
- What you saw and heard
- Environmental details

Reflective Notes:
- Your interpretations
- Emerging patterns
- Questions and hypotheses

Methodological Notes:
- What worked/didn't work
- Adjustments needed
- Observer bias to watch for
```

### Analyzing Ethnographic Data

**Step 1: Immersion**
- Read all field notes multiple times
- Watch recordings
- Immerse in the data

**Step 2: Coding**
- Identify recurring themes
- Tag observations with codes
- Group related codes

**Step 3: Pattern Identification**
- Look for:
  - Repeated behaviors
  - Contradictions
  - Unexpected findings
  - Environmental influences
  - Social dynamics

**Step 4: Insight Development**
- Connect patterns to research questions
- Develop actionable insights
- Support with evidence (quotes, photos)

**Step 5: Validation**
- Share findings with participants (member checking)
- Triangulate with other data sources
- Test interpretations with stakeholders

## Diary Studies

Longitudinal self-reported research capturing behaviors, attitudes, and experiences over time.

### Study Design

**Define Study Goals**

Diary studies excel at:
- Understanding habits and routines
- Tracking behavior changes over time
- Capturing in-the-moment experiences
- Identifying triggers and contexts
- Documenting product usage patterns

**Example Goals:**
- How do users integrate our app into their morning routine?
- What triggers users to open our app?
- How does usage change over the first 30 days?
- What contexts lead to feature discovery?

**Select Logging Protocol**

**1. Interval-Contingent (Time-Based)**

Participants log at regular intervals:
- Daily (e.g., end of day reflection)
- Twice daily (morning and evening)
- Weekly (Sunday recap)

**Best For:**
- Routine tracking
- Habit formation
- General experience over time

**Example Prompts:**
- "Describe your experience with [product] today."
- "What challenges did you face today?"
- "Rate your satisfaction today (1-10)."

**2. Signal-Contingent (Prompted)**

Participants receive notifications to log:
- Random times throughout day
- Specific times (e.g., after lunch)
- Triggered by app usage

**Best For:**
- Capturing diverse contexts
- Reducing recall bias
- Experience sampling

**Example Prompts:**
- "What are you doing right now?"
- "How are you feeling?"
- "Are you using [product]? Why or why not?"

**3. Event-Contingent (Activity-Based)**

Participants log when specific events occur:
- After using product
- When encountering problem
- When achieving goal

**Best For:**
- Specific behavior tracking
- Problem identification
- Usage pattern analysis

**Example Prompts:**
- "You just used [feature]. How did it go?"
- "Describe what you were trying to accomplish."
- "Did you encounter any issues?"

**Determine Study Duration**

**Short-Term (3-7 days):**
- Specific behaviors or events
- High-frequency activities
- Intensive data collection

**Medium-Term (1-2 weeks):**
- Habit tracking
- Routine understanding
- Balanced depth and breadth

**Long-Term (3-4 weeks):**
- Behavior change
- Product adoption
- Attitude evolution

**Trade-offs:**
- Longer = more data, but higher dropout
- Shorter = easier completion, but less pattern visibility

### Implementation

**Choose Diary Platform**

**Dedicated Diary Apps:**
- **dscout:** Mobile-first, video/photo capture, robust analytics
- **Indeemo:** Video diaries, real-time monitoring, follow-up questions
- **ExperienceFellow:** Multimedia entries, collaborative analysis
- **Ethnio:** Screener + diary + scheduling in one

**Survey Platforms:**
- **Typeform:** Beautiful UX, conditional logic
- **Google Forms:** Free, simple, familiar
- **SurveyMonkey:** Advanced features, integrations

**Messaging Apps:**
- **WhatsApp/Telegram:** Familiar, low friction, multimedia
- **Slack:** Good for team-based studies
- **SMS:** Universal, but limited media

**Custom Solutions:**
- **Airtable:** Flexible forms, database backend
- **Notion:** Rich entries, collaborative
- **Custom app:** Full control, higher cost

**Selection Criteria:**
- Participant tech comfort
- Media types needed (text, photo, video)
- Budget
- Analysis capabilities
- Mobile vs. desktop

**Create Entry Prompts**

**Prompt Design Principles:**
- **Specific:** "What feature did you use?" not "How was your day?"
- **Open-ended:** Encourage elaboration
- **Contextual:** Ask about specific moments
- **Varied:** Mix question types to maintain engagement

**Example Prompt Sequence (Daily Diary):**

**Day 1-3 (Onboarding):**
- "Describe your first impression of [product]."
- "What was easy? What was confusing?"
- "Upload a screenshot of something you liked or disliked."

**Day 4-7 (Usage Patterns):**
- "When did you use [product] today?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish?"
- "Record a short video showing how you used it."

**Day 8-14 (Deeper Insights):**
- "How has [product] fit into your routine?"
- "What would make it more useful?"
- "Compare [product] to alternatives you've used."

**Media Prompts:**
- "Take a photo of where you use [product]."
- "Record a 30-second video of your workspace."
- "Screenshot a moment of frustration or delight."

**Recruit and Onboard Participants**

**Recruitment:**
- Target 10-20 participants (expect 20-30% dropout)
- Screen for commitment and articulation
- Offer appropriate compensation ($100-300 for 2-week study)

**Onboarding Session (30-45 min):**
1. Explain study purpose and expectations
2. Walk through diary platform
3. Complete practice entry together
4. Answer questions
5. Provide contact info for support

**Onboarding Materials:**
- Welcome email with instructions
- Video tutorial (2-3 min)
- FAQ document
- Reminder schedule

**Manage Ongoing Study**

**Participant Engagement:**
- Send daily/weekly reminders
- Respond to entries with follow-up questions
- Acknowledge participation ("Thanks for sharing!")
- Provide progress updates ("You're halfway through!")

**Monitoring:**
- Track completion rates daily
- Reach out to non-responders
- Adjust prompts if needed
- Note technical issues

**Incentive Structure:**
- Milestone bonuses (50% at midpoint, 50% at completion)
- Bonus for high-quality entries
- Timely payment (within 1 week of completion)

**Conduct Follow-Up Interviews**

Interview subset of participants (5-8) to:
- Clarify ambiguous entries
- Explore emerging themes
- Gather additional context
- Validate interpretations

**Interview Guide:**
1. Review their diary entries together
2. Ask about specific moments or patterns
3. Probe on interesting or unexpected findings
4. Explore themes across participants

### Analyzing Diary Data

**Step 1: Organize Data**
- Export all entries to spreadsheet or database
- Tag by participant, date, prompt
- Transcribe video/audio entries
- Organize media files

**Step 2: Initial Review**
- Read all entries chronologically per participant
- Note initial impressions and patterns
- Identify standout moments

**Step 3: Coding**
- Develop coding scheme (themes, behaviors, sentiments)
- Tag entries with codes
- Use qualitative analysis software (Dovetail, NVivo, Atlas.ti)

**Step 4: Cross-Participant Analysis**
- Look for patterns across participants
- Compare different segments
- Identify outliers and edge cases

**Step 5: Temporal Analysis**
- Track changes over time
- Identify inflection points
- Note progression patterns

**Step 6: Synthesis**
- Develop key insights
- Support with evidence (quotes, media)
- Create visualizations (journey maps, timelines)

## Advanced Interviewing Techniques

### Interview Planning

**Define Interview Objectives**

Be clear about what you want to learn:
- Exploratory: Understand problem space
- Evaluative: Validate concepts or designs
- Generative: Discover opportunities

**Create Discussion Guide**

**Structure:**
1. **Opening (5 min)**
   - Introduce yourself and study
   - Build rapport
   - Explain format and consent

2. **Warm-Up (5-10 min)**
   - Easy, non-threatening questions
   - Build comfort and trust
   - Gather context

3. **Core Questions (30-40 min)**
   - Main research objectives
   - Open-ended questions
   - Probing and follow-ups

4. **Closing (5 min)**
   - "Anything else you'd like to share?"
   - Thank participant
   - Explain next steps

**Example Discussion Guide: Productivity App**

**Opening:**
- "Thanks for joining today. I'm [name], and I'm researching how people manage their tasks and projects. This will take about 45 minutes. I'll ask you some questions about your current workflow. There are no right or wrong answers—I'm just interested in your experience. Is it okay if I record this for my notes?"

**Warm-Up:**
- "Tell me a bit about your role and what a typical day looks like."
- "How do you currently keep track of your tasks and projects?"

**Core Questions:**
- "Walk me through the last time you planned your week. What did that process look like?"
- "What tools or systems do you use? How did you choose them?"
- "What works well about your current approach? What's frustrating?"
- "Tell me about a time when you felt overwhelmed by your tasks. What happened?"
- "How do you decide what to work on next?"

**Closing:**
- "Is there anything else about how you manage your work that we haven't covered?"
- "Thanks so much for your time. Your insights are really helpful. I'll send you the incentive within a week."

### Conducting Interviews

**Active Listening**

**Techniques:**
- **Pause before responding:** Count to 3-5 seconds. Participants often fill silence with valuable insights.
- **Reflect back:** "So what I'm hearing is..." (confirms understanding)
- **Minimal encouragers:** "Mm-hmm," "I see," "Tell me more"
- **Avoid interrupting:** Let them finish, even if they ramble

**Non-Verbal Cues:**
- Maintain eye contact (but not staring)
- Nod to show engagement
- Lean in slightly
- Mirror their energy level

**Probing Techniques**

**1. Clarifying Probes**
- "What did you mean by [term]?"
- "Can you explain that a bit more?"
- "I want to make sure I understand..."

**2. Elaboration Probes**
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What else?"
- "Can you give me an example?"

**3. Contrast Probes**
- "How does that compare to [alternative]?"
- "What's different about [scenario]?"
- "How has that changed over time?"

**4. Specific Example Probes**
- "Walk me through the last time that happened."
- "Describe a specific instance."
- "What did you do step-by-step?"

**5. Emotion Probes**
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "What was going through your mind?"
- "What was that experience like?"

**The Five Whys**

Dig deeper to uncover root causes:

**Example:**
```
User: "I stopped using the app."
Why? "It wasn't helpful."
Why? "It didn't show me what I needed."
Why? "The dashboard was cluttered."
Why? "There were too many metrics I didn't care about."
Why? "I couldn't customize what I saw."

Insight: Need customizable dashboard.
```

**Caution:** Don't interrogate. Make it conversational.

**Avoiding Bias**

**Leading Questions:**
- ❌ "Don't you think this feature is useful?"
- ✓ "How do you feel about this feature?"

**Loaded Questions:**
- ❌ "Why do you love our product?"
- ✓ "What's your experience with our product been like?"

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Actively seek disconfirming evidence
- Ask "What doesn't work?" not just "What works?"
- Don't cherry-pick quotes that support your hypothesis

**Recency Bias:**
- Review all data, not just recent interviews
- Look for patterns across all participants

**Handling Difficult Situations**

**Participant is Quiet/Monosyllabic:**
- Use more specific prompts
- Ask for examples and stories
- Try different question formats
- Accept that some people are less talkative

**Participant Rambles:**
- Politely redirect: "That's interesting. Going back to..."
- Use time checks: "We have about 20 minutes left, so I want to make sure we cover..."
- Ask more specific questions

**Participant is Overly Critical:**
- Acknowledge their frustration
- Probe for specifics: "What exactly was frustrating?"
- Ask about positive experiences too
- Don't get defensive

**Technical Issues:**
- Have backup plan (phone call if video fails)
- Test tech before interview
- Start recording immediately
- Save recordings in multiple places

### Post-Interview Process

**Debrief Notes**

Immediately after interview (5-10 min):
- Key takeaways
- Surprising findings
- Quotes to remember
- Questions for follow-up
- Reflections on interview quality

**Transcription**

**Options:**
- Automated: Otter.ai, Rev, Descript (fast, affordable)
- Manual: Time-consuming but thorough
- Hybrid: Auto-transcribe, then clean up

**Transcription Tips:**
- Include timestamps
- Note non-verbal cues [laughs], [pauses]
- Mark unclear sections [inaudible]
- Anonymize PII

**Analysis Preparation**

- Organize transcripts in consistent format
- Tag with participant metadata
- Create analysis plan
- Schedule synthesis session
