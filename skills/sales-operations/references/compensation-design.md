# Sales Compensation Design

Frameworks and methodologies for designing effective sales compensation plans that motivate performance, align with business goals, and ensure fairness across territories and roles.

---

## Compensation Design Fundamentals

### Purpose and Objectives

Sales compensation plans serve multiple strategic purposes:

1. **Motivate Desired Behaviors**: Incentivize activities and outcomes aligned with business priorities
2. **Attract and Retain Talent**: Offer competitive, market-aligned compensation
3. **Drive Revenue Growth**: Focus sales efforts on high-value opportunities
4. **Ensure Fairness**: Provide equitable earning potential across territories and roles
5. **Control Costs**: Manage sales expense as a percentage of revenue

### Key Principles

**Alignment**: Compensation should directly support strategic business objectives
- If goal is new customer acquisition, weight new business heavily
- If goal is expansion, incentivize upsell and cross-sell
- If goal is retention, reward renewals and customer success metrics

**Simplicity**: Reps should easily understand how they earn
- Avoid overly complex formulas or multiple modifiers
- Provide clear examples and calculators
- Enable reps to self-calculate expected earnings

**Achievability**: Targets should be challenging but attainable
- Target 60-80% of reps achieving quota
- Set quotas based on territory potential, not just top-down allocation
- Adjust for market conditions and territory maturity

**Competitiveness**: Total compensation should be market-competitive
- Benchmark against industry and geography
- Consider total on-target earnings (OTE), not just base
- Account for benefits, equity, and other compensation elements

**Transparency**: Reps should trust the compensation system
- Clearly communicate plan mechanics and changes
- Provide regular performance updates and earnings statements
- Establish fair dispute resolution processes

## Compensation Plan Components

### Base Salary

**Purpose**: Provide stable, predictable income to cover living expenses

**Typical Range by Role:**
- **Inside Sales/SDR**: 60-70% of OTE
- **Mid-Market AE**: 50-60% of OTE
- **Enterprise AE**: 40-50% of OTE
- **Sales Management**: 70-80% of OTE

**Considerations:**
- Higher base = more stability, less risk, potentially lower motivation
- Lower base = higher upside, more risk, stronger performance incentive
- Market norms vary by industry, geography, and company stage

### Variable Compensation (Commission/Bonus)

**Purpose**: Reward achievement of specific goals and metrics

**Types:**

1. **Commission**: Percentage of revenue or margin
   - Uncapped or capped
   - Tiered rates (higher % at higher attainment)
   - Flat rate or accelerators

2. **Quota-Based Bonus**: Payout based on % of quota achieved
   - Threshold (minimum attainment for payout, typically 50-70%)
   - Target (100% quota achievement)
   - Excellence (above-quota performance, often 120%+)

3. **MBO/Discretionary Bonus**: Based on qualitative objectives
   - Strategic initiatives
   - Team collaboration
   - Customer satisfaction metrics

### Accelerators and Decelerators

**Accelerators**: Higher commission rates at higher performance levels
- Example: 10% commission up to 100% quota, 15% above 100%
- Purpose: Reward top performers, drive overachievement

**Decelerators**: Lower rates below target performance
- Example: 5% commission below 80% quota, 10% above 80%
- Purpose: Ensure profitability, motivate minimum performance

### SPIFs (Sales Performance Incentive Funds)

**Purpose**: Short-term incentives for specific behaviors or products

**Use Cases:**
- New product launch promotion
- End-of-quarter pipeline push
- Competitive displacement campaigns
- Specific customer segment targeting

**Best Practices:**
- Time-limited (typically 30-90 days)
- Clear, simple rules
- Meaningful payout amounts
- Aligned with strategic priorities
- Communicated broadly and frequently

### Draws and Guarantees

**Recoverable Draw**: Advance against future commissions
- Rep must "pay back" through future earnings
- Common for new hires during ramp period

**Non-Recoverable Draw**: Guaranteed minimum earnings
- Not required to be paid back
- Provides income stability during ramp or territory building

**Typical Structure:**
- Month 1-3: 100% of variable at target
- Month 4-6: 75% of variable at target
- Month 7-9: 50% of variable at target
- Month 10+: Full plan in effect

## Compensation Plan Types

### 1. Straight Commission

**Structure**: Earnings based solely on sales performance, no base salary

**Formula**: Earnings = Sales × Commission Rate

**Best For:**
- Transactional, high-volume sales
- Independent contractors or agents
- Highly entrepreneurial sales cultures

**Advantages:**
- Direct pay-for-performance alignment
- Variable cost structure (only pay when revenue generated)
- Attracts self-motivated, high-performing reps

**Disadvantages:**
- High income volatility for reps
- Difficult to attract talent in competitive markets
- May encourage short-term thinking over customer relationships

### 2. Base + Commission

**Structure**: Fixed base salary plus commission on sales

**Formula**: Earnings = Base Salary + (Sales × Commission Rate)

**Best For:**
- Most B2B sales roles
- Moderate to long sales cycles
- Balance of new business and account management

**Advantages:**
- Income stability with performance upside
- Easier to attract and retain talent
- Flexibility to adjust base/variable mix by role

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher fixed costs for company
- Requires careful quota setting to manage costs
- Can be complex with multiple commission rates or products

### 3. Base + Quota Bonus

**Structure**: Fixed base salary plus bonus for achieving quota milestones

**Formula**: Earnings = Base Salary + (Quota Attainment % × Target Bonus)

**Example Payout Table:**
| Quota Attainment | Payout % of Target Bonus |
|------------------|-------------------------|
| <50% | 0% |
| 50-74% | 25% |
| 75-99% | 50% |
| 100-119% | 100% |
| 120-149% | 150% |
| 150%+ | 200% |

**Best For:**
- Enterprise sales with long cycles
- Team-based selling environments
- Roles with multiple objectives beyond revenue

**Advantages:**
- Predictable payout at target performance
- Easy to understand and communicate
- Can incorporate multiple metrics (revenue, retention, satisfaction)

**Disadvantages:**
- Less direct connection between individual deals and earnings
- Requires accurate quota setting
- May not motivate incremental effort between tiers

### 4. Tiered Commission

**Structure**: Commission rate increases at higher performance levels

**Example:**
- 0-80% of quota: 8% commission
- 81-100% of quota: 10% commission
- 101-120% of quota: 12% commission
- 121%+ of quota: 15% commission

**Best For:**
- Driving overachievement
- Competitive sales environments
- Uncapped earning potential cultures

**Advantages:**
- Strong motivation to exceed quota
- Rewards top performers disproportionately
- Can drive significant overachievement

**Disadvantages:**
- Can be expensive at high attainment levels
- May encourage sandbagging or deal timing manipulation
- Complexity in calculation and communication

### 5. Gross Margin Commission

**Structure**: Commission based on profit margin rather than revenue

**Formula**: Earnings = Base Salary + (Gross Margin × Commission Rate)

**Best For:**
- Products with variable costs or discounting
- Encouraging profitable deal structures
- Preventing excessive discounting

**Advantages:**
- Aligns rep incentives with company profitability
- Discourages heavy discounting
- Rewards value selling over price competition

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires transparent margin visibility
- Can be complex to calculate and explain
- May limit rep flexibility in negotiations

### 6. Team-Based Compensation

**Structure**: Portion of compensation based on team or company performance

**Formula**: Earnings = Base + Individual Variable + Team Variable

**Best For:**
- Collaborative selling environments (SDR + AE + CSM)
- Overlay specialists (solutions engineers, industry experts)
- Encouraging knowledge sharing and teamwork

**Advantages:**
- Promotes collaboration over competition
- Aligns individual and team success
- Reduces internal conflict and politics

**Disadvantages:**
- Individual contribution may feel diluted
- High performers may resent carrying low performers
- Requires strong team culture and trust

## Quota Setting Methodologies

### Top-Down Quota Allocation

**Process:**
1. Start with company revenue target
2. Subtract expected revenue from existing customers (renewals, expansions)
3. Divide remaining new business target by number of reps
4. Adjust for ramp time, attrition, and productivity assumptions

**Formula**: Individual Quota = (Total Target - Existing Revenue) / (# Reps × Productivity Factor)

**Advantages:**
- Ensures quotas sum to company target
- Simple and straightforward
- Aligns sales capacity with business plan

**Disadvantages:**
- Doesn't account for territory differences
- May set unachievable quotas in weak territories
- Can demotivate reps in challenging markets

### Bottom-Up Quota Allocation

**Process:**
1. Analyze territory potential (TAM, historical performance, pipeline)
2. Set quota based on achievable target for each territory
3. Sum individual quotas to determine total sales capacity
4. Adjust territories or headcount if total doesn't meet company target

**Formula**: Individual Quota = Territory Potential × Target Attainment Rate

**Advantages:**
- Accounts for territory differences
- More achievable and fair quotas
- Higher rep morale and motivation

**Disadvantages:**
- May not sum to company revenue target
- Requires detailed territory analysis
- Can be time-consuming and data-intensive

### Hybrid Quota Allocation

**Process:**
1. Start with top-down company target
2. Analyze territory potential and historical performance
3. Weight quotas based on territory characteristics
4. Iterate to balance fairness and company target

**Formula**: Individual Quota = Base Quota × Territory Weight Factor

**Territory Weight Factors:**
- Market size and TAM
- Historical performance and trends
- Competitive intensity
- Territory maturity (new vs. established)
- Rep experience and tenure

**Advantages:**
- Balances company needs and territory reality
- More equitable than pure top-down
- Maintains accountability to company target

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires sophisticated analysis
- Can be perceived as subjective
- Needs clear communication of weighting methodology

### Historical Performance + Growth

**Process:**
1. Calculate prior year actual performance by territory
2. Apply growth factor based on company targets and market conditions
3. Adjust for known changes (new products, market shifts, competition)

**Formula**: Individual Quota = Prior Year Actual × (1 + Growth Rate) × Adjustment Factor

**Advantages:**
- Grounded in proven performance
- Accounts for territory-specific trends
- Easier to defend and explain

**Disadvantages:**
- Penalizes high performers (higher base = higher quota)
- Rewards low performers (lower base = lower quota)
- May perpetuate historical inequities

## Compensation Plan Design Process

### Step 1: Define Strategic Objectives

Identify what the compensation plan should drive:
- New customer acquisition
- Expansion revenue from existing customers
- Product mix or strategic product adoption
- Market share in specific segments
- Customer retention and satisfaction
- Profitability and margin improvement

### Step 2: Benchmark Market Compensation

Research competitive compensation levels:
- Industry compensation surveys (e.g., SaaS, manufacturing, services)
- Geographic market data
- Role-specific benchmarks (SDR, AE, AM, etc.)
- Total OTE, base/variable split, and typical attainment rates

**Sources:**
- Compensation consulting firms (Radford, Mercer, Comptryx)
- Industry associations and peer networks
- Recruiting firms and talent market data
- Public company proxy statements

### Step 3: Determine Compensation Philosophy

Decide where to position compensation:
- **Lead the market** (75th+ percentile): Attract top talent, highly competitive
- **Match the market** (50th percentile): Competitive, balanced approach
- **Lag the market** (25th percentile): Cost-conscious, rely on other factors (culture, equity, mission)

**Consider:**
- Company stage and funding
- Talent market competitiveness
- Employer brand and non-monetary value proposition
- Equity compensation and other benefits

### Step 4: Design Plan Structure

Select plan type and components:
- Base salary amount and percentage of OTE
- Variable compensation type (commission, bonus, or hybrid)
- Performance metrics and weights
- Payout curves (linear, tiered, accelerated)
- Caps, thresholds, and cliffs

### Step 5: Set Quotas and Targets

Determine individual and team quotas:
- Use appropriate quota-setting methodology
- Ensure quotas are achievable (target 60-80% attainment)
- Account for territory differences
- Align total quotas with company revenue plan

### Step 6: Model Financial Impact

Project costs and outcomes under various scenarios:
- **Scenario 1**: 100% of reps at 100% quota (target case)
- **Scenario 2**: Historical attainment distribution (realistic case)
- **Scenario 3**: 80% of reps at 80% quota (conservative case)
- **Scenario 4**: Top 20% at 150% quota (upside case)

**Calculate:**
- Total compensation expense
- Compensation as % of revenue
- Cost per dollar of revenue
- Comparison to budget and prior year

### Step 7: Test and Validate

Review plan with stakeholders:
- **Sales Leadership**: Alignment with strategy, achievability
- **Finance**: Cost implications, budget fit
- **HR**: Market competitiveness, equity, compliance
- **Sales Reps** (sample): Clarity, motivation, fairness

**Validation Checks:**
- Can reps easily calculate their earnings?
- Does the plan motivate desired behaviors?
- Are quotas achievable but challenging?
- Is total cost within budget?
- Does the plan comply with legal requirements?

### Step 8: Communicate and Launch

Roll out the plan effectively:
- **Timing**: Announce 30-60 days before effective date
- **Materials**: Plan documents, examples, FAQs, calculators
- **Training**: Manager training, rep Q&A sessions
- **Support**: Dedicated resources for questions during transition

**Communication Elements:**
- Rationale for plan design and any changes
- Clear explanation of mechanics and calculations
- Examples showing earnings at various attainment levels
- Timeline and effective dates
- Resources for questions and support

### Step 9: Monitor and Optimize

Track plan performance and make adjustments:
- **Monthly**: Attainment rates, payout amounts, cost tracking
- **Quarterly**: Plan effectiveness, unintended consequences, rep feedback
- **Annually**: Comprehensive review and redesign for next year

**Red Flags:**
- Very high or very low attainment rates (outside 60-80% range)
- Unexpected cost overruns or savings
- Rep complaints about fairness or clarity
- Behaviors misaligned with strategic goals
- High turnover or difficulty recruiting

## Special Considerations

### Multi-Product Compensation

When selling multiple products with different strategic priorities:

**Weighted Quota Approach:**
- Assign different weights to different products
- Example: Strategic new product = 1.5x credit, mature product = 1.0x credit
- Total quota measured in "quota credits" rather than dollars

**Separate Quotas:**
- Set distinct quotas for each product line
- Require minimum attainment in each to earn full bonus
- Example: Must achieve 80% in both Product A and Product B to earn 100% bonus

**SPIFs for Strategic Products:**
- Base plan on total revenue
- Add temporary SPIFs to drive adoption of new or strategic products

### New Hire Ramp Plans

Provide income stability during onboarding and ramp:

**Typical Ramp Schedule:**
- **Months 1-3**: 100% of target variable (guarantee or draw)
- **Months 4-6**: 75% of target variable + 25% at-risk
- **Months 7-9**: 50% of target variable + 50% at-risk
- **Months 10-12**: 25% of target variable + 75% at-risk
- **Month 13+**: Full plan, 100% at-risk

**Quota Ramp:**
- Reduce quota proportionally during ramp period
- Example: 50% quota in months 4-6, 75% in months 7-9, 100% in month 10+

### Sales Management Compensation

Align manager incentives with team performance:

**Typical Structure:**
- 70-80% base salary
- 20-30% variable compensation

**Variable Components:**
- 60-80% based on team quota attainment
- 20-40% based on individual objectives (hiring, retention, development)

**Considerations:**
- Should managers have individual quotas? (Generally no for frontline managers)
- How to handle team changes and turnover?
- Balance team results with individual rep development

### Compensation for Overlay Roles

Design plans for specialists who support but don't own deals:

**Solutions Engineers / Sales Engineers:**
- Lower variable % (20-30% of OTE)
- Based on team or supported AE performance
- May include technical win rate or customer satisfaction metrics

**Industry or Product Specialists:**
- Overlay credit (e.g., 10-20% of deal value for deals they support)
- Team-based component
- May include adoption or usage metrics for their specialty area

**Customer Success / Account Management:**
- Base compensation higher (60-70% of OTE)
- Variable based on retention, expansion, and satisfaction metrics
- May include net revenue retention (NRR) or customer health scores
