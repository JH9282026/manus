# Microphone Techniques

Advanced microphone selection, placement, and recording techniques for capturing professional-quality audio across various sources.

---

## Microphone Types and Characteristics

### Dynamic Microphones

**Operating Principle:**
Electromagnetic induction using a diaphragm attached to a coil moving within a magnetic field.

**Characteristics:**
- Rugged and durable construction
- High SPL (Sound Pressure Level) handling
- Minimal sensitivity to room acoustics
- No external power required
- Less sensitive to transient detail

**Common Applications:**
- Live vocal performance
- Drum kit (kick, snare, toms)
- Guitar and bass amplifiers
- High-volume brass instruments
- Broadcast and podcasting

**Popular Models:**
- Shure SM57: Industry standard for instruments and snare
- Shure SM58: Vocal performance standard
- Sennheiser MD421: Versatile for drums and guitar amps
- Electro-Voice RE20: Broadcast and bass instruments

---

### Condenser Microphones

**Operating Principle:**
Capacitance variation between diaphragm and backplate, requiring phantom power (+48V).

**Characteristics:**
- High sensitivity and transient response
- Extended frequency response
- Lower noise floor
- Captures fine detail and nuance
- Requires phantom power
- More fragile than dynamic mics

**Large-Diaphragm Condensers (LDC):**
- Diaphragm ≥ 1 inch
- Warm, full sound
- Excellent for vocals and acoustic instruments
- Examples: Neumann U87, AKG C414, Audio-Technica AT2020, Rode NT1

**Small-Diaphragm Condensers (SDC):**
- Diaphragm < 1 inch
- Accurate, detailed sound
- Better transient response
- Ideal for acoustic instruments, cymbals, room ambience
- Examples: DPA 4011, Neumann KM184, AKG C451

---

### Ribbon Microphones

**Operating Principle:**
Thin metal ribbon suspended in magnetic field, moving in response to sound waves.

**Characteristics:**
- Smooth, natural high-frequency response
- Figure-8 polar pattern (bidirectional)
- Fragile ribbon element
- Lower output level
- Vintage, warm character

**Applications:**
- Vocals (especially for bright sources)
- String sections and orchestral recording
- Guitar amplifiers (smooth high end)
- Room ambience and stereo techniques

**Popular Models:**
- Royer R-121: Guitar amps and brass
- AEA R84: Vintage character
- sE Electronics VR1/VR2: Modern, durable ribbons

---

## Polar Patterns

### Cardioid

**Pickup Pattern:**
Heart-shaped, most sensitive to front, rejects rear.

**Characteristics:**
- Maximum rejection at 180° (directly behind)
- Proximity effect (bass boost when close)
- Good isolation from room and stage noise

**Applications:**
- Studio vocal recording
- Live sound reinforcement
- Instrument close-miking
- Situations requiring rear rejection

---

### Omnidirectional

**Pickup Pattern:**
Equal sensitivity in all directions (360°).

**Characteristics:**
- No proximity effect
- Natural, accurate frequency response
- Picks up room ambience equally
- No off-axis coloration

**Applications:**
- Room ambience and natural reverb
- Orchestral and ensemble recording
- Measurement and analysis
- Situations where room sound is desirable

---

### Figure-8 (Bidirectional)

**Pickup Pattern:**
Equal sensitivity front and rear, maximum rejection at sides (90° and 270°).

**Characteristics:**
- Rejects sound from sides
- Strong proximity effect
- Natural for stereo techniques (Blumlein)
- Common in ribbon microphones

**Applications:**
- Stereo recording techniques
- Duet recording (two sources facing each other)
- Room ambience with side rejection
- Mid-Side (M-S) stereo recording

---

### Supercardioid and Hypercardioid

**Pickup Pattern:**
Narrower front lobe than cardioid, some rear sensitivity.

**Characteristics:**
- Tighter front pickup than cardioid
- Maximum rejection at 120-150° (sides/rear)
- Useful for focused pickup in noisy environments

**Applications:**
- Live sound (focused pickup, reduced feedback)
- Film/video boom microphones
- Instrument isolation in ensemble recording

## Vocal Recording Techniques

### Microphone Selection

**Large-Diaphragm Condenser (Most Common):**
- Warm, full-bodied sound
- Captures vocal detail and presence
- Industry standard for studio vocals

**Dynamic Microphone:**
- Aggressive, present sound
- Handles high SPL (screaming, shouting)
- Reduces room noise in untreated spaces
- Popular in rock, metal, hip-hop

**Ribbon Microphone:**
- Smooth, vintage character
- Tames harsh or bright vocals
- Requires careful handling and positioning

### Placement and Distance

**Standard Distance: 6-12 inches**
- Balanced tone and room rejection
- Moderate proximity effect
- Allows natural performance movement

**Close (2-4 inches):**
- Maximum proximity effect (bass boost)
- Intimate, breathy sound
- Increased plosive risk
- Requires pop filter

**Distant (12-24 inches):**
- Reduced proximity effect
- More room ambience
- Natural, open sound
- Less plosive issues

### Angle and Positioning

**On-Axis (Directly Facing):**
- Maximum presence and clarity
- Increased plosive sensitivity
- Brightest tone

**Off-Axis (Angled 15-30°):**
- Reduced plosives
- Slightly warmer tone
- Maintained clarity
- Recommended starting point

**Above or Below:**
- Angling mic down from above or up from below
- Further reduces plosives
- Alters tonal balance

### Accessories

**Pop Filter:**
- Reduces plosive bursts (p, b, t sounds)
- Position 2-4 inches from microphone
- Fabric or metal mesh options

**Shock Mount:**
- Isolates microphone from vibrations
- Reduces handling noise and floor rumble
- Essential for condenser microphones

**Reflection Filter:**
- Absorbs reflections behind microphone
- Improves isolation in untreated rooms
- Not a substitute for proper acoustic treatment

## Instrument Recording Techniques

### Acoustic Guitar

**Single Microphone:**
- Position: 12th fret, 6-12 inches away, angled toward soundhole
- Closer to soundhole: More bass and body
- Closer to 12th fret: Balanced, clear tone
- Closer to bridge: Brighter, more attack

**Stereo Techniques:**
- **X-Y Pair:** Two cardioid mics at 90-135° angle, capsules close together
- **Spaced Pair:** Two mics 12-24 inches apart, aimed at different parts of guitar
- **Mid-Side:** One cardioid (mid) and one figure-8 (side) for adjustable width

**Microphone Choice:**
- Small-diaphragm condenser: Accurate, detailed
- Large-diaphragm condenser: Warmer, fuller
- Ribbon: Smooth, vintage character

---

### Electric Guitar Amplifier

**Close Miking:**
- Position: 1-4 inches from speaker cone
- On-axis (center of cone): Bright, aggressive
- Off-axis (edge of cone): Warmer, less harsh
- Angled: Balanced tone

**Microphone Choice:**
- Shure SM57: Industry standard, versatile
- Sennheiser MD421: Fuller, more bass
- Royer R-121 (ribbon): Smooth, natural

**Dual Miking:**
- Combine close mic (SM57) with room mic (condenser or ribbon)
- Blend for depth and ambience
- Phase-align for coherent sound

**DI + Amp:**
- Record direct signal and amplifier simultaneously
- Blend for fullness and clarity
- DI provides clean signal for reamping

---

### Bass Guitar and Bass Amplifier

**Direct Input (DI):**
- Clean, consistent low-end
- Essential for modern bass recording
- Active or passive DI box

**Amplifier Miking:**
- Dynamic mic (Electro-Voice RE20, AKG D112) on speaker
- Position near center for attack, edge for warmth
- 2-6 inches from speaker

**Hybrid Approach:**
- Blend DI (clarity, definition) with amp (character, warmth)
- High-pass filter on amp mic to avoid low-end conflict

---

### Drum Kit

**Kick Drum:**
- **Inside:** Dynamic mic (AKG D112, Shure Beta 52A) near beater for attack
- **Outside:** Large-diaphragm condenser or dynamic for body and tone
- **Dual Miking:** Blend inside (attack) and outside (body)

**Snare Drum:**
- **Top:** Dynamic mic (Shure SM57) angled toward center, 1-2 inches above rim
- **Bottom:** Small-diaphragm condenser for snare wire detail (phase-invert)

**Toms:**
- Dynamic mics (Sennheiser MD421, Shure SM57) angled toward center
- 1-3 inches above drumhead

**Overheads:**
- **Stereo Pair:** Capture cymbals and overall kit balance
- **X-Y:** Mono-compatible, focused image
- **Spaced Pair:** Wide stereo image, more room sound
- **ORTF:** 17cm spacing, 110° angle, balanced width and focus
- Small-diaphragm condensers preferred

**Room Mics:**
- Large-diaphragm condensers or ribbons
- 3-10 feet from kit
- Capture ambience and natural reverb
- Blend with close mics for depth

---

### Piano

**Grand Piano:**
- **Lid Open:** Stereo pair above strings
  - **X-Y:** 6-12 inches above strings, over hammers
  - **Spaced Pair:** One mic over bass strings, one over treble
- **Lid Closed:** Mics near lid edge or underneath

**Upright Piano:**
- Remove front panel if possible
- Stereo pair aimed at soundboard
- Experiment with height and distance

**Microphone Choice:**
- Small-diaphragm condensers: Accurate, detailed
- Large-diaphragm condensers: Warmer, fuller
- Ribbon: Vintage, smooth character

---

## Stereo Recording Techniques

### X-Y (Coincident Pair)

**Setup:**
- Two cardioid mics, capsules as close as possible
- Angled 90-135° apart
- Aimed at sound source

**Characteristics:**
- Excellent mono compatibility
- Focused stereo image
- Minimal phase issues
- Good for close sources

---

### Spaced Pair (A-B)

**Setup:**
- Two omnidirectional or cardioid mics
- Spaced 3-10 feet apart
- Parallel or angled toward source

**Characteristics:**
- Wide stereo image
- More room ambience
- Potential phase issues in mono
- Good for large sources (orchestra, choir)

---

### ORTF

**Setup:**
- Two cardioid mics
- 17cm (6.7 inches) spacing
- 110° angle

**Characteristics:**
- Balanced width and focus
- Good mono compatibility
- Mimics human ear spacing and angle
- Versatile for many sources

---

### Mid-Side (M-S)

**Setup:**
- One cardioid mic (mid) facing source
- One figure-8 mic (side) perpendicular to source
- Requires M-S decoding (sum and difference)

**Characteristics:**
- Adjustable stereo width in post-production
- Perfect mono compatibility (use mid channel only)
- Excellent for film and broadcast
- Requires M-S decoder or DAW processing

**Decoding:**
- Left = Mid + Side
- Right = Mid - Side (inverted)
- Adjust side level for width control

---

### Blumlein Pair

**Setup:**
- Two figure-8 mics at 90° angle
- Capsules coincident
- Creates natural, spacious stereo

**Characteristics:**
- Natural room sound
- Excellent depth and ambience
- Requires good-sounding room
- Often used with ribbon microphones

## Advanced Techniques

### Phase Alignment

**Identifying Phase Issues:**
- Sum to mono and listen for thinness or cancellation
- Visual inspection of waveforms
- Phase correlation meter

**Correcting Phase:**
- Invert polarity on one channel (180° flip)
- Time-align tracks by shifting samples
- Adjust microphone positions during recording

### Proximity Effect Management

**Understanding Proximity Effect:**
- Bass boost when close to directional microphones
- Increases as distance decreases
- Absent in omnidirectional mics

**Using Proximity Effect:**
- Enhance warmth and intimacy in vocals
- Add weight to thin sources
- Create dramatic tonal shifts

**Controlling Proximity Effect:**
- Increase distance for neutral tone
- Use omnidirectional pattern
- Apply high-pass filter in post

### Multi-Microphone Phase Coherence

**3:1 Rule:**
- Distance between mics should be at least 3x the distance from each mic to its source
- Minimizes phase cancellation and comb filtering
- Critical for drum kit and ensemble recording

**Phase Checking:**
- Monitor in mono during setup
- Listen for fullness vs. thinness
- Adjust positions to maximize coherence
