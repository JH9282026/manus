# Compensation Strategy

Comprehensive guide to developing compensation philosophy, salary benchmarking, market positioning, and equity compensation design.

---

## Compensation Philosophy Development

### Purpose of Compensation Philosophy

A compensation philosophy is a formal statement that defines how an organization intends to position itself against the market and reward employees. It serves as:

- **Guiding Principle:** Framework for all compensation decisions
- **Communication Tool:** Explains approach to employees and candidates
- **Strategic Alignment:** Links compensation to business objectives
- **Decision Framework:** Provides consistency in pay decisions
- **Competitive Positioning:** Defines market stance

### Key Components

**1. Market Positioning**

Define where you position compensation relative to market:

**Lead Strategy (Above Market)**
- **Target:** 65th-75th percentile or higher
- **Rationale:** Attract and retain top talent, competitive advantage in tight markets
- **Trade-offs:** Higher costs, may not be sustainable for all roles
- **When to Use:**
  - Critical, hard-to-fill roles (specialized engineers, data scientists)
  - Competitive talent markets (tech hubs like San Francisco, New York)
  - High-growth companies needing rapid talent acquisition
  - Roles with direct revenue impact

**Match Strategy (At Market)**
- **Target:** 50th percentile (median)
- **Rationale:** Competitive without overpaying, sustainable, industry standard
- **Trade-offs:** May lose talent to higher-paying competitors
- **When to Use:**
  - Most roles in stable organizations
  - Balanced approach across functions
  - Mature companies with established employer brand
  - When combined with strong benefits and culture

**Lag Strategy (Below Market)**
- **Target:** 25th-40th percentile
- **Rationale:** Lower costs, can offset with equity, benefits, or flexibility
- **Trade-offs:** Harder to attract talent, higher turnover risk
- **When to Use:**
  - Early-stage startups (offset with generous equity)
  - Non-critical or easily filled roles
  - Organizations with strong non-cash value proposition
  - Cost-constrained environments

**Hybrid Approach (Most Common)**
- Different positioning for different role categories
- Example:
  - Engineering: 65th percentile (critical talent)
  - Sales: 50th percentile base + high variable (performance-driven)
  - Operations: 50th percentile (market standard)
  - Administrative: 40th percentile (offset with benefits)

**2. Total Rewards Mix**

Define balance of compensation components:

**Cash Compensation:**
- **Base Salary:** Fixed, predictable income
- **Variable Pay:** Bonuses, commissions tied to performance
- **Sign-On Bonuses:** One-time payment to attract candidates
- **Retention Bonuses:** Incentive to stay through critical period

**Equity Compensation:**
- **Stock Options:** Right to purchase shares at set price
- **RSUs:** Shares granted upon vesting
- **ESPPs:** Employee purchase programs at discount
- **Phantom Stock/SARs:** Cash-settled equity equivalents

**Benefits:**
- **Health & Wellness:** Medical, dental, vision, mental health
- **Retirement:** 401k match, pension
- **Time Off:** Vacation, sick, parental leave
- **Insurance:** Life, disability

**Perks:**
- **Flexibility:** Remote work, flexible hours
- **Development:** Tuition reimbursement, conferences
- **Lifestyle:** Gym memberships, commuter benefits, meals

**Example Mix by Company Stage:**

*Early-Stage Startup:*
- Base Salary: 60-70% of market (lag)
- Equity: Top quartile (generous options)
- Benefits: Basic health insurance
- Perks: Flexibility, learning opportunities

*Growth-Stage Company:*
- Base Salary: 50th percentile (match)
- Equity: Moderate (options or RSUs)
- Benefits: Comprehensive (health, 401k match, PTO)
- Perks: Professional development, wellness programs

*Public Company:*
- Base Salary: 50th-65th percentile (match to lead)
- Equity: RSUs, ESPP
- Benefits: Comprehensive and competitive
- Perks: Extensive (on-site amenities, sabbaticals)

**3. Location Approach**

**Location-Specific Benchmarks:**
- Pay varies by geographic market
- Reflects local cost of living and talent supply/demand
- **Pros:** Fair to local market, competitive in each location
- **Cons:** Complex administration, potential internal equity issues
- **Example:** Software Engineer in San Francisco: $150K, same role in Austin: $120K

**Location Multipliers:**
- Central benchmark (e.g., national median) with location adjustments
- Multipliers based on cost of living indices
- **Pros:** Simpler administration, consistent framework
- **Cons:** May not perfectly reflect local markets
- **Example:** Base benchmark $100K, San Francisco multiplier 1.5x = $150K, Austin 1.0x = $100K

**Location-Agnostic:**
- Same pay regardless of employee location
- **Pros:** Supports remote-first culture, simple, perceived as fair
- **Cons:** May overpay in low-cost areas, underpay in high-cost areas
- **Example:** All Software Engineers paid $130K regardless of location
- **Trend:** Increasingly popular for fully remote companies (GitLab, Zapier)

**4. Performance Differentiation**

How compensation varies based on performance:

**Merit Increase Budget Allocation:**
- **Top Performers (Top 10%):** 8-12% increases
- **High Performers (Next 20%):** 5-7% increases
- **Solid Performers (Middle 50%):** 3-4% increases
- **Developing Performers (Bottom 20%):** 0-2% increases

**Bonus Differentiation:**
- **Exceeds Expectations:** 150-200% of target bonus
- **Meets Expectations:** 100% of target bonus
- **Below Expectations:** 0-50% of target bonus

**Promotion Increases:**
- Typically 10-20% increase for promotion to next level
- Brings employee to appropriate range for new level

**5. Internal Equity vs. External Competitiveness**

Balance fairness within organization with market competitiveness:

**Internal Equity:**
- Employees in similar roles with similar experience paid similarly
- Transparent job leveling and salary bands
- Prevents resentment and perceived unfairness

**External Competitiveness:**
- Pay competitive with market to attract and retain talent
- May require paying new hires more than tenured employees
- Can create internal equity tensions

**Balancing Approach:**
- Regular market adjustments for underpaid employees
- Transparent communication about market changes
- Salary bands that accommodate both internal and external hires
- Periodic equity reviews and corrections

### Sample Compensation Philosophy Statement

**Example 1: Tech Startup**

"At [Company], we believe in rewarding our team members competitively while aligning their success with the company's long-term growth. Our compensation philosophy is:

- **Market Positioning:** We target the 50th percentile for base salary and the 75th percentile for total compensation (including equity) for all roles. For critical technical roles, we target the 65th percentile for base salary.

- **Total Rewards:** We offer competitive base salaries, performance-based bonuses, generous equity grants, comprehensive health benefits, and flexible work arrangements. Early employees receive larger equity grants to reflect their higher risk and impact.

- **Location:** We use location-specific benchmarks, recognizing that talent markets vary by geography. We aim to be competitive in each market where we hire.

- **Performance:** We differentiate compensation based on performance and impact. Top performers receive significantly higher merit increases and bonuses.

- **Equity:** We conduct annual pay equity audits and are committed to fair, transparent compensation practices."

**Example 2: Established Enterprise**

"Our compensation philosophy supports our mission to attract, retain, and motivate a diverse, high-performing workforce:

- **Market Positioning:** We target the 50th percentile (median) for total compensation across all roles, with flexibility to pay up to the 75th percentile for exceptional talent or critical roles.

- **Total Rewards:** We provide competitive base salaries, annual performance bonuses, RSU grants for eligible employees, industry-leading benefits (health, retirement, PTO), and extensive professional development opportunities.

- **Location:** We use location-specific salary ranges that reflect local market conditions and cost of living.

- **Performance:** Compensation increases and bonuses are tied to individual performance, team results, and company success. We recognize and reward high performers.

- **Transparency:** We publish salary ranges for all roles and conduct annual pay equity reviews to ensure fairness across gender, race, and other demographics."

## Salary Benchmarking

### Benchmarking Process

**Step 1: Define Objectives**
- What questions are you trying to answer?
  - Are we paying competitively for [specific role]?
  - Should we adjust our salary bands?
  - What should we offer this candidate?
  - Are there pay equity issues?

**Step 2: Establish Job Architecture**

**Job Families:**
- Group similar roles (Engineering, Sales, Marketing, Operations)
- Enables consistent benchmarking and career paths

**Job Levels:**
- Define clear levels within each family
- Example (Engineering): IC1 (Junior), IC2 (Mid), IC3 (Senior), IC4 (Staff), IC5 (Principal), IC6 (Distinguished)
- Example (Management): M1 (Manager), M2 (Senior Manager), M3 (Director), M4 (VP)

**Job Descriptions:**
- Clear, detailed descriptions for each role and level
- Include: responsibilities, requirements, scope, decision-making authority
- Ensures accurate matching to benchmark data

**Step 3: Identify Peer Group**

Companies you compete with for talent:

**Criteria:**
- **Industry:** Same or adjacent industries
- **Geography:** Same talent markets
- **Size:** Similar revenue, headcount, or stage
- **Stage:** Startup, growth, public company
- **Business Model:** B2B, B2C, SaaS, marketplace, etc.

**Size:** 10-20 peer companies for robust benchmarking

**Example Peer Group (Series B SaaS Company in San Francisco):**
- Other Series B-C SaaS companies in Bay Area
- Similar ARR ($10-50M)
- Similar headcount (100-300 employees)
- Competing for same talent (product, engineering, sales)

**Step 4: Select Data Sources**

**Industry Salary Surveys:**
- **Radford:** Tech industry standard, comprehensive
- **Mercer:** Broad industry coverage, global
- **Compdata:** Multiple industries, customizable
- **Pros:** High-quality, detailed, peer-specific
- **Cons:** Expensive ($5K-50K+), annual updates (can lag market)

**Government Data:**
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS):** Free, comprehensive
- **Pros:** Free, authoritative
- **Cons:** Broad categories, lags market, limited detail

**Compensation Databases:**
- **Payscale, Glassdoor, Salary.com:** Crowdsourced data
- **Levels.fyi:** Tech-focused, equity data
- **Pros:** Free or low-cost, real-time, easy access
- **Cons:** Data quality varies, self-reported, limited validation

**Consulting Firms:**
- **Korn Ferry, Willis Towers Watson, Aon:** Custom benchmarking
- **Pros:** Expert analysis, tailored to your needs
- **Cons:** Expensive, time-intensive

**Modern Platforms:**
- **Carta Total Compensation:** Real-time data, equity benchmarks
- **Ravio, Pave, Figures:** Real-time, user-friendly, tech-focused
- **Pros:** Real-time, equity data, easy to use
- **Cons:** Subscription cost, may have limited peer data

**Best Practice:** Use multiple sources for validation and comprehensive view

**Step 5: Collect and Analyze Data**

**Data Points to Collect:**
- **Base Salary:** 25th, 50th (median), 75th percentiles
- **Variable Compensation:** Target and actual bonuses, commissions
- **Equity:** Stock options, RSUs (grant value, not just number of shares)
- **Total Cash Compensation:** Base + actual variable
- **Total Compensation:** Total cash + equity value

**Segmentation:**
- **Geography:** San Francisco vs. Austin vs. Remote
- **Industry:** SaaS vs. E-commerce vs. Fintech
- **Company Size:** <100 employees vs. 100-500 vs. 500+
- **Experience:** Years of experience, education level

**Analysis:**
- Compare your current compensation to market percentiles
- Identify roles that are underpaid or overpaid
- Look for patterns (e.g., all engineers underpaid, sales overpaid)
- Consider total compensation, not just base salary

**Step 6: Establish Salary Bands**

**Salary Band Structure:**
- **Minimum:** Typically 25th percentile or 80-90% of midpoint
- **Midpoint:** Target market positioning (e.g., 50th percentile)
- **Maximum:** Typically 75th percentile or 110-120% of midpoint

**Example Salary Band:**
- **Role:** Product Manager II
- **Market Data (50th percentile):** $130,000
- **Minimum:** $110,000 (85% of midpoint)
- **Midpoint:** $130,000 (target)
- **Maximum:** $150,000 (115% of midpoint)

**Band Width:**
- **Narrow Bands (15-20% range):** Entry-level, highly standardized roles
- **Medium Bands (30-40% range):** Most professional roles
- **Wide Bands (50%+ range):** Executive, highly variable roles

**Progression Within Band:**
- New hires typically enter at minimum to midpoint
- High performers progress toward maximum over time
- Employees at maximum may need promotion to next level for further increases

**Step 7: Apply Benchmarks**

**New Hire Offers:**
- Use salary band and candidate's experience/skills
- Consider internal equity (what are similar employees paid?)
- Factor in negotiation room
- Document rationale for offer

**Market Adjustments:**
- Identify employees below minimum of salary band
- Prioritize adjustments for high performers and critical roles
- Communicate adjustments as market corrections, not performance-based

**Merit Increases:**
- Use benchmarks to inform annual merit budget
- Ensure increases keep pace with market movement
- Differentiate based on performance

**Promotion Increases:**
- Move employee to appropriate range for new level
- Typically 10-20% increase
- Ensure new salary is competitive for new role

**Step 8: Refresh Regularly**

**Frequency:**
- **Annual:** Minimum for most organizations
- **Bi-Annual:** For fast-moving markets (tech, finance)
- **Quarterly:** For critical roles in highly competitive markets

**Triggers for Ad-Hoc Benchmarking:**
- Difficulty hiring for specific role
- High turnover in specific function
- Major market shifts (e.g., pandemic remote work changes)
- Company growth or funding round

## Modern Compensation Analytics

### Limitations of Traditional Benchmarking

**Market Speed:**
- Traditional surveys published annually, lag real-time market
- Tech skills and roles evolve faster than annual surveys
- New skills command immediate premiums not reflected in data

**Pay Transparency:**
- Increasing pay transparency laws expose inconsistencies quickly
- Public salary disclosures (job postings, Glassdoor) create real-time pressure

**Regional Differences:**
- National benchmarks miss significant local variations
- Remote work complicates geographic pay strategies

**Reactive Decisions:**
- Lagging data leads to reactive compensation decisions
- Playing catch-up rather than strategic planning

### Real-Time Compensation Analytics

**Job Postings as Market Signals:**
- Analyze millions of job postings with salary ranges
- Understand how compensation is moving in real-time
- Identify emerging roles and skill premiums
- Track competitor hiring and pay strategies

**Continuous Pay Gap Analysis:**
- Real-time monitoring of salary distributions
- Proactive identification of pay gaps across demographics
- Immediate alerts when disparities emerge

**Workforce Compensation Trends:**
- Track how salary expectations evolve over time
- Identify seasonal patterns and market shifts
- Forecast future compensation needs

**Platforms:**
- **JobsPikr:** Job market data and compensation analytics
- **Carta Total Compensation:** Real-time equity and cash benchmarks
- **Pave, Ravio, Figures:** Modern compensation platforms with real-time data

## Equity Compensation Strategy

### Why Equity Matters

**Alignment:**
- Aligns employee interests with company success
- Employees benefit directly from company growth
- Encourages long-term thinking

**Cash Conservation:**
- Reduces cash compensation needs (critical for startups)
- Preserves runway and financial flexibility
- Allows hiring before revenue scales

**Retention:**
- Vesting schedules encourage employees to stay
- Unvested equity is "golden handcuffs"
- Particularly effective for high-growth companies

**Attraction:**
- Significant upside potential attracts risk-tolerant talent
- Differentiator in competitive markets
- Appeals to entrepreneurial mindset

### Equity Types Comparison

| Type | How It Works | Tax Treatment | Best For |
|------|--------------|---------------|----------|
| **Stock Options (ISOs)** | Right to buy shares at strike price | Favorable (long-term capital gains if held) | Startups, early employees |
| **Stock Options (NSOs)** | Right to buy shares at strike price | Ordinary income on spread at exercise | Contractors, advisors, late-stage |
| **RSUs** | Shares granted upon vesting | Ordinary income upon vesting | Public companies, late-stage |
| **ESPPs** | Purchase at discount via payroll | Ordinary income on discount, capital gains on appreciation | Public companies, broad-based |
| **SARs** | Cash payment equal to stock appreciation | Ordinary income | Private companies avoiding dilution |
| **Phantom Stock** | Cash payment based on stock value | Ordinary income | Private companies, executives |

### Equity Grant Sizing

**By Role and Stage:**

*Seed Stage (Pre-Product/Market Fit):*
- **Founding Team:** 5-20% each (co-founders)
- **First 10 Employees:** 0.5-2% each
- **VPs:** 1-2%
- **Directors:** 0.5-1%
- **Individual Contributors:** 0.1-0.5%

*Series A ($2-15M raised):*
- **C-Suite (hired):** 1-3%
- **VPs:** 0.5-1.5%
- **Directors:** 0.25-0.75%
- **Senior ICs:** 0.1-0.3%
- **Mid-Level ICs:** 0.05-0.15%

*Series B+ ($15M+ raised):*
- **C-Suite:** 0.5-1.5%
- **VPs:** 0.25-0.75%
- **Directors:** 0.1-0.3%
- **Senior ICs:** 0.05-0.15%
- **Mid-Level ICs:** 0.01-0.05%

*Public Company:*
- Typically RSUs, smaller % but more liquid
- **VPs:** 0.05-0.2%
- **Directors:** 0.02-0.1%
- **ICs:** 0.005-0.05%

**Factors Influencing Grant Size:**
- **Role Seniority:** Higher levels receive larger grants
- **Market Competitiveness:** Hot markets require larger grants
- **Cash Compensation:** Lower cash often offset with higher equity
- **Company Stage:** Earlier stage = larger % but higher risk
- **Individual Negotiation:** Top candidates may negotiate higher

### Vesting Schedules

**Standard 4-Year Vest with 1-Year Cliff:**
- **Year 1:** 0% vests (cliff)
- **1-Year Anniversary:** 25% vests
- **Years 2-4:** Remaining 75% vests monthly (2.08% per month)
- **Rationale:** Encourages 1-year commitment, industry standard

**Variations:**
- **Immediate Vesting:** Rare, for executives or special situations
- **3-Year Vest:** Faster, for competitive markets or senior hires
- **5-Year Vest:** Longer retention, for critical roles
- **Back-Loaded:** More vests in later years (e.g., 10/20/30/40)
- **Performance-Based:** Vests upon achieving milestones (revenue, product launch)

**Acceleration Clauses:**
- **Single-Trigger:** Vesting accelerates upon acquisition (risky for acquirer)
- **Double-Trigger:** Vesting accelerates upon acquisition + termination (common)
- **Partial Acceleration:** 50% or 1 year accelerates upon double-trigger

### Refresher Grants

**Purpose:**
- Retain employees whose initial grants have vested or lost value
- Maintain equity as retention incentive
- Reward high performers

**Timing:**
- Annual refresher grants (common at public companies)
- Ad-hoc grants for promotions, retention risk, exceptional performance
- Upon vesting of initial grant (to maintain "golden handcuffs")

**Sizing:**
- Typically smaller than initial grant (25-50% of initial)
- Based on performance, role, retention risk
- Vest over 4 years (new vesting schedule)

### Dilution Management

**Employee Equity Pool:**
- Reserve 10-20% of company for employee equity
- Replenish pool with each funding round
- Balance new hire needs with existing employee dilution

**Dilution Communication:**
- Transparently explain how funding rounds dilute ownership %
- Emphasize that smaller % of larger pie can be more valuable
- Provide regular updates on company valuation and equity value

**Example:**
- Employee granted 0.5% at Series A ($10M valuation) = $50K value
- After Series B, ownership dilutes to 0.35% but valuation is $50M
- New value: 0.35% x $50M = $175K (3.5x increase despite dilution)

### Equity Communication

**Offer Letters:**
- Clearly state: number of shares, % of company, strike price (options), vesting schedule
- Explain what equity means and potential value
- Provide scenarios (conservative, moderate, optimistic outcomes)

**Ongoing Updates:**
- Regular all-hands updates on company valuation
- Annual equity statements showing current value
- Transparency about dilution and refresher grant strategy

**Education:**
- Equity 101 sessions for new hires
- Explain difference between options and RSUs
- Tax implications and exercise strategies
- Resources: blog posts, FAQs, one-on-one sessions

## Compensation Decision-Making

### New Hire Offers

**Process:**
1. **Determine Role and Level:** Match candidate to job architecture
2. **Review Salary Band:** Identify min, midpoint, max for role
3. **Assess Candidate:** Experience, skills, market demand, current compensation
4. **Consider Internal Equity:** What are similar employees paid?
5. **Determine Offer:** Base salary, equity, bonus target, benefits
6. **Document Rationale:** Why this offer? Ensures consistency and defensibility

**Offer Components:**
- **Base Salary:** Within salary band, typically min to midpoint for new hires
- **Equity:** Based on role, level, stage (see equity grant sizing)
- **Bonus Target:** % of base salary (e.g., 10-20% for most roles, higher for sales/exec)
- **Sign-On Bonus:** One-time payment to offset lost bonus, relocation, or sweeten offer
- **Benefits:** Standard package (health, 401k, PTO)
- **Start Date and Other Terms:** Relocation assistance, remote work, etc.

**Negotiation:**
- Anticipate negotiation, leave room in initial offer
- Understand candidate's priorities (cash vs. equity, flexibility, title)
- Be prepared to justify offer with market data
- Know your walk-away point
- Document final agreed terms

### Merit Increases

**Annual Merit Process:**
1. **Set Budget:** Determine overall merit increase budget (e.g., 3-5% of payroll)
2. **Performance Ratings:** Ensure all employees have current performance ratings
3. **Allocate Budget:** Differentiate based on performance
   - Top Performers: 8-12%
   - High Performers: 5-7%
   - Solid Performers: 3-4%
   - Developing Performers: 0-2%
4. **Manager Recommendations:** Managers propose increases for their teams
5. **Calibration:** HR and leadership review for consistency and equity
6. **Approval:** Final approval by leadership
7. **Communication:** Managers communicate increases to employees

**Considerations:**
- **Position in Range:** Employees at minimum get larger increases than those at maximum
- **Market Movement:** Ensure increases keep pace with market (benchmark data)
- **Internal Equity:** Avoid creating pay disparities
- **Promotion vs. Merit:** Promotions typically 10-20%, separate from merit

### Promotions

**Promotion Increase:**
- **Typical Range:** 10-20% increase
- **Factors:** Size of level jump, current position in range, market data for new level
- **Goal:** Bring employee to appropriate range for new level (typically min to midpoint)

**Process:**
1. **Promotion Criteria:** Clear criteria for each level (skills, scope, impact)
2. **Nomination:** Manager nominates employee for promotion
3. **Review:** Calibration across organization to ensure consistency
4. **Approval:** Leadership approves promotions
5. **Compensation Adjustment:** Determine new salary and equity (if applicable)
6. **Communication:** Manager communicates promotion and new compensation

### Market Adjustments

**When to Make Market Adjustments:**
- Employee below minimum of salary band
- Market has moved significantly since last benchmarking
- Retention risk due to below-market compensation
- Pay equity audit reveals disparities

**Process:**
1. **Identify Underpaid Employees:** Compare current pay to updated salary bands
2. **Prioritize:** High performers, critical roles, retention risks
3. **Determine Adjustment:** Bring to minimum of band or higher based on performance
4. **Budget:** Ensure budget available (may be separate from merit budget)
5. **Communication:** Frame as market correction, not performance-based

**Communication Example:**
"We've completed our annual compensation benchmarking and identified that your current salary is below market for your role and experience. Effective [date], your salary will increase from $X to $Y to align with market rates. This adjustment is separate from your annual merit increase and reflects our commitment to competitive, equitable compensation."

### Counter-Offers

**When Employee Receives Outside Offer:**
1. **Understand Situation:** Why are they leaving? Is it compensation, role, culture, manager?
2. **Assess Value:** Is this employee critical? High performer? Difficult to replace?
3. **Review Compensation:** Are they underpaid relative to market and internal equity?
4. **Consider Counter-Offer:** If retention is critical and compensation is root cause
5. **Make Decision:** Counter-offer, let them go, or address non-compensation issues

**Counter-Offer Guidelines:**
- Only counter if you would have proactively given raise/promotion
- Don't counter if performance or fit issues exist
- Address root cause (if it's manager or role, compensation won't fix)
- Be aware: employees who accept counter-offers often leave within 6-12 months
- Consider impact on team morale and internal equity

**Counter-Offer Components:**
- Salary increase to match or exceed outside offer
- Promotion if warranted
- Equity refresher grant
- Bonus or retention bonus
- Role or responsibility changes

## Pay Equity and Compliance

### Pay Equity Audits

**Objective:** Identify and remediate unjustified pay disparities across protected classes (gender, race, ethnicity, age, etc.)

**Process:**
1. **Data Collection:**
   - Compensation (base, bonus, equity)
   - Demographics (gender, race, ethnicity)
   - Job level, tenure, performance ratings
   - Location, department

2. **Statistical Analysis:**
   - Regression analysis controlling for legitimate factors (level, tenure, performance)
   - Identify unexplained pay gaps
   - Segment by department, level, location

3. **Investigation:**
   - Understand root causes of disparities
   - Historical inequities, biased hiring/promotion, inconsistent pay practices

4. **Remediation:**
   - Adjust compensation to close unjustified gaps
   - Prioritize largest gaps and most affected groups
   - Budget for adjustments (separate from merit budget)

5. **Prevention:**
   - Implement controls to prevent future disparities
   - Standardized salary bands, offer approval processes
   - Manager training on bias in compensation decisions

6. **Monitoring:**
   - Ongoing tracking (quarterly or annually)
   - Early detection of emerging gaps

**Target:** <5% unexplained variance across protected classes

**Frequency:** Annually or bi-annually

**Legal Considerations:**
- Consult legal counsel before conducting audit
- Privilege analysis to protect from discovery in litigation
- Document remediation efforts

### Pay Transparency

**Salary Ranges in Job Postings:**
- **Required:** Many states/cities (California, Colorado, New York City, Washington)
- **Best Practice:** Even where not required, builds trust and attracts candidates
- **Format:** "$100,000 - $140,000 base salary" or "$100,000 - $140,000 total compensation"

**Internal Transparency:**
- Share salary ranges with current employees
- Explain how ranges are determined (market data, job evaluation)
- Provide resources for employees to understand their pay

**Compensation Communication:**
- Annual total rewards statements
- Regular compensation reviews with managers
- Clear explanation of how compensation decisions are made
- FAQs and resources on compensation philosophy

**Manager Training:**
- Train managers to discuss compensation confidently
- Provide talking points and scripts
- Role-play difficult conversations
- Emphasize transparency and honesty

### Legal Compliance

**Equal Pay Act:**
- Prohibits pay discrimination based on sex for substantially equal work
- Exceptions: seniority, merit, quantity/quality of production, factors other than sex

**Title VII (Civil Rights Act):**
- Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin
- Applies to all compensation decisions

**Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA):**
- Prohibits age discrimination (40+)

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA):**
- Prohibits disability discrimination

**State and Local Laws:**
- Often more stringent than federal (e.g., California Fair Pay Act)
- Pay transparency requirements
- Salary history bans (can't ask about prior compensation)

**Best Practices:**
- Conduct regular pay equity audits
- Document compensation decisions with objective criteria
- Train managers on legal requirements and bias
- Standardize processes (salary bands, offer approvals)
- Consult legal counsel on compliance
