# Music Theory Fundamentals

Comprehensive guide to core music theory concepts.

## The Musical Alphabet and Pitch

### Note Names and Octaves

**Natural Notes** — A, B, C, D, E, F, G (white keys on piano)

**Accidentals** — Sharps (#) raise pitch by half step, flats (♭) lower pitch by half step

**Enharmonic Equivalents** — Different names for same pitch (C# = D♭, F# = G♭)

**Octave** — Distance of 12 half steps; notes repeat at higher/lower pitches

**Scientific Pitch Notation** — System using numbers to indicate octave (C4 = middle C, A4 = 440 Hz)

### Half Steps and Whole Steps

**Half Step (Semitone)** — Smallest interval in Western music; adjacent keys on piano

**Whole Step (Tone)** — Two half steps; skip one key on piano

**Chromatic Scale** — All 12 notes in an octave using only half steps

## Intervals

### Interval Quality and Number

**Interval Number** — Count letter names from bottom to top note (C to E = 3rd)

**Interval Quality** — Describes exact size: perfect, major, minor, augmented, diminished

### Perfect Intervals

**Perfect Unison (P1)** — Same note (0 half steps)

**Perfect Fourth (P4)** — 5 half steps (C to F)

**Perfect Fifth (P5)** — 7 half steps (C to G)

**Perfect Octave (P8)** — 12 half steps (C to C)

### Major and Minor Intervals

**Major Second (M2)** — 2 half steps (C to D)

**Minor Second (m2)** — 1 half step (C to D♭)

**Major Third (M3)** — 4 half steps (C to E)

**Minor Third (m3)** — 3 half steps (C to E♭)

**Major Sixth (M6)** — 9 half steps (C to A)

**Minor Sixth (m6)** — 8 half steps (C to A♭)

**Major Seventh (M7)** — 11 half steps (C to B)

**Minor Seventh (m7)** — 10 half steps (C to B♭)

### Augmented and Diminished Intervals

**Augmented** — One half step larger than major or perfect

**Diminished** — One half step smaller than minor or perfect

**Tritone** — Augmented fourth or diminished fifth (6 half steps); highly dissonant

### Interval Inversion

**Inversion Rule** — Interval number + inverted interval number = 9

**Quality Changes** — Major becomes minor, augmented becomes diminished, perfect stays perfect

**Example** — Major 3rd (C-E) inverts to minor 6th (E-C)

## Scales and Modes

### Major Scale Construction

**Formula** — W-W-H-W-W-W-H (W = whole step, H = half step)

**C Major Scale** — C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C (no sharps or flats)

**Scale Degrees** — Tonic (1), Supertonic (2), Mediant (3), Subdominant (4), Dominant (5), Submediant (6), Leading Tone (7)

**Key Signatures** — Sharps or flats at beginning of staff indicating key

### Minor Scales

**Natural Minor** — W-H-W-W-H-W-W (relative to major scale starting on 6th degree)

**Harmonic Minor** — Natural minor with raised 7th degree (creates leading tone)

**Melodic Minor** — Raised 6th and 7th ascending, natural minor descending

**Relative Minor** — Shares key signature with major scale (A minor = C major)

**Parallel Minor** — Same tonic as major scale (C minor vs. C major)

### Church Modes

**Ionian** — Major scale (W-W-H-W-W-W-H)

**Dorian** — Minor scale with raised 6th (W-H-W-W-W-H-W)

**Phrygian** — Minor scale with lowered 2nd (H-W-W-W-H-W-W)

**Lydian** — Major scale with raised 4th (W-W-W-H-W-W-H)

**Mixolydian** — Major scale with lowered 7th (W-W-H-W-W-H-W)

**Aeolian** — Natural minor scale (W-H-W-W-H-W-W)

**Locrian** — Diminished scale with lowered 2nd and 5th (H-W-W-H-W-W-W)

### Pentatonic Scales

**Major Pentatonic** — Five-note scale (1-2-3-5-6); versatile and consonant

**Minor Pentatonic** — Five-note scale (1-♭3-4-5-♭7); common in blues and rock

**Blues Scale** — Minor pentatonic with added ♭5 (blue note)

## Chord Construction

### Triad Formation

**Building Triads** — Stack two thirds (root, 3rd, 5th)

**Major Triad** — Root, major 3rd (4 half steps), perfect 5th (7 half steps)

**Minor Triad** — Root, minor 3rd (3 half steps), perfect 5th (7 half steps)

**Diminished Triad** — Root, minor 3rd, diminished 5th (6 half steps)

**Augmented Triad** — Root, major 3rd, augmented 5th (8 half steps)

### Seventh Chords

**Major Seventh (Maj7)** — Major triad + major 7th (C-E-G-B)

**Dominant Seventh (7)** — Major triad + minor 7th (C-E-G-B♭)

**Minor Seventh (m7)** — Minor triad + minor 7th (C-E♭-G-B♭)

**Half-Diminished Seventh (m7♭5)** — Diminished triad + minor 7th (C-E♭-G♭-B♭)

**Fully Diminished Seventh (dim7)** — Diminished triad + diminished 7th (C-E♭-G♭-B♭♭)

### Extended Chords

**Ninth Chords** — Add 9th (2nd octave higher) to seventh chord

**Eleventh Chords** — Add 11th (4th octave higher) to ninth chord

**Thirteenth Chords** — Add 13th (6th octave higher) to eleventh chord

**Altered Extensions** — Sharped or flatted extensions (♭9, #9, #11, ♭13)

### Chord Inversions

**Root Position** — Root in bass (C-E-G)

**First Inversion** — Third in bass (E-G-C)

**Second Inversion** — Fifth in bass (G-C-E)

**Third Inversion** — Seventh in bass (seventh chords only)

**Figured Bass** — Notation system indicating inversions (6, 6/4, 6/5, 4/3, 4/2)

## Diatonic Harmony

### Harmonizing the Major Scale

**I** — Major (tonic function)

**ii** — Minor (predominant function)

**iii** — Minor (tonic function)

**IV** — Major (predominant function)

**V** — Major (dominant function)

**vi** — Minor (tonic function)

**vii°** — Diminished (dominant function)

### Harmonizing the Minor Scale

**i** — Minor (tonic function)

**ii°** — Diminished (predominant function)

**III** — Major (tonic function)

**iv** — Minor (predominant function)

**v** — Minor (dominant function, often raised to V)

**VI** — Major (predominant function)

**VII** — Major (dominant function, often raised to vii°)

### Functional Harmony

**Tonic Function** — Stability and resolution (I, iii, vi)

**Predominant Function** — Prepares dominant (ii, IV)

**Dominant Function** — Creates tension, resolves to tonic (V, vii°)

**Typical Progression** — Tonic → Predominant → Dominant → Tonic

## Rhythm and Meter

### Note Values

**Whole Note** — 4 beats in 4/4 time

**Half Note** — 2 beats

**Quarter Note** — 1 beat

**Eighth Note** — 1/2 beat

**Sixteenth Note** — 1/4 beat

**Dotted Notes** — Add half the note's value (dotted quarter = 1.5 beats)

**Triplets** — Three notes in space of two (quarter note triplet = 3 notes in 2 beats)

### Time Signatures

**Simple Meter** — Beat divides into two (2/4, 3/4, 4/4)

**Compound Meter** — Beat divides into three (6/8, 9/8, 12/8)

**Asymmetric Meter** — Irregular groupings (5/4, 7/8, 11/8)

**Top Number** — Number of beats per measure

**Bottom Number** — Note value receiving one beat

### Tempo Markings

**Largo** — Very slow (40-60 BPM)

**Adagio** — Slow (66-76 BPM)

**Andante** — Walking pace (76-108 BPM)

**Moderato** — Moderate (108-120 BPM)

**Allegro** — Fast (120-168 BPM)

**Presto** — Very fast (168-200 BPM)

**BPM** — Beats per minute (precise tempo indication)

## Key Relationships

### Circle of Fifths

**Clockwise Movement** — Add one sharp (C → G → D → A → E → B → F# → C#)

**Counterclockwise Movement** — Add one flat (C → F → B♭ → E♭ → A♭ → D♭ → G♭ → C♭)

**Relative Minors** — Inner circle shows relative minor keys

**Closely Related Keys** — Adjacent keys on circle (differ by one accidental)

### Modulation

**Modulation** — Changing from one key to another within a piece

**Pivot Chord** — Chord common to both keys used for smooth transition

**Direct Modulation** — Abrupt key change without preparation

**Common Modulations** — To dominant (V), subdominant (IV), relative minor/major, parallel minor/major
