# People Analytics Frameworks

Comprehensive guide to people analytics strategy, maturity models, organizational readiness, governance, and program design.

---

## People Analytics Maturity Model

### Level 1: Reactive Reporting

**Characteristics:**
- Manual data collection and reporting
- Basic headcount and turnover reports
- Spreadsheet-based analysis
- Ad-hoc requests from leadership
- No dedicated analytics team
- Backward-looking only

**Analytics Type:** Descriptive (what happened?)

**Tools:** Excel, basic HRIS reports

**Value:** Minimal - answers basic questions but no strategic insight

**Typical Organizations:** Small companies, traditional industries, early-stage HR functions

**Next Steps:**
- Invest in HRIS with reporting capabilities
- Standardize key metrics and definitions
- Establish regular reporting cadence
- Build data quality and governance

### Level 2: Proactive Reporting

**Characteristics:**
- Automated dashboards and reports
- Standard HR metrics tracked consistently
- Regular reporting cadence (monthly/quarterly)
- Some diagnostic analysis (why did it happen?)
- HR team has basic analytics skills
- Data quality improving

**Analytics Type:** Descriptive + Diagnostic

**Tools:** HRIS with dashboards, basic BI tools (Tableau, Power BI)

**Value:** Moderate - identifies trends and issues, enables informed decisions

**Typical Organizations:** Mid-sized companies, maturing HR functions

**Next Steps:**
- Hire dedicated analytics person/team
- Implement BI platform for advanced visualization
- Begin segmentation and correlation analysis
- Integrate data across HR systems

### Level 3: Strategic Analytics

**Characteristics:**
- Dedicated people analytics team
- Advanced analytics and segmentation
- Predictive models for key outcomes (turnover, performance)
- Proactive insights shared with leadership
- Data-driven HR strategy
- Strong data governance

**Analytics Type:** Descriptive + Diagnostic + Predictive

**Tools:** Advanced BI platforms, statistical software (R, Python), specialized HR analytics platforms (Visier)

**Value:** High - anticipates issues, informs strategy, drives business outcomes

**Typical Organizations:** Large enterprises, tech companies, data-driven organizations

**Next Steps:**
- Build advanced predictive models
- Develop prescriptive analytics (recommendations)
- Integrate analytics into all HR processes
- Expand analytics to broader workforce (contractors, gig workers)

### Level 4: Predictive and Prescriptive

**Characteristics:**
- Sophisticated predictive models across all HR domains
- Prescriptive recommendations (what should we do?)
- Real-time analytics and alerts
- AI and machine learning integrated
- Analytics embedded in HR workflows
- Continuous experimentation and optimization

**Analytics Type:** All four (Descriptive + Diagnostic + Predictive + Prescriptive)

**Tools:** Advanced analytics platforms, machine learning, AI, real-time dashboards

**Value:** Very High - drives competitive advantage, optimizes workforce, strategic business partner

**Typical Organizations:** Leading tech companies, data-driven enterprises, analytics-first organizations

**Next Steps:**
- Continuous innovation and refinement
- Expand to external workforce and ecosystem
- Integrate with business analytics
- Thought leadership and industry influence

## People Analytics Strategy Framework

### 1. Define Vision and Objectives

**Vision:**
What role should people analytics play in the organization?
- Support HR operations and efficiency?
- Enable data-driven talent decisions?
- Drive business outcomes and competitive advantage?
- Transform HR into strategic business partner?

**Objectives:**
What specific outcomes do you want to achieve?
- Reduce turnover by X%
- Improve quality of hire
- Increase employee engagement
- Optimize workforce costs
- Improve diversity and inclusion
- Enhance leadership pipeline

**Success Metrics:**
How will you measure success?
- Adoption and usage of analytics
- Impact on key HR metrics (turnover, engagement, etc.)
- Business impact (revenue, productivity, cost savings)
- Stakeholder satisfaction
- ROI of analytics investments

### 2. Assess Current State

**Data Maturity:**
- What HR data do you have?
- How clean, complete, and integrated is it?
- What are the gaps?

**Analytics Capability:**
- What analytics skills exist in HR?
- What tools and technology are available?
- What analytics are currently being done?

**Organizational Readiness:**
- Is leadership supportive of data-driven decisions?
- Is there a culture of analytics and experimentation?
- Are managers equipped to use analytics?

**Gaps:**
- What's missing to achieve your vision?
- Data, skills, tools, culture, processes?

### 3. Build Analytics Capability

**Talent:**
- **Hire:** Data analysts, data scientists, analytics translators
- **Develop:** Train existing HR team on analytics and data literacy
- **Partner:** Collaborate with IT, data science, finance teams
- **Outsource:** Consultants for specialized projects or expertise gaps

**Skills Needed:**
- **Technical:** Statistics, data analysis, data visualization, machine learning, programming (SQL, Python, R)
- **Business:** HR domain expertise, business acumen, strategic thinking
- **Communication:** Storytelling, data visualization, stakeholder management

**Team Structure:**
- **Centralized:** Single people analytics team serving all of HR
- **Embedded:** Analytics specialists embedded in HR functions (talent acquisition, L&D, etc.)
- **Hybrid:** Central team + embedded specialists

**Typical Team (100-500 employees):**
- 1-2 people analytics specialists

**Typical Team (500-5,000 employees):**
- People Analytics Manager
- 2-4 Data Analysts
- 1-2 Data Scientists (for predictive modeling)

**Typical Team (5,000+ employees):**
- Head of People Analytics
- Analytics Managers for different domains
- 5-10 Data Analysts
- 2-5 Data Scientists
- Data Engineers for infrastructure

### 4. Establish Data Foundation

**Data Integration:**
- Integrate data across HR systems (HRIS, ATS, LMS, performance management, engagement surveys)
- Connect HR data to business data (revenue, productivity, customer satisfaction)
- Create single source of truth (data warehouse or data lake)

**Data Quality:**
- Audit data for completeness, accuracy, consistency
- Establish data quality standards and validation rules
- Implement data governance (ownership, stewardship, policies)
- Regular data quality monitoring and remediation

**Data Security and Privacy:**
- Ensure compliance with GDPR, CCPA, and other regulations
- Implement access controls and data security measures
- Anonymize or pseudonymize data where appropriate
- Clear policies on data usage and employee privacy

### 5. Select Technology and Tools

**HRIS:**
- Core system for employee data
- Should have robust reporting and analytics capabilities
- Examples: Workday, SuccessFactors, Oracle HCM, BambooHR

**Business Intelligence (BI):**
- Data visualization and dashboarding
- Self-service analytics for HR and managers
- Examples: Tableau, Power BI, Looker, Qlik

**Specialized HR Analytics Platforms:**
- Purpose-built for people analytics
- Predictive models, benchmarking, advanced analytics
- Examples: Visier, Crunchr, ChartHop, HRBench

**Statistical/ML Tools:**
- Advanced analytics and predictive modeling
- Examples: R, Python, SAS, SPSS

**Data Infrastructure:**
- Data warehouse or data lake for integrated data
- ETL tools for data integration
- Examples: Snowflake, Databricks, AWS Redshift

**Selection Criteria:**
- Ease of use (for HR team and managers)
- Integration with existing systems
- Scalability and performance
- Cost (licensing, implementation, maintenance)
- Vendor support and roadmap

### 6. Prioritize Use Cases

**High-Impact, High-Feasibility:**
Start here - quick wins that build credibility
- Turnover dashboards and basic prediction
- Hiring metrics and source effectiveness
- Engagement survey analysis and action planning

**High-Impact, Low-Feasibility:**
Invest to enable - high value but requires capability building
- Advanced turnover prediction with interventions
- Performance forecasting and succession planning
- Workforce planning and skills gap analysis

**Low-Impact, High-Feasibility:**
Low priority - easy but limited value
- Basic headcount reports
- Standard compliance reporting

**Low-Impact, Low-Feasibility:**
Avoid - not worth the effort
- Complex analyses with limited business relevance
- Vanity metrics that don't drive decisions

**Prioritization Framework:**
1. **Business Impact:** How much does this matter to business outcomes?
2. **Feasibility:** Do we have the data, skills, and tools to do this?
3. **Stakeholder Interest:** Is leadership asking for this?
4. **Strategic Alignment:** Does this support our people analytics vision?

### 7. Implement and Scale

**Pilot Approach:**
- Start with one high-impact use case
- Pilot with one department or business unit
- Gather feedback and refine
- Demonstrate value and build credibility
- Scale to rest of organization

**Change Management:**
- Communicate vision and benefits
- Involve stakeholders early
- Provide training and support
- Celebrate wins and share success stories
- Address concerns and resistance

**Governance:**
- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Decision-making processes
- Standards and best practices
- Regular reviews and adjustments

### 8. Measure and Optimize

**Track Metrics:**
- **Adoption:** Usage of dashboards, analytics requests, manager engagement
- **Impact:** Changes in key HR metrics (turnover, engagement, quality of hire)
- **Business Value:** Revenue impact, cost savings, productivity gains
- **ROI:** Return on analytics investments

**Continuous Improvement:**
- Regular feedback from stakeholders
- Iterate on models and dashboards
- Expand to new use cases
- Stay current with analytics trends and best practices

## Analytics Governance

### Data Governance

**Data Ownership:**
- Clear owners for each data domain (compensation, performance, engagement)
- Owners responsible for data quality and definitions
- Escalation path for data issues

**Data Stewardship:**
- Data stewards ensure data quality and compliance
- Regular audits and quality checks
- Documentation of data sources, definitions, transformations

**Data Access:**
- Role-based access controls
- Principle of least privilege (access only what's needed)
- Audit trails for sensitive data access
- Clear policies on who can access what data

**Data Privacy:**
- Compliance with GDPR, CCPA, and other regulations
- Employee consent where required
- Anonymization or pseudonymization for analytics
- Clear communication about data usage
- Employee rights (access, correction, deletion)

**Data Retention:**
- Policies on how long data is retained
- Secure deletion when no longer needed
- Balance between analytics needs and privacy

### Analytics Governance

**Standards and Best Practices:**
- Consistent methodologies and definitions
- Documentation of models and assumptions
- Peer review of analyses
- Version control for models and reports

**Quality Assurance:**
- Validation of models and predictions
- Testing of dashboards and reports
- Regular audits for accuracy
- Feedback loops for continuous improvement

**Ethical Use:**
- Principles for ethical use of analytics
- Avoid punitive use of analytics
- Transparency with employees about how data is used
- Bias audits for predictive models
- Human oversight of automated decisions

**Communication:**
- Clear guidelines on how to communicate analytics insights
- Approval processes for sharing sensitive analyses
- Consistent messaging and storytelling
- Accessibility (plain language, visualizations)

## Organizational Readiness

### Leadership Support

**Executive Sponsorship:**
- CHRO or CEO champion for people analytics
- Visible support and advocacy
- Resource allocation (budget, headcount)
- Inclusion in strategic discussions

**Data-Driven Culture:**
- Leadership models data-driven decision-making
- Decisions backed by data, not just intuition
- Tolerance for experimentation and learning from data
- Celebration of analytics-driven wins

**Investment:**
- Budget for analytics team, tools, training
- Long-term commitment (analytics is a journey, not a project)
- Patience for ROI (may take 12-18 months to show full impact)

### Manager Enablement

**Analytics Literacy:**
- Training on how to read and interpret analytics
- Understanding of key metrics and what drives them
- Ability to ask good questions of data
- Comfort with data-driven conversations

**Access to Insights:**
- Self-service dashboards for managers
- Regular reports on their teams
- Easy access to analytics team for questions
- Mobile-friendly access

**Action-Oriented:**
- Analytics tied to actionable recommendations
- Clear guidance on what to do with insights
- Support for implementing interventions
- Accountability for acting on insights

### HR Team Readiness

**Data Literacy:**
- Basic understanding of statistics and analytics
- Ability to interpret dashboards and reports
- Comfort with data-driven decision-making
- Training and development in analytics

**Collaboration:**
- Partnership between analytics team and HR functions
- Analytics embedded in HR processes
- Regular communication and feedback
- Shared goals and metrics

**Mindset Shift:**
- From gut-feel to data-driven decisions
- From reactive to proactive
- From administrative to strategic
- Embrace experimentation and learning

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Poor Data Quality

**Problem:**
- Incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccurate data
- Data scattered across multiple systems
- No single source of truth

**Solutions:**
- Conduct comprehensive data audit
- Establish data governance and standards
- Implement data validation rules
- Invest in data integration and cleaning
- Train employees on proper data entry
- Start with best available data, improve over time

### Challenge 2: Lack of Analytics Skills

**Problem:**
- HR team lacks statistical and analytical expertise
- No dedicated analytics resources
- Difficulty hiring analytics talent

**Solutions:**
- Hire dedicated people analytics specialists
- Upskill existing HR team (training, certifications)
- Partner with IT, data science, or finance teams
- Use user-friendly analytics platforms requiring less technical skill
- Outsource to consultants for specialized projects

### Challenge 3: Resistance to Change

**Problem:**
- Managers prefer gut-feel decisions over data
- Fear of being measured or held accountable
- Skepticism about analytics value

**Solutions:**
- Demonstrate quick wins with pilot projects
- Show ROI and business impact
- Involve managers in analytics design
- Provide training on data interpretation
- Emphasize analytics as decision support, not replacement
- Leadership modeling and endorsement

### Challenge 4: Privacy and Compliance Concerns

**Problem:**
- Employee concerns about data privacy
- Regulatory compliance (GDPR, CCPA)
- Risk of algorithmic bias

**Solutions:**
- Ensure GDPR, CCPA compliance
- Anonymize sensitive data
- Conduct bias audits regularly
- Transparent communication about data usage
- Employee consent and opt-out options
- Ethics review board for analytics projects
- Never use analytics punitively

### Challenge 5: Proving ROI

**Problem:**
- Difficult to isolate impact of analytics from other factors
- Leadership wants clear ROI
- Long time to show results

**Solutions:**
- Establish clear baseline metrics
- Use control groups where possible
- Track leading and lagging indicators
- Gather qualitative feedback and testimonials
- Calculate cost savings (e.g., reduced turnover costs)
- Be patient: cultural change takes 12-18 months
- Share incremental wins along the way

### Challenge 6: Siloed Data and Systems

**Problem:**
- Data scattered across multiple HR systems
- No integration between systems
- Manual data consolidation required

**Solutions:**
- Invest in data integration tools (ETL, APIs)
- Implement data warehouse or data lake
- Select HRIS with strong integration capabilities
- Standardize on fewer, integrated systems
- Prioritize integration in vendor selection

## Analytics Operating Model

### Centralized Model

**Structure:**
- Single people analytics team serving all of HR
- Reports to CHRO or Head of HR

**Pros:**
- Consistent methodologies and standards
- Efficient use of specialized skills
- Easier to build critical mass of expertise
- Clear accountability

**Cons:**
- May be disconnected from business needs
- Can become bottleneck for requests
- Less embedded in day-to-day HR work

**Best For:**
- Smaller organizations (<5,000 employees)
- Early-stage analytics programs
- Organizations with limited analytics resources

### Embedded Model

**Structure:**
- Analytics specialists embedded in HR functions
- Talent acquisition analyst, L&D analyst, compensation analyst, etc.
- Report to functional HR leaders

**Pros:**
- Close to business needs and context
- Deep functional expertise
- Faster turnaround on requests
- Analytics integrated into HR processes

**Cons:**
- Risk of inconsistent methodologies
- Duplication of effort
- Harder to share best practices
- May lack advanced analytics skills

**Best For:**
- Large organizations (10,000+ employees)
- Mature analytics programs
- Organizations with significant analytics resources

### Hybrid Model (Most Common)

**Structure:**
- Central people analytics team (strategy, advanced analytics, governance)
- Embedded analysts in key HR functions
- Central team sets standards, embedded analysts execute

**Pros:**
- Balance of consistency and responsiveness
- Leverages both specialized and functional expertise
- Scalable and flexible

**Cons:**
- Requires strong coordination
- Potential for confusion on roles
- More complex to manage

**Best For:**
- Medium to large organizations (1,000+ employees)
- Maturing analytics programs
- Organizations wanting both strategic and operational analytics

**Typical Structure:**
- **Central Team:** Head of People Analytics, Data Scientists, Analytics Architects, Governance Lead
- **Embedded:** Talent Acquisition Analyst, Total Rewards Analyst, L&D Analyst, Workforce Planning Analyst
- **Dotted Line:** Embedded analysts report to functional leaders, dotted line to Head of People Analytics for methodology and standards
