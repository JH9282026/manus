# Corporate Governance

Comprehensive framework for aligning management actions with shareholder interests through oversight, incentives, and accountability mechanisms.

---

## Governance Fundamentals

### Agency Theory

**Principal-Agent Problem**: Separation of ownership (shareholders) and control (managers) creates conflicts of interest.

**Shareholders (Principals) Want**:
- Maximize firm value
- Long-term wealth creation
- Efficient capital allocation
- Appropriate risk-taking

**Managers (Agents) May Want**:
- Job security
- Empire building (growth for its own sake)
- Perquisites and compensation
- Reduced personal risk
- Short-term performance (if compensation tied to it)

### Types of Agency Costs

**Monitoring Costs**:
- Board of directors expenses
- Audit fees
- Information systems
- Shareholder activism costs

**Bonding Costs**:
- Management guarantees
- Financial reporting
- Voluntary disclosure
- Compliance systems

**Residual Loss**:
- Value lost due to divergence between management actions and shareholder interests
- Hardest to measure but often largest component

### Stakeholder Theory

Firms should consider interests of all stakeholders, not just shareholders.

**Key Stakeholders**:
- Shareholders
- Employees
- Customers
- Suppliers
- Creditors
- Communities
- Government

**Debate**:
- **Shareholder Primacy**: Maximize shareholder value; other stakeholders protected by contracts
- **Stakeholder Approach**: Balance interests of all stakeholders
- **Enlightened Shareholder Value**: Maximize long-term shareholder value by considering all stakeholders

## Board of Directors

### Board Responsibilities

**Fiduciary Duties**:
1. **Duty of Care**: Make informed, careful decisions
2. **Duty of Loyalty**: Act in company's best interest, avoid conflicts
3. **Duty of Good Faith**: Act honestly and with proper purpose

**Key Functions**:
- **Strategic Oversight**: Approve major strategies and transactions
- **CEO Selection and Evaluation**: Hire, evaluate, compensate, and if necessary, replace CEO
- **Risk Oversight**: Ensure appropriate risk management systems
- **Financial Oversight**: Review financial statements, approve budgets
- **Succession Planning**: Ensure continuity of leadership
- **Compliance**: Oversee legal and regulatory compliance

### Board Structure

**Board Size**:
- **Small Boards** (5-9 members): More effective, better communication, less free-riding
- **Large Boards** (12+ members): More expertise, better connections, but coordination challenges
- **Optimal**: Research suggests 7-9 members for most companies

**Board Composition**:

| Director Type | Definition | Typical % | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------------|------------|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Inside Directors | Company executives | 10-20% | Deep company knowledge | Lack independence |
| Affiliated Directors | Business relationships with company | 0-10% | Industry expertise | Potential conflicts |
| Independent Directors | No material relationship with company | 70-90% | Objectivity, credibility | May lack company-specific knowledge |

**Best Practice**: Majority (ideally 75%+) independent directors

### Board Committees

**Audit Committee**:
- **Composition**: All independent directors, at least one financial expert
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Oversee financial reporting
  - Select and oversee external auditors
  - Review internal controls
  - Oversee compliance programs

**Compensation Committee**:
- **Composition**: All independent directors
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Design executive compensation
  - Evaluate CEO performance
  - Approve equity grants
  - Review compensation disclosures

**Nominating/Governance Committee**:
- **Composition**: All independent directors
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Identify and recruit director candidates
  - Evaluate board performance
  - Develop governance policies
  - Oversee board education

**Risk Committee** (increasingly common):
- **Composition**: Mix of independent and inside directors
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Oversee enterprise risk management
  - Review risk appetite and limits
  - Monitor key risks

### Board Leadership Structure

**Combined CEO/Chairman**:
- *Advantages*: Clear leadership, efficient decision-making, accountability
- *Disadvantages*: Concentration of power, reduced oversight, potential conflicts

**Separate CEO/Chairman**:
- *Advantages*: Better oversight, clearer roles, reduced conflicts
- *Disadvantages*: Potential confusion, power struggles

**Lead Independent Director** (compromise):
- CEO is chairman, but lead independent director coordinates independent directors
- Chairs executive sessions without management
- Serves as liaison between independent directors and CEO

**Best Practice**: Separate CEO/Chairman or strong lead independent director

### Board Effectiveness

**Characteristics of Effective Boards**:
- Appropriate mix of skills and experience
- True independence (not just technical)
- Adequate time commitment (15-20+ days per year)
- Access to information and management
- Constructive challenge of management
- Regular executive sessions without management
- Ongoing education
- Formal evaluation process

**Red Flags**:
- Rubber-stamp board (no dissent)
- Dominated by CEO
- Interlocking directorates (directors serve on each other's boards)
- Excessive board tenure (>15 years)
- Too many other board commitments
- Lack of relevant expertise

## Executive Compensation

### Compensation Components

**Base Salary**:
- Fixed annual cash compensation
- Typically 10-30% of total compensation for CEOs
- Benchmarked against peers

**Annual Bonus**:
- Variable cash compensation based on annual performance
- Typically 20-40% of total compensation
- Tied to financial metrics (earnings, revenue, ROIC)

**Long-Term Incentives**:
- Stock options, restricted stock, performance shares
- Typically 40-70% of total compensation
- Vest over 3-5 years
- Align with long-term shareholder interests

**Benefits and Perquisites**:
- Retirement plans, insurance, perks
- Typically <10% of total compensation
- Increasingly scrutinized

### Compensation Design Principles

**Pay for Performance**:
- Significant portion variable and at-risk
- Tied to metrics that drive shareholder value
- Differentiate rewards based on performance

**Alignment with Shareholders**:
- Substantial equity ownership requirements
- Long vesting periods
- Clawback provisions
- No hedging or pledging of company stock

**Competitive but Not Excessive**:
- Benchmark against appropriate peers
- Avoid "Lake Wobegon effect" (everyone above average)
- Consider total shareholder return, not just peer pay

**Transparency**:
- Clear disclosure of pay philosophy
- Detailed metrics and targets
- Explanation of pay decisions

### Equity Compensation

**Stock Options**:
- Right to buy stock at exercise price (typically grant-date price)
- Value increases with stock price
- *Advantages*: Strong alignment, upside potential
- *Disadvantages*: Reward stock price increases even if below market, no downside risk, can encourage excessive risk-taking

**Restricted Stock**:
- Actual shares granted, vest over time
- Retain value even if stock price declines
- *Advantages*: Retention tool, less dilutive than options
- *Disadvantages*: Weaker pay-performance link, executives gain even if performance is poor

**Performance Shares**:
- Shares granted based on achieving performance targets
- Targets typically 3-5 year metrics (TSR, ROIC, EPS growth)
- *Advantages*: Strong pay-performance link, focus on long-term goals
- *Disadvantages*: Complex, difficult to set appropriate targets

**Best Practice**: Mix of vehicles, with emphasis on performance shares

### Performance Metrics

**Financial Metrics**:

| Metric | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| EPS Growth | Simple, widely understood | Can be manipulated, ignores capital employed |
| Revenue Growth | Measures market share gains | Ignores profitability |
| ROIC | Measures capital efficiency | Can discourage investment |
| Total Shareholder Return (TSR) | Direct shareholder measure | Affected by market factors beyond management control |
| Free Cash Flow | Harder to manipulate | Can be volatile |

**Non-Financial Metrics**:
- Customer satisfaction
- Employee engagement
- ESG goals
- Strategic milestones

**Best Practice**: Balanced scorecard with 3-5 metrics, mix of financial and strategic

### Compensation Problems and Solutions

**Problem: Short-Termism**
- *Cause*: Heavy weighting on annual bonuses
- *Solution*: Increase long-term incentive proportion, use 3-5 year performance periods

**Problem: Excessive Risk-Taking**
- *Cause*: Options with no downside, bonus plans with no caps
- *Solution*: Clawback provisions, stock ownership requirements, risk-adjusted metrics

**Problem: Pay Without Performance**
- *Cause*: Weak metrics, low targets, discretionary bonuses
- *Solution*: Rigorous target-setting, relative performance metrics (vs. peers), no guaranteed bonuses

**Problem: Mega-Grants**
- *Cause*: Retention concerns, competitive pressure
- *Solution*: Regular annual grants instead of sporadic large grants, performance-based vesting

### Say-on-Pay

Shareholder advisory vote on executive compensation.

**Requirements** (US):
- Vote at least every 3 years
- Non-binding but influential
- Failure (<50% support) triggers review

**Best Practices**:
- Engage with shareholders proactively
- Respond to concerns
- Clear disclosure of pay rationale
- Align pay with performance

## Shareholder Rights

### Voting Rights

**One Share, One Vote**:
- Standard structure
- Democratic, proportional to ownership

**Dual-Class Stock**:
- Multiple classes with different voting rights
- Founders/insiders retain control with minority economic interest
- *Advantages*: Protects long-term vision, defends against short-term pressure
- *Disadvantages*: Entrenches management, reduces accountability
- *Examples*: Google, Facebook, Berkshire Hathaway

**Voting Issues**:
- Director elections
- Executive compensation (say-on-pay)
- Mergers and acquisitions
- Charter and bylaw amendments
- Shareholder proposals

### Director Elections

**Plurality Voting**:
- Candidate with most votes wins
- Can win with single vote if unopposed
- Difficult to remove directors

**Majority Voting**:
- Candidate must receive >50% of votes
- Directors who fail to receive majority must resign
- Better accountability

**Best Practice**: Majority voting with director resignation policy

### Shareholder Proposals

Shareholders can submit proposals for vote at annual meeting.

**Common Topics**:
- Governance changes (declassify board, eliminate supermajority provisions)
- Executive compensation (say-on-pay frequency, clawbacks)
- Environmental and social issues
- Political spending disclosure

**Requirements** (US):
- Own $2,000 or 1% of shares for 1+ years
- Proposal <500 words
- Company can exclude if ordinary business, not significant, or violates law

### Proxy Access

Shareholders can nominate directors on company's proxy statement.

**Typical Terms**:
- Own 3% of shares for 3+ years
- Nominate up to 20-25% of board
- Group of up to 20 shareholders can aggregate holdings

**Impact**:
- Increases board accountability
- Reduces cost of director nominations
- Rarely used but important threat

## Takeover Defenses

### Pre-Offer Defenses

**Poison Pill (Shareholder Rights Plan)**:
- Triggered when acquirer crosses threshold (typically 10-20%)
- Existing shareholders (except acquirer) can buy shares at discount
- Massively dilutes acquirer's stake
- *Purpose*: Give board time to evaluate offers, negotiate better terms
- *Criticism*: Entrenches management, can block value-creating deals

**Staggered Board (Classified Board)**:
- Directors serve 3-year terms, only 1/3 elected each year
- Takes 2 years to gain board control
- *Purpose*: Provides continuity, defends against hostile takeovers
- *Criticism*: Reduces accountability, delays change

**Supermajority Provisions**:
- Require >50% vote for major actions (e.g., 80% to approve merger)
- Makes takeovers more difficult
- *Criticism*: Gives veto power to minority

**Fair Price Provisions**:
- Require acquirer to pay all shareholders same price
- Prevents two-tier offers (low price for remaining shareholders)

### Post-Offer Defenses

**White Knight**:
- Find friendly acquirer to outbid hostile bidder
- Preserves some management influence

**Pac-Man Defense**:
- Target makes counter-offer for acquirer
- Rarely successful, very expensive

**Crown Jewel Defense**:
- Sell most valuable assets to make company less attractive
- Extreme measure, often blocked by courts

**Greenmail**:
- Buy back shares from hostile bidder at premium
- Eliminates threat but rewards raider
- Now rare due to tax penalties and reputational damage

**Litigation**:
- Sue acquirer for antitrust, securities violations, etc.
- Delays process, increases costs

### Evaluating Takeover Defenses

**Arguments For**:
- Give board time to evaluate offers
- Enable negotiation of better terms
- Protect long-term strategy from short-term pressure
- Prevent coercive offers

**Arguments Against**:
- Entrench management
- Block value-creating deals
- Reduce takeover premium
- Weaken market for corporate control

**Best Practice**: Moderate defenses (e.g., poison pill with reasonable trigger, no staggered board)

## Institutional Investors and Activism

### Types of Institutional Investors

**Passive Investors**:
- Index funds (Vanguard, BlackRock, State Street)
- Hold diversified portfolios
- Cannot easily sell (would affect index tracking)
- Increasingly active in governance

**Active Investors**:
- Mutual funds, hedge funds
- Select stocks based on research
- Can sell if dissatisfied
- Engage on governance and strategy

**Pension Funds**:
- Public and private pension funds
- Long-term horizon
- Active in governance, especially public pensions (CalPERS, CalSTRS)

**Sovereign Wealth Funds**:
- Government investment funds
- Very long-term horizon
- Generally passive but increasing engagement

### Shareholder Activism

**Activist Strategies**:

**Governance Activism**:
- Push for board changes
- Improve executive compensation
- Enhance shareholder rights
- Increase transparency

**Operational Activism**:
- Improve margins
- Reduce costs
- Divest non-core assets
- Change strategy

**Financial Activism**:
- Increase leverage
- Return cash to shareholders (dividends, buybacks)
- Pursue M&A
- Spin off divisions

**Activist Tactics**:
1. **Private Engagement**: Meet with management and board
2. **Public Campaign**: Open letters, presentations, media
3. **Proxy Contest**: Nominate directors, solicit votes
4. **Takeover Bid**: Acquire company (rare for activists)

**Activist Success Factors**:
- Credible plan to create value
- Support from other shareholders
- Underperforming target
- Clear governance issues
- Experienced activist with track record

### Responding to Activists

**Proactive Measures**:
- Maintain strong performance
- Engage regularly with shareholders
- Have clear, compelling strategy
- Optimize capital structure
- Strong governance practices

**Reactive Measures**:
- Evaluate activist's proposals objectively
- Engage constructively
- Communicate plan to all shareholders
- Consider settlement (board seats, strategic changes)
- Prepare for proxy contest if necessary

**Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Dismissing activist without consideration
- Personal attacks
- Ignoring other shareholders
- Defensive actions that destroy value (poison pill without cause)

## Disclosure and Transparency

### Financial Reporting

**Required Disclosures** (US public companies):
- **10-K**: Annual report with audited financials
- **10-Q**: Quarterly report with unaudited financials
- **8-K**: Current report for material events
- **Proxy Statement**: Annual meeting information, executive compensation

**Best Practices**:
- Clear, understandable language
- Non-GAAP metrics explained and reconciled
- Forward-looking information (with safe harbor)
- Segment reporting
- Key performance indicators

### Earnings Guidance

**Arguments For**:
- Reduces information asymmetry
- Helps analysts and investors
- Demonstrates management confidence

**Arguments Against**:
- Encourages short-term focus
- Pressure to meet guidance can lead to bad decisions
- Guidance often inaccurate

**Trend**: Many companies eliminating quarterly guidance, focusing on long-term

### ESG Disclosure

Environmental, Social, and Governance reporting.

**Common Frameworks**:
- **GRI** (Global Reporting Initiative): Comprehensive sustainability reporting
- **SASB** (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board): Industry-specific, financially material
- **TCFD** (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures): Climate risk
- **Integrated Reporting**: Combines financial and non-financial

**Key ESG Topics**:
- Carbon emissions and climate risk
- Diversity and inclusion
- Labor practices
- Board composition and independence
- Political spending
- Data privacy and security

**Investor Demand**: Institutional investors increasingly require ESG disclosure

## International Governance

### Governance Systems

**Anglo-American Model** (US, UK):
- Dispersed ownership
- Active capital markets
- Strong shareholder rights
- Single-tier board
- Market for corporate control

**Continental European Model** (Germany, France):
- Concentrated ownership (families, banks)
- Two-tier board (supervisory and management)
- Stakeholder orientation
- Less active takeover market
- Codetermination (employee board representation in Germany)

**Asian Model** (Japan, South Korea):
- Cross-shareholdings (keiretsu in Japan, chaebol in South Korea)
- Bank-centered financing
- Weak shareholder rights
- Family control common
- Improving governance in recent years

### Convergence vs. Divergence

**Convergence View**:
- Globalization and competition drive convergence toward best practices
- Institutional investors push for common standards
- Evidence: Increased board independence, say-on-pay spreading globally

**Divergence View**:
- Path dependence and institutional complementarities
- Legal and cultural differences persist
- Evidence: Ownership structures remain distinct, stakeholder vs. shareholder orientation

**Reality**: Partial convergence in formal structures, but significant differences remain in practice

## Governance Best Practices Summary

### Board Structure
- [ ] Majority (75%+) independent directors
- [ ] Separate CEO/Chairman or strong lead independent director
- [ ] Board size 7-9 members
- [ ] All-independent audit, compensation, and nominating committees
- [ ] Regular executive sessions without management
- [ ] Annual board and director evaluations
- [ ] Director education program

### Shareholder Rights
- [ ] One share, one vote (or sunset provision for dual-class)
- [ ] Majority voting for directors
- [ ] Annual election of all directors (no staggered board)
- [ ] Proxy access
- [ ] Simple majority vote requirements (no supermajority)
- [ ] Right to call special meetings
- [ ] Right to act by written consent

### Executive Compensation
- [ ] Significant portion at-risk and performance-based
- [ ] Long-term incentives (3-5 year performance periods)
- [ ] Rigorous, objective performance metrics
- [ ] Stock ownership requirements
- [ ] Clawback provisions
- [ ] No hedging or pledging of company stock
- [ ] Clear, transparent disclosure
- [ ] Regular shareholder engagement on pay

### Disclosure and Engagement
- [ ] Clear, timely financial reporting
- [ ] Regular earnings calls and investor meetings
- [ ] ESG disclosure using recognized framework
- [ ] Responsive to shareholder concerns
- [ ] Proactive engagement with major shareholders
