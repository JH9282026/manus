# Recording Setup Guide

This reference provides comprehensive information on setting up a Foley recording environment, from room acoustics to equipment selection and configuration.

## The Foley Stage

### Ideal Characteristics

A Foley stage is a specialized recording environment designed for performing and capturing Foley sound effects.

**Essential Features**:
- **Acoustic treatment**: Controlled, dry sound environment
- **Foley pits**: Built-in surfaces for footsteps
- **Adequate space**: Room for movement and props
- **Visual monitoring**: Large screen for picture playback
- **Separate control room**: For mixer/engineer
- **Prop storage**: Organized, accessible collection

**Size Considerations**:
- **Minimum**: 10' x 12' (small home setup)
- **Typical**: 15' x 20' (professional small stage)
- **Large**: 25' x 30'+ (major facility)
- **Height**: 8-10' minimum ceiling

**Layout**:
- Performance area in center
- Foley pits accessible
- Props along walls
- Monitor visible from performance area
- Microphone stands positioned strategically
- Clear pathways for movement

### Acoustic Treatment

**Goal**: Create dry, controlled acoustic environment

**Why Dry Acoustics?**
- Reverb added in post-production
- Matches scene environment
- Flexibility in mixing
- Clean, isolated sounds
- Easier to edit

**Treatment Methods**:

**1. Absorption**
- **Acoustic panels**: Foam or fiberglass on walls
- **Bass traps**: Corner placement for low frequencies
- **Ceiling treatment**: Prevent ceiling reflections
- **Carpet or rugs**: Reduce floor reflections (except Foley pits)

**2. Diffusion**
- **Diffusers**: Scatter reflections
- **Irregular surfaces**: Break up standing waves
- **Bookcases, furniture**: Natural diffusion
- **Balance with absorption**: Not too dead

**3. Isolation**
- **Soundproofing**: Prevent outside noise
- **Floating floor**: Reduce vibration transmission
- **Sealed doors and windows**: Block sound leakage
- **HVAC noise control**: Quiet ventilation

**Target Acoustics**:
- **RT60** (reverberation time): 0.2-0.4 seconds
- **Noise floor**: Below -50 dBFS (quieter is better)
- **Frequency response**: Even across spectrum

**Testing**:
- Clap test (should sound dry, no echo)
- Record silence (check noise floor)
- Record test sounds (check for reflections)
- Adjust treatment as needed

### Foley Pits

**Purpose**: Built-in surfaces for footstep recording

**Common Surfaces**:
- **Wood**: Hardwood floor, planks
- **Concrete**: Smooth or textured
- **Gravel**: Small stones, various sizes
- **Sand**: Fine or coarse
- **Tile**: Ceramic or stone
- **Carpet**: Various pile heights
- **Metal**: Grating, sheet metal
- **Dirt**: Packed earth

**Construction**:
- Recessed into floor or raised platforms
- Typically 3' x 4' or 4' x 4' each
- Multiple pits for different surfaces
- Easily accessible
- Replaceable materials

**Maintenance**:
- Keep surfaces clean
- Replace worn materials
- Prevent contamination between surfaces
- Refresh gravel, sand periodically

**Alternatives** (for smaller spaces):
- Portable surface boxes
- Removable panels
- Layered materials
- Outdoor recording for specific surfaces

### Alternative Spaces

**Home Studio Conversion**:
- Spare room or garage
- Add acoustic treatment
- Portable Foley pits
- Smaller prop collection
- Budget-friendly option

**Portable Setup**:
- Minimal treatment
- Portable surfaces
- Location recording
- For specific ambiences

**Shared Spaces**:
- Rent studio time
- Shared Foley stage
- Cost-effective for occasional use

**Outdoor Recording**:
- Natural surfaces
- Specific ambiences
- Requires quiet location
- Weather-dependent

## Equipment Selection

### Microphones

**Condenser Microphones**: Industry standard for Foley

**Why Condensers?**
- High sensitivity
- Wide frequency response
- Detailed capture
- Transient response
- Professional quality

**Types**:

**1. Large Diaphragm Condensers (LDC)**
- **Characteristics**: Warm, full sound; less sensitive to positioning
- **Common models**: Neumann U87, AKG C414, Audio-Technica AT4050
- **Best for**: General Foley, cloth, props
- **Price range**: $300-$3,000+

**2. Small Diaphragm Condensers (SDC)**
- **Characteristics**: Accurate, detailed; better transient response
- **Common models**: Neumann KM184, DPA 4011, Rode NT5
- **Best for**: Footsteps, detailed sounds, stereo recording
- **Price range**: $200-$1,500+ (each)

**3. Shotgun Microphones**
- **Characteristics**: Highly directional; focused pickup
- **Common models**: Sennheiser MKH416, Rode NTG3, Audio-Technica AT4053b
- **Best for**: Isolating specific sounds, rejecting room noise
- **Price range**: $300-$1,500+

**Polar Patterns**:

**Cardioid**
- Most common
- Rejects rear sound
- Good for general Foley
- Reduces room reflections

**Hypercardioid/Supercardioid**
- More directional
- Tighter pickup pattern
- Better isolation
- Some rear pickup

**Omnidirectional**
- Picks up all directions
- Natural sound
- More room sound
- Less common for Foley

**Figure-8 (Bidirectional)**
- Front and rear pickup
- Rejects sides
- Used in M/S recording
- Specialized applications

**Budget Recommendations**:
- **Entry**: Audio-Technica AT2020, Rode NT1-A ($100-250)
- **Mid**: Audio-Technica AT4050, Rode NT2-A ($300-500)
- **Professional**: Neumann U87, AKG C414, DPA 4011 ($1,000-3,000+)

### Audio Interface

**Purpose**: Convert analog microphone signal to digital

**Requirements**:
- **Phantom power**: +48V for condenser mics
- **Preamps**: Clean, low-noise
- **Sample rate**: 48 kHz minimum (96 kHz preferred)
- **Bit depth**: 24-bit
- **Inputs**: At least 2 (for stereo), more for multiple mics
- **Outputs**: Monitor outputs
- **Connectivity**: USB, Thunderbolt, or PCIe

**Common Models**:
- **Entry**: Focusrite Scarlett 2i2, PreSonus AudioBox ($100-200)
- **Mid**: Universal Audio Apollo Twin, Focusrite Clarett ($500-1,000)
- **Professional**: Universal Audio Apollo x8, RME Fireface ($1,500-3,000+)

**Key Specifications**:
- **Preamp quality**: Low noise, high gain
- **Converters**: High-quality AD/DA conversion
- **Latency**: Low latency for monitoring
- **Stability**: Reliable drivers

### Monitoring

**Headphones**:
- **Closed-back**: Prevent bleed into microphones
- **Comfortable**: For long sessions
- **Accurate**: Flat frequency response
- **Common models**: Sony MDR-7506, Beyerdynamic DT770, Sennheiser HD280

**Studio Monitors** (for control room):
- **Accurate**: Flat response
- **Appropriate size**: Match room size
- **Placement**: Proper positioning
- **Common models**: Yamaha HS series, KRK Rokit, Genelec 8000 series

**Headphone Amplifier** (if needed):
- Multiple headphone outputs
- Individual volume control
- Clean amplification

### Microphone Accessories

**Microphone Stands**:
- **Boom stands**: Adjustable height and reach
- **Straight stands**: For fixed positions
- **Desktop stands**: For low placement
- **Sturdy construction**: Prevent movement
- **Quantity**: Multiple stands for different setups

**Shock Mounts**:
- Isolate mic from vibrations
- Reduce handling noise
- Specific to microphone model
- Essential for condenser mics

**Pop Filters/Windscreens**:
- Reduce plosives (if recording vocals)
- Protect microphone
- Less critical for Foley than vocals

**Cables**:
- **XLR cables**: Balanced, professional
- **Quality**: Good shielding, low noise
- **Length**: Appropriate for setup (10-25 feet typical)
- **Quantity**: Enough for all mics plus spares

### Recording System

**Computer**:
- **Processor**: Multi-core (i5/i7 or equivalent)
- **RAM**: 16 GB minimum (32 GB+ preferred)
- **Storage**: SSD for system and projects, HDD for archives
- **Operating System**: Mac or Windows (stable, updated)

**Digital Audio Workstation (DAW)**:
- **Video playback**: Essential for Foley
- **Multi-track recording**: Multiple takes and layers
- **Editing tools**: Precise editing and sync
- **Common choices**: Pro Tools, Reaper, Nuendo, Logic Pro

**Video Playback**:
- **In DAW**: Most modern DAWs support video
- **External monitor**: Large screen for artist
- **Sync**: Timecode or manual sync
- **Format**: H.264, ProRes, or other compatible

**Storage**:
- **Project drive**: Fast SSD
- **Archive drive**: Large HDD or NAS
- **Backup**: Redundant storage (RAID, cloud)
- **Organization**: Logical folder structure

## Microphone Placement

### General Principles

**Close Miking**:
- Capture detail
- Minimize room sound
- Typical distance: 6 inches to 4 feet
- Adjust based on sound source

**Appropriate Distance**:
- Too close: Proximity effect (bass boost), unnatural
- Too far: Room sound, less detail
- Sweet spot: Balance detail and naturalness

**Angle**:
- Aim at sound source
- Consider polar pattern
- Adjust for best sound
- Experiment

**Avoid Proximity Effect**:
- Bass boost when too close
- Especially with cardioid mics
- Back off if sound is too boomy
- Or use for creative effect

### Specific Techniques

**Footsteps**:
- **Height**: 2-4 feet above surface
- **Angle**: Angled down toward feet
- **Position**: Slightly in front of performance area
- **Stereo**: Spaced pair (3-6 feet apart) for width

**Cloth Movement**:
- **Distance**: 6-12 inches from fabric
- **Position**: Where fabric is being moved
- **Angle**: Aimed at fabric
- **Caution**: Avoid breath noise from performer

**Props and Impacts**:
- **Variable**: Depends on prop and action
- **Close**: For detailed sounds (6-18 inches)
- **Moderate**: For natural sound (1-3 feet)
- **Experiment**: Find best position for each prop

**Doors**:
- **Position**: Near door, capture full action
- **Height**: Mid-door level
- **Distance**: 1-3 feet
- **Capture**: Latch, hinges, impact

**General Objects**:
- **Start close**: Then adjust
- **Listen**: Monitor while adjusting
- **Document**: Note successful placements
- **Consistency**: Use same placement for similar sounds

### Stereo Recording Techniques

**Spaced Pair (A/B)**:
- Two mics separated by distance
- Typically 3-6 feet apart
- Captures width and space
- Natural stereo image
- Good for footsteps, ambience

**X/Y (Coincident Pair)**:
- Two mics at same point, angled apart
- Typically 90-135 degrees
- Mono-compatible
- Precise stereo image
- Good for detailed sounds

**M/S (Mid-Side)**:
- One cardioid (mid) + one figure-8 (side)
- Adjustable stereo width in post
- Mono-compatible
- Flexible
- More complex setup

**ORTF**:
- Two cardioid mics, 17 cm apart, 110° angle
- Simulates human hearing
- Natural stereo image
- Good general-purpose technique

**Choosing Technique**:
- **Spaced pair**: Wide, spacious sounds
- **X/Y**: Precise, focused sounds
- **M/S**: Maximum flexibility
- **ORTF**: Natural, balanced

### Mono vs. Stereo

**Mono Recording**:
- **Advantages**: Easier to position in post, simpler setup
- **Disadvantages**: No spatial information
- **Best for**: Sounds that will be panned in post, simple setups

**Stereo Recording**:
- **Advantages**: Spatial information, natural width, immersive
- **Disadvantages**: More complex, harder to position in post
- **Best for**: Ambiences, footsteps, complex scenes

**Workflow Consideration**:
- Some prefer mono for flexibility
- Others prefer stereo for realism
- Depends on project and workflow
- Discuss with mixer/editor

## Recording Levels and Settings

### Gain Staging

**Principle**: Set appropriate levels throughout signal chain

**Process**:

**1. Microphone Preamp Gain**
- Adjust for healthy signal level
- Avoid clipping (red lights)
- Typical target: -12 to -6 dBFS peaks
- Leave headroom for unexpected loud sounds

**2. Interface Input Level**
- Usually controlled by preamp gain
- Monitor input meters
- Adjust as needed

**3. DAW Track Level**
- Usually unity (0 dB)
- Adjust fader for monitoring only
- Record at input level

**Headroom**:
- Leave space below 0 dBFS
- Prevents clipping
- Typical peaks: -6 to -12 dBFS
- Allows for unexpected loud sounds

**Consistency**:
- Maintain similar levels across takes
- Easier for editor
- Consistent sound quality
- Document settings

### Sample Rate and Bit Depth

**Sample Rate**:
- **48 kHz**: Industry standard for film/video
- **96 kHz**: Higher quality, more headroom for processing
- **44.1 kHz**: Music standard (less common for film)
- **Recommendation**: 48 kHz minimum, 96 kHz for high-end work

**Bit Depth**:
- **24-bit**: Professional standard
- **16-bit**: Consumer standard (insufficient for production)
- **32-bit float**: Emerging standard
