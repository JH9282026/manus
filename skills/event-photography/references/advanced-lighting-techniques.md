## Bounced Flash Mastery

### Understanding Bounce Flash Physics

Bounced flash transforms a small, harsh light source (flash head) into a large, soft light source (ceiling or wall). The larger the effective light source relative to the subject, the softer and more flattering the light.

**Inverse Square Law**:
- Light intensity decreases with the square of the distance
- Flash → ceiling (8 feet) → subject (8 feet) = 16 feet total travel
- Requires more flash power than direct flash
- Results in softer, more even illumination

### Optimal Bounce Surfaces

**Best Surfaces**:
- **White or Light Neutral Ceiling**: Reflects maximum light, no color cast
- **White Walls**: Side bounce creates directional, dimensional light
- **Height**: 8-12 foot ceilings ideal; higher requires more power

**Surfaces to Avoid**:
- **Colored Surfaces**: Create color casts (red wall = red light on subjects)
- **Dark Surfaces**: Absorb too much light, insufficient reflection
- **Very High Ceilings**: 15+ feet, light becomes too diffused and weak
- **Textured or Patterned**: Can create uneven light distribution

### Bounce Flash Techniques

**Straight Ceiling Bounce**:
- Aim flash directly upward (90°)
- Creates soft, even overhead light
- Good for general coverage
- Can create shadows under eyes and nose
- Solution: Use built-in bounce card to add catchlights

**45° Angle Bounce**:
- Tilt flash 45° behind you toward ceiling
- Light comes from above and slightly in front
- More flattering than straight up
- Reduces under-eye shadows

**Wall Bounce**:
- Aim flash at wall to side of subject
- Creates directional, dimensional light
- Mimics window light
- More dramatic than ceiling bounce
- Position yourself so wall is on subject's "good side"

**Bounce Card Technique**:
- Small white card attached to flash head
- Reflects small amount of light forward while most bounces
- Adds catchlights to eyes
- Fills in shadows under eyes and chin

### Flash Exposure Compensation

Bounced flash often requires adjustment:

- **Start at 0 EV**: Take test shot
- **Too Dark**: Increase flash exposure compensation (+1 to +2 EV)
- **Too Bright**: Decrease flash exposure compensation (-1 to -2 EV)
- **Balanced Look**: Flash should fill shadows, not overpower ambient light
- **Subtle Fill**: -1 to -1.7 EV for natural look with ambient light visible

## Off-Camera Flash Setups

### Single Off-Camera Flash

**Equipment**:
- Speedlight or strobe
- Wireless trigger (radio or optical)
- Light stand
- Modifier (umbrella, softbox, or bare)

**Positioning**:
- **45/45 Rule**: 45° to side, 45° above subject eye level
- Creates dimensional light with natural-looking shadows
- Mimics portrait studio lighting

**Modifiers**:
- **Shoot-Through Umbrella**: Soft, even light, easy setup
- **Reflective Umbrella**: More directional, slightly harder light
- **Small Softbox (24"x24")**: Portable, soft light, more controlled than umbrella
- **Bare Flash**: Hard, dramatic light for edgy look

**Applications**:
- Step-and-repeat photography
- Formal portraits at events
- Small group photos
- VIP headshots

### Two-Light Setup

**Key + Fill Configuration**:

**Key Light**:
- Main light source, 45° to side and above
- Provides primary illumination and defines form
- Typically 2-3x brighter than fill light

**Fill Light**:
- Positioned opposite key light or near camera
- Softens shadows created by key light
- Set to 1/2 to 1/3 power of key light
- Can use reflector instead of second flash

**Key + Rim Configuration**:

**Key Light**:
- Same as above, main illumination

**Rim/Hair Light**:
- Positioned behind subject, aimed at back of head/shoulders
- Creates separation from background
- Adds dimension and polish
- Set to similar or slightly lower power than key

### Step-and-Repeat Photography

**Setup**:
- Branded backdrop (sponsor logos, event branding)
- One or two flashes on light stands
- Consistent positioning for every shot

**Lighting Configuration**:
- **Single Flash**: 45° to side, 6-8 feet from subject, with umbrella or softbox
- **Two Flashes**: One on each side at 45°, equal power for even illumination
- **Camera Settings**: Manual mode, f/5.6-f/8, ISO 100-400, shutter 1/160-1/200s

**Workflow**:
- Set up and test before guests arrive
- Lock camera on tripod for consistent framing
- Use remote trigger or assistant to fire camera
- Shoot 3-5 frames per group for variety
- Quick turnaround—guests waiting in line

## Mixed Lighting Scenarios

### Understanding Color Temperature

**Common Light Sources**:
- **Daylight**: 5000-6500K (blue-white)
- **LED**: 3000-5000K (varies widely)
- **Tungsten/Incandescent**: 2700-3200K (warm orange)
- **Fluorescent**: 4000-5000K (green tint)
- **Candlelight**: 1800-2000K (very warm orange)

**The Problem**:
Mixing light sources creates competing color casts. Camera can only set one white balance, so some lights will appear correct while others have color casts.

### Strategies for Mixed Lighting

**1. Shoot RAW and Correct in Post**:
- Most flexible approach
- Adjust white balance per image or in batches
- Use selective color correction for different areas
- Time-consuming for large galleries

**2. Gel Your Flash to Match Ambient**:
- **CTO (Color Temperature Orange) Gel**: Converts flash (5500K) to tungsten (3200K)
- **CTB (Color Temperature Blue) Gel**: Converts flash to cooler daylight
- **Plus Green Gel**: Matches fluorescent lighting
- Set camera white balance to match ambient light
- Flash now matches ambient, creating consistent color

**3. Overpower Ambient with Flash**:
- Use flash as primary light source
- Reduce ambient light influence by:
  - Increasing shutter speed (up to sync speed, e.g., 1/200s)
  - Closing aperture (f/5.6-f/8)
  - Lowering ISO (100-400)
- Flash becomes dominant light, minimizing mixed lighting
- Can create unnatural look if ambient is completely eliminated

**4. Embrace the Mixed Lighting**:
- Accept warm tungsten and cool daylight in same frame
- Can create mood and atmosphere
- Set white balance for skin tones (most important)
- Allow backgrounds to have color variation
- Works well for candid event photography

### Practical Mixed Lighting Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Reception Hall with Tungsten Uplighting and LED Overheads**:
- **Solution**: Shoot RAW, set white balance to 3500K (compromise)
- Use bounced flash with 1/2 CTO gel to warm flash slightly
- Correct individual images in post as needed

**Scenario 2: Daytime Event with Windows and Interior Tungsten Lights**:
- **Solution**: Position subjects near windows when possible
- Use flash with 1/4 CTO gel to warm slightly
- Set white balance to 4500K
- Expose for window light, use flash as fill

**Scenario 3: Evening Outdoor Event with String Lights and LED Uplighting**:
- **Solution**: Embrace warm glow of string lights
- Set white balance to 3200K for warm, romantic look
- Use flash sparingly, only for fill or key portraits
- Allow ambient light to dominate for atmosphere

## Low-Light Shooting Without Flash

### When Flash is Prohibited or Inappropriate

- Ceremonies (weddings, religious services)
- Theater or concert performances
- Intimate moments (first dance, candlelit dinner)
- Venues with strict no-flash policies

### Camera Settings for Extreme Low Light

**Exposure Triangle Adjustments**:

**ISO**:
- Push to 3200, 6400, or higher
- Modern full-frame cameras handle ISO 12,800+ acceptably
- Noise is preferable to motion blur
- Plan to use noise reduction in post

**Aperture**:
- Shoot wide open (f/1.4, f/1.8, f/2.8)
- Sacrifice depth of field for light gathering
- Focus precisely—shallow DOF is unforgiving

**Shutter Speed**:
- Minimum 1/125s for stationary subjects
- 1/250s for moving subjects
- Use image stabilization to gain 2-3 stops
- Brace yourself or use available support

**Example Settings**:
- **Dim Reception**: ISO 3200, f/2.8, 1/200s
- **Dark Concert**: ISO 6400, f/1.8, 1/250s
- **Candlelit Ceremony**: ISO 5000, f/1.4, 1/160s

### Lens Selection for Low Light

**Fast Primes**:
- **35mm f/1.4**: Wide enough for environmental context, excellent low-light performance
- **50mm f/1.4 or f/1.8**: Classic focal length, affordable, great bokeh
- **85mm f/1.4 or f/1.8**: Portrait length, beautiful compression and subject isolation

**Fast Zooms**:
- **24-70mm f/2.8**: Versatile range, professional standard
- **70-200mm f/2.8**: Reach for distant subjects, excellent for ceremonies

**Image Stabilization**:
- In-body (IBIS) or lens-based stabilization
- Gain 3-5 stops of stabilization
- Allows slower shutter speeds with stationary subjects
- Does NOT freeze subject motion—only camera shake

### Finding and Using Available Light

**Light Sources to Exploit**:
- **Windows**: Position subjects near windows for soft, directional light
- **Lamps and Practical Lights**: Table lamps, floor lamps, decorative lighting
- **String Lights and Uplighting**: Create ambient glow and background interest
- **Candles**: Warm, romantic light (very low intensity, requires high ISO)
- **Stage Lights**: Concerts and performances have dramatic lighting
- **Exit Signs and Ambient Venue Lighting**: Every bit of light helps

**Positioning Strategy**:
- Move subjects toward light sources when possible
- Shoot with light source behind you (illuminating subject's face)
- Backlight can create silhouettes or rim light (artistic but challenging)

### Post-Processing for High-ISO Images

**Noise Reduction**:
- **AI-Powered Tools**: Topaz DeNoise AI, DxO PureRAW, Adobe Denoise
- Apply before other adjustments for best results
- Balance noise reduction with detail preservation
- Luminance noise (grain) more acceptable than color noise (splotches)

**Exposure and Contrast**:
- Lift shadows carefully (reveals more noise)
- Protect highlights (easily blown in low light)
- Add contrast to compensate for flat low-light images

**Sharpening**:
- Apply moderate sharpening
- Over-sharpening emphasizes noise
- Use masking to sharpen only edges, not smooth areas

## Creative Lighting Techniques

### Dragging the Shutter

**Technique**:
- Use slower shutter speed (1/15s to 1/60s) with flash
- Flash freezes subject, slow shutter captures ambient light and motion blur
- Creates dynamic, energetic images

**Applications**:
- Dance floor photography
- Concerts and performances
- Nighttime cityscapes with people

**Settings**:
- **Rear Curtain Sync**: Flash fires at end of exposure, motion blur trails behind subject (more natural)
- **Shutter Speed**: 1/15s to 1/30s for significant blur, 1/60s for subtle effect
- **Aperture**: f/4-f/5.6 to control ambient light
- **ISO**: 400-1600 depending on ambient light level

**Execution**:
- Subject must be relatively still at moment of flash
- Camera movement or subject movement creates blur
- Experiment with different shutter speeds for desired effect

### Silhouettes and Backlighting

**Creating Silhouettes**:
- Position subject between camera and bright light source (window, sunset, stage light)
- Expose for background (bright area)
- Subject becomes dark silhouette
- Emphasizes shape and outline

**Settings**:
- Spot meter on bright background
- Underexpose by 1-2 stops for deep silhouette
- Avoid lens flare by positioning subject to block light source

**Backlit with Fill**:
- Same setup as silhouette
- Add fill flash or reflector to illuminate subject's face
- Creates rim light around subject with visible facial features
- Dramatic and dimensional

### Colored Lighting and Gels

**Using Venue Lighting Creatively**:
- Uplighting, DJ lights, and colored stage lights create mood
- Embrace color rather than correcting it
- Position subjects in colored light for creative effect

**Gelling Your Flash**:
- Attach colored gels to flash for creative effects
- **Warm Gels (CTO)**: Romantic, sunset-like warmth
- **Cool Gels (CTB)**: Modern, edgy, nightclub vibe
- **Creative Colors**: Red, blue, green for artistic portraits

**Mixing Colored Flash with Ambient**:
- Gel flash one color, ambient light another
- Creates separation and visual interest
- Example: Blue-gelled flash on subject, warm tungsten background
