# Skin Tone Correction

## The Skin Tone Line

On vectorscope, natural skin tones fall along a specific angle (~145°) between yellow and red, regardless of ethnicity. This represents universal undertones of human skin.

---

## Skin Tone Correction Process

### Step 1: Isolate Skin
1. Use HSL qualifiers to select skin tones
2. Refine to avoid hair, clothing, background
3. Add masks if needed for precision
4. Feather selection edges

### Step 2: Analyze on Vectorscope
1. Solo the skin selection
2. Check position relative to skin tone line
3. Identify if too saturated, too red/yellow

### Step 3: Correct Hue
- Too orange/red: Rotate toward yellow
- Too yellow: Rotate toward red
- Make subtle adjustments (2-5°)

### Step 4: Adjust Saturation
- Reduce if oversaturated (common issue)
- Slightly increase if washed out
- Different complexions have different natural saturation

### Step 5: Refine Luminance
- Ensure proper exposure on faces
- Maintain detail and texture
- Avoid crushing or blowing out

---

## Skin Tone by Complexion

| Complexion | Common Issues | Corrections |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| Light | Oversaturation, redness | Reduce sat, shift from red |
| Medium | Yellow-green cast | Shift toward warm |
| Dark | Crushed detail, ash | Proper exposure, warmth |

### Universal Principles
- All skin falls on same vectorscope line
- Different saturation and luminance levels
- Natural warmth in all skin tones
- Avoid color casts (green, magenta)

---

## Protecting Skin During Grading

### Skin Protection Workflow
1. Qualify and isolate skin
2. Apply creative grade to everything except skin
3. Grade skin separately with reduced intensity
4. Blend carefully at edges

### Common Problems to Avoid
- Turning skin orange in teal/orange grades
- Making skin look sickly green
- Oversaturating to unnatural levels
- Crushing detail in darker skin
- Blowing out highlights in lighter skin

---

## Face-Specific Corrections

### Power Window Techniques
- Oval mask on face for targeted adjustment
- Soft edges to blend naturally
- Track mask for moving subjects

### Common Face Corrections
- Reduce under-eye shadows
- Even out skin tones
- Reduce blemish visibility
- Add subtle warmth to faces

---

## Multiple Subjects

### Matching Multiple Skin Tones
1. Correct each subject separately
2. Match overall feel and luminance
3. Maintain natural differences
4. Unify creative grade impact

### Group Shots
- Balance exposure across faces
- Unify color temperature
- Consider relative contrast
- Maintain individual character
