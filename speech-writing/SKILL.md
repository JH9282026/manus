# Speech Writing

## Introduction to Speech Writing

Speech writing is the art and craft of creating content specifically designed to be delivered orally to an audience. Unlike other forms of writing, speeches are written for the ear, not the eye—they must sound natural when spoken aloud, connect emotionally with listeners, and achieve their purpose in a single pass without the luxury of re-reading.

Effective speech writing combines storytelling, persuasion, and performance. The speechwriter must understand not only the message but also the speaker's voice, the audience's expectations, and the context of delivery. A well-crafted speech can inspire movements, change minds, celebrate achievements, comfort the grieving, and create lasting memories.

This skill covers the fundamentals of speech writing, from understanding different speech types to mastering rhetorical devices, crafting compelling narratives, and collaborating effectively with speakers.

---

## Why Speech Writing Is Unique

Speech writing differs fundamentally from other forms of writing:

### Written for the Ear, Not the Eye
- **No re-reading**: Listeners can't go back to clarify confusion
- **Real-time processing**: Content must be immediately understandable
- **Rhythm matters**: Words must flow naturally when spoken
- **Pauses are punctuation**: Silence creates emphasis and allows absorption

### The Speaker-Audience Connection
- **Physical presence**: Body language and vocal delivery add meaning
- **Shared experience**: Speaker and audience occupy the same moment
- **Emotional immediacy**: Feelings transfer directly through voice
- **Feedback loops**: Audience reactions influence delivery

### Temporal Constraints
- **Fixed duration**: Speeches have strict time limits
- **Linear progression**: Ideas must build sequentially
- **One chance**: No opportunity for revision during delivery
- **Memory demands**: Content must be memorable in the moment

## Types of Speeches

### Keynote Speeches
**Purpose**: Set the tone for an event, inspire, and unite the audience around a central theme.

**Characteristics**:
- Big-picture thinking and vision
- Inspirational and motivational tone
- Often opens or closes major events
- Typically 20-45 minutes
- Establishes themes others will reference

**Example contexts**: Industry conferences, corporate annual meetings, political conventions

### Executive Speeches
**Purpose**: Communicate organizational vision, strategy, or important announcements.

**Characteristics**:
- Strategic messaging
- Balance of vision and substance
- Stakeholder-focused
- Often includes data and metrics
- Must project leadership and confidence

**Example contexts**: Earnings calls, company town halls, industry presentations, shareholder meetings

### Conference Presentations
**Purpose**: Share expertise, research findings, or insights with professional peers.

**Characteristics**:
- Educational focus
- Evidence-based content
- Clear takeaways
- Often includes visual aids
- Q&A typically follows

**Example contexts**: Academic conferences, industry summits, professional development events

### Commencement Speeches
**Purpose**: Mark a transition, offer wisdom, and inspire graduates.

**Characteristics**:
- Reflective and forward-looking
- Universal themes with personal stories
- Inspirational without being preachy
- Appropriate humor welcome
- Typically 15-20 minutes

**Example contexts**: University graduations, high school ceremonies, professional certifications

### Award Acceptance Speeches
**Purpose**: Express gratitude and share the significance of the recognition.

**Characteristics**:
- Grateful but not gushing
- Acknowledges contributors
- Often brief (2-5 minutes)
- Personal and genuine
- May reference the award's meaning

**Example contexts**: Industry awards, organizational recognition, competitive honors

### Toasts and Roasts
**Purpose**: Celebrate an individual or occasion with warmth and often humor.

**Characteristics**:
- Personal anecdotes and stories
- Appropriate humor (roasts push boundaries; toasts stay warmer)
- Emotional connection
- Brief (toasts: 2-3 minutes; roasts: 5-10 minutes)
- Ends with a tribute or raising glasses

**Example contexts**: Weddings, retirement parties, milestone celebrations, farewell events

### Political Speeches
**Purpose**: Persuade, mobilize, announce policy, or address the public.

**Characteristics**:
- Clear policy positions
- Emotional appeals
- Call to action
- Sound bites for media coverage
- Varies greatly in length

**Example contexts**: Campaign rallies, legislative addresses, press conferences, inaugural addresses

### Motivational Speeches
**Purpose**: Inspire action, change mindsets, or energize audiences.

**Characteristics**:
- Personal transformation stories
- Universal principles
- High energy delivery
- Clear actionable takeaways
- Often emotionally intense

**Example contexts**: Sales conferences, team meetings, self-improvement events, youth programs

### Eulogy and Memorial Speeches
**Purpose**: Honor the deceased and comfort the grieving.

**Characteristics**:
- Celebration of life
- Personal memories and stories
- Acknowledgment of loss
- Comfort and hope
- Typically 5-10 minutes

**Example contexts**: Funeral services, memorial gatherings, tribute events

### Sales Presentations
**Purpose**: Persuade prospects to take action (purchase, partner, invest).

**Characteristics**:
- Problem-solution structure
- Clear value proposition
- Compelling evidence
- Strong call to action
- Often interactive

**Example contexts**: Pitch meetings, product launches, investor presentations, trade shows

## Speech Writing Fundamentals

### Understanding the Audience

Before writing a single word, research your audience thoroughly:

**Demographics**:
- Age range and generational considerations
- Professional background and expertise level
- Cultural and geographic diversity
- Political or social affiliations
- Education level

**Psychographics**:
- What do they care about most?
- What problems keep them up at night?
- What are their aspirations?
- What shared experiences unite them?
- What language and references resonate?

**Relationship to Speaker**:
- How well do they know the speaker?
- What's their existing perception?
- What authority does the speaker have with this audience?
- Are there skeptics who need convincing?

**Audience Questions to Answer**:
- Why should I listen?
- What's in it for me?
- How does this apply to my situation?
- What do you want me to do?

### Defining the Purpose and Message

Every speech needs a clear purpose and central message:

**Purpose Types**:
- **Inform**: Share knowledge, explain concepts, present data
- **Persuade**: Change minds, motivate action, build support
- **Inspire**: Elevate thinking, create possibility, energize
- **Entertain**: Delight, amuse, create memorable moments
- **Commemorate**: Honor, remember, mark occasions

**The Core Message**:
- Distill to one sentence: "If the audience remembers only one thing..."
- Make it specific and actionable
- Ensure it's relevant to the audience
- Test: Can someone repeat it to another person?

**Example**:
- Weak: "Innovation is important for business success."
- Strong: "Small experiments that fail fast lead to breakthrough innovations that last."

### Occasion and Context

The event context shapes every aspect of the speech:

**Formal vs. Informal**:
- Formal: Structured, polished, limited humor
- Informal: Conversational, flexible, warmer tone

**Event Timing**:
- Opening: Set energy, establish themes
- Mid-event: Build on previous content, maintain engagement
- Closing: Synthesize, inspire, call to action

**Physical Environment**:
- Large auditorium vs. intimate room
- Standing vs. seated audience
- Indoor vs. outdoor venue
- Presence of cameras or media

**Preceding and Following Content**:
- What comes before? Build on it.
- What comes after? Set it up.
- Other speakers' topics and styles

### Time Constraints and Length

Speech length varies by type and context:

**General Guidelines**:
- Toast: 2-3 minutes (250-400 words)
- Award acceptance: 2-5 minutes (300-650 words)
- Eulogy: 5-10 minutes (650-1,300 words)
- Conference presentation: 15-20 minutes (2,000-2,600 words)
- Keynote: 20-45 minutes (2,600-5,900 words)
- TED Talk: 18 minutes maximum (2,300 words)

**Words Per Minute**:
- Average speaking rate: 120-150 words per minute
- Slower (emotional content): 100-120 wpm
- Faster (energetic content): 150-170 wpm
- Always build in pause time

### Speaker's Voice and Style

The speech must sound like the speaker, not the writer:

**Researching the Speaker**:
- Listen to previous speeches and interviews
- Note vocabulary preferences and patterns
- Understand comfort with humor
- Identify signature phrases or expressions
- Learn their stories and experiences

**Matching Voice**:
- Use their natural speech patterns
- Incorporate their areas of expertise
- Reference their personal experiences
- Match their comfort level with emotion
- Align with their public persona

## Speech Structure and Organization

### Opening/Introduction

The opening sets everything in motion:

**Functions of the Opening**:
- Capture attention immediately
- Establish connection with audience
- Set tone and energy level
- Preview what's to come
- Build speaker credibility

**Time Allocation**: 10-15% of total speech

### Body (Main Points)

The body delivers the substance:

**Functions of the Body**:
- Develop main arguments or themes
- Provide evidence and examples
- Tell supporting stories
- Build logical progression
- Create emotional peaks

**Time Allocation**: 70-80% of total speech

### Conclusion

The conclusion creates lasting impact:

**Functions of the Conclusion**:
- Summarize key points
- Deliver call to action
- Create emotional climax
- Provide memorable closing
- Leave audience with clear takeaway

**Time Allocation**: 10-15% of total speech

### The Rule of Three

Humans naturally process information in threes:

**Why It Works**:
- Memorable and complete
- Creates rhythm and pattern
- Feels satisfying and balanced
- Easy to follow and recall

**Applications**:
- Three main points
- Three examples per point
- Three-part phrases
- Three-act structure

**Famous Examples**:
- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- "Government of the people, by the people, for the people"
- "Tell them what you're going to tell them, tell them, tell them what you told them"

### Signposting and Transitions

Help the audience follow your journey:

**Signposting Techniques**:
- "I want to share three ideas with you today..."
- "First... Second... Finally..."
- "Now let's turn to..."
- "The most important point is..."

**Transition Types**:
- **Additive**: "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Building on this..."
- **Contrastive**: "However," "On the other hand," "Despite this..."
- **Causal**: "Therefore," "As a result," "This leads us to..."
- **Sequential**: "Next," "Following that," "The final step..."

## Crafting Powerful Openings

### Attention-Grabbing Techniques

The first 30 seconds determine whether you have the audience:

**1. Startling Statistic**
> "Every eight seconds, someone dies from tobacco use. That's one person every eight seconds. In the time it takes me to give this speech, 225 people will have died from tobacco-related causes."

**2. Provocative Question**
> "What if everything you believe about success is wrong? What if the very habits you've cultivated are holding you back?"

**3. Compelling Story**
> "At 3 AM on a Tuesday morning, I found myself standing in a hospital corridor, about to make the hardest decision of my life..."

**4. Bold Statement**
> "Artificial intelligence will eliminate 40% of jobs in this room within the next decade. But that's not the bad news. The bad news is we're not preparing for what comes next."

**5. Vivid Image**
> "Imagine a world where your children's children have never seen a starry night sky—where light pollution has erased the universe from view."

**6. Historical Reference**
> "Exactly 50 years ago today, a man walked on the moon. Today, we stand at another frontier—one that will define the next 50 years."

**7. Personal Confession**
> "I failed. Three times. Publicly. Embarrassingly. And those failures taught me everything I know about success."

### Hooks and Opening Lines

The first sentence matters most:

**Effective Opening Lines**:
- Direct and specific
- Create curiosity or tension
- Promise value to the audience
- Set the emotional tone
- Avoid clichés ("Webster's dictionary defines...")

**Weak vs. Strong**:
- Weak: "Today I'd like to talk about leadership."
- Strong: "The best leader I ever met couldn't read."

### Establishing Credibility

Why should the audience listen to this speaker?

**Credibility Elements**:
- Relevant experience or expertise
- Personal connection to the topic
- Shared identity with the audience
- Acknowledged by the introducer
- Demonstrated through story, not stated directly

**Show, Don't Tell**:
- Weak: "I'm an expert in supply chain management."
- Strong: "After 20 years of building supply chains across four continents, I've learned one thing: the biggest problems never show up on a spreadsheet."

### Preview of Main Points

Let the audience know where you're going:

**Preview Structure**:
> "Today, I'll share three principles that transformed our company: the courage to fail fast, the discipline to listen deeply, and the humility to change course."

**Benefits of Preview**:
- Reduces cognitive load
- Creates anticipation
- Provides roadmap for memory
- Establishes speaker organization

## Developing the Body

### Main Points and Supporting Evidence

Each main point needs robust support:

**Evidence Types**:
- Statistics and data
- Expert testimony
- Case studies
- Research findings
- Historical precedent

**Evidence Criteria**:
- Relevant to the point
- Credible sources
- Current and accurate
- Understandable to the audience
- Memorable when presented

### Stories and Anecdotes

Stories make abstract concepts concrete:

**Story Selection**:
- Illustrates the point clearly
- Features relatable characters
- Contains conflict or challenge
- Has clear resolution or lesson
- Appropriate length for context

**Story Structure**:
- Setup: Introduce character, context, stakes
- Conflict: Present the challenge
- Resolution: Show the outcome
- Lesson: Connect to speech point

### Data and Statistics

Numbers need context to resonate:

**Making Data Memorable**:
- Round numbers when precision isn't critical
- Use comparisons: "That's the population of Texas"
- Visualize: "If you stacked those dollars, they'd reach the moon"
- Personalize: "That's 10 people in this room"
- Repeat the key number

**Example**:
- Weak: "The program saved $47.3 million."
- Strong: "The program saved enough money to hire 500 teachers for 10 years. That's 50,000 students who could have a better future."

### Examples and Illustrations

Concrete examples make concepts accessible:

**Example Selection**:
- Familiar to the audience
- Specific, not generic
- Recent when possible
- Diverse representation
- Memorable and vivid

### Logical Flow and Coherence

Ideas must connect seamlessly:

**Organization Patterns**:
- **Chronological**: Past to present to future
- **Problem-Solution**: Challenge then answer
- **Cause-Effect**: Situation then consequences
- **Topical**: Related themes grouped together
- **Spatial**: Physical or geographic progression
- **Compare-Contrast**: Similarities and differences

## Creating Memorable Conclusions

### Summarizing Key Points

Reinforce without merely repeating:

**Summary Techniques**:
- Restate differently: Use new language for same ideas
- Prioritize: Emphasize the most important point
- Connect: Show how points relate to each other
- Apply: Show audience how to use the information

### Call-to-Action

What should the audience do next?

**Effective Calls-to-Action**:
- Specific and clear
- Achievable and realistic
- Time-bound when appropriate
- Emotionally motivated
- Easy to remember

**CTA Examples**:
- "Before you leave today, I want you to text one person who believed in you when you didn't believe in yourself."
- "Monday morning, schedule one 15-minute meeting with someone outside your department."
- "Tonight, have one conversation with your child without any screens present."

### Inspirational Endings

Leave the audience elevated:

**Inspirational Techniques**:
- Vision of the future
- Challenge to greatness
- Affirmation of capability
- Connection to larger purpose
- Expression of confidence in audience

### Circular Structure (Callback to Opening)

Return to where you started for satisfying closure:

**Circular Technique**:
- Reference the opening story or statement
- Show how understanding has deepened
- Create sense of completion
- Demonstrate the journey traveled

**Example**:
- Opening: "Three years ago, I stood in an empty warehouse with nothing but a dream and a maxed-out credit card."
- Closing: "That empty warehouse is now our headquarters, employing 200 people. Your empty warehouse is out there too. The question is: will you walk through the door?"

### Final Memorable Statement

The last words linger longest:

**Memorable Closing Techniques**:
- Powerful quote (properly attributed)
- Memorable phrase or sentence
- Return to core message
- Challenge or question
- Image that captures the theme

**Famous Closing Lines**:
- "I have a dream today." — Martin Luther King Jr.
- "Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country." — John F. Kennedy
- "Stay hungry. Stay foolish." — Steve Jobs

## Storytelling in Speeches

### The Power of Narrative

Stories are the most powerful communication tool:

**Why Stories Work**:
- Activate multiple brain regions
- Create emotional connection
- Are easier to remember than facts
- Build empathy and understanding
- Make abstract concepts concrete
- Transcend cultural barriers

### Personal Stories vs. Third-Party Stories

Both have their place:

**Personal Stories**:
- Build authenticity and vulnerability
- Establish credibility through experience
- Create emotional connection
- Risk: Can seem self-centered if overused

**Third-Party Stories**:
- Demonstrate broader principles
- Bring in diverse perspectives
- Can be more dramatic
- Risk: Less personal connection

**Balance**: Use personal stories to establish connection, third-party stories to illustrate principles.

### Story Structure

Every good story follows a pattern:

**Basic Structure**:
1. **Setup**: Introduce character, setting, stakes
2. **Conflict**: Present the challenge or obstacle
3. **Rising Action**: Build tension
4. **Climax**: Moment of decision or revelation
5. **Resolution**: Outcome and lesson

**Simplified for Speeches**:
1. **Situation**: What was the context?
2. **Complication**: What went wrong?
3. **Resolution**: How was it solved?
4. **Application**: What does this mean for us?

### Emotional Connection

Stories must connect emotionally:

**Emotional Elements**:
- Relatable characters (like the audience)
- Universal themes (love, fear, hope, struggle)
- Specific sensory details
- Vulnerable moments
- Stakes that matter

### Authenticity

Authentic stories resonate most deeply:

**Authenticity Principles**:
- Tell true stories (or clearly label fiction)
- Include your real emotions
- Acknowledge complexity
- Don't over-polish or rehearse spontaneity
- Share failures, not just successes

## Rhetorical Devices and Techniques

### Repetition and Parallelism

Repetition creates rhythm and emphasis:

**Techniques**:
- Repeat key phrases for emphasis
- Use parallel structure for lists
- Create rhythm through repetition
- Build intensity through accumulation

**Example**:
> "We will fight on the beaches, we will fight on the landing grounds, we will fight in the fields and in the streets, we will fight in the hills." — Winston Churchill

### Metaphors and Analogies

Make complex ideas accessible:

**Effective Metaphors**:
- Familiar to the audience
- Accurately represent the concept
- Create vivid mental images
- Can be extended throughout the speech

**Example**:
> "Life is a journey" allows discussion of paths, obstacles, destinations, companions, crossroads, etc.

### Rhetorical Questions

Engage the audience's thinking:

**Uses**:
- Create curiosity
- Prompt self-reflection
- Transition between points
- Build toward conclusions

**Example**:
> "What would you attempt if you knew you couldn't fail?"

### Alliteration and Assonance

Create pleasing sound patterns:

**Alliteration**: Same consonant sounds
> "Brilliant, bold, and brave leaders..."

**Assonance**: Same vowel sounds
> "The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain."

Use sparingly—too much sounds gimmicky.

### Tricolon (Rule of Three)

Three-part phrases are naturally satisfying:

**Examples**:
- "Veni, vidi, vici" (I came, I saw, I conquered)
- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- "Blood, sweat, and tears"

### Anaphora and Epistrophe

Strategic repetition at beginnings or endings:

**Anaphora**: Same word(s) begin successive phrases
> "I have a dream that one day... I have a dream that one day... I have a dream that one day..."

**Epistrophe**: Same word(s) end successive phrases
> "Government of the people, by the people, for the people..."

## Writing for the Spoken Word

### Conversational Language

Write like people talk:

**Principles**:
- Use contractions (don't, won't, can't)
- Prefer simple words over complex
- Use active voice predominantly
- Include personal pronouns (I, you, we)
- Allow sentence fragments where natural

### Short Sentences and Simple Words

Clarity trumps sophistication:

**Guidelines**:
- Average sentence length: 15-20 words
- Prefer one-syllable words when possible
- Avoid jargon unless universal
- Choose concrete over abstract
- Test by reading aloud

**Example**:
- Written: "The implementation of the strategic initiative resulted in optimization of operational efficiency."
- Spoken: "We made one change. It made everything work better."

### Rhythm and Pacing

Create variation to maintain interest:

**Techniques**:
- Vary sentence length
- Alternate long explanations with short punchy statements
- Use questions to break patterns
- Build to key moments with shorter phrases
- Allow breathing room with longer, flowing passages

### Pauses and Emphasis

Silence is a powerful tool:

**Use Pauses**:
- Before important statements (creates anticipation)
- After important statements (allows absorption)
- To replace "um" and "uh"
- To let laughter or emotion settle
- To regain composure

**Emphasis Markers**:
- "The most important thing I'll say today is..."
- "I want you to really hear this..."
- "Let me say that again..."

### Avoiding Complex Constructions

Simplify for spoken comprehension:

**Avoid**:
- Nested clauses
- Long parenthetical insertions
- Complex conditional structures
- Multiple negatives
- Passive constructions

## Humor in Speeches

### When to Use Humor

Humor serves specific purposes:

**Good Uses**:
- Build rapport and connection
- Relieve tension
- Make points memorable
- Demonstrate humanity
- Create energy shift

**When to Avoid**:
- Serious or solemn occasions (unless very skilled)
- When it undermines credibility
- When timing is wrong
- When it distracts from the message

### Types of Humor

**Self-Deprecating**: Safest form; shows humility
> "When I was asked to speak about success, I called my mom. She said, 'Finally, something you know about.'"

**Observational**: Shared experiences the audience recognizes
> "We've all sat through that meeting that could have been an email..."

**Anecdotal**: Funny stories from personal experience
> Real, specific moments that naturally produce laughter

**Situational**: References to the event or context
> Jokes about the venue, timing, or shared experience

### Timing and Delivery

Humor requires precise execution:

**Principles**:
- Commit fully—hesitation kills jokes
- Pause before the punchline
- Don't signal jokes are coming
- Don't laugh at your own jokes (before delivery)
- Have a recovery if it doesn't land
- Move on quickly from failed humor

### Cultural Sensitivity

Know your audience's boundaries:

**Considerations**:
- What's funny varies culturally
- Professional vs. casual settings differ
- Generation gaps in humor references
- Translation challenges for international audiences
- When in doubt, leave it out

### Avoiding Offensive Humor

Safe ground only:

**Never Use**:
- Jokes about race, gender, religion, disability
- Sexual humor
- Political humor (in most contexts)
- Humor at others' expense
- Inside jokes that exclude audience members

## Emotional Appeal and Persuasion

### Ethos, Pathos, Logos

Aristotle's three modes of persuasion:

**Ethos (Credibility)**:
- Speaker's character and expertise
- Established through experience and knowledge
- Built through honesty and goodwill
- Demonstrated, not claimed

**Pathos (Emotion)**:
- Appeal to audience feelings
- Created through stories and vivid language
- Connects message to values
- Motivates action beyond logic

**Logos (Logic)**:
- Appeal to reason and evidence
- Supported by facts, data, examples
- Follows clear argument structure
- Addresses counterarguments

**Balance**: Most speeches need all three; adjust proportions based on purpose and audience.

### Emotional Triggers

Understand what moves people:

**Core Emotions**:
- Hope and aspiration
- Fear and anxiety
- Pride and belonging
- Anger and injustice
- Love and connection
- Curiosity and wonder

### Building Empathy

Help audience feel what you feel:

**Techniques**:
- Specific sensory details
- Present-tense narration
- Direct address ("Imagine you...")
- Universal experiences
- Vulnerable admission

### Inspirational Messaging

Elevate the audience's sense of possibility:

**Elements**:
- Vision of better future
- Confidence in audience capability
- Call to higher purpose
- Challenge with support
- Connection to something larger

## Collaborating with the Speaker

### Understanding the Speaker's Voice

The speech must sound like them:

**Research Methods**:
- Review previous speeches and presentations
- Watch interviews and media appearances
- Read their writing
- Note vocabulary, cadence, and style
- Understand their comfort zones

### Interview and Research Process

Gather essential information:

**Key Questions**:
- What's the single most important message?
- What stories from your experience illustrate this?
- What do you want the audience to feel?
- What do you want them to do afterward?
- What are you worried about with this speech?
- Who will be in the audience?

### Drafting and Revision

Collaborative iteration:

**Process**:
1. Initial brief and interview
2. Outline development and approval
3. First draft
4. Speaker review and feedback
5. Revision(s)
6. Read-through together
7. Final polish

### Rehearsal and Feedback

Practice makes perfect:

**Rehearsal Process**:
- Read through for flow
- Time the full delivery
- Identify trouble spots
- Practice transitions
- Adjust for natural delivery
- Final run-through

### Speaker Comfort and Confidence

Set the speaker up for success:

**Support Elements**:
- Speech in preferred format (full text, outline, cards)
- Backup copies
- Key phrases highlighted
- Pronunciation guides for difficult words
- Confident, not apologetic, language

## Speech Length and Timing

### Words Per Minute Guidelines

Calculate accurately:

| Speaking Style | Words Per Minute |
|---------------|------------------|
| Slow/Deliberate | 100-120 wpm |
| Moderate | 120-150 wpm |
| Fast/Energetic | 150-170 wpm |
| Average | 130 wpm |

### Adjusting for Delivery Style

Factors affecting timing:

- Emotional content = slower delivery
- Technical content = slower for comprehension
- Humor = add pause time for laughter
- International audiences = slower and clearer
- High-energy motivational = can be faster

### Building in Pause Time

Account for non-speaking moments:

**Add Time For**:
- Dramatic pauses
- Audience response (laughter, applause)
- Slide transitions
- Movement on stage
- Drinking water
- Unplanned moments

**Rule of Thumb**: Add 10-15% to calculated time.

### Timing Different Speech Types

| Speech Type | Duration | Word Count |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| Toast | 2-3 min | 250-400 |
| Award acceptance | 2-5 min | 300-650 |
| Eulogy | 5-10 min | 650-1,300 |
| TED Talk | 18 min max | 2,300 |
| Conference presentation | 15-20 min | 2,000-2,600 |
| Keynote | 20-45 min | 2,600-5,900 |

## Visual Aids and Slides

### When to Use Slides

Slides support, not replace, the speech:

**Use Slides For**:
- Data visualization
- Images that add meaning
- Complex processes or diagrams
- Key quotes or statistics
- Emotional imagery

**Avoid Slides When**:
- Content is purely narrative
- Slides would distract
- Technical problems are likely
- Intimacy is essential

### Slide Design Principles

Less is more:

**Guidelines**:
- One idea per slide
- Minimal text (6 words or fewer)
- High-contrast, readable fonts
- Professional, consistent design
- No clip art or cheesy images
- Full-bleed impactful images

### Integrating Visuals with Speech

Seamless connection:

**Techniques**:
- Don't read slides verbatim
- Pause while audience absorbs visual
- Refer to slides explicitly when needed
- Know your transitions cold
- Have non-slide backup

### Avoiding Slide Dependency

The speaker is primary:

**Principles**:
- Slides enhance, don't carry
- Be able to deliver without slides
- Don't let slides dictate structure
- Maintain eye contact with audience, not screen
- Practice with and without slides

## Adapting Speeches for Different Contexts

### Virtual vs. In-Person Speeches

Each environment demands adjustment:

**Virtual Adaptations**:
- Shorter overall length (20% reduction)
- More frequent engagement moments
- Direct address to camera
- Consideration of technical issues
- Simplified visual aids
- More explicit transitions

**In-Person Advantages**:
- Physical presence and movement
- Direct audience feedback
- More flexibility in delivery
- Natural energy exchange
- Longer attention spans

### Large vs. Small Audiences

Scale affects intimacy:

**Large Audience**:
- Bigger gestures and voice
- Less personal interaction
- Universal themes and examples
- Cleaner, bolder visuals
- Strong opening and closing

**Small Audience**:
- More conversational
- Can include interaction
- More personal stories
- Eye contact with individuals
- Flexibility to adjust

### Formal vs. Informal Settings

Match the tone to the context:

**Formal**:
- Polished language
- Complete sentences
- Limited humor
- Professional demeanor
- Structured delivery

**Informal**:
- Conversational tone
- Flexibility in structure
- More humor welcome
- Personal stories
- Interactive elements

### International Audiences

Cross-cultural considerations:

**Adaptations**:
- Slower, clearer delivery
- Avoid idioms and colloquialisms
- Universal references and examples
- Cultural sensitivity in humor
- Awareness of translation needs
- Respect for different communication styles

## Speech Delivery Considerations

### Reading vs. Memorization vs. Notes

Choose the right approach:

**Reading (Full Script)**:
- Pros: Precise wording, confidence
- Cons: Less natural, limits eye contact
- Best for: Policy announcements, legal precision

**Memorization**:
- Pros: Natural delivery, freedom of movement
- Cons: Risk of forgetting, can sound rehearsed
- Best for: Short speeches, experienced speakers

**Notes/Outline**:
- Pros: Balance of preparation and spontaneity
- Cons: Requires more skill, can lose place
- Best for: Most presentations, confident speakers

**Hybrid**: Memorize opening and closing, use notes for body

### Teleprompter Usage

Professional delivery aid:

**Tips**:
- Practice with the equipment
- Break text into short phrases
- Vary speed to avoid robotic delivery
- Know content well enough to ad-lib
- Have backup printed script

### Vocal Variety and Emphasis

Voice is an instrument:

**Elements**:
- **Pace**: Speed up for excitement, slow for emphasis
- **Volume**: Louder for energy, softer for intimacy
- **Pitch**: Vary to avoid monotone
- **Pauses**: Strategic silence for impact
- **Articulation**: Clear pronunciation

### Body Language and Gestures

Physical presence matters:

**Guidelines**:
- Open posture (uncrossed arms)
- Purposeful movement (not pacing)
- Eye contact across audience
- Natural gestures that enhance message
- Grounded, confident stance

## Editing and Refining Speeches

### Reading Aloud

The essential test:

**Process**:
- Read every draft aloud
- Listen for awkward phrasing
- Note where you run out of breath
- Mark natural pause points
- Identify tongue-twisters

### Cutting Unnecessary Content

Less is often more:

**What to Cut**:
- Redundant points
- Weak examples
- Tangents
- Filler phrases
- Anything that doesn't serve the purpose

**Editing Rule**: If in doubt, cut it out.

### Strengthening Weak Sections

Identify and improve:

**Warning Signs**:
- Vague language
- Passive voice
- Missing examples
- Weak transitions
- Unclear purpose

**Strengthening Techniques**:
- Add specific examples
- Include stories
- Sharpen language
- Improve transitions
- Cut and redistribute time

### Polishing Language

Final refinement:

**Polish Areas**:
- Opening sentence (perfect it)
- Closing statement (memorize it)
- Key phrases (make them quotable)
- Transitions (smooth them)
- Stories (add sensory details)

## Common Speech Writing Mistakes

### Content Mistakes
1. **Too many points**: Trying to cover everything dilutes impact
2. **No clear message**: Audience leaves confused
3. **All facts, no stories**: Information without emotion
4. **All stories, no substance**: Entertainment without value
5. **Wrong audience assumptions**: Missing the mark

### Structure Mistakes
6. **Weak opening**: Losing audience immediately
7. **No clear organization**: Ideas scattered randomly
8. **Missing transitions**: Jumping between points
9. **Trailing off**: No memorable conclusion
10. **Running long**: Disrespecting audience time

### Style Mistakes
11. **Written, not spoken**: Language too formal
12. **Jargon overload**: Alienating audience
13. **Clichés**: Boring and forgettable
14. **Speaker voice mismatch**: Doesn't sound authentic
15. **Trying too hard**: Forced humor or emotion

### Delivery Mistakes
16. **Over-scripting**: No room for natural delivery
17. **Under-preparing**: Hoping for spontaneity
18. **Ignoring timing**: Running over or under
19. **Slide dependency**: Reading from screens
20. **No rehearsal**: Discovering problems on stage

## Best Practices for Speech Writing

### Before Writing
1. **Research thoroughly**: Audience, occasion, speaker
2. **Define the purpose**: One clear objective
3. **Identify the core message**: What must they remember?
4. **Gather material**: Stories, data, examples
5. **Create outline**: Structure before sentences

### During Writing
6. **Write for the ear**: Read everything aloud
7. **Use the rule of three**: For structure and emphasis
8. **Include stories**: Make abstract concrete
9. **Match speaker voice**: Authentic to them
10. **Build emotional arc**: Don't be flat throughout

### After Writing
11. **Cut ruthlessly**: Less is more
12. **Read aloud repeatedly**: Catch all issues
13. **Time accurately**: Include pauses
14. **Collaborate with speaker**: Get their input
15. **Prepare for delivery**: Right format for speaker

### Speech Writing Checklist

**Pre-Writing**:
- [ ] Audience clearly defined
- [ ] Purpose identified
- [ ] Core message crystallized
- [ ] Time constraints known
- [ ] Speaker voice researched

**Draft Review**:
- [ ] Strong, attention-grabbing opening

- [ ] Stories and examples included
- [ ] Transitions smooth
- [ ] Memorable conclusion
- [ ] Call-to-action clear
- [ ] Appropriate for occasion
- [ ] Matches speaker voice

**Final Check**:
- [ ] Read aloud completely
- [ ] Timed accurately
- [ ] Approved by speaker
- [ ] Backup copies ready
- [ ] Delivery format prepared

## Conclusion

Speech writing is a craft that combines strategic thinking, emotional intelligence, storytelling ability, and technical skill. The best speeches don't just convey information—they move people to think differently, feel deeply, and act decisively.

Remember: every speech is written for a specific speaker, delivered to a specific audience, on a specific occasion. There are no templates for greatness, only principles to guide you. Master the fundamentals, study the greats, practice relentlessly, and always keep the audience at the center of every word you write.

The goal isn't a perfect speech on paper—it's a perfect moment between speaker and audience, where words become connection, information becomes inspiration, and a message becomes a memory that changes something in the world.
