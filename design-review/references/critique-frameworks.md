# Design Critique Frameworks

## Overview

Design critique is a structured evaluation of design work focused on improvement, not judgment. Effective critique requires clear frameworks, psychological safety, and focus on objectives rather than personal preferences.

---

## Critique vs Feedback

**Critique**: Structured, objective evaluation against goals and principles. Focuses on 'does this solve the problem?' Example: 'The hierarchy isn't clear because the headings are the same size as body text.'

**Feedback**: Subjective reactions and preferences. Focuses on 'do I like this?' Example: 'I don't like the blue color.'

**Why Critique is Better**: Feedback is personal and hard to act on. Critique is objective and actionable. Critique builds design skills. Feedback just collects opinions.

---

## The Critique Framework

**1. Context**: What problem is this solving? Who is the user? What are the constraints? What stage is this design in?

**2. Objectives**: What is this design trying to achieve? What are the success criteria?

**3. Evaluation**: How well does the design meet the objectives? What works? What doesn't? Why?

**4. Suggestions**: What specific changes would improve the design? What alternatives could be explored?

**5. Next Steps**: What should the designer do next? What needs more exploration?

---

## Liz Lerman's Critical Response Process

**Step 1 - Statements of Meaning**: Responders share what was meaningful, interesting, or exciting. Builds positive foundation.

**Step 2 - Designer Questions**: Designer asks specific questions they need answered. Responders provide focused feedback.

**Step 3 - Neutral Questions**: Responders ask neutral questions to understand intent. 'What were you trying to achieve with X?' Not 'Why did you make X so ugly?'

**Step 4 - Permissioned Opinions**: Responders ask permission before offering opinions. 'I have an idea about the navigation. Would you like to hear it?' Designer can decline.

**Benefits**: Designer maintains control, reduces defensiveness, focuses on specific needs, builds psychological safety.

## Evaluation Criteria

**Usability**: Can users accomplish their goals? Is the interface learnable? Are errors preventable/recoverable?

**Accessibility**: Can people with disabilities use this? Does it meet WCAG standards? Is it keyboard navigable?

**Visual Design**: Is the hierarchy clear? Is it aesthetically pleasing? Does it match brand guidelines?

**Content**: Is the copy clear and concise? Is the tone appropriate? Is information architecture logical?

**Technical Feasibility**: Can this be built within constraints? Are there performance concerns? Does it work across devices?

**Business Goals**: Does this support business objectives? Will it drive desired user behaviors? Is it differentiated from competitors?

---

## Giving Effective Critique

**Be Specific**: 'The navigation is confusing' is vague. 'The navigation has 12 top-level items, which exceeds the 7±2 rule for working memory' is specific.

**Be Objective**: Ground critique in principles, not preferences. 'I don't like blue' is subjective. 'The blue fails contrast requirements' is objective.

**Focus on Problems, Not Solutions**: Describe what isn't working and why. Let designer solve it. Exception: If you have a specific solution to suggest, frame it as one option to consider.

**Balance Positive and Negative**: Start with what works well. Builds trust and shows you're not just criticizing.

**Ask Questions**: 'What were you trying to achieve here?' or 'Have you considered X?' Questions invite dialogue rather than dictating changes.

---

## Receiving Critique

**Don't Defend**: Resist the urge to explain or justify. Just listen and take notes. You can clarify if something is misunderstood, but don't argue.

**Ask Clarifying Questions**: If critique is vague, ask for specifics. 'Can you show me where the hierarchy breaks down?'

**Separate Self from Work**: Critique of your design is not critique of you. Emotional distance is hard but essential.

**Take Time to Process**: You don't need to respond immediately. Thank reviewers, reflect on feedback, then decide what to act on.

**You Don't Have to Implement Everything**: Critique is input, not orders. Consider all feedback, but make your own decisions about what to change.
