# Stakeholder Review Management

## Overview

Stakeholder reviews gather input from business leaders, product managers, and other decision-makers. Managing these reviews requires clear communication, expectation setting, and structured feedback collection.

---

## Pre-Review Preparation

**Set Clear Objectives**: What decisions need to be made? What feedback do you need? What's out of scope for this review?

**Provide Context**: Share background before the meeting. Include problem statement, user research findings, design goals, and constraints.

**Choose the Right Fidelity**: Early reviews can use low-fidelity wireframes. Later reviews need high-fidelity mockups. Match fidelity to the questions you need answered.

**Prepare Presentation**: Walk through user flows, not just static screens. Tell a story. Explain your rationale.

**Anticipate Concerns**: Think about what stakeholders might object to. Prepare responses or alternatives.

---

## Running the Review

**Start with Context**: Remind everyone of the problem, users, and goals. Don't assume they remember from last time.

**Present the Design**: Walk through key flows and screens. Explain your thinking. Point out specific areas where you need input.

**Facilitate Discussion**: Ask specific questions. 'Does this approach align with our business goals?' is better than 'What do you think?'

**Capture Feedback**: Designate a note-taker or record the session. Capture both what was said and who said it.

**Manage Scope**: If discussion goes off-topic, acknowledge the concern and suggest addressing it separately. 'That's important. Let's add it to the parking lot and come back to it.'

**Close with Next Steps**: Summarize decisions made, open questions, and what you'll do next. Confirm timeline.

---

## Handling Difficult Feedback

**The HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)**: Senior stakeholder overrides design with personal preference. 

**Response**: Ground discussion in user research and business goals. 'That's an interesting idea. Our research showed users expect X. How do you think we should balance that?'

**Design by Committee**: Everyone has conflicting opinions. No consensus.

**Response**: Acknowledge all input, then propose testing multiple approaches. 'I'm hearing different perspectives. Let's prototype two versions and test with users.'

**Scope Creep**: Stakeholders request additional features or changes beyond original scope.

**Response**: Acknowledge the request, explain impact on timeline/resources, and ask for prioritization. 'We can add that, but it will delay launch by two weeks. Is that acceptable?'

**Vague Feedback**: 'Make it pop' or 'It doesn't feel right.'

**Response**: Ask probing questions. 'What specifically isn't working? Can you show me an example of what you mean?'

---

## Managing Conflicting Feedback

**Identify the Root Cause**: Are stakeholders solving for different problems? Do they have different assumptions about users?

**Find Common Ground**: What do all stakeholders agree on? Start there and work outward.

**Prioritize by Impact**: Which feedback addresses the most critical user needs or business goals?

**Propose Testing**: When opinions conflict, suggest user testing to get objective data.

**Escalate if Necessary**: If stakeholders can't align, escalate to a decision-maker. Present options with pros/cons and recommend a path forward.

---

## Post-Review Follow-up

**Send Summary**: Document decisions, action items, and open questions. Share with all attendees within 24 hours.

**Clarify Ambiguities**: If anything was unclear, follow up individually with stakeholders before proceeding.

**Update Designs**: Incorporate approved changes. If you're deviating from feedback, explain why.

**Share Progress**: Keep stakeholders updated on implementation. Prevents surprises and builds trust.

**Close the Loop**: When design is launched, share results. Show how their input contributed to success.
