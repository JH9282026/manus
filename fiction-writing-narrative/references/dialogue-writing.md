# Dialogue Writing

Comprehensive guide to crafting authentic, purposeful dialogue that reveals character, advances plot, and creates tension.

## Functions of Dialogue

### 1. Reveal Character

**Personality Through Speech**:
- Word choice reveals education, background, personality
- Sentence structure shows thinking style (analytical, emotional, scattered)
- Topics discussed reveal interests, values, obsessions
- What's avoided reveals fears, shame, secrets

**Example**:
- **Formal character**: "I must respectfully disagree with your assessment."
- **Casual character**: "Nah, you're way off on that one."
- **Evasive character**: "Well, that's... I mean, it depends on how you look at it."

---

### 2. Advance Plot

**Information Exchange**:
- Characters share clues, plans, decisions
- Reveals new information to reader through conversation
- Moves story forward through character choices expressed in dialogue

**Action Through Dialogue**:
- Characters make decisions ("I'm going to confront him.")
- Characters take verbal action (confessions, accusations, proposals)
- Dialogue itself is the event (argument, negotiation, revelation)

**Avoid**: Exposition dumps where characters explain things they both already know just to inform reader.

---

### 3. Create Conflict and Tension

**Disagreement**:
- Characters want different things
- Opposing viewpoints clash
- Arguments and debates

**Misunderstanding**:
- Characters misinterpret each other
- Assumptions lead to conflict
- Lack of communication creates problems

**Subtext and Tension**:
- What's unsaid creates tension
- Characters dance around difficult topics
- Underlying emotions simmer beneath surface

---

### 4. Establish Relationships

**Dynamics**:
- Power balance (who dominates, who defers)
- Intimacy level (formal vs. familiar)
- History (references to shared past)
- Emotional connection (affection, resentment, obligation)

**Change**:
- Relationships evolve through dialogue
- Reconciliation, betrayal, deepening bonds
- Shifts in power or intimacy

---

### 5. Convey Subtext

**Subtext**: The real meaning beneath the words

**Techniques**:
- Characters say one thing, mean another
- Evasion and deflection
- Sarcasm and irony
- Silence and what's not said

**Example**:
- **Surface**: "That's a lovely dress."
- **Subtext** (with context): "I can't believe you wore that to my wedding."

---

## Dialogue Best Practices

### Make Each Voice Distinct

**Vocabulary**:
- Educated character uses complex words
- Working-class character uses simpler, direct language
- Teenager uses current slang
- Historical character uses period-appropriate language

**Sentence Structure**:
- Analytical character speaks in complete, logical sentences
- Emotional character speaks in fragments, run-ons
- Confident character speaks in declarative statements
- Uncertain character speaks in questions, qualifiers

**Speech Patterns**:
- Catchphrases or repeated words
- Verbal tics (\"um,\" \"like,\" \"you know\")
- Unique expressions or metaphors
- Accent or dialect (use sparingly, avoid stereotypes)

**Test**: Cover character names. Can you tell who's speaking by voice alone?

---

### Keep It Purposeful

**Every Line Should**:
- Reveal character
- Advance plot
- Create conflict or tension
- Establish or develop relationships
- Ideally, do multiple things at once

**Cut**:
- Unnecessary greetings (\"Hi.\" \"How are you?\" \"Fine, thanks.\")
- Small talk that doesn't reveal character or create tension
- Repetitive information
- Exposition that could be shown through action

**Exception**: Small talk can be purposeful if it reveals character (awkward character makes stilted small talk) or creates tension (characters avoid important topic with small talk).

---

### Sound Natural Yet Crafted

**Natural Elements**:
- Contractions (\"I'm\" not \"I am\")
- Incomplete sentences (\"You coming?\" not \"Are you coming?\")
- Interruptions and overlaps
- Pauses and trailing off (\"I thought maybe we could... never mind.\")
- Filler words (\"um,\" \"uh,\" \"well\") - use sparingly

**Crafted Elements**:
- More concise than real speech (cut rambling)
- More purposeful than real speech (every line serves function)
- More interesting than real speech (avoid boring exchanges)

**Balance**: Dialogue should feel real without being a transcript of actual speech.

**Technique**: Read dialogue aloud. Does it sound like something a person would actually say?

---

### Use Dialogue Tags Sparingly

**\"Said\" is Invisible**:
- Readers' eyes skip over \"said\"
- Use \"said\" as default tag
- Avoid fancy synonyms (\"exclaimed,\" \"ejaculated,\" \"opined\")

**Action Beats Instead of Tags**:
- **Tag**: \"I don't believe you,\" she said angrily.
- **Beat**: \"I don't believe you.\" She slammed the door.

**When to Use Tags**:
- Clarify who's speaking (especially with multiple characters)
- After long stretch of untagged dialogue
- When action beat doesn't fit

**When to Skip Tags**:
- Two-person conversation (alternate speakers, readers can follow)
- Voice is so distinct reader knows who's speaking
- Action beat makes speaker clear

---

### Avoid Adverbs in Tags

**Problem**: \"He said angrily\" tells emotion instead of showing it.

**Solution**: Show emotion through dialogue itself or action beat.

**Examples**:
- **Weak**: \"I hate you,\" she said sadly.
- **Strong**: \"I hate you.\" Her voice broke. \"I really do.\"

- **Weak**: \"Get out,\" he said angrily.
- **Strong**: \"Get out.\" He pointed at the door, hand shaking.

---

## Dialogue Formatting

### Basic Rules

**Punctuation Inside Quotes**:
- \"I'm leaving,\" she said.
- \"Are you sure?\" he asked.
- \"Yes!\" she shouted.

**Comma Before Tag**:
- \"I don't know,\" he said.
- NOT: \"I don't know.\" He said.

**Period After Tag**:
- She said, \"I'm tired.\"
- \"I'm tired,\" she said.

**New Paragraph for New Speaker**:
```
\"Are you coming?\" John asked.
\"I don't think so,\" Mary replied.
\"Why not?\"
\"I'm tired.\"
```

---

### Action Beats

**Format**:
- Action beat is separate sentence, not part of dialogue tag
- Use period after dialogue, not comma

**Examples**:
- **Correct**: \"I'm leaving.\" She grabbed her coat.
- **Incorrect**: \"I'm leaving,\" she grabbed her coat.

**Placement**:
- Before dialogue: She grabbed her coat. \"I'm leaving.\"
- After dialogue: \"I'm leaving.\" She grabbed her coat.
- Middle of dialogue: \"I'm leaving.\" She grabbed her coat. \"Don't try to stop me.\"

---

### Long Speeches

**Multiple Paragraphs**:
- Open quote at start of each paragraph
- Close quote only at end of final paragraph

**Example**:
```
\"I need to tell you something,\" he began. \"Something I should have said years ago.

\"When we were kids, I made a mistake. A terrible mistake that I've regretted every day since.

\"I'm the one who told them where you were hiding.\"
```

**Alternative**: Break long speech with action beats or other character's reactions.

---

### Internal Dialogue

**Thoughts**:
- Can use italics (optional)
- No quotation marks
- Use same POV as narration

**Examples**:
- *What am I doing?* she thought.
- What am I doing? (in close third person, thought tag often unnecessary)

**Balance**: Don't overuse internal dialogue. Show thoughts through action and external dialogue when possible.

---

## Subtext and Implication

### Creating Subtext

**Say One Thing, Mean Another**:
- Surface meaning vs. true meaning
- Context and tone reveal real message

**Example**:
- \"That's an interesting choice.\" (Surface: neutral observation. Subtext: I think it's a bad choice.)

**Techniques**:
- Sarcasm and irony
- Understatement
- Deflection and evasion
- Silence (what's not said)

---

### Evasion and Deflection

**Character Avoids Topic**:
- Changes subject
- Gives vague answer
- Answers different question
- Uses humor to deflect

**Example**:
```
\"Where were you last night?\"
\"Why do you ask?\"
\"Just answer the question.\"
\"I was out.\"
\"Out where?\"
\"Does it matter?\"
```

**Purpose**: Creates tension, reveals character's discomfort or secrecy.

---

### Silence and Pauses

**What's Not Said**:
- Character refuses to answer
- Awkward silence
- Trailing off mid-sentence

**Formatting**:
- Em dash for interruption: \"I thought we could—\"
- Ellipsis for trailing off: \"I don't know...\"
- Action beat for pause: \"I love you.\" He waited. She said nothing.

**Purpose**: Silence can be more powerful than words. Shows character's inability or unwillingness to speak.

---

## Dialogue and Conflict

### Disagreement

**Direct Conflict**:
- Characters argue openly
- Opposing viewpoints clash
- Raised voices, accusations

**Example**:
```
\"You're wrong.\"
\"I'm not wrong. You're just too stubborn to admit I'm right.\"
\"Stubborn? You're the one who won't listen to reason!\"
```

---

### Passive-Aggressive Conflict

**Indirect Conflict**:
- Polite surface, hostile subtext
- Backhanded compliments
- Veiled insults

**Example**:
```
\"I love what you've done with the place. So... eclectic.\"
\"Thank you. I was going for 'lived-in,' not 'sterile' like some people.\"
\"Oh, I'm sure you succeeded.\"
```

---

### Misunderstanding

**Characters Talk Past Each Other**:
- Different assumptions
- Miscommunication
- Lack of clarity

**Example**:
```
\"I need you to be honest with me.\"
\"I am being honest.\"
\"Then why won't you tell me where you were?\"
\"Because it doesn't matter.\"
\"It matters to me!\"
\"That's not what I meant.\"
```

**Purpose**: Creates frustration and tension, drives plot through miscommunication.

---

## Dialogue Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. \"As You Know\" Dialogue

**Problem**: Characters explain things they both already know, just to inform reader.

**Example**:
```
\"As you know, Bob, we've been married for twenty years and have three children.\"
```

**Solution**: Find natural way to convey information, or trust reader to infer from context.

---

### 2. On-the-Nose Dialogue

**Problem**: Characters state exactly what they feel or mean, no subtext.

**Example**:
```
\"I'm angry at you because you forgot my birthday.\"
```

**Solution**: Show emotion through subtext, action, and implication.

**Better**:
```
\"Did you have a nice day?\"
\"Fine.\"
\"Anything special happen?\"
\"Nope. Nothing at all.\"
```

---

### 3. All Characters Sound the Same

**Problem**: Every character has same voice, vocabulary, speech patterns.

**Solution**: Develop distinct voice for each character based on background, personality, age, education.

**Test**: Cover names and see if you can tell who's speaking.

---

### 4. Overuse of Names

**Problem**: Characters use each other's names too often in conversation.

**Example**:
```
\"John, I need to talk to you.\"
\"What is it, Mary?\"
\"John, I'm worried about you.\"
\"Don't worry about me, Mary.\"
```

**Solution**: People rarely use names in conversation. Use names only for emphasis or clarity.

**Better**:
```
\"I need to talk to you.\"
\"What is it?\"
\"I'm worried about you.\"
\"Don't be.\"
```

---

### 5. Overly Formal or Perfect Speech

**Problem**: Characters speak in complete, grammatically perfect sentences.

**Example**:
```
\"I am going to the store. Would you like me to purchase anything for you?\"
```

**Solution**: Use contractions, fragments, and natural speech patterns.

**Better**:
```
\"I'm heading to the store. Need anything?\"
```

---

### 6. Exposition Dumps

**Problem**: Characters deliver long speeches of backstory or explanation.

**Example**:
```
\"As you know, our father died when we were young, leaving our mother to raise us alone. She worked two jobs to support us, and we never had much money, but we were happy. Then, when I was sixteen, she got sick...\"
```

**Solution**: Weave information naturally through conversation, or use narration for necessary backstory.

---

### 7. Overuse of Dialect

**Problem**: Heavy dialect is difficult to read and can feel stereotypical.

**Example**:
```
\"Ah reckon we oughta be gittin' on down ta th' store 'fore it gits too late, don'tcha think?\"
```

**Solution**: Suggest accent through word choice, rhythm, and occasional dialect words, not phonetic spelling.

**Better**:
```
\"Reckon we should head to the store before it gets too late, don't you think?\"
```

---

## Dialogue Techniques

### Interruption

**Purpose**: Shows urgency, conflict, or character dynamics.

**Format**: Use em dash (—)

**Example**:
```
\"I think we should—\"
\"I don't care what you think.\"
```

---

### Overlapping Dialogue

**Purpose**: Creates chaos, urgency, or realism in group scenes.

**Technique**: Indicate multiple characters speaking at once.

**Example**:
```
\"We need to leave now,\" John said.
\"But what about—\" Mary started.
\"There's no time!\" Sarah interrupted.
\"Everyone calm down,\" Tom said, but no one listened.
```

---

### Silence as Dialogue

**Purpose**: Powerful response, shows character's inability or refusal to speak.

**Example**:
```
\"Do you love me?\"
He said nothing.
\"Answer me.\"
Still, he remained silent.
```

---

### Subtext Through Context

**Technique**: Same words mean different things in different contexts.

**Example 1** (Romantic):
```
\"I've been thinking about you.\"
She smiled. \"Have you?\"
```

**Example 2** (Threatening):
```
\"I've been thinking about you.\"
She stepped back. \"Have you?\"
```

**Context** (action beats, setting, prior events) changes meaning of identical dialogue.

---

## Dialogue in Different Genres

### Literary Fiction

**Characteristics**:
- Subtle, layered subtext
- Realistic, often mundane topics
- Character-focused, less plot-driven
- May include long, philosophical exchanges

**Example**:
```
\"Do you ever wonder if we're happy?\"
\"I don't know. Are we supposed to wonder?\"
\"I suppose not.\"
```

---

### Thriller/Mystery

**Characteristics**:
- Terse, fast-paced
- Information reveals clues
- Interrogation and confrontation
- Evasion and secrets

**Example**:
```
\"Where were you at midnight?\"
\"Home.\"
\"Alone?\"
\"Yes.\"
\"Can anyone verify that?\"
\"No.\"
```

---

### Romance

**Characteristics**:
- Emotional, intimate
- Banter and flirtation
- Vulnerability and confession
- Misunderstandings and reconciliation

**Example**:
```
\"I can't stop thinking about you.\"
\"You shouldn't say things like that.\"
\"Why not?\"
\"Because it makes it harder to pretend I don't feel the same way.\"
```

---

### Fantasy/Science Fiction

**Characteristics**:
- World-building through dialogue
- Exposition of magic/technology (avoid info dumps)
- Formal or archaic speech (fantasy)
- Technical jargon (sci-fi)

**Example** (Fantasy):
```
\"The Shadowlands are forbidden.\"
\"Since when do you follow the Council's rules?\"
\"Since they started executing people who don't.\"
```

## Dialogue Exercises

### Eavesdropping Exercise

**Practice**: Listen to real conversations (coffee shop, public transit).

**Observe**:
- How people actually talk (fragments, interruptions, filler words)
- Speech patterns and rhythms
- Topics and how they shift
- Subtext and what's not said

**Apply**: Incorporate natural speech patterns into your dialogue (but remember to craft and condense).

---

### Voice Differentiation Exercise

**Practice**: Write same scene with different characters.

**Example**: Three characters order coffee.
- Character A (busy executive): \"Large black coffee. To go.\"
- Character B (indecisive people-pleaser): \"Um, I'll have... do you have decaf? Actually, maybe regular. What do you recommend?\"
- Character C (chatty retiree): \"I'll take a medium coffee, cream and sugar. Beautiful day, isn't it? Reminds me of when I used to...\"

**Goal**: Each character's voice should be distinct and consistent.

---

### Subtext Exercise

**Practice**: Write scene where characters say one thing but mean another.

**Example**: Two friends, one secretly angry at the other.
```
\"Nice of you to show up.\"
\"Sorry I'm late.\"
\"Oh, don't worry about it. It's only been an hour.\"
\"I said I'm sorry.\"
\"And I said don't worry about it.\"
```

**Goal**: Surface dialogue is polite, subtext is hostile.

---

## Dialogue Revision Checklist

**Voice**:
- [ ] Does each character have distinct voice?
- [ ] Is voice consistent for each character?
- [ ] Does dialogue sound natural when read aloud?

**Purpose**:
- [ ] Does every line serve a function (character, plot, conflict, relationship)?
- [ ] Have I cut unnecessary small talk and greetings?
- [ ] Is exposition woven naturally (no \"as you know\" dialogue)?

**Subtext**:
- [ ] Is there subtext and implication (not all on-the-nose)?
- [ ] Do characters sometimes say one thing and mean another?
- [ ] Is silence used effectively?

**Conflict**:
- [ ] Does dialogue create or reveal conflict?
- [ ] Do characters disagree or misunderstand?
- [ ] Is there tension beneath the surface?

**Formatting**:
- [ ] Are dialogue tags used sparingly (mostly \"said\")?
- [ ] Are action beats used instead of tags when possible?
- [ ] Are adverbs in tags eliminated?
- [ ] Is punctuation correct?
- [ ] New paragraph for each speaker?

**Craft**:
- [ ] Is dialogue concise (cut rambling)?
- [ ] Are contractions used (natural speech)?
- [ ] Are interruptions and pauses used effectively?
- [ ] Is dialect suggested, not phonetically spelled?
- [ ] Do characters avoid using names too often?
