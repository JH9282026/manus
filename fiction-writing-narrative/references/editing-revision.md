# Editing & Revision

Comprehensive guide to revising and editing fiction manuscripts, from structural revision to line-level polish.

## Revision vs. Editing

### Revision (Big Picture)

**Focus**: Structure, plot, character, pacing, theme

**Questions**:
- Does the story work as a whole?
- Are plot holes or inconsistencies present?
- Do characters have clear arcs?
- Is pacing effective?
- Does theme emerge naturally?

**Actions**:
- Rewrite scenes or chapters
- Cut or add major sections
- Restructure plot
- Deepen character development
- Strengthen theme

---

### Editing (Line Level)

**Focus**: Prose, style, grammar, word choice

**Questions**:
- Is prose clear and engaging?
- Are sentences varied and rhythmic?
- Is word choice precise?
- Are grammar and punctuation correct?

**Actions**:
- Tighten sentences
- Replace weak verbs and adjectives
- Fix grammar and punctuation
- Eliminate repetition
- Polish prose

---

## Revision Process

### Step 1: Take a Break

**Why**: Gain distance and objectivity

**How Long**: Minimum 2 weeks, ideally 4-6 weeks for novel

**During Break**: Work on different project, read, or rest

**Result**: Return with fresh eyes, see problems more clearly

---

### Step 2: Read Through (No Editing)

**Purpose**: Experience story as reader, not writer

**Process**:
- Read entire manuscript without stopping to edit
- Take notes on big-picture issues (plot holes, pacing, character)
- Note emotional response (where engaged, where bored)
- Identify patterns (repeated issues)

**Resist**: Urge to fix line-level issues (save for later)

---

### Step 3: Structural Revision

**Focus**: Plot, character arcs, pacing, theme

#### Plot

**Check**:
- [ ] Does inciting incident occur early enough?
- [ ] Are stakes clear and escalating?
- [ ] Does midpoint raise stakes or reverse direction?
- [ ] Is climax satisfying and earned?
- [ ] Are all plot threads resolved?
- [ ] Are there plot holes or inconsistencies?

**Fix**:
- Reorder scenes for better pacing
- Add scenes to fill plot holes
- Cut scenes that don't advance plot
- Strengthen cause-and-effect connections
- Ensure protagonist drives plot (not passive)

---

#### Character

**Check**:
- [ ] Does protagonist have clear goal and motivation?
- [ ] Is character arc present and satisfying?
- [ ] Are secondary characters distinct and purposeful?
- [ ] Do characters make choices consistent with personality?
- [ ] Do relationships develop and change?

**Fix**:
- Deepen character motivation
- Strengthen character arc (ensure change is earned)
- Give secondary characters distinct voices and purposes
- Ensure character choices drive plot
- Develop relationships through action and dialogue

---

#### Pacing

**Check**:
- [ ] Does opening hook reader quickly?
- [ ] Does middle sag or drag?
- [ ] Is climax rushed or drawn out?
- [ ] Are there long stretches without conflict or tension?
- [ ] Is there balance between action and reflection?

**Fix**:
- Cut slow opening (start closer to inciting incident)
- Add complications to sagging middle
- Give climax adequate space
- Increase tension in slow sections
- Vary pacing (fast action, slow reflection)

---

#### Theme

**Check**:
- [ ] Does theme emerge naturally (not preachy)?
- [ ] Do character arc and plot support theme?
- [ ] Are thematic elements woven throughout?
- [ ] Is theme clear but not heavy-handed?

**Fix**:
- Strengthen connection between character arc and theme
- Add thematic resonance to key scenes
- Cut overly didactic passages
- Trust reader to infer theme

---

### Step 4: Scene-Level Revision

**For Each Scene, Ask**:
- What is the purpose of this scene?
- Does it advance plot or develop character?
- Does it create or resolve conflict?
- Does something change by scene's end?
- Could this scene be cut or combined with another?

**Scene Elements to Check**:
- **Goal**: Does POV character have clear goal?
- **Conflict**: Are there obstacles to goal?
- **Outcome**: Does scene end with change or complication?
- **Setting**: Is setting vivid and purposeful?
- **Dialogue**: Is dialogue purposeful and distinct?
- **Interiority**: Do we understand POV character's thoughts and feelings?

**Fix**:
- Cut scenes that don't serve clear purpose
- Combine scenes that cover similar ground
- Strengthen scene goals and conflicts
- Add sensory details to setting
- Ensure each scene ends with change or complication

---

### Step 5: Line-Level Editing

**Focus**: Prose, style, word choice, grammar

#### Tighten Prose

**Cut**:
- Unnecessary words (\"very,\" \"really,\" \"just,\" \"that\")
- Redundancies (\"free gift,\" \"past history,\" \"advance planning\")
- Filler phrases (\"in order to\" → \"to,\" \"due to the fact that\" → \"because\")
- Weak intensifiers (\"very tired\" → \"exhausted\")

**Example**:
- **Before**: \"She was very tired and really needed to get some sleep.\"
- **After**: \"She was exhausted and needed sleep.\"

---

#### Strengthen Verbs

**Replace Weak Verbs**:
- \"Walked\" → \"strode,\" \"shuffled,\" \"staggered,\" \"crept\"
- \"Said\" → (keep \"said\" for tags, but vary action beats)
- \"Looked\" → \"glared,\" \"peered,\" \"glanced,\" \"stared\"
- \"Went\" → \"rushed,\" \"wandered,\" \"fled,\" \"marched\"

**Eliminate \"To Be\" Verbs** (when possible):
- **Before**: \"She was running down the street.\"
- **After**: \"She ran down the street.\"

**Avoid Passive Voice**:
- **Passive**: \"The door was opened by her.\"
- **Active**: \"She opened the door.\"

---

#### Vary Sentence Structure

**Mix**:
- Short sentences (impact, urgency)
- Medium sentences (standard narrative)
- Long sentences (flow, complexity)

**Example**:
- **Monotonous**: \"She walked to the door. She opened it. She saw him standing there. She gasped.\"
- **Varied**: \"She walked to the door and opened it. He stood there. She gasped.\"

**Techniques**:
- Start sentences differently (not all subject-verb)
- Vary sentence length
- Use fragments for emphasis (sparingly)
- Combine short, choppy sentences

---

#### Eliminate Clich\u00e9s

**Common Clich\u00e9s**:
- \"Avoid like the plague\"
- \"Quiet as a mouse\"
- \"Cold as ice\"
- \"Time stood still\"
- \"Her heart skipped a beat\"

**Solution**: Create fresh, original comparisons or state directly.

**Example**:
- **Clich\u00e9**: \"He was strong as an ox.\"
- **Fresh**: \"He could lift a hay bale with one hand.\"

---

#### Show, Don't Tell

**Telling**: States emotion or fact directly

**Showing**: Demonstrates through action, dialogue, sensory details

**Examples**:
- **Telling**: \"She was angry.\"
- **Showing**: \"Her jaw clenched. She slammed the door.\"

- **Telling**: \"The room was messy.\"
- **Showing**: \"Clothes covered the floor. Dishes teetered in stacks on the desk.\"

**When to Tell**: Transitions, unimportant information, passage of time

---

#### Cut Adverbs

**Problem**: Adverbs often tell instead of show, weaken prose

**Examples**:
- **Weak**: \"He ran quickly.\"
- **Strong**: \"He sprinted.\"

- **Weak**: \"She said angrily.\"
- **Strong**: \"She slammed her fist on the table. 'Get out.'\"

**Exception**: Adverbs are acceptable when they add necessary information (\"He spoke quietly\" if volume matters).

---

#### Eliminate Filter Words

**Filter Words**: Remind reader they're reading (distance reader from experience)

**Common Filters**:
- Saw, heard, felt, noticed, realized, thought, wondered, decided

**Examples**:
- **Filtered**: \"She saw the car approaching.\"
- **Direct**: \"The car approached.\"

- **Filtered**: \"He felt the cold wind on his face.\"
- **Direct**: \"Cold wind bit his face.\"

**Exception**: Use filters when character's perception is important (\"She thought she saw movement\" - uncertainty is key).

---

### Step 6: Consistency Check

**Verify**:
- [ ] Character names, descriptions, ages consistent
- [ ] Timeline makes sense (no contradictions)
- [ ] Setting details consistent
- [ ] POV consistent within scenes
- [ ] Verb tense consistent
- [ ] Character knowledge consistent (don't know things they shouldn't)

**Tools**:
- Character and setting notes
- Timeline
- Search function (find all mentions of character/place)

---

### Step 7: Read Aloud

**Purpose**: Catch awkward phrasing, repetition, rhythm issues

**Process**:
- Read entire manuscript aloud (or use text-to-speech)
- Note where you stumble or sentences feel awkward
- Listen for repetitive words or phrases
- Feel rhythm and flow

**Fix**:
- Rewrite awkward sentences
- Vary repetitive sentence structures
- Adjust rhythm (mix short and long sentences)

## Common Fiction Issues and Fixes

### Info Dumping

**Problem**: Large blocks of exposition or backstory

**Example**:
```
John had grown up in a small town in Ohio. His father had been a mechanic, and his mother had worked at the local diner. He had two sisters, both older, who had moved away as soon as they could. John had always felt like an outsider in his family...
```

**Fix**: Weave information naturally through dialogue, action, and reflection

**Better**:
```
\"You're from Ohio, right?\" she asked.
\"Small town. You wouldn't know it.\"
\"Try me.\"
He shrugged. \"My dad fixed cars. My mom served coffee. My sisters couldn't wait to leave.\" He paused. \"Neither could I.\"
```

---

### Purple Prose

**Problem**: Overly ornate, flowery language

**Example**:
```
The resplendent sun descended majestically beyond the cerulean horizon, casting its luminous golden rays across the undulating emerald meadow.
```

**Fix**: Use clear, precise language

**Better**:
```
The sun set over the green meadow, painting the grass gold.
```

---

### Weak Opening

**Problem**: Story starts too slowly, with weather, waking up, or backstory

**Example**:
```
It was a dark and stormy night. Rain pounded against the windows of the old house. Inside, Sarah lay in bed, unable to sleep. She had lived in this house for twenty years...
```

**Fix**: Start with action, dialogue, or conflict

**Better**:
```
The crash woke Sarah. She sat up, heart pounding. Someone was in the house.
```

---

### Sagging Middle

**Problem**: Act 2 feels slow, repetitive, or aimless

**Symptoms**:
- Same type of scene repeats
- No clear direction or escalation
- Reader loses interest

**Fix**:
- Add midpoint reversal (raise stakes, change direction)
- Introduce new complications or characters
- Reveal information that changes protagonist's understanding
- Cut unnecessary scenes
- Increase internal conflict

---

### Rushed Ending

**Problem**: Climax and resolution feel hurried

**Symptoms**:
- Climax resolved too quickly
- Character change not earned
- Loose ends not tied up
- Deus ex machina (external force solves problem)

**Fix**:
- Give climax adequate space (don't rush final confrontation)
- Ensure protagonist solves problem (not luck or external force)
- Show character change through action
- Resolve major plot threads
- Include brief denouement (aftermath)

---

### Flat Characters

**Problem**: Characters feel one-dimensional or stereotypical

**Symptoms**:
- Character has no flaws or only flaws
- Character doesn't change
- Character is purely good or evil
- Character's actions are predictable

**Fix**:
- Give character contradictions (tough exterior, soft heart)
- Add flaws to heroes, sympathetic traits to villains
- Create internal conflict (want vs. need)
- Make character's choices difficult (no easy answers)
- Show character change through arc

---

### Telling Instead of Showing

**Problem**: Emotions and events stated directly instead of demonstrated

**Example**:
```
She was nervous about the interview. She was worried she wouldn't get the job.
```

**Fix**: Show through physical reactions, actions, dialogue

**Better**:
```
She checked her watch for the third time. Her palms were slick with sweat. What if they asked about the gap in her resume?
```

---

## Self-Editing Techniques

### The Reverse Outline

**Process**:
1. After drafting, create outline of what you actually wrote
2. Summarize each scene or chapter in one sentence
3. Note purpose of each scene (plot, character, theme)
4. Identify gaps, repetitions, or unnecessary scenes

**Benefits**:
- See structure clearly
- Identify pacing issues
- Find scenes that don't serve purpose
- Spot missing information or plot holes

---

### The Search-and-Destroy Method

**Process**:
1. Identify your overused words or phrases (\"just,\" \"very,\" \"really,\" \"that,\" \"was\")
2. Use Find function to locate all instances
3. Evaluate each: Is it necessary? Can it be cut or replaced?
4. Delete or revise

**Common Targets**:
- Weak verbs (\"was,\" \"went,\" \"got\")
- Filler words (\"just,\" \"really,\" \"very\")
- Filter words (\"saw,\" \"heard,\" \"felt\")
- Adverbs (words ending in \"-ly\")
- Clich\u00e9s

---

### The Highlighter Method

**Process**:
1. Print manuscript (or use digital highlighting)
2. Highlight different elements in different colors:
   - Yellow: Action
   - Blue: Dialogue
   - Green: Description
   - Pink: Internal monologue
3. Look for balance and variety
4. Identify sections with too much of one element

**Benefits**:
- Visual representation of balance
- Identify \"talking heads\" (all dialogue, no action/description)
- Find static sections (all description, no action/dialogue)

---

### The Critique Partner Method

**Process**:
1. Find trusted critique partners or beta readers
2. Exchange manuscripts
3. Provide specific feedback on structure, character, prose
4. Revise based on feedback (but trust your instincts)

**What to Ask For**:
- Where did you lose interest?
- Which characters felt real? Which felt flat?
- Were there confusing parts?
- Did the ending satisfy you?
- What did you think the theme was?

## Revision Checklist

### Structural Revision

**Plot**:
- [ ] Inciting incident occurs early (within first 10-15%)
- [ ] Stakes are clear and escalating
- [ ] Midpoint raises stakes or reverses direction
- [ ] Climax is satisfying and earned
- [ ] All plot threads resolved
- [ ] No plot holes or inconsistencies
- [ ] Protagonist drives plot (not passive)

**Character**:
- [ ] Protagonist has clear goal and motivation
- [ ] Character arc is present and satisfying
- [ ] Secondary characters are distinct and purposeful
- [ ] Characters make consistent choices
- [ ] Relationships develop and change
- [ ] Characters have flaws and strengths

**Pacing**:
- [ ] Opening hooks reader quickly
- [ ] Middle doesn't sag or drag
- [ ] Climax has adequate space
- [ ] Balance between action and reflection
- [ ] Tension maintained throughout

**Theme**:
- [ ] Theme emerges naturally
- [ ] Character arc and plot support theme
- [ ] Theme is clear but not heavy-handed

---

### Scene-Level Revision

**For Each Scene**:
- [ ] Scene has clear purpose (plot, character, or both)
- [ ] POV character has goal
- [ ] Conflict or obstacles present
- [ ] Something changes by scene's end
- [ ] Setting is vivid and purposeful
- [ ] Dialogue is purposeful and distinct
- [ ] POV character's thoughts and feelings clear

---

### Line-Level Editing

**Prose**:
- [ ] Unnecessary words cut
- [ ] Weak verbs strengthened
- [ ] Sentence structure varied
- [ ] Clich\u00e9s eliminated
- [ ] Show, don't tell (where appropriate)
- [ ] Adverbs cut or justified
- [ ] Filter words eliminated
- [ ] Passive voice minimized

**Consistency**:
- [ ] Character details consistent
- [ ] Timeline makes sense
- [ ] Setting details consistent
- [ ] POV consistent within scenes
- [ ] Verb tense consistent

**Polish**:
- [ ] Grammar and punctuation correct
- [ ] Spelling checked
- [ ] Formatting consistent
- [ ] Read aloud for flow and rhythm

---

## When to Stop Revising

### Signs You're Done

- You're making changes, then changing them back
- Feedback from multiple readers is positive
- You've addressed all major issues
- Further changes are minor and subjective
- You're excited to share the work

### Signs You're Not Done

- Beta readers identify consistent issues
- You know something isn't working but haven't fixed it
- Plot holes or inconsistencies remain
- Characters feel flat or inconsistent
- Pacing drags or rushes

### Perfectionism vs. Excellence

**Perfectionism**: Endless tweaking, fear of finishing, paralysis

**Excellence**: Addressing real issues, improving craft, knowing when work is ready

**Remember**: No manuscript is perfect. At some point, it's time to let go and move forward.

## Preparing for Submission

### Final Polish

1. **Proofread**: Check for typos, grammar, punctuation
2. **Format**: Follow submission guidelines (font, spacing, margins)
3. **Consistency**: Verify all details are consistent
4. **Read Aloud**: Final check for flow and rhythm

### Manuscript Formatting (Standard)

- **Font**: Times New Roman, 12pt
- **Spacing**: Double-spaced
- **Margins**: 1 inch all sides
- **Indentation**: 0.5 inch first line indent (not tab)
- **Header**: Last name / Title / Page number (top right)
- **Title Page**: Title, author name, word count, contact info

### Query Letter and Synopsis

**Query Letter**:
- Hook (1-2 sentences)
- Brief summary (1 paragraph)
- Credentials and bio (1 paragraph)
- Thank you and contact info

**Synopsis**:
- 1-2 pages, single-spaced
- Cover entire plot, including ending
- Focus on main character and plot
- Write in present tense, third person

## Revision Mindset

### Embrace the Process

- First drafts are supposed to be rough
- Revision is where the real writing happens
- Every writer revises (you're not alone)
- Revision improves craft and teaches you

### Be Ruthless, But Kind

- Cut what doesn't serve the story (even if you love it)
- Don't be precious about your words
- But also recognize what's working and keep it
- Trust your instincts

### Seek Feedback, But Trust Yourself

- Beta readers and critique partners are valuable
- Consider all feedback, but don't implement all of it
- If multiple readers identify same issue, address it
- Ultimately, it's your story - trust your vision

### Take Breaks

- Revision is mentally exhausting
- Step away when frustrated or stuck
- Return with fresh perspective
- Celebrate progress and milestones

---

## Revision Timeline Example

**Novel (80,000 words)**:

- **Week 1-2**: Read through, take notes (no editing)
- **Week 3-6**: Structural revision (plot, character, pacing)
- **Week 7-8**: Scene-level revision
- **Week 9-10**: Line-level editing
- **Week 11**: Consistency check, read aloud
- **Week 12**: Beta readers
- **Week 13-14**: Revise based on feedback
- **Week 15**: Final polish and proofread
- **Week 16**: Format and prepare for submission

**Total**: 4 months (adjust based on manuscript needs and your schedule)

**Remember**: This is a guideline, not a rule. Some manuscripts need more time, some less.
