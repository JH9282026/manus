# Fiction Writing & Narrative

Craft compelling fictional narratives through structured storytelling, character development, authentic dialogue, and effective prose techniques.

## Overview

This skill covers the complete fiction writing process: story structure and plotting, character creation and development, dialogue writing, narrative voice, scene construction, and revision strategies. Use it when writing novels, short stories, novellas, flash fiction, or any narrative fiction project requiring strong storytelling fundamentals.

## Story Structure Selection

| Structure | Best For | Key Elements | Complexity |
|-----------|----------|--------------|------------|
| Three-Act Structure | Most genres, traditional narratives | Setup, Confrontation, Resolution | Beginner-Friendly |
| Hero's Journey | Adventure, fantasy, transformation stories | 12 stages from ordinary world to return | Intermediate |
| Save the Cat | Commercial fiction, screenplays adapted to prose | 15 beats with specific page counts | Intermediate |
| Freytag's Pyramid | Literary fiction, classical structure | Exposition, Rising Action, Climax, Falling Action, Dénouement | Beginner-Friendly |
| In Medias Res | Thrillers, action, fast-paced stories | Start in middle of action, flashback to setup | Advanced |
| Nonlinear | Literary fiction, experimental narratives | Multiple timelines, fragmented structure | Advanced |
| Kishotenketsu | Character-focused, literary, Eastern narratives | Introduction, Development, Twist, Conclusion (no conflict required) | Intermediate |

See `/references/story-structure.md` for detailed breakdowns, examples, and application strategies for each structure.

## Core Narrative Components

### The Four Elements of Fiction

**Action**: Events that occur, what characters do
- Plot progression
- Physical movement and activity
- Cause and effect sequences
- Conflict and resolution

**Description**: Sensory details of setting, characters, objects
- Visual imagery (what characters see)
- Sensory details (sound, smell, touch, taste)
- Atmosphere and mood
- World-building elements

**Dialogue**: Spoken words between characters
- Reveals character personality and voice
- Advances plot through conversation
- Creates conflict and tension
- Establishes relationships

**Commentary**: Internal thoughts, feelings, exposition
- Character thoughts and emotions
- Backstory and context
- Narrative reflection
- Thematic exploration

**Balance**: Effective fiction balances all four elements. Too much of any one creates imbalance (all dialogue = talking heads, all description = static scenes, all action = exhausting pace, all commentary = telling not showing).

## Character Development Framework

### Character Dimensions

**External Characteristics** (Observable):
- Physical appearance (age, height, build, features)
- Mannerisms and habits (gestures, speech patterns)
- Occupation and skills
- Social status and relationships
- Possessions and environment

**Internal Characteristics** (Psychological):
- Desires and goals (what they want)
- Fears and vulnerabilities (what they avoid)
- Beliefs and values (what they stand for)
- Contradictions and flaws (what makes them human)
- Backstory and formative experiences

**Character Arc Types**:

| Arc Type | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Positive Change | Character overcomes flaw, grows | Scrooge in *A Christmas Carol* |
| Negative Change | Character succumbs to flaw, falls | Walter White in *Breaking Bad* |
| Flat Arc | Character stays true to beliefs, changes world | Atticus Finch in *To Kill a Mockingbird* |
| Disillusionment | Character loses innocence or idealism | Holden Caulfield in *Catcher in the Rye* |

See `/references/character-development.md` for character creation exercises, arc planning, and techniques for revealing character through action and dialogue.

## Dialogue Writing Principles

### Functions of Dialogue

1. **Reveal Character**: Speech patterns, word choice, topics show personality
2. **Advance Plot**: Characters share information, make decisions, take action
3. **Create Conflict**: Disagreements, misunderstandings, opposing goals
4. **Establish Relationships**: Dynamics between characters emerge through conversation
5. **Convey Subtext**: What's unsaid is often more important than what's said

### Dialogue Best Practices

**Make Each Voice Distinct**:
- Vary vocabulary (educated vs. colloquial, formal vs. casual)
- Adjust sentence structure (short and choppy vs. long and flowing)
- Include unique speech patterns (catchphrases, verbal tics)
- Reflect background (regional dialect, cultural influences, profession)

**Keep It Purposeful**:
- Every line should serve a function (character, plot, conflict, relationship)
- Avoid small talk unless it reveals character or creates tension
- Cut unnecessary greetings, pleasantries, exposition
- Make dialogue do double or triple duty when possible

**Sound Natural Yet Crafted**:
- Read dialogue aloud to test authenticity
- Use contractions and informal speech (people rarely speak in complete sentences)
- Include interruptions, pauses, trailing off
- Avoid overly polished or rehearsed-sounding speech

**Show, Don't Tell**:
- Use dialogue to demonstrate emotions rather than state them
- Let characters reveal information through conversation, not exposition dumps
- Avoid "as you know" dialogue (characters explaining things they both know)

**Use Dialogue Tags Sparingly**:
- "Said" is invisible and preferred
- Use action beats instead of tags when possible
- Avoid adverbs in tags ("he said angrily" → show anger through words/actions)
- Vary tag placement (before, after, middle of dialogue)

See `/references/dialogue-writing.md` for advanced techniques, common mistakes, subtext strategies, and dialogue formatting rules.

## Point of View (POV) Selection

| POV | Pronouns | Intimacy | Limitations | Best For |
|-----|----------|----------|-------------|----------|
| First Person | I, me, we | Very intimate | Limited to narrator's knowledge | Character-driven, voice-heavy narratives |
| Second Person | You | Immersive, unusual | Difficult to sustain, can feel gimmicky | Experimental, choose-your-own-adventure |
| Third Person Limited | He, she, they (one character's perspective) | Intimate yet flexible | Limited to one character per scene | Most commercial fiction |
| Third Person Omniscient | He, she, they (multiple perspectives, narrator knows all) | Flexible, broad scope | Can feel distant, harder to master | Epic stories, multiple storylines |
| Third Person Objective | He, she, they (camera-like, no internal access) | Detached, cinematic | No access to thoughts/feelings | Minimalist, Hemingway-style prose |

**POV Consistency**: Maintain consistent POV within scenes. Avoid "head-hopping" (switching POV mid-scene) unless using omniscient narrator.

## Scene Construction

### Scene vs. Sequel (Dwight Swain's Framework)

**Scene** (Action, External):
1. **Goal**: Character enters with clear objective
2. **Conflict**: Obstacles prevent achieving goal
3. **Disaster**: Goal fails or succeeds with complications

**Sequel** (Reaction, Internal):
1. **Reaction**: Emotional response to disaster
2. **Dilemma**: Character weighs options
3. **Decision**: Character chooses new goal (leads to next scene)

**Pattern**: Scene → Sequel → Scene → Sequel creates rhythm of action and reflection.

### Scene Essentials

**Every Scene Should**:
- Advance the plot or develop character (ideally both)
- Have a clear purpose (why is this scene in the story?)
- Create or resolve conflict
- Change something (situation, relationship, character understanding)
- Engage the reader (tension, emotion, curiosity)

**Scene Elements**:
- **Setting**: Where and when the scene takes place
- **Characters**: Who is present and their goals
- **Action**: What happens physically
- **Dialogue**: What characters say
- **Interiority**: What POV character thinks and feels
- **Sensory Details**: What characters perceive (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)

## Narrative Voice and Style

### Voice Components

**Diction**: Word choice (simple vs. complex, formal vs. casual, concrete vs. abstract)

**Syntax**: Sentence structure (short and punchy vs. long and flowing, simple vs. complex)

**Rhythm**: Pacing of sentences and paragraphs (varied for interest, consistent for mood)

**Tone**: Attitude toward subject (serious, humorous, ironic, melancholic, optimistic)

**Perspective**: Narrator's relationship to story (involved, detached, reliable, unreliable)

### Developing Your Voice

1. **Read Widely**: Absorb different voices, identify what resonates
2. **Write Regularly**: Voice emerges through practice and consistency
3. **Be Authentic**: Write in a voice that feels natural, not forced
4. **Revise for Consistency**: Ensure voice remains consistent throughout manuscript
5. **Experiment**: Try different voices in different projects to find your range

## Pacing and Tension

### Pacing Techniques

**Speed Up Pacing**:
- Shorter sentences and paragraphs
- More action, less description
- Dialogue without lengthy tags or beats
- Cut unnecessary words and scenes
- Increase stakes and urgency

**Slow Down Pacing**:
- Longer sentences and paragraphs
- More description and sensory details
- Internal reflection and commentary
- Detailed dialogue with beats and pauses
- Lower stakes, contemplative moments

**Vary Pacing**: Alternate fast and slow sections for rhythm and reader engagement. Fast action followed by slow reflection creates natural breathing room.

### Creating Tension

**Techniques**:
- **Conflict**: Characters want opposing things
- **Obstacles**: Prevent characters from achieving goals
- **Stakes**: Make clear what characters stand to lose
- **Deadlines**: Add time pressure
- **Uncertainty**: Keep outcome in doubt
- **Foreshadowing**: Hint at future trouble
- **Dramatic Irony**: Reader knows something character doesn't

## Show vs. Tell

### When to Show

**Show** (dramatize, use sensory details, action, dialogue):
- Important emotional moments
- Character-defining actions
- Key plot events
- Relationship dynamics
- Conflict and tension

**Example (Telling)**: "She was angry."

**Example (Showing)**: "Her jaw clenched. She slammed the door so hard the frame rattled."

### When to Tell

**Tell** (summarize, state directly):
- Transitions between scenes
- Unimportant background information
- Passage of time
- Repetitive actions
- Information dumps (use sparingly)

**Example**: "The next three weeks passed in a blur of meetings and deadlines."

**Balance**: Effective fiction uses both showing and telling strategically. Show the important moments, tell the connective tissue.

## Common Fiction Writing Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem | Solution |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Info Dumping | Large blocks of exposition or backstory | Weave information naturally through dialogue, action, and reflection |
| Purple Prose | Overly ornate, flowery language | Use clear, precise language; cut unnecessary adjectives and adverbs |
| Passive Voice | Weakens action and clarity | Use active voice ("She opened the door" not "The door was opened by her") |
| Telling Emotions | Stating feelings directly | Show emotions through physical reactions, actions, dialogue |
| Inconsistent POV | Head-hopping, unclear perspective | Maintain consistent POV within scenes; signal POV shifts clearly |
| Weak Verbs | Generic verbs (walked, said, looked) | Use specific, vivid verbs (strode, whispered, glared) |
| Clichés | Overused phrases and metaphors | Create fresh, original comparisons and descriptions |
| Flat Characters | One-dimensional, stereotypical | Give characters contradictions, flaws, desires, and depth |

## Writing Process

### Drafting Approaches

**Plotter** (Outline First):
- Create detailed outline before drafting
- Know major plot points and character arcs
- Reduces rewriting, provides structure
- Risk: Can feel constrained or mechanical

**Pantser** (Discover as You Write):
- Start with premise or character, discover story through writing
- Allows spontaneity and organic development
- Risk: May require extensive revision, potential plot holes

**Plantser** (Hybrid):
- Outline major beats, discover details while writing
- Balance structure and spontaneity
- Most common approach

**Choose Based On**: Personal preference, project type, experience level. Experiment to find what works for you.

### Daily Writing Practice

**Set Goals**:
- Word count (500, 1,000, 2,000 words per day)
- Time-based (30 minutes, 1 hour, 2 hours per day)
- Scene or chapter completion

**Create Routine**:
- Write at same time each day (builds habit)
- Designate writing space (signals brain it's writing time)
- Minimize distractions (turn off internet, phone, notifications)

**Overcome Resistance**:
- Start with low-pressure warm-up (freewriting, journaling)
- Give yourself permission to write badly (first drafts are supposed to be rough)
- Focus on progress, not perfection
- Reward yourself for meeting goals
