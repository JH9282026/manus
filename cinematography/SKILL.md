# Cinematography

Apply visual storytelling principles through deliberate shot composition, lens selection, camera movement, and framing.

## Overview

This skill provides decision frameworks for the core cinematographic choices that shape how audiences experience a story — shot size, angle, movement, lens, depth of field, and aspect ratio. It focuses on the *why* behind each choice and when to apply specific techniques.

## Shot Size Selection Guide

| Shot Size | Abbreviation | Shows | Emotional Effect | Common Use |
|-----------|-------------|-------|-----------------|------------|
| Extreme Wide | EWS | Full environment, tiny subject | Isolation, epic scale | Establishing locations, landscapes |
| Wide / Full | WS | Full subject + environment | Context, geography | Scene openers, master shots |
| Medium Wide | MWS | Subject knees up + partial environment | Casual context | Walk-and-talks, group blocking |
| Medium | MS | Subject waist up | Neutral, conversational | Dialogue, interviews |
| Medium Close-Up | MCU | Subject chest up | Engaged, personal | Standard dialogue coverage |
| Close-Up | CU | Face fills frame | Intimate, emotional | Reactions, emotional beats |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Single detail (eye, hand, object) | Intensity, tension | Key story details, suspense |
| Insert | INS | Object or action detail | Informational focus | Props, hands, screens |

## Camera Angle Decision Framework

| Angle | Description | Psychological Effect | Use When |
|-------|------------|---------------------|----------|
| Eye level | Camera at subject eye height | Neutral, objective | Default for dialogue, interviews |
| Low angle | Camera below subject looking up | Power, dominance, heroism | Introducing strong characters, reveal shots |
| High angle | Camera above subject looking down | Vulnerability, weakness, overview | Showing isolation, weakness, surveillance |
| Dutch / canted | Camera tilted on roll axis | Disorientation, unease, tension | Psychological unrest, horror, action chaos |
| Bird's eye | Directly overhead | God-like perspective, abstraction | Graphic compositions, dance, maps |
| Worm's eye | Ground level looking up | Extreme drama, alien perspective | Awe, supernatural, child's view |

## Lens Selection Guide

| Focal Length | Category | Characteristics | Best For |
|-------------|----------|----------------|----------|
| 14–24mm | Ultra-wide | Deep DOF, visible distortion, exaggerated perspective | Architecture, landscapes, disorientation |
| 24–35mm | Wide | Moderate distortion, environmental context | Master shots, walk-and-talks, Steadicam |
| 35–50mm | Normal | Minimal distortion, close to human eye | Dialogue, documentary, naturalistic |
| 50–85mm | Short tele | Flattering compression, shallow DOF | Portraits, interviews, close-ups |
| 85–135mm | Medium tele | Strong compression, very shallow DOF | Beauty shots, emotional close-ups |
| 135–300mm+ | Long tele | Extreme compression, flat background | Surveillance feel, sports, isolation |

### Aperture and Depth of Field

- **Shallow DOF (f/1.4–f/2.8):** Isolates subject, cinematic look, requires precise focus pulling.
- **Medium DOF (f/4–f/5.6):** Balance of separation and context, forgiving for movement.
- **Deep DOF (f/8–f/16):** Everything in focus, documentary/realism, less cinematic separation.
- Rule: wider aperture + longer lens + closer subject = shallower DOF.

## Camera Movement Decision Matrix

| Movement | Equipment | Effect | Use When |
|----------|-----------|--------|----------|
| Static / locked-off | Tripod, hi-hat | Stability, formality, observation | Dialogue, interviews, symmetry |
| Pan (horizontal pivot) | Fluid head | Reveal, follow action | Scanning environments, following subject |
| Tilt (vertical pivot) | Fluid head | Reveal height, power dynamics | Tall buildings, standing character reveals |
| Dolly (track in/out) | Dolly + track, slider | Intimacy (push in), release (pull out) | Emotional beats, tension building |
| Truck (lateral track) | Dolly + track | Parallel movement with subject | Walking conversations, processions |
| Steadicam / gimbal | Stabilizer rig | Floating, immersive, subjective | Following characters, long takes |
| Crane / jib | Crane arm | Sweeping vertical + horizontal | Establishing shots, reveals |
| Handheld | Shoulder rig or bare | Raw energy, urgency, realism | Action, documentary, chaos |
| Zoom (optical) | Zoom lens | Voyeuristic, unsettling | 70s style, thriller, documentary |
| Vertigo / contra-zoom | Dolly + zoom opposite | Disorientation, realization moment | Iconic "Jaws" effect, psychological shift |

## Aspect Ratio Selection

| Ratio | Name | Look | Common Use |
|-------|------|------|------------|
| 2.39:1 | Anamorphic / Scope | Ultra-wide, cinematic, horizontal emphasis | Epic features, landscapes, sci-fi |
| 1.85:1 | Theatrical flat | Wide, standard cinema | Most narrative features |
| 16:9 (1.78:1) | HD widescreen | Standard broadcast | TV, web, documentary |
| 4:3 (1.33:1) | Academy | Boxy, intimate, retro | Period pieces, arthouse, intimate stories |
| 9:16 | Vertical | Mobile-native | Social media, TikTok, Reels |
| 1:1 | Square | Symmetrical, graphic | Instagram, stylized |

## Shot List Planning Workflow

1. Break script into scenes, identify emotional beats per scene.
2. Determine coverage strategy: master + singles, oner, montage.
3. Choose shot sizes that serve the emotional arc (wider for context → tighter for emotion).
4. Select lenses based on desired distortion and DOF.
5. Plan camera movements that are motivated by character or story action.
6. Map angles that reinforce power dynamics or emotional states.
7. Note aspect ratio and any framing constraints.
8. Compile into a shot list with scene number, shot size, lens, movement, and notes.

## Composition Principles

- **Rule of Thirds:** Place key elements at intersection points of a 3×3 grid.
- **Headroom:** Leave appropriate space above the subject's head (varies by shot size).
- **Lead Room / Nose Room:** Frame space in the direction the subject faces or moves.
- **Leading Lines:** Use environmental lines to guide the eye to the subject.
- **Frame within Frame:** Use doorways, windows, arches to add depth.
- **Symmetry:** Centered composition for formality, power, or unease (Kubrick style).
- **Negative Space:** Empty areas to convey isolation, tension, or contemplation.

## Best Practices

1. **Motivate every choice** — every shot size, angle, and movement should serve the story.
2. **Coverage is king** — always get a clean master; you can't fix missing coverage in the edit.
3. **Match eye lines** — maintain consistent screen direction in dialogue scenes.
4. **180-degree rule** — keep the camera on one side of the action axis unless intentionally crossing for disorientation.
5. **Shoot for the edit** — think about how shots will cut together before rolling.
6. **Test lenses on location** — bokeh, flare, and distortion vary between lenses.
