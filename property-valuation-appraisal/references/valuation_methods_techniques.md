# Property Valuation Methods and Techniques

## Overview

Property valuation employs various methods and techniques to determine real estate value. Professional appraisers select the most appropriate approaches based on property type, available data, and valuation purpose.

## The Three Primary Approaches

### 1. Sales Comparison Approach (Market Approach)

#### Detailed Methodology

**Step 1: Identify Comparable Properties**

**Selection Criteria**:
- **Location**: Same neighborhood or comparable area
- **Property Type**: Similar use and classification
- **Size**: Comparable square footage and lot size
- **Age**: Similar construction date or condition
- **Quality**: Comparable construction quality
- **Features**: Similar amenities and characteristics
- **Sale Date**: Recent sales (typically within 6-12 months)

**Number of Comparables**:
- Minimum: 3 comparables
- Ideal: 5-7 comparables
- More comparables provide better reliability

**Step 2: Verify Sales Data**

**Verification Process**:
- Confirm sale price from public records
- Verify sale conditions (arm's length transaction)
- Check for unusual financing
- Identify personal property included
- Confirm property condition at sale
- Verify any seller concessions

**Data Sources**:
- Multiple Listing Service (MLS)
- Public records
- County assessor
- Title companies
- Real estate agents
- Buyers and sellers

**Step 3: Analyze Differences**

**Elements of Comparison**:

**Property Rights**:
- Fee simple vs. leasehold
- Easements
- Restrictions
- Encumbrances

**Financing Terms**:
- Cash vs. financed
- Interest rate
- Down payment
- Seller financing

**Conditions of Sale**:
- Arm's length transaction
- Motivated seller
- Time pressure
- Related parties

**Market Conditions**:
- Time of sale
- Market trends
- Supply and demand
- Economic conditions

**Location**:
- Neighborhood quality
- Proximity to amenities
- School district
- Traffic patterns
- Views

**Physical Characteristics**:
- Size (GLA, lot size)
- Age and condition
- Quality of construction
- Room count
- Bathrooms
- Garage
- Basement
- Pool
- Other amenities

**Step 4: Make Adjustments**

**Adjustment Process**:
- Identify differences between subject and comparable
- Quantify the value impact of each difference
- Apply adjustments to comparable's sale price
- Adjust comparable to match subject property

**Adjustment Direction**:
- **Comparable Superior**: Subtract value (downward adjustment)
- **Comparable Inferior**: Add value (upward adjustment)

**Adjustment Methods**:

**Paired Sales Analysis**:
- Compare two similar properties differing in one feature
- Calculate value difference
- Use as adjustment amount

**Cost Approach**:
- Estimate cost to add/remove feature
- Adjust for depreciation
- Use as adjustment amount

**Market Extraction**:
- Analyze multiple sales
- Extract adjustment from market data
- Statistical analysis

**Example Adjustments**:
```
Subject Property: 2,000 sq ft, 3 bed, 2 bath, 2-car garage, no pool

Comparable 1: $400,000
- 2,100 sq ft (+100 sq ft): -$10,000 ($100/sq ft)
- 3 bed, 2 bath: No adjustment
- 2-car garage: No adjustment
- Pool: -$20,000
- Better condition: -$5,000
Adjusted Price: $365,000

Comparable 2: $380,000
- 1,900 sq ft (-100 sq ft): +$10,000
- 3 bed, 2 bath: No adjustment
- 1-car garage: +$10,000
- No pool: No adjustment
- Similar condition: No adjustment
Adjusted Price: $400,000

Comparable 3: $390,000
- 2,000 sq ft: No adjustment
- 3 bed, 2 bath: No adjustment
- 2-car garage: No adjustment
- No pool: No adjustment
- Slightly worse condition: +$5,000
Adjusted Price: $395,000
```

**Step 5: Reconcile Value Indications**

**Reconciliation Process**:
- Review all adjusted values
- Assess quality of each comparable
- Consider reliability of adjustments
- Weight comparables appropriately
- Arrive at value conclusion

**Weighting Factors**:
- Similarity to subject
- Number of adjustments
- Size of adjustments
- Reliability of data
- Verification quality

**Final Value Estimate**:
- Not a simple average
- Professional judgment
- Reasoned conclusion
- Supported by analysis

**Example Reconciliation**:
```
Comparable 1: $365,000 (many adjustments, less weight)
Comparable 2: $400,000 (few adjustments, more weight)
Comparable 3: $395,000 (minimal adjustments, most weight)

Indicated Value: $390,000 - $395,000
Final Estimate: $392,500
```

### 2. Cost Approach

#### Detailed Methodology

**Step 1: Estimate Land Value**

**Land Valuation Methods**:

**Sales Comparison**:
- Compare to similar vacant land sales
- Adjust for differences
- Most common method

**Allocation**:
- Analyze improved property sales
- Extract land value percentage
- Apply to subject

**Extraction**:
- Subtract improvement value from total sale price
- Requires accurate improvement value

**Development Method**:
- Estimate finished lot value
- Subtract development costs
- For raw land

**Land Value Considerations**:
- Size and shape
- Topography
- Utilities available
- Zoning
- Access
- Location

**Step 2: Estimate Replacement/Reproduction Cost**

**Cost Types**:

**Replacement Cost**:
- Cost to build equivalent structure
- Using current methods and materials
- Same utility and function
- Most commonly used

**Reproduction Cost**:
- Cost to build exact replica
- Using original methods and materials
- Exact duplicate
- Used for historic properties

**Cost Components**:

**Direct Costs**:
- Labor
- Materials
- Equipment
- Contractor profit and overhead

**Indirect Costs**:
- Financing during construction
- Property taxes during construction
- Insurance during construction
- Professional fees (architect, engineer)
- Marketing and sales
- Legal fees
- Permits and fees

**Entrepreneurial Profit**:
- Developer's profit
- Compensation for risk
- Coordination and expertise

**Cost Estimating Methods**:

**Comparative Method (Square Foot)**:
- Cost per square foot from similar buildings
- Adjust for differences
- Quick and simple
- Most common for mass appraisal

**Example**:
```
Subject: 2,000 sq ft house
Comparable new construction: $150/sq ft
Adjustment for quality: +$10/sq ft
Adjusted cost: $160/sq ft
Replacement Cost New: 2,000 × $160 = $320,000
```

**Unit-in-Place Method**:
- Cost per unit for each component
- Foundation: $ per sq ft
- Framing: $ per sq ft
- Roofing: $ per sq ft
- More detailed than square foot

**Quantity Survey Method**:
- Detailed itemization
- Labor, materials, overhead, profit
- Most accurate
- Time-consuming
- Rarely used by appraisers

**Cost Data Sources**:
- Marshall & Swift
- RS Means
- Local contractors
- Recent construction
- Cost manuals

**Step 3: Estimate Accrued Depreciation**

**Depreciation Types**:

**Physical Deterioration**:

**Curable**:
- Deferred maintenance
- Cost to cure < value added
- Examples: Painting, minor repairs

**Incurable**:
- Short-lived items (roof, HVAC)
- Long-lived items (structure, foundation)
- Cost to cure > value added

**Functional Obsolescence**:

**Superadequacy (Curable)**:
- Excess quality or size
- More than market requires
- Example: Gold-plated fixtures

**Deficiency (Curable)**:
- Lacking expected features
- Cost to add < value added
- Example: Missing bathroom

**Superadequacy (Incurable)**:
- Excess that can't be economically corrected
- Example: Oversized foundation

**Deficiency (Incurable)**:
- Missing features that can't be economically added
- Example: Low ceilings

**External Obsolescence**:
- Factors outside property
- Always incurable
- Examples:
  - Neighborhood decline
  - Traffic pattern changes
  - Economic conditions
  - Environmental issues
  - Zoning changes

**Depreciation Estimation Methods**:

**Age-Life Method**:
```
Depreciation = (Effective Age / Total Economic Life) × Cost New

Example:
Effective Age: 15 years
Total Economic Life: 50 years
Cost New: $320,000
Depreciation = (15/50) × $320,000 = $96,000
```

**Breakdown Method**:
- Quantify each type of depreciation
- Most accurate
- Most complex

**Market Extraction Method**:
- Use comparable sales
- Extract depreciation from market
- Requires good comparable data

**Step 4: Calculate Indicated Value**

**Formula**:
```
Land Value
+ Replacement Cost New
- Total Accrued Depreciation
= Indicated Value by Cost Approach

Example:
Land Value: $100,000
Replacement Cost New: $320,000
Physical Deterioration: -$60,000
Functional Obsolescence: -$10,000
External Obsolescence: -$15,000
Total Depreciation: -$85,000
Indicated Value: $335,000
```

### 3. Income Approach

#### Detailed Methodology

**Step 1: Estimate Potential Gross Income (PGI)**

**Income Sources**:
- Rental income (market rent)
- Parking income
- Laundry income
- Storage income
- Vending income
- Other income

**Market Rent Analysis**:
- Survey comparable rentals
- Adjust for differences
- Determine market rent for subject
- Multiply by number of units

**Example**:
```
10-unit apartment building
Market rent: $1,200/unit/month
Annual PGI: 10 × $1,200 × 12 = $144,000
```

**Step 2: Deduct Vacancy and Collection Loss**

**Vacancy Rate**:
- Based on market data
- Historical property performance
- Market conditions
- Typical: 5-10%

**Collection Loss**:
- Uncollectible rent
- Bad debts
- Typical: 1-3%

**Example**:
```
PGI: $144,000
Vacancy Rate: 7%
Collection Loss: 2%
Total Loss: 9%
Vacancy & Collection Loss: $144,000 × 0.09 = $12,960
```

**Step 3: Add Miscellaneous Income**

**Other Income**:
- Parking fees
- Laundry
- Storage
- Pet fees
- Application fees
- Late fees

**Example**:
```
Laundry income: $2,400/year
Parking income: $1,200/year
Total Miscellaneous: $3,600
```

**Step 4: Calculate Effective Gross Income (EGI)**

**Formula**:
```
EGI = PGI - Vacancy & Collection Loss + Miscellaneous Income

Example:
PGI: $144,000
- Vacancy & Collection: -$12,960
+ Miscellaneous Income: +$3,600
EGI: $134,640
```

**Step 5: Estimate Operating Expenses**

**Operating Expense Categories**:

**Fixed Expenses**:
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- License fees

**Variable Expenses**:
- Utilities
- Repairs and maintenance
- Management fees
- Advertising
- Legal and accounting
- Supplies
- Landscaping
- Snow removal
- Pest control

**Reserves for Replacement**:
- Roof
- HVAC
- Appliances
- Parking lot
- Other capital items

**Expenses NOT Included**:
- Mortgage payments (debt service)
- Depreciation (accounting)
- Income taxes
- Capital improvements

**Example**:
```
Property Taxes: $12,000
Insurance: $3,000
Utilities: $8,000
Repairs & Maintenance: $6,000
Management (5% of EGI): $6,732
Landscaping: $2,400
Reserves: $4,000
Total Operating Expenses: $42,132
```

**Step 6: Calculate Net Operating Income (NOI)**

**Formula**:
```
NOI = EGI - Operating Expenses

Example:
EGI: $134,640
Operating Expenses: -$42,132
NOI: $92,508
```

**Step 7: Select Capitalization Rate**

**Cap Rate Definition**:
- Rate of return on investment
- Relationship between NOI and value
- Market-derived

**Cap Rate Sources**:
- Comparable sales
- Market surveys
- Investor surveys
- Published data

**Cap Rate Extraction**:
```
Cap Rate = NOI / Sale Price

Example Comparable:
Sale Price: $1,200,000
NOI: $96,000
Cap Rate: $96,000 / $1,200,000 = 8.0%
```

**Cap Rate Factors**:
- Property type
- Location
- Quality
- Age and condition
- Market conditions
- Risk level
- Growth potential

**Typical Cap Rates**:
- Class A properties: 4-6%
- Class B properties: 6-8%
- Class C properties: 8-10%+

**Step 8: Calculate Property Value**

**Direct Capitalization Formula**:
```
Value = NOI / Cap Rate

Example:
NOI: $92,508
Cap Rate: 8.0%
Value: $92,508 / 0.08 = $1,156,350
Rounded: $1,155,000
```

**Yield Capitalization (DCF)**:
- Projects future cash flows
- Discounts to present value
- More complex
- Used for properties with changing income

## Additional Valuation Techniques

### Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM)

**Purpose**: Quick valuation estimate

**Formula**:
```
GRM = Sale Price / Gross Annual Rent
Value = GRM × Gross Annual Rent

Example:
Comparable Sale: $400,000
Gross Annual Rent: $48,000
GRM: $400,000 / $48,000 = 8.33

Subject Property:
Gross Annual Rent: $50,000
Estimated Value: 8.33 × $50,000 = $416,500
```

**Advantages**:
- Quick and simple
- Useful for screening
- Easy to understand

**Limitations**:
- Ignores expenses
- Ignores vacancy
- Less accurate than income approach
- Best for similar properties

### Price Per Square Foot

**Purpose**: Quick comparison tool

**Formula**:
```
Price per SF = Sale Price / Square Footage
Estimated Value = Price per SF × Subject SF

Example:
Comparable: $400,000 / 2,000 SF = $200/SF
Subject: 2,100 SF
Estimated Value: $200 × 2,100 = $420,000
```

**Considerations**:
- Varies by location
- Varies by property type
- Varies by quality
- Use within same submarket
- Adjust for differences

### Value Per Door (Multifamily)

**Purpose**: Compare multifamily properties

**Formula**:
```
Value per Door = Sale Price / Number of Units
Estimated Value = Value per Door × Subject Units

Example:
Comparable: $1,200,000 / 10 units = $120,000/door
Subject: 12 units
Estimated Value: $120,000 × 12 = $1,440,000
```

### Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

**Purpose**: Comprehensive investment analysis

**Process**:
1. Project future cash flows (5-10 years)
2. Estimate terminal value (reversion)
3. Select discount rate
4. Calculate present value of all cash flows

**Example**:
```
Year 1 NOI: $100,000
Growth Rate: 3% annually
Holding Period: 5 years
Terminal Cap Rate: 8.5%
Discount Rate: 10%

Year 1: $100,000 / 1.10 = $90,909
Year 2: $103,000 / 1.10² = $85,124
Year 3: $106,090 / 1.10³ = $79,685
Year 4: $109,273 / 1.10⁴ = $74,632
Year 5: $112,551 / 1.10⁵ = $69,876

Terminal Value: $112,551 × 1.03 / 0.085 = $1,364,189
PV of Terminal: $1,364,189 / 1.10⁵ = $847,024

Total PV: $90,909 + $85,124 + $79,685 + $74,632 + $69,876 + $847,024
Total Value: $1,247,250
```

**Advantages**:
- Comprehensive analysis
- Accounts for time value of money
- Flexible for various scenarios
- Detailed investment analysis

**Limitations**:
- Complex calculations
- Requires accurate projections
- Sensitive to assumptions
- Time-consuming

## Reconciliation of Value Indications

### Purpose
Analyze and weight the value indications from different approaches to arrive at a final value conclusion.

### Process

**1. Review Each Approach**:
- Sales comparison indication
- Cost approach indication
- Income approach indication

**2. Assess Reliability**:
- Quality of data
- Appropriateness of method
- Number of adjustments
- Market acceptance

**3. Consider Property Type**:
- Residential: Sales comparison primary
- Income property: Income approach primary
- New construction: Cost approach relevant
- Unique property: Cost approach may be primary

**4. Weight Approaches**:
- Not a mathematical average
- Professional judgment
- Reasoned analysis
- Supported conclusion

**Example Reconciliation**:
```
Sales Comparison Approach: $392,500
- 5 good comparables
- Minimal adjustments
- Most reliable for residential
- Weight: 70%

Cost Approach: $335,000
- Depreciation estimates subjective
- Property is 15 years old
- Less reliable for older properties
- Weight: 10%

Income Approach: $380,000
- Limited rental market data
- Not primary use
- Less applicable
- Weight: 20%

Weighted Average:
($392,500 × 0.70) + ($335,000 × 0.10) + ($380,000 × 0.20)
= $274,750 + $33,500 + $76,000
= $384,250

Final Value Conclusion: $385,000
(Rounded for practical purposes)
```

## Conclusion

Property valuation requires understanding and skillfully applying multiple methods and techniques. The three primary approaches—sales comparison, cost, and income—each provide valuable insights. Professional appraisers select and weight these approaches based on property type, available data, and valuation purpose, ultimately reconciling the indications into a well-supported value conclusion. Mastery of these methods, combined with market knowledge and professional judgment, enables accurate and credible property valuations.
