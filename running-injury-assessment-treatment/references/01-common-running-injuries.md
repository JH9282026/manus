# Common Running Injuries: Assessment and Overview

## Introduction

Running injuries are prevalent among both recreational and competitive runners, with annual injury rates ranging from 20% to 79% depending on the population studied. Understanding the most common running injuries, their mechanisms, risk factors, and clinical presentations is essential for effective assessment, treatment, and prevention.

## Most Common Running Injuries

### 1. Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)

**Prevalence**: 16-25% of all running injuries

**Description**: Pain around or behind the kneecap (patella), typically worsening with activities that load the patellofemoral joint.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Abnormal patellar tracking due to muscle imbalances
- Weakness in hip abductors and external rotators
- Quadriceps weakness or imbalance
- Overuse and repetitive stress
- Sudden increases in training volume or intensity

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Anterior knee pain, often described as "behind the kneecap"
- Pain worsens with running, especially downhill
- Pain with prolonged sitting ("theater sign")
- Pain with stairs, squatting, or kneeling
- Crepitus (grinding sensation) may be present
- No swelling typically

**Risk Factors**:
- Hip muscle weakness (gluteus medius, gluteus maximus)
- Quadriceps weakness or vastus medialis oblique (VMO) dysfunction
- Excessive foot pronation
- High training volume or rapid increases
- Previous knee injury
- Female gender (2:1 ratio)

**Assessment**:
- Patellar compression test (pain with compression and movement)
- Single-leg squat assessment (knee valgus, hip drop)
- Hip strength testing
- Patellar tracking observation
- Functional movement assessment

### 2. Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)

**Prevalence**: 5-14% of all running injuries

**Description**: Lateral (outside) knee pain caused by friction of the iliotibial band over the lateral femoral epicondyle.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Repetitive friction during knee flexion and extension
- Hip abductor weakness leading to increased hip adduction
- Excessive foot pronation
- Running on cambered surfaces
- Inadequate recovery between runs

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Sharp or burning pain on outside of knee
- Pain typically begins after a specific distance or time
- Pain worsens with continued running
- Pain with downhill running or stairs
- Tenderness over lateral femoral epicondyle
- Pain may radiate up or down the leg

**Risk Factors**:
- Hip abductor weakness
- Increased weekly mileage
- Downhill running
- Track running (always turning same direction)
- Worn or inappropriate footwear
- Leg length discrepancy

**Assessment**:
- Noble compression test
- Ober's test (IT band tightness)
- Hip abductor strength testing
- Single-leg stance assessment
- Gait analysis

### 3. Achilles Tendinopathy

**Prevalence**: 6-11% of all running injuries

**Description**: Pain and dysfunction of the Achilles tendon, classified as insertional (at heel attachment) or mid-portion (2-6 cm above heel).

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Repetitive tensile loading exceeding tendon capacity
- Sudden increases in training volume or intensity
- Hill running or speed work
- Inadequate calf strength or flexibility
- Poor running mechanics

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Pain and stiffness in Achilles tendon
- Morning stiffness that improves with activity
- Pain at start of run that may decrease with warm-up
- Tenderness to palpation
- Possible tendon thickening or nodules
- Pain with calf raises or hopping

**Risk Factors**:
- Sudden training changes
- Calf muscle weakness or tightness
- Excessive pronation
- Age (more common in masters runners)
- Previous Achilles injury
- Fluoroquinolone antibiotic use
- Limited ankle dorsiflexion

**Assessment**:
- Palpation for tenderness and thickening
- Royal London Hospital Test (pain with palpation during dorsiflexion)
- Calf strength testing (single-leg heel raises)
- Ankle range of motion assessment
- Hop test

### 4. Plantar Fasciitis

**Prevalence**: 5-10% of all running injuries

**Description**: Pain in the heel and bottom of the foot caused by inflammation or degeneration of the plantar fascia.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Repetitive tensile stress on plantar fascia
- Excessive foot pronation
- Tight calf muscles limiting ankle dorsiflexion
- Sudden increases in mileage or intensity
- Running on hard surfaces

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Sharp pain in heel, especially first steps in morning
- Pain at start of run that may improve with warm-up
- Pain worsens with prolonged standing or after rest
- Tenderness at medial calcaneal tubercle
- Pain with passive dorsiflexion of toes

**Risk Factors**:
- Limited ankle dorsiflexion
- High or low arches
- Calf tightness
- Sudden training increases
- Obesity or rapid weight gain
- Age (40-60 years most common)
- Occupations requiring prolonged standing

**Assessment**:
- Palpation of plantar fascia and heel
- Windlass test (pain with passive toe extension)
- Ankle dorsiflexion range of motion
- Foot posture assessment
- Single-leg balance test

### 5. Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)

**Prevalence**: 13-20% of running injuries in some populations

**Description**: Pain along the inner edge of the shinbone (tibia), typically in the lower two-thirds.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Repetitive stress on tibia and surrounding muscles
- Excessive foot pronation
- Sudden increases in training volume or intensity
- Running on hard surfaces
- Inadequate footwear

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Diffuse pain along medial tibial border
- Pain during and after running
- Tenderness over large area (>5 cm) of medial tibia
- Pain may be bilateral
- No focal point tenderness (distinguishes from stress fracture)

**Risk Factors**:
- Novice runners or returning after layoff
- Sudden training increases
- Excessive foot pronation
- Female gender
- Previous history of shin splints
- High BMI
- Running on hard or uneven surfaces

**Assessment**:
- Palpation along medial tibial border
- Hop test (pain with hopping)
- Single-leg heel raise
- Foot posture assessment
- Gait analysis
- Rule out stress fracture (focal tenderness, tuning fork test)

### 6. Stress Fractures

**Prevalence**: 1-20% depending on population (higher in military, competitive runners)

**Common Locations**: Tibia (most common), metatarsals, fibula, femur, pelvis

**Description**: Small cracks in bone caused by repetitive stress exceeding bone's ability to repair.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Repetitive loading without adequate recovery
- Bone remodeling outpaced by stress
- Sudden increases in training
- Inadequate nutrition (especially calcium, vitamin D)
- Hormonal factors (female athlete triad)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Focal, pinpoint pain
- Pain worsens with activity, improves with rest
- Tenderness over specific bone location
- Pain with hopping or percussion
- Possible swelling
- Night pain in advanced cases

**Risk Factors**:
- Female gender (2-10x higher risk)
- Low bone density
- Nutritional deficiencies
- Menstrual irregularities (female athlete triad)
- Sudden training increases
- Previous stress fracture
- Biomechanical abnormalities

**Assessment**:
- Focal palpation for tenderness
- Hop test (unable to hop or severe pain)
- Tuning fork test (pain with vibration)
- Fulcrum test (for femoral stress fractures)
- Imaging: X-ray (often negative initially), MRI (gold standard), bone scan

**Red Flags**: High-risk locations (femoral neck, anterior tibia, navicular, fifth metatarsal base) require immediate referral

### 7. Hamstring Strains

**Prevalence**: 5-8% of running injuries

**Description**: Tear or strain of hamstring muscles, typically at the musculotendinous junction.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Eccentric contraction during terminal swing phase of running
- Sudden acceleration or sprinting
- Inadequate warm-up
- Muscle fatigue
- Previous hamstring injury

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Sudden, sharp pain in back of thigh
- Pain with running, especially sprinting
- Tenderness and possible bruising
- Pain with stretching or resisted knee flexion
- Possible palpable defect in severe strains

**Grading**:
- Grade I: Mild strain, minimal loss of function
- Grade II: Moderate strain, partial tear, significant pain and weakness
- Grade III: Severe strain, complete tear, severe pain and loss of function

**Risk Factors**:
- Previous hamstring injury (strongest predictor)
- Hamstring weakness or imbalance with quadriceps
- Poor flexibility
- Inadequate warm-up
- Fatigue
- Age (increases with age)

**Assessment**:
- Palpation for tenderness and defects
- Resisted knee flexion test
- Straight leg raise (pain with stretch)
- Active knee extension test
- Functional tests (single-leg bridge, Nordic hamstring)

### 8. Calf Strains

**Prevalence**: 2-5% of running injuries

**Description**: Tear or strain of gastrocnemius or soleus muscles.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Sudden push-off or acceleration
- Eccentric loading during deceleration
- Inadequate warm-up
- Fatigue
- Sudden increase in speed work or hill running

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Sudden pain in calf, often described as "pop" or "snap"
- Pain with walking, especially push-off
- Tenderness and possible swelling
- Pain with resisted plantarflexion
- Possible bruising (may appear days later)

**Risk Factors**:
- Age (more common in masters runners)
- Previous calf injury
- Inadequate warm-up
- Sudden training changes
- Dehydration
- Calf weakness or tightness

**Assessment**:
- Palpation for tenderness and defects
- Resisted plantarflexion test
- Thompson test (to rule out Achilles rupture)
- Single-leg heel raise
- Functional hop test

### 9. Ankle Sprains

**Prevalence**: Variable, more common in trail running

**Description**: Ligament injury, most commonly lateral ankle ligaments (anterior talofibular ligament).

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Inversion injury (rolling ankle outward)
- Uneven terrain
- Stepping in hole or off curb
- Fatigue leading to decreased proprioception

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Pain and swelling around ankle
- Bruising
- Difficulty weight-bearing
- Instability or "giving way" sensation
- Tenderness over affected ligaments

**Grading**:
- Grade I: Mild stretch, minimal swelling, no instability
- Grade II: Partial tear, moderate swelling, some instability
- Grade III: Complete tear, severe swelling, significant instability

**Risk Factors**:
- Previous ankle sprain (strongest predictor)
- Inadequate rehabilitation from previous injury
- Poor proprioception
- Weak ankle stabilizers
- Trail or uneven terrain running
- Fatigue

**Assessment**:
- Palpation of ligaments and bones
- Anterior drawer test (anterior talofibular ligament)
- Talar tilt test (calcaneofibular ligament)
- Ottawa ankle rules (to determine need for X-ray)
- Balance and proprioception testing

### 10. Hip and Gluteal Pain

**Conditions**: Greater trochanteric pain syndrome, gluteal tendinopathy, hip flexor strains

**Prevalence**: 3-5% of running injuries

**Description**: Pain in hip region, lateral hip, or gluteal area.

**Mechanism of Injury**:
- Overuse and repetitive stress
- Hip muscle weakness or imbalance
- Sudden training increases
- Biomechanical abnormalities

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Pain in hip, lateral thigh, or gluteal region
- Pain with running, especially hills or stairs
- Pain lying on affected side
- Tenderness over greater trochanter or gluteal muscles
- Pain with resisted hip movements

**Risk Factors**:
- Female gender
- Age (more common in masters runners)
- Sudden training increases
- Hip muscle weakness
- Leg length discrepancy
- Excessive hip adduction during running

**Assessment**:
- Palpation of greater trochanter and gluteal muscles
- Resisted hip abduction and external rotation
- Single-leg stance (Trendelenburg test)
- FABER test (flexion, abduction, external rotation)
- Thomas test (hip flexor tightness)

## General Assessment Principles

### History Taking

**Key Questions**:
- When did the pain start? (Sudden vs. gradual onset)
- What were you doing when it started?
- Where exactly is the pain?
- What makes it better or worse?
- Have you had this before?
- Any recent changes in training (volume, intensity, surface, footwear)?
- Any other symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness, swelling)?

### Physical Examination

**Components**:
1. **Observation**: Gait, posture, swelling, asymmetry
2. **Palpation**: Tenderness, temperature, swelling, defects
3. **Range of Motion**: Active and passive, compare to unaffected side
4. **Strength Testing**: Resisted movements, compare to unaffected side
5. **Special Tests**: Injury-specific provocative tests
6. **Functional Tests**: Hop, squat, single-leg stance, running gait

### Red Flags Requiring Immediate Referral

- Suspected fracture (severe pain, inability to bear weight, deformity)
- Suspected Achilles tendon rupture (inability to plantarflex, positive Thompson test)
- Severe swelling or bruising
- Numbness or tingling (nerve involvement)
- Severe weakness
- Fever or systemic symptoms (infection)
- Night pain or pain at rest (possible serious pathology)
- High-risk stress fracture locations
- Chest pain or shortness of breath

## Conclusion

Understanding common running injuries, their mechanisms, presentations, and risk factors is essential for effective assessment and management. While each injury has specific characteristics, many share common themes: overuse, training errors, biomechanical abnormalities, and muscle weakness or imbalances. Comprehensive assessment including detailed history, thorough physical examination, and appropriate functional testing enables accurate diagnosis and guides treatment planning. Early recognition and appropriate management can prevent minor issues from becoming chronic problems and facilitate safe return to running.
