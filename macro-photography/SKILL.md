# Macro Photography

Reveal intricate details of small subjects through extreme close-up photography at high magnification.

## Overview

Macro photography captures subjects at life-size (1:1) magnification or greater, revealing details invisible to the naked eye. Success requires specialized equipment, precise focusing techniques, managing extremely shallow depth of field, and mastering macro-specific lighting. This skill covers macro lenses and alternatives, magnification ratios, focus stacking, lighting strategies, and techniques for various macro subjects.

## Essential Equipment

### Macro Lenses
**True Macro**: 1:1 magnification ratio (subject appears life-size on sensor)

**Focal Lengths**:
- **35-60mm**: Lightweight, inexpensive, requires close proximity (shadows, scaring insects)
- **90-105mm**: Versatile, good working distance, ideal for most macro work
- **150-200mm**: Long working distance (shy subjects), heavier, more expensive

**Recommended**: 90-105mm range for balance of working distance and portability

### Macro Alternatives
**Extension Tubes**:
- Hollow tubes between camera and lens
- Increase magnification of existing lenses
- Affordable ($50-200)
- Reduce light reaching sensor
- Less flexible than dedicated macro lens

**Close-Up Filters**:
- Screw-on lens attachments
- Reduce minimum focus distance
- Very affordable ($20-100)
- Lower optical quality
- Convenient and portable

**Reversing Rings**:
- Mount lens backward on camera
- Turns standard lens into magnifier
- Budget option ($10-30)
- Loss of autofocus and aperture control
- Reduced image quality

### Support Equipment
**Tripod**: Essential for stability at high magnification
**Macro Focusing Rail**: Precise camera positioning for focus stacking
**Remote Trigger**: Eliminates camera shake

## Magnification and Depth of Field

### Understanding Magnification
**1:1 (Life-Size)**: Subject same size on sensor as in real life
**1:2 (Half Life-Size)**: Subject half size on sensor
**2:1 (2x Life-Size)**: Subject twice as large on sensor (extreme macro)

### Depth of Field Challenge
**Extremely Shallow DOF**:
- At 1:1 magnification, DOF measured in millimeters
- f/16: ~2-3mm depth of field
- f/2.8: <1mm depth of field

**Managing DOF**:
- **Narrow Aperture (f/11-f/22)**: Increases DOF, but causes diffraction (softness)
- **Focus Stacking**: Combine multiple images at different focus points
- **Subject Angle**: Align camera parallel to subject's plane for maximum sharpness

## Lighting Techniques

### Natural Light
**Soft, Diffused Light**:
- Overcast days or shade
- Diffuser between sun and subject
- Reflectors to fill shadows

**Challenges**:
- Wind moves subjects
- Changing light conditions
- Limited control

### Flash Lighting
**Ring Flash**:
- Encircles lens, even illumination
- Prevents lens shadow
- Can create flat lighting

**Twin Flash**:
- Two flash heads on either side of lens
- More dimensional than ring flash
- Adjustable power ratio

**Off-Camera Flash**:
- Position flash at angle for dimensional light
- Diffuse with softbox or diffuser
- More natural-looking results

### Continuous Lighting
**LED Panels**:
- See lighting effect in real-time
- Easier for beginners
- Allows video
- Less powerful than flash

## Focus Stacking

### What is Focus Stacking?
Combining multiple images focused at different distances to create one image with extended depth of field.

### Process
1. **Setup**: Camera on tripod, subject stationary
2. **Capture Series**: Take 10-50 images, adjusting focus slightly between each
3. **Import to Software**: Photoshop, Helicon Focus, Zerene Stacker
4. **Align and Blend**: Software combines sharp areas from each image
5. **Result**: Single image with front-to-back sharpness

### When to Use
- Insects (entire body in focus)
- Flowers (all petals sharp)
- Product photography (complete sharpness)
- Any subject requiring extensive depth of field

## Camera Settings

**Aperture**: f/8-f/16 (balance DOF and diffraction)
**ISO**: 100-400 (minimize noise)
**Shutter Speed**: 1/100s+ (handheld), slower on tripod
**Focus**: Manual (precise control)
**Image Stabilization**: Off on tripod

## Subjects and Techniques

### Insects
- Early morning (drowsy, less movement)
- Approach slowly and carefully
- Focus on eyes
- Fast shutter speed (1/250s+) if handheld

### Flowers
- Calm days (no wind)
- Diffused natural light
- Focus on stamen or center
- Consider focus stacking

### Textures and Patterns
- Fabric, wood grain, rust, peeling paint
- Emphasize details and patterns
- Side lighting for texture
- Abstract compositions

### Water Droplets
- Spray bottle for controlled droplets
- Backlight for translucency
- Reflections in droplets
- Macro lens or extension tubes
