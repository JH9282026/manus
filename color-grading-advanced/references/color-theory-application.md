## Color Harmony Systems

### Complementary Colors

**Opposite on Color Wheel**
- Creates maximum contrast and visual interest
- Examples: Blue/Orange, Red/Cyan, Yellow/Purple
- Most common in cinema: Teal and Orange

**Teal and Orange Workflow**
1. Push shadows toward teal/cyan
2. Push highlights toward orange/amber
3. Maintain skin tones in orange range
4. Adjust intensity for subtle or dramatic effect
5. Balance to avoid oversaturation

### Analogous Colors

**Adjacent on Color Wheel**
- Creates harmonious, cohesive look
- Examples: Blue-Cyan-Green, Red-Orange-Yellow
- Feels natural and pleasing to eye

**Application**
- Use for scenes requiring unity and calm
- Gradually shift through analogous range
- Maintain consistency within color family

### Triadic Colors

**Evenly Spaced on Color Wheel**
- Three colors 120 degrees apart
- Examples: Red-Yellow-Blue, Orange-Green-Purple
- Vibrant and balanced

**Application**
- Use one color as dominant
- Others as accents
- Creates dynamic, energetic compositions

### Monochromatic

**Single Hue with Variations**
- Different saturations and luminance of one color
- Creates unified, sophisticated look
- Examples: Various blues, range of greens

**Application**
- Emphasize mood through single color
- Use tonal variation for depth
- Effective for stylized or artistic pieces

## Color Contrast Techniques

### Hue Contrast

**Different Colors**
- Juxtapose distinct hues for visual interest
- Complementary colors create strongest contrast
- Use to direct attention and create focal points

### Saturation Contrast

**Vibrant vs. Muted**
- Saturated subject against desaturated background
- Draws eye to saturated areas
- Creates depth and separation

**Workflow**
1. Desaturate overall image
2. Use secondary correction to restore saturation to subject
3. Adjust intensity for desired effect
4. Maintain natural appearance

### Luminance Contrast

**Light vs. Dark**
- Bright subject against dark background (or inverse)
- Creates drama and focus
- Essential for directing viewer attention

**High-Key vs. Low-Key**
- **High-Key**: Bright, even lighting, minimal shadows (comedy, commercial)
- **Low-Key**: Dark, dramatic shadows, high contrast (thriller, horror)

### Temperature Contrast

**Warm vs. Cool**
- Warm foreground, cool background (or inverse)
- Creates depth and atmosphere
- Separates subject from environment

**Application**
1. Identify foreground and background elements
2. Push foreground toward warm (orange/yellow)
3. Push background toward cool (blue/cyan)
4. Adjust intensity for subtlety or drama

## Skin Tone Management

### Ideal Skin Tone Placement

**Vectorscope Skin Tone Line**
- Runs from center toward orange/red
- All skin tones should fall on or near this line
- Distance from center indicates saturation
- Angle indicates hue

**Luminance Ranges**
- Fair skin: 60-70 IRE
- Medium skin: 40-50 IRE
- Dark skin: 25-35 IRE
- Adjust based on lighting and creative intent

### Correcting Skin Tones

**Using Qualifier**
1. Select skin tone hue range
2. Refine selection to isolate skin
3. Adjust hue to align with skin tone line
4. Adjust saturation for healthy appearance
5. Adjust luminance if needed

**Using Color Warper**
1. Identify skin tone hue in warper
2. Warp toward ideal skin tone hue
3. Adjust saturation for specific luminance ranges
4. Refine for natural appearance

### Matching Skin Tones Across Shots

**Consistency Workflow**
1. Establish ideal skin tone in hero shot
2. Save as reference still
3. Match subsequent shots to reference
4. Use vectorscope to verify alignment
5. Adjust for lighting variations while maintaining hue

## Color Grading for Time of Day

### Golden Hour

**Characteristics**
- Warm, golden light
- Soft shadows
- Glowing highlights
- Reduced contrast

**Grading Approach**
1. Warm color temperature (3000-4000K feel)
2. Lift shadows slightly (reduce contrast)
3. Add golden/amber to highlights
4. Increase saturation in warm tones
5. Soften overall image slightly

### Blue Hour

**Characteristics**
- Cool, blue ambient light
- Soft, even illumination
- Minimal shadows
- Ethereal quality

**Grading Approach**
1. Cool color temperature (6000-8000K feel)
2. Push overall image toward blue
3. Reduce contrast for soft look
4. Desaturate slightly for muted palette
5. Maintain detail in shadows

### Midday/Harsh Sun

**Characteristics**
- Neutral to slightly warm
- Hard shadows
- High contrast
- Intense highlights

**Grading Approach**
1. Neutral to slightly warm temperature
2. Increase contrast
3. Manage highlights to avoid clipping
4. Deepen shadows for dimension
5. Reduce saturation slightly for realistic look

### Night/Moonlight

**Characteristics**
- Cool, blue-tinted
- Low overall luminance
- High contrast
- Minimal color saturation

**Grading Approach**
1. Cool color temperature (blue tint)
2. Lower overall exposure
3. Increase contrast
4. Desaturate colors significantly
5. Maintain detail in shadows (avoid pure black)

## Color Symbolism in Storytelling

### Character Arc Through Color

**Color Progression**
- Start character in one color palette
- Gradually shift palette as character evolves
- Final palette reflects character transformation

**Example: Innocence to Experience**
- Beginning: Bright, saturated, warm colors
- Middle: Gradual desaturation, cooling
- End: Muted, cool, sophisticated palette

### Location-Based Color Coding

**Distinct Palettes for Locations**
- Assign specific color palette to each location
- Helps audience orient spatially
- Creates visual variety
- Reinforces narrative structure

**Example: Dual Worlds**
- Real world: Desaturated, cool, realistic
- Fantasy world: Saturated, warm, stylized
- Clear visual distinction aids storytelling

### Emotional Beats Through Color

**Shifting Palette for Emotion**
- Adjust color to reflect emotional state
- Warm for positive emotions
- Cool for negative emotions
- Desaturate for emotional numbness

## Advanced Color Techniques

### Selective Color Grading

**Isolate Single Color**
1. Desaturate entire image
2. Use qualifier to select specific color
3. Restore saturation to selected color only
4. Adjust intensity for effect

**Applications**
- Draw attention to specific element
- Create stylized, artistic look
- Emphasize symbolic object

### Color Bleeding and Interaction

**Simulating Color Bounce**
- Add subtle color from environment to subject
- Reflects realistic light interaction
- Enhances integration of subject in scene

**Technique**
1. Identify dominant environmental color
2. Add subtle amount to subject shadows
3. Adjust based on proximity to colored surface
4. Maintain subtlety for realism

### Color Grading for Depth

**Atmospheric Perspective**
- Distant objects cooler and less saturated
- Foreground warmer and more saturated
- Creates sense of depth and scale

**Workflow**
1. Separate foreground, midground, background
2. Warm and saturate foreground
3. Cool and desaturate background
4. Adjust midground as transition
5. Blend for natural appearance

## Color Trends and Styles

### Contemporary Cinematic Looks

**Modern Blockbuster**
- Teal and orange color scheme
- High contrast
- Saturated colors
- Clean, polished look

**Indie/Arthouse**
- Muted, desaturated palette
- Natural color relationships
- Subtle grading
- Film-like grain and texture

**Commercial/Advertising**
- Vibrant, punchy colors
- High saturation
- Clean highlights
- Energetic feel

**Period/Historical**
- Warm, nostalgic tones
- Reduced saturation
- Film emulation
- Appropriate color palette for era

### Evolving Your Color Palette

**Building Signature Style**
1. Study films and colorists you admire
2. Identify common color relationships
3. Experiment with variations
4. Develop consistent approach
5. Refine based on feedback and results

**Avoiding Trends**
- Understand why trends work
- Apply principles, not just presets
- Adapt to story and genre
- Develop timeless approach
