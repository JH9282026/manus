# Litigation Fundamentals Reference

## Introduction

This reference provides comprehensive coverage of fundamental litigation concepts, principles, and frameworks that form the foundation for effective dispute resolution practice. Understanding these fundamentals is essential for navigating the complexities of civil litigation and making strategic decisions throughout the litigation lifecycle.

## Civil Litigation Framework

### Jurisdiction and Venue

**Subject Matter Jurisdiction:**

Subject matter jurisdiction refers to a court's authority to hear a particular type of case. In the United States, this involves understanding:

- **Federal Question Jurisdiction**: Cases arising under federal law, the Constitution, or treaties (28 U.S.C. § 1331)
- **Diversity Jurisdiction**: Cases between citizens of different states with amount in controversy exceeding $75,000 (28 U.S.C. § 1332)
- **Supplemental Jurisdiction**: Federal court authority to hear state law claims related to federal claims (28 U.S.C. § 1367)
- **Removal Jurisdiction**: Defendant's ability to remove cases from state to federal court (28 U.S.C. § 1441)
- **State Court General Jurisdiction**: State courts' broad authority to hear most civil cases
- **State Court Limited Jurisdiction**: Specialized courts (small claims, probate, family) with restricted authority

**Personal Jurisdiction:**

Personal jurisdiction addresses a court's power over the parties:

- **General Jurisdiction**: Court has authority over defendant for any claim (typically where defendant is domiciled or incorporated)
- **Specific Jurisdiction**: Court has authority over defendant for claims arising from defendant's contacts with the forum state
- **Minimum Contacts Test**: Constitutional requirement that defendant have sufficient contacts with forum state such that jurisdiction does not offend "traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice"
- **Long-Arm Statutes**: State laws extending jurisdiction to out-of-state defendants with specified contacts
- **Service of Process**: Proper delivery of complaint and summons to establish jurisdiction

**Venue:**

Venue determines the appropriate geographic location for litigation:

- **Federal Venue**: Generally proper where any defendant resides, where substantial part of events occurred, or where any defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1391)
- **State Venue**: Varies by state but typically based on defendant's residence or where cause of action arose
- **Forum Non Conveniens**: Doctrine allowing dismissal when another forum is significantly more convenient
- **Transfer of Venue**: Moving case to different court within same system (28 U.S.C. § 1404)
- **Venue Strategy**: Selecting favorable forum based on substantive law, procedural rules, jury pool, and judicial tendencies

### Causes of Action and Legal Theories

**Contract Claims:**

- **Breach of Contract**: Failure to perform contractual obligations without legal excuse
  - Elements: Valid contract, plaintiff performance or excuse, defendant breach, damages
  - Defenses: Statute of frauds, impossibility, frustration of purpose, unconscionability, duress, mistake
- **Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing**: Violation of implied duty to act in good faith
- **Quantum Meruit/Unjust Enrichment**: Recovery for value of services rendered absent valid contract
- **Promissory Estoppel**: Enforcement of promise relied upon to promisee's detriment

**Tort Claims:**

- **Negligence**: Breach of duty of care causing damages
  - Elements: Duty, breach, causation (actual and proximate), damages
  - Defenses: Contributory/comparative negligence, assumption of risk, statute of limitations
- **Intentional Torts**: Deliberate harmful acts
  - Battery, assault, false imprisonment, intentional infliction of emotional distress
  - Fraud/misrepresentation, defamation, invasion of privacy
  - Conversion, trespass to land, trespass to chattels
- **Strict Liability**: Liability without fault for abnormally dangerous activities or defective products
- **Professional Malpractice**: Negligence by professionals (legal, medical, accounting)

**Business Torts:**

- **Fraud**: Intentional misrepresentation inducing reliance and causing damages
  - Elements: False representation, knowledge of falsity (scienter), intent to induce reliance, justifiable reliance, damages
- **Tortious Interference**: Intentional interference with contractual or business relationships
- **Unfair Competition**: Deceptive business practices, trade secret misappropriation, false advertising
- **Defamation**: False statements harming reputation (libel for written, slander for spoken)

**Statutory Claims:**

- **Employment Discrimination**: Title VII, ADA, ADEA, state fair employment laws
- **Securities Violations**: Securities Act of 1933, Securities Exchange Act of 1934, state blue sky laws
- **Antitrust**: Sherman Act, Clayton Act, state antitrust statutes
- **Consumer Protection**: State consumer protection acts, TCPA, FDCPA
- **Intellectual Property**: Patent Act, Copyright Act, Lanham Act (trademarks)

### Parties and Standing

**Party Designations:**

- **Plaintiff/Petitioner**: Party initiating lawsuit
- **Defendant/Respondent**: Party defending against claims
- **Cross-Defendant**: Party defending against cross-claim
- **Third-Party Defendant**: Party brought into lawsuit by defendant
- **Intervenor**: Non-party seeking to join ongoing litigation
- **Amicus Curiae**: "Friend of the court" providing information on legal issues

**Standing Requirements:**

- **Injury in Fact**: Concrete and particularized harm
- **Causation**: Injury fairly traceable to defendant's conduct
- **Redressability**: Favorable decision likely to remedy injury
- **Prudential Standing**: Court-created limitations on standing
- **Organizational Standing**: Organizations suing on behalf of members

**Joinder of Parties:**

- **Permissive Joinder**: Multiple plaintiffs or defendants may be joined if claims arise from same transaction and involve common questions (FRCP 20)
- **Compulsory Joinder**: Required joinder of necessary parties whose absence would impair their interests or prevent complete relief (FRCP 19)
- **Impleader**: Defendant bringing in third party who may be liable for plaintiff's claim (FRCP 14)
- **Intervention**: Non-party joining lawsuit to protect their interests (FRCP 24)
- **Interpleader**: Stakeholder depositing disputed property with court and requiring claimants to litigate among themselves (FRCP 22)

### Pleadings and Motions

**Complaint:**

The complaint initiates litigation and must contain:

- **Caption**: Court, parties, case number
- **Jurisdictional Statement**: Basis for court's jurisdiction
- **Factual Allegations**: Sufficient facts to state plausible claim (Twombly/Iqbal standard)
- **Legal Claims**: Causes of action with elements
- **Prayer for Relief**: Requested remedies (damages, injunction, declaratory relief)
- **Demand for Jury Trial**: If jury trial desired

**Answer:**

Defendant's response to complaint must:

- **Admit or Deny**: Respond to each allegation
- **Affirmative Defenses**: Assert defenses beyond simple denial (statute of limitations, waiver, estoppel, etc.)
- **Counterclaims**: Claims against plaintiff (compulsory if arising from same transaction, permissive otherwise)
- **Cross-Claims**: Claims against co-parties
- **Timing**: Generally 21 days after service (FRCP 12(a))

**Pre-Answer Motions:**

- **Motion to Dismiss (FRCP 12(b))**: Challenges complaint's legal sufficiency
  - 12(b)(1): Lack of subject matter jurisdiction
  - 12(b)(2): Lack of personal jurisdiction
  - 12(b)(3): Improper venue
  - 12(b)(4)/(5): Insufficient process or service
  - 12(b)(6): Failure to state claim upon which relief can be granted
  - 12(b)(7): Failure to join necessary party
- **Motion for More Definite Statement (FRCP 12(e))**: Request for clarification of vague pleading
- **Motion to Strike (FRCP 12(f))**: Remove insufficient defenses or scandalous material

**Amended Pleadings:**

- **Amendment as of Right**: One amendment within 21 days of serving pleading or responsive pleading (FRCP 15(a)(1))
- **Amendment by Leave of Court**: Court permission required after deadline, freely given when justice requires (FRCP 15(a)(2))
- **Relation Back**: Amendment relates back to original filing date if arises from same conduct and party had notice (FRCP 15(c))

## Litigation Timeline and Stages

### Pre-Litigation Phase

**Investigation and Fact-Gathering:**

- Client interviews and document collection
- Witness identification and interviews
- Public records research
- Expert consultation for technical issues
- Preservation letters to potential defendants
- Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests if applicable

**Demand Letters and Pre-Suit Negotiation:**

- Formal demand outlining claims and requested relief
- Opportunity for early resolution without litigation costs
- Establishes good faith effort to resolve dispute
- May be required by contract or statute before filing suit
- Creates record of damages and legal position

**Strategic Planning:**

- Forum selection (federal vs. state, geographic venue)
- Party identification (individual defendants, corporate entities, doe defendants)
- Claim selection (balancing strength, damages, and complexity)
- Timing considerations (statute of limitations, business cycles, evidence availability)
- Budget development and fee arrangement

### Pleadings Stage (Months 0-3)

**Complaint Filing and Service:**

- Draft and file complaint with court
- Pay filing fees
- Serve defendant with complaint and summons
- File proof of service with court

**Defendant's Response:**

- Pre-answer motions (typically motion to dismiss)
- Answer with affirmative defenses and counterclaims
- Third-party complaints if applicable

**Reply and Further Pleadings:**

- Plaintiff's reply to counterclaims
- Responses to third-party complaints
- Amended pleadings as needed

### Discovery Phase (Months 3-12+)

**Initial Disclosures:**

- FRCP 26(a)(1) requires automatic disclosure of:
  - Witnesses with discoverable information
  - Documents supporting claims or defenses
  - Damages computation
  - Insurance agreements
- Due within 14 days of Rule 26(f) conference

**Written Discovery:**

- **Interrogatories**: Written questions (typically limited to 25)
- **Requests for Production**: Document and ESI requests
- **Requests for Admission**: Requests to admit facts or document authenticity
- Response deadline typically 30 days

**Depositions:**

- Oral examination of witnesses under oath
- Notice requirements (typically 30 days for parties, subpoena for non-parties)
- Duration limits (typically one day of seven hours per deponent)
- Videotaped depositions for trial use

**Expert Discovery:**

- Expert designation deadlines
- Expert reports (FRCP 26(a)(2))
- Expert depositions
- Rebuttal experts

**Discovery Disputes:**

- Meet and confer requirements before filing motions
- Motions to compel discovery responses
- Protective orders limiting discovery
- Sanctions for discovery violations

### Pre-Trial Phase (Months 12-18)

**Dispositive Motions:**

- **Motion for Summary Judgment (FRCP 56)**: No genuine dispute of material fact, movant entitled to judgment as matter of law
- **Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (FRCP 12(c))**: Based solely on pleadings, no disputed facts
- **Motion for Partial Summary Judgment**: Resolves some but not all issues

**Pre-Trial Conference:**

- Final pre-trial conference with judge (FRCP 16)
- Pre-trial order establishing:
  - Witness lists and exhibit lists
  - Stipulated facts and legal issues
  - Motions in limine
  - Trial schedule and time limits
  - Jury instructions

**Trial Preparation:**

- Witness preparation and subpoenas
- Exhibit preparation and authentication
- Trial brief and proposed jury instructions
- Opening statement and closing argument preparation
- Demonstrative exhibit creation
- Mock trial or focus group (for high-stakes cases)

### Trial Phase (Days to Weeks)

**Jury Selection (Voir Dire):**

- Jury panel questioning
- Challenges for cause (unlimited)
- Peremptory challenges (limited number, no discriminatory basis)
- Jury selection strategy

**Trial Proceedings:**

1. **Opening Statements**: Plaintiff, then defendant outline case
2. **Plaintiff's Case-in-Chief**: Plaintiff presents evidence and witnesses
3. **Defendant's Motion for Judgment as Matter of Law (FRCP 50(a))**: Challenges sufficiency of plaintiff's evidence
4. **Defendant's Case-in-Chief**: Defendant presents evidence and witnesses
5. **Plaintiff's Rebuttal**: Plaintiff responds to defendant's case
6. **Closing Arguments**: Plaintiff, defendant, plaintiff's rebuttal
7. **Jury Instructions**: Court instructs jury on applicable law
8. **Jury Deliberation**: Jury deliberates in private
9. **Verdict**: Jury returns verdict
10. **Judgment**: Court enters judgment on verdict

**Evidence Presentation:**

- Direct examination of witnesses
- Cross-examination
- Redirect and recross examination
- Exhibit introduction and authentication
- Expert testimony
- Objections and rulings

### Post-Trial Phase

**Post-Trial Motions:**

- **Motion for Judgment as Matter of Law (FRCP 50(b))**: Renewed motion challenging verdict's legal sufficiency
- **Motion for New Trial (FRCP 59)**: Request for new trial based on errors, newly discovered evidence, or excessive/inadequate damages
- **Motion to Alter or Amend Judgment (FRCP 59(e))**: Request to modify judgment
- **Motion for Attorney's Fees and Costs**: If authorized by statute or contract

**Judgment Enforcement:**

- Writ of execution for money judgments
- Garnishment of wages or bank accounts
- Liens on real or personal property
- Debtor examinations
- Contempt proceedings for injunction violations

**Appeals:**

- Notice of appeal (typically within 30 days of judgment)
- Appellate brief preparation
- Oral argument
- Appellate decision
- Further appeals (certiorari to higher courts)

## Burden of Proof and Standards

### Burden of Proof

**Burden of Production:**

- Obligation to present evidence on particular issue
- Initially on plaintiff to establish prima facie case
- May shift to defendant for affirmative defenses
- Failure to meet burden may result in directed verdict or summary judgment

**Burden of Persuasion:**

- Obligation to convince factfinder to required degree of certainty
- Remains with party asserting claim or defense
- Does not shift during trial

### Standards of Proof

**Preponderance of the Evidence:**

- Standard for most civil cases
- "More likely than not" or "greater than 50% probability"
- Evidence tips scales in favor of party with burden

**Clear and Convincing Evidence:**

- Higher standard for certain civil matters
- "Highly probable" or "reasonably certain"
- Applied to fraud, punitive damages, termination of parental rights, civil commitment
- More than preponderance but less than beyond reasonable doubt

**Beyond a Reasonable Doubt:**

- Criminal standard, rarely applicable in civil cases
- May apply to criminal contempt proceedings
- Highest burden of proof

## Remedies and Relief

### Monetary Damages

**Compensatory Damages:**

- **Economic Damages**: Quantifiable financial losses
  - Lost profits or wages
  - Medical expenses
  - Property damage
  - Cost of repair or replacement
- **Non-Economic Damages**: Subjective, non-financial losses
  - Pain and suffering
  - Emotional distress
  - Loss of consortium
  - Reputational harm

**Punitive Damages:**

- Awarded to punish egregious conduct and deter future misconduct
- Requires showing of malice, fraud, or reckless disregard
- Subject to constitutional limitations (typically single-digit ratio to compensatory damages)
- Not available for breach of contract absent independent tort

**Liquidated Damages:**

- Pre-determined damages specified in contract
- Enforceable if reasonable estimate of actual damages and actual damages difficult to calculate
- Distinguished from unenforceable penalties

**Nominal Damages:**

- Token amount (e.g., $1) when right violated but no actual damages proven
- Establishes legal wrong and may support attorney's fees award

### Equitable Relief

**Injunctions:**

- **Temporary Restraining Order (TRO)**: Emergency relief without notice to defendant, limited duration (typically 14 days)
- **Preliminary Injunction**: Relief pending trial, requires showing of likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and public interest
- **Permanent Injunction**: Final relief after trial on merits
- **Mandatory Injunction**: Requires affirmative action
- **Prohibitory Injunction**: Prohibits specific conduct

**Specific Performance:**

- Court order requiring party to perform contractual obligations
- Available when monetary damages inadequate (unique goods, real property)
- Not available for personal service contracts

**Declaratory Judgment:**

- Court declaration of parties' rights and obligations
- Available when actual controversy exists
- Often sought to clarify contractual or statutory interpretation

**Restitution and Disgorgement:**

- Return of benefits unjustly obtained
- Disgorgement of profits from wrongful conduct
- Constructive trust on wrongfully obtained property

### Other Relief

**Attorney's Fees:**

- Generally not recoverable absent statute or contract ("American Rule")
- Exceptions: Fee-shifting statutes (civil rights, consumer protection), prevailing party provisions in contracts, sanctions for bad faith conduct

**Costs:**

- Prevailing party typically recovers certain costs (filing fees, service costs, deposition transcripts, witness fees)
- Taxable costs more limited than actual litigation expenses

**Interest:**

- **Pre-Judgment Interest**: Interest on damages from date of injury to judgment
- **Post-Judgment Interest**: Interest on judgment amount until paid
- Rates and availability vary by jurisdiction

## Ethical Considerations in Litigation

### Candor to the Tribunal

- Duty not to make false statements of fact or law to court
- Obligation to disclose adverse controlling authority
- Prohibition on offering evidence known to be false
- Duty to correct false evidence previously offered
- Applies even when client demands otherwise

### Fairness to Opposing Party and Counsel

- Prohibition on obstructing access to evidence
- Duty not to falsify evidence or assist witness in testifying falsely
- Obligation to make reasonable efforts to comply with discovery
- Prohibition on improper ex parte communications with represented parties
- Duty to disclose material facts when necessary to avoid assisting client fraud

### Meritorious Claims and Contentions

- Rule 11 requirement that pleadings, motions, and papers be well-grounded in fact and law
- Prohibition on frivolous claims or defenses
- Reasonable pre-filing investigation required
- Continuing duty to reassess positions as case develops
- Sanctions for violations (monetary sanctions, attorney's fees, dismissal)

### Conflicts of Interest

- Prohibition on representing clients with directly adverse interests
- Limitation on representing clients with materially limited interests
- Informed consent required for certain conflicts
- Screening procedures for imputed conflicts
- Special rules for former client conflicts and government lawyer conflicts

### Confidentiality and Privilege

- Attorney-client privilege protects confidential communications for legal advice
- Work product doctrine protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation
- Inadvertent disclosure issues and clawback procedures
- Crime-fraud exception to privilege
- Ethical duty of confidentiality broader than evidentiary privilege

## Conclusion

Mastering litigation fundamentals provides the essential foundation for effective dispute resolution practice. These core concepts—jurisdiction, causes of action, procedural stages, burdens of proof, remedies, and ethical obligations—form the framework within which all litigation strategy and tactics operate. A thorough understanding of these fundamentals enables practitioners to navigate complex disputes, make sound strategic decisions, and advocate effectively for clients while maintaining professional and ethical standards.
