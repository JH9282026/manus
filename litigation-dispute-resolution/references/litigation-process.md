# Litigation Process Reference

## Introduction

This reference provides detailed coverage of the civil litigation process from pre-filing through post-trial proceedings. Understanding each stage's requirements, strategic considerations, and tactical options is essential for effective case management and advocacy.

## Pre-Litigation Investigation and Planning

### Initial Client Consultation

**Information Gathering:**

- **Factual Background**: Detailed chronology of events leading to dispute
- **Parties**: Identify all potential plaintiffs, defendants, and witnesses
- **Documents**: Collect contracts, correspondence, invoices, photographs, and other evidence
- **Damages**: Quantify economic losses and identify non-economic harms
- **Objectives**: Understand client's goals (monetary recovery, injunction, vindication, business resolution)
- **Constraints**: Budget limitations, timeline concerns, publicity considerations

**Conflict Check:**

- Screen for conflicts with current or former clients
- Check for conflicts with adverse parties or witnesses
- Obtain conflict waivers if necessary and appropriate
- Document conflict analysis

**Engagement Agreement:**

- Scope of representation
- Fee structure (hourly, contingency, flat fee, hybrid)
- Cost allocation and advance deposits
- Communication protocols
- Termination provisions
- Conflicts disclosure and waiver

### Legal and Factual Investigation

**Legal Research:**

- Identify applicable causes of action and legal theories
- Research elements, defenses, and burdens of proof
- Analyze statute of limitations and procedural deadlines
- Review controlling case law and statutory authority
- Assess choice of law issues
- Identify potential dispositive issues

**Fact Investigation:**

- **Witness Interviews**: Interview client and favorable witnesses; memorialize statements
- **Document Collection**: Gather all relevant documents from client and public sources
- **Site Inspection**: Visit relevant locations and document conditions
- **Expert Consultation**: Engage experts for preliminary assessment of technical issues
- **Public Records**: Search corporate records, property records, court records, regulatory filings
- **Social Media**: Review publicly available social media for relevant information
- **Background Research**: Investigate opposing parties' financial condition, litigation history, reputation

**Preservation:**

- Send litigation hold letters to client and potential defendants
- Preserve physical evidence
- Preserve electronic evidence (emails, documents, databases)
- Document chain of custody
- Photograph or video relevant conditions before they change

### Strategic Planning

**Forum Selection:**

- **Federal vs. State Court**: Consider substantive law differences, procedural rules, jury pools, judicial expertise
- **Geographic Venue**: Evaluate convenience, local rules, jury demographics, historical verdicts
- **Judge Shopping**: Research assigned judges' backgrounds, tendencies, and prior rulings
- **Removal Risk**: If filing in state court, assess defendant's ability to remove to federal court

**Party Selection:**

- Identify all potential defendants (individuals, corporations, doe defendants)
- Consider strategic advantages of including or excluding certain parties
- Evaluate joint and several liability implications
- Assess collectability and insurance coverage
- Consider third-party practice and contribution claims

**Claim Selection:**

- Balance strong claims with weaker but potentially valuable claims
- Consider claim interactions and consistency
- Evaluate jury appeal of different theories
- Assess damages available under each claim
- Consider federal question jurisdiction implications

**Timing:**

- Statute of limitations deadlines
- Business cycle considerations
- Witness availability and memory degradation
- Document preservation concerns
- Strategic advantages of filing first vs. waiting
- Coordination with related proceedings

### Demand Letters and Pre-Suit Settlement

**Demand Letter Components:**

- Factual summary establishing liability
- Legal basis for claims
- Damages calculation with supporting documentation
- Demand for specific relief
- Deadline for response
- Consequences of non-response (litigation filing)

**Strategic Considerations:**

- **Advantages**: May achieve early resolution, demonstrates good faith, preserves evidence, establishes damages baseline
- **Disadvantages**: Reveals legal theories, may trigger defendant's preparation, could waive claims if poorly drafted
- **Timing**: Balance between allowing defendant time to investigate and maintaining pressure
- **Tone**: Professional but firm; avoid inflammatory language that hardens positions

**Pre-Suit Negotiation:**

- Engage in settlement discussions before incurring litigation costs
- Exchange information to facilitate evaluation
- Consider structured settlement or business solutions
- Document settlement discussions (with "without prejudice" protection)
- Formalize settlement if reached

## Pleadings Stage

### Complaint Drafting

**Caption:**

- Court name and location
- Case number (assigned upon filing)
- Party names and designations
- Document title

**Jurisdictional Allegations:**

- Subject matter jurisdiction basis (federal question, diversity, supplemental)
- Personal jurisdiction allegations (residence, minimum contacts, consent)
- Venue allegations (residence, events location)

**Factual Allegations:**

- **Plausibility Standard**: Must contain sufficient facts to state plausible claim for relief (Twombly/Iqbal)
- **Organization**: Chronological or topical organization
- **Specificity**: Fraud claims require heightened pleading (FRCP 9(b))
- **Incorporation**: Incorporate key documents by reference
- **Consistency**: Ensure consistency across claims and with evidence

**Claims for Relief:**

- Separate count for each cause of action
- Incorporate general allegations
- State elements of each claim
- Connect facts to legal elements
- Plead alternative or inconsistent theories if appropriate

**Prayer for Relief:**

- Specific monetary damages (or "in excess of" jurisdictional minimum)
- Injunctive relief with specificity
- Declaratory relief
- Attorney's fees and costs (if authorized)
- Punitive damages (if applicable)
- General relief ("such other relief as the court deems just and proper")

**Jury Demand:**

- Include if jury trial desired
- Specify issues for jury determination
- Waived if not timely demanded

**Signature and Verification:**

- Attorney signature with bar number and contact information
- Rule 11 certification (reasonable inquiry, well-grounded in fact and law, not for improper purpose)
- Verification under oath if required

### Filing and Service

**Filing:**

- Electronic filing in most courts
- Pay filing fees (or file fee waiver application)
- Obtain case number and assigned judge
- Comply with local rules for formatting and attachments

**Service of Process:**

- **Methods**: Personal service, substitute service, service on agent, mail (if authorized)
- **Timing**: Generally within 90 days of filing (FRCP 4(m))
- **Proof of Service**: File affidavit or certificate of service
- **Special Service**: Corporate agents, government entities, foreign defendants
- **Waiver of Service**: Request defendant waive formal service (FRCP 4(d))

### Defendant's Response

**Pre-Answer Motions (FRCP 12):**

**Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Subject Matter Jurisdiction (12(b)(1)):**
- Challenges court's authority to hear case type
- Can be raised at any time
- Burden on plaintiff to establish jurisdiction

**Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction (12(b)(2)):**
- Challenges court's authority over defendant
- Waived if not raised in first response
- Burden on plaintiff to establish minimum contacts

**Motion to Dismiss for Improper Venue (12(b)(3)):**
- Challenges geographic location
- Waived if not raised timely
- Court may transfer rather than dismiss

**Motion to Dismiss for Failure to State Claim (12(b)(6)):**
- Challenges legal sufficiency of complaint
- Accepts allegations as true
- Dismisses if no plausible claim even if facts proven
- May be granted with leave to amend

**Answer:**

**Admissions and Denials:**
- Admit, deny, or state insufficient knowledge for each allegation
- Specific denials required; general denials disfavored
- Failure to deny constitutes admission
- Deny on information and belief if lacking knowledge

**Affirmative Defenses:**
- Must be pleaded or waived
- Common defenses: statute of limitations, waiver, estoppel, release, accord and satisfaction, failure to mitigate, contributory/comparative negligence, assumption of risk, statute of frauds, laches, unclean hands
- Plead even if inconsistent with denials

**Counterclaims:**
- **Compulsory**: Arises from same transaction or occurrence; must be asserted or waived (FRCP 13(a))
- **Permissive**: Unrelated to plaintiff's claims; may be asserted (FRCP 13(b))
- Plaintiff must respond to counterclaims

**Cross-Claims:**
- Claims against co-parties (co-defendants or co-plaintiffs)
- Must arise from same transaction or occurrence
- Always permissive, never compulsory

**Third-Party Claims (Impleader):**
- Defendant brings in third party who may be liable for plaintiff's claim
- Common for indemnity or contribution claims
- Requires court permission if filed more than 14 days after answer (FRCP 14)

### Amended Pleadings

**Amendment as of Right (FRCP 15(a)(1)):**

- One amendment within 21 days of serving pleading, or
- Within 21 days of responsive pleading or motion, whichever is earlier
- No court permission required

**Amendment by Leave of Court (FRCP 15(a)(2)):**

- After deadline, requires court permission or opposing party consent
- "Leave shall be freely given when justice so requires"
- Courts consider: undue delay, bad faith, prejudice to opponent, futility

**Relation Back (FRCP 15(c)):**

- Amendment relates back to original filing date if:
  - Arises from same conduct, transaction, or occurrence, and
  - New party received notice and knew or should have known they would be sued but for mistake
- Critical for statute of limitations purposes

## Discovery Phase

### Discovery Planning

**Rule 26(f) Conference:**

- Meet and confer within 21 days before scheduling conference
- Discuss claims, defenses, settlement, and discovery plan
- Address ESI issues, privilege, and timing
- Submit joint discovery plan to court

**Scheduling Order (FRCP 16):**

- Court issues order establishing:
  - Discovery deadlines and phases
  - Motion filing deadlines
  - Expert designation deadlines
  - Pre-trial conference date
  - Trial date
- Modification requires showing of good cause

**Discovery Scope (FRCP 26(b)(1)):**

- Relevant to any party's claim or defense
- Proportional to needs of case considering:
  - Importance of issues at stake
  - Amount in controversy
  - Parties' relative access to information
  - Parties' resources
  - Importance of discovery in resolving issues
  - Burden versus benefit

### Initial Disclosures (FRCP 26(a)(1))

**Required Disclosures (within 14 days of Rule 26(f) conference):**

- **Witnesses**: Individuals likely to have discoverable information supporting disclosing party's claims or defenses
- **Documents**: Documents in possession, custody, or control supporting claims or defenses
- **Damages Computation**: Itemization and supporting documents
- **Insurance**: Insurance agreements covering potential liability

**Strategic Considerations:**

- Disclose favorable witnesses and documents
- Avoid over-disclosure of marginal materials
- Update as new information discovered
- Use to assess opponent's case early

### Interrogatories (FRCP 33)

**Characteristics:**

- Written questions requiring written answers under oath
- Limited to 25 questions including subparts (absent stipulation or court order)
- Only to parties, not non-parties
- 30-day response deadline

**Effective Uses:**

- Identify witnesses, documents, and facts
- Obtain contentions and legal theories
- Establish basic facts and chronology
- Identify expert opinions and bases
- Obtain damages calculations

**Drafting Tips:**

- Clear, specific questions
- Avoid compound questions
- Define terms to prevent evasive answers
- Request identification of documents and witnesses
- Use contention interrogatories strategically

**Responding:**

- Answer fully and specifically
- Object to improper questions (overly broad, burdensome, privileged)
- Produce documents in lieu of detailed answers when appropriate
- Supplement if new information discovered

### Requests for Production (FRCP 34)

**Scope:**

- Documents, ESI, and tangible things
- Entry onto land for inspection
- Within responding party's possession, custody, or control
- 30-day response deadline

**Effective Requests:**

- Specific categories with clear definitions
- Reasonable time periods
- Specify format for ESI (native, PDF, etc.)
- Request metadata if relevant
- Include boilerplate definitions and instructions

**ESI Considerations:**

- Identify custodians and data sources
- Discuss search terms and methodology
- Address inaccessible data and cost-shifting
- Negotiate clawback agreements for privileged documents
- Specify format and metadata

**Responding:**

- Produce responsive documents organized by request or as kept in ordinary course
- Object to overly broad, burdensome, or disproportionate requests
- Produce documents subject to objections or withhold entirely
- Provide privilege log for withheld documents
- Bates-stamp produced documents

### Depositions (FRCP 30, 31)

**Oral Depositions (FRCP 30):**

**Notice Requirements:**
- 30 days' notice for parties
- Subpoena for non-parties with reasonable notice
- Specify date, time, location, and deponent
- Attach document requests (subpoena duces tecum for non-parties)

**Limitations:**
- Presumptive limit of 10 depositions per side
- One day of seven hours per deponent
- Court order or stipulation required to exceed limits

**Procedure:**
- Court reporter administers oath
- Examining attorney asks questions
- Objections stated concisely (form, foundation, relevance)
- Witness answers unless privilege asserted
- Other parties may examine
- Transcript prepared and reviewed by witness if requested

**Defending Depositions:**

**Preparation:**
- Review relevant documents with witness
- Explain deposition process and ground rules
- Discuss anticipated topics and difficult areas
- Prepare witness on listening carefully, answering only question asked, correcting mistakes
- Review prior testimony or statements for consistency

**During Deposition:**
- Object to improper questions (form, foundation, privilege)
- Instruct not to answer only for privilege, to enforce court order, or to allow motion for protective order
- Avoid coaching or excessive objections
- Take breaks to confer with witness
- Rehabilitate on cross-examination if needed

**Taking Depositions:**

**Preparation:**
- Review all documents and prior testimony
- Outline topics and key questions
- Identify impeachment material
- Prepare exhibits
- Anticipate objections and evasive answers

**Techniques:**
- Establish background and credibility
- Use open-ended questions for information gathering
- Use leading questions to pin down testimony
- Listen to answers and follow up
- Impeach with prior inconsistent statements or documents
- Preserve testimony for trial use
- Avoid arguing or showing anger

**Written Depositions (FRCP 31):**

- Written questions served on party or non-party
- Deponent answers before officer under oath
- Rarely used except for routine matters or distant witnesses

### Requests for Admission (FRCP 36)

**Purpose:**

- Establish undisputed facts
- Authenticate documents
- Narrow issues for trial
- Eliminate need for proof at trial

**Scope:**

- Requests to admit facts
- Requests to admit genuineness of documents
- Requests to admit application of law to fact
- 30-day response deadline

**Responding:**

- Admit, deny, or state insufficient knowledge after reasonable inquiry
- Partial admissions with explanations
- Objections to improper requests
- Deemed admitted if not timely responded
- Difficult to withdraw or amend admissions

**Strategic Use:**

- Serve after document discovery to authenticate key documents
- Use to establish elements of claims or defenses
- Request admissions on damages calculations
- Combine with summary judgment motion
- Seek cost-shifting if opponent denies then fails to prove at trial (FRCP 37(c)(2))

### Expert Discovery (FRCP 26(a)(2))

**Expert Designation:**

- Disclose identity of experts and subject matter
- Timing per scheduling order (typically plaintiff first, then defendant)
- Rebuttal experts after initial experts

**Expert Reports:**

**Retained Experts (FRCP 26(a)(2)(B)):**
- Written report containing:
  - Opinions and bases
  - Facts and data considered
  - Exhibits
  - Qualifications and publications
  - Compensation
  - Prior testimony (4 years)
- Signed by expert

**Non-Retained Experts (FRCP 26(a)(2)(C)):**
- Treating physicians, employees with relevant knowledge
- Disclosure of subject matter and summary of opinions
- No written report required

**Expert Depositions:**

- Permitted after report disclosure
- No presumptive time limit
- Expert fees paid by deposing party
- Critical for understanding opinions, testing bases, and impeachment

**Expert Challenges:**

- **Daubert/Frye Motions**: Challenge admissibility of expert testimony
  - Daubert factors: testability, peer review, error rate, general acceptance
  - Expert must be qualified and methodology reliable
  - Opinions must fit facts of case
- Timing: typically before trial, sometimes during trial

### Discovery Disputes

**Meet and Confer Requirement:**

- Good faith effort to resolve dispute before filing motion
- Document meet and confer efforts
- Narrow issues for court resolution

**Motions to Compel (FRCP 37(a)):**

- Filed when party fails to respond or provides inadequate responses
- Must show meet and confer efforts
- Court may order compliance, award expenses, or impose sanctions
- Typically must be filed before discovery deadline

**Protective Orders (FRCP 26(c)):**

- Sought by party resisting discovery
- Grounds: undue burden, expense, privileged, confidential, harassing
- Court may limit scope, method, or timing of discovery
- Confidentiality orders protect trade secrets and sensitive information

**Sanctions (FRCP 37):**

- **Failure to Comply with Order**: Dismissal, default, contempt, adverse inference, attorney's fees
- **Failure to Preserve ESI**: Sanctions if prejudice shown and culpability (negligence to intentional destruction)
- **Failure to Admit**: Cost-shifting if party denies request for admission then fails to prove at trial

### Privilege and Work Product

**Attorney-Client Privilege:**

- Protects confidential communications between client and attorney for legal advice
- Elements: communication, confidential, between client and attorney, for legal advice
- Waived by disclosure to third parties or subject matter waiver
- Crime-fraud exception: no privilege for communications in furtherance of crime or fraud

**Work Product Doctrine (FRCP 26(b)(3)):**

- Protects documents and tangible things prepared in anticipation of litigation
- **Fact Work Product**: Discoverable upon showing substantial need and inability to obtain equivalent without undue hardship
- **Opinion Work Product**: Attorney mental impressions, conclusions, opinions; nearly absolute protection
- Not waived by disclosure to client or experts

**Privilege Logs:**

- Required for withheld documents
- Identify: author, recipient, date, description, privilege basis
- Sufficient detail to assess privilege without revealing privileged information
- Due when production made or shortly thereafter

**Inadvertent Disclosure:**

- **Clawback Agreements**: Parties agree inadvertent disclosure doesn't waive privilege
- **Quick Peek Agreements**: Producing party provides documents for review before asserting privilege
- **FRE 502**: Federal rule limiting waiver from inadvertent disclosure if reasonable precautions taken

## Pre-Trial Motions

### Summary Judgment (FRCP 56)

**Standard:**

- No genuine dispute of material fact
- Movant entitled to judgment as matter of law
- Court views evidence in light most favorable to non-movant
- Non-movant must show genuine dispute, not just speculation

**Timing:**

- May be filed until 30 days after close of discovery (or per scheduling order)
- Partial summary judgment on some issues or claims

**Procedure:**

- **Motion**: Statement of undisputed facts with record citations
- **Opposition**: Dispute material facts with record citations, argue legal issues
- **Reply**: Respond to opposition arguments
- **Hearing**: Oral argument (discretionary)
- **Decision**: Grant, deny, or grant in part

**Strategic Considerations:**

- **Movant**: File when clear no factual dispute on key element; use to eliminate weak claims or defenses
- **Non-Movant**: Create factual disputes through affidavits and evidence; argue material facts genuinely disputed
- **Partial Summary Judgment**: Narrow issues for trial, establish liability leaving only damages, or vice versa

### Motions in Limine

**Purpose:**

- Exclude or admit evidence before trial
- Obtain advance ruling on evidentiary issues
- Prevent prejudicial evidence from being mentioned to jury

**Common Subjects:**

- Prior bad acts or criminal history
- Settlement negotiations
- Insurance coverage
- Hearsay statements
- Expert testimony admissibility
- Demonstrative exhibits
- Witness credibility evidence

**Timing:**

- Filed before trial (typically with pre-trial submissions)
- May be raised during trial if issue arises unexpectedly

**Strategic Use:**

- Shape trial narrative by excluding harmful evidence
- Obtain advance ruling to avoid mid-trial disputes
- Preserve record for appeal
- Force opponent to disclose trial strategy

### Other Pre-Trial Motions

**Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (FRCP 12(c)):**

- Based solely on pleadings
- No disputed facts; movant entitled to judgment as matter of law
- Rarely granted; summary judgment more common

**Motion to Amend Scheduling Order:**

- Modify discovery deadlines, expert deadlines, or trial date
- Requires showing of good cause
- Courts reluctant to modify absent compelling circumstances

**Motion to Exclude Expert:**

- Daubert/Frye motion challenging expert admissibility
- Expert unqualified, methodology unreliable, or opinions don't fit facts
- Often dispositive if expert critical to claim or defense

**Motion for Separate Trials (FRCP 42(b)):**

- Bifurcate liability and damages
- Sever claims or parties
- Granted for convenience, prejudice avoidance, or efficiency

## Trial

### Pre-Trial Preparation

**Witness Preparation:**

- Review anticipated testimony
- Prepare for direct and cross-examination
- Review documents witness will be questioned about
- Discuss courtroom procedures and demeanor
- Conduct mock examination
- Prepare for impeachment attempts

**Exhibit Preparation:**

- Organize exhibits logically
- Prepare exhibit list and pre-mark exhibits
- Ensure admissibility (authentication, relevance, hearsay exceptions)
- Create demonstrative exhibits (charts, timelines, summaries)
- Prepare technology for presentation
- Exchange exhibits with opponent per local rules

**Trial Brief:**

- Summary of facts, claims, and defenses
- Legal issues and authorities
- Evidentiary issues
- Requested jury instructions
- Damages calculation

**Jury Instructions:**

- Propose instructions on law applicable to claims and defenses
- Use pattern instructions when available
- Draft special verdict form
- Identify contested instructions

**Opening Statement Preparation:**

- Develop compelling narrative
- Identify themes
- Outline key facts and evidence
- Prepare visual aids
- Anticipate opponent's story

**Closing Argument Preparation:**

- Outline argument structure
- Identify key evidence and testimony
- Prepare responses to anticipated opponent arguments
- Draft argument addressing jury instructions
- Prepare damages argument

### Jury Selection (Voir Dire)

**Purpose:**

- Identify and remove biased jurors
- Begin persuasion process
- Establish rapport

**Process:**

- **Jury Panel**: Prospective jurors called to courtroom
- **Preliminary Questions**: Judge or attorneys ask background questions
- **Individual Questioning**: Follow-up on concerning responses
- **Challenges for Cause**: Unlimited challenges for demonstrated bias
- **Peremptory Challenges**: Limited number (typically 3-6 per side) without stated reason, but not for discriminatory reasons (Batson)

**Strategy:**

- Research jury pool demographics
- Develop juror profile (favorable and unfavorable characteristics)
- Prepare questions to elicit biases
- Listen carefully to responses
- Observe body language and demeanor
- Use challenges strategically
- Avoid Batson violations (race/gender discrimination)

### Trial Procedure

**Opening Statements:**

- **Plaintiff First**: Outline case, introduce themes, preview evidence
- **Defendant**: Respond to plaintiff's story, present defense narrative
- Not argument; statement of expected evidence
- Establish credibility and rapport with jury
- Roadmap for jury to understand evidence

**Plaintiff's Case-in-Chief:**

- Present witnesses and exhibits proving claims
- Direct examination of witnesses
- Defendant cross-examines
- Plaintiff redirect examination
- Introduce exhibits through witnesses
- Establish elements of each claim

**Defendant's Motion for Judgment as Matter of Law (FRCP 50(a)):**

- After plaintiff rests, defendant may move for judgment
- Argues plaintiff failed to present sufficient evidence
- Rarely granted; high standard
- Preserves issue for post-trial motion and appeal

**Defendant's Case-in-Chief:**

- Present defense witnesses and exhibits
- Challenge plaintiff's evidence
- Establish affirmative defenses
- Plaintiff cross-examines defendant's witnesses

**Plaintiff's Rebuttal:**

- Limited to responding to defendant's case
- Cannot present case-in-chief evidence omitted earlier
- Rehabilitate impeached witnesses

**Closing Arguments:**

- **Plaintiff**: Summarize evidence, argue law, request verdict
- **Defendant**: Respond to plaintiff, argue evidence insufficient, request defense verdict
- **Plaintiff Rebuttal**: Respond to defendant's argument
- Argument, not testimony
- Connect evidence to jury instructions
- Appeal to reason and justice

**Jury Instructions:**

- Judge instructs jury on applicable law
- Parties propose instructions; judge decides which to give
- Object to erroneous instructions to preserve appeal
- Jury receives written instructions in most jurisdictions

**Jury Deliberation:**

- Jury deliberates in private
- May request exhibits, testimony readback, or instruction clarification
- Reaches verdict (unanimous in federal court, varies in state court)

**Verdict:**

- General verdict: finds for plaintiff or defendant, specifies damages
- Special verdict: answers specific factual questions
- Jury polled if requested

### Evidence Presentation

**Direct Examination:**

- Open-ended questions allowing witness to tell story
- Establish witness credibility and knowledge
- Introduce exhibits through witness
- Avoid leading questions (except preliminary matters)
- Organize chronologically or topically

**Cross-Examination:**

- Leading questions permitted and preferred
- Impeach witness credibility
- Elicit favorable facts
- Limit scope to direct examination subjects (plus credibility)
- Techniques: prior inconsistent statements, bias, lack of knowledge, contradiction with other evidence

**Objections:**

- **Relevance**: Evidence not relevant to issue in case
- **Hearsay**: Out-of-court statement offered for truth (with numerous exceptions)
- **Leading**: Leading question on direct examination
- **Speculation**: Witness guessing or assuming facts
- **Lack of Foundation**: Insufficient basis for testimony or exhibit
- **Improper Character Evidence**: Propensity evidence (with exceptions)
- **Privilege**: Attorney-client, spousal, doctor-patient, etc.
- **Best Evidence Rule**: Original document required (with exceptions)
- **Cumulative**: Repetitive evidence
- **Prejudicial**: Probative value substantially outweighed by unfair prejudice

**Exhibit Introduction:**

1. Mark exhibit for identification
2. Show to opposing counsel
3. Lay foundation through witness testimony
4. Offer exhibit into evidence
5. Respond to objections
6. Publish to jury (display or distribute)

## Post-Trial Proceedings

### Post-Trial Motions

**Renewed Motion for Judgment as Matter of Law (FRCP 50(b)):**

- Must have filed FRCP 50(a) motion during trial
- Filed within 28 days of judgment
- Argues insufficient evidence to support verdict
- High standard; rarely granted
- Preserves issue for appeal

**Motion for New Trial (FRCP 59):**

- Filed within 28 days of judgment
- Grounds:
  - Errors of law during trial
  - Verdict against weight of evidence
  - Newly discovered evidence
  - Excessive or inadequate damages
  - Jury misconduct
  - Attorney misconduct
- Court has discretion to grant
- May grant conditional new trial (new trial unless remittitur/additur accepted)

**Motion to Alter or Amend Judgment (FRCP 59(e)):**

- Filed within 28 days of judgment
- Correct legal or factual errors
- Limited grounds; not opportunity to re-argue case

### Judgment Enforcement

**Writ of Execution:**

- Court order directing sheriff to seize and sell debtor's property
- Applies to personal property and real property
- Exemptions protect certain property (homestead, tools of trade, etc.)

**Garnishment:**

- Court order directing third party to pay creditor from funds owed to debtor
- Wage garnishment (limited by federal and state law)
- Bank account garnishment
- Garnishment of accounts receivable

**Judgment Liens:**

- Record judgment to create lien on debtor's real property
- Lien attaches to current and after-acquired property
- Enforced through foreclosure or sale

**Debtor Examinations:**

- Court-ordered examination of judgment debtor's assets and income
- Debtor must appear and answer questions under oath
- Identify assets for execution or garnishment

**Contempt:**

- For violation of injunctions or court orders
- Civil contempt: coercive, ends when compliance achieved
- Criminal contempt: punitive, requires proof beyond reasonable doubt

### Appeals

**Notice of Appeal:**

- Filed within 30 days of judgment (FRAP 4)
- Jurisdictional deadline; cannot be extended absent extraordinary circumstances
- Specifies judgment appealed and parties

**Appellate Briefs:**

- **Appellant's Brief**: Identifies errors, provides legal argument, cites record
- **Appellee's Brief**: Responds to appellant's arguments, argues affirmance
- **Reply Brief**: Appellant responds to appellee's brief
- Strict formatting and length requirements

**Standard of Review:**

- **De Novo**: Legal issues reviewed without deference
- **Abuse of Discretion**: Discretionary rulings (evidentiary, procedural) reviewed deferentially
- **Clearly Erroneous**: Factual findings reviewed deferentially
- **Sufficiency of Evidence**: Whether reasonable jury could reach verdict

**Oral Argument:**

- Typically 15-30 minutes per side
- Panel asks questions
- Focus on key issues and weaknesses

**Appellate Decision:**

- Affirm, reverse, reverse and remand, or modify
- Written opinion (published or unpublished)
- Further appeal to higher court (certiorari, typically discretionary)

## Conclusion

The civil litigation process is complex, multi-staged, and governed by detailed procedural rules. Mastering each stage—from pre-litigation investigation through post-trial proceedings—is essential for effective advocacy. Strategic decision-making at each juncture, thorough preparation, and adherence to procedural requirements position cases for successful resolution, whether through settlement, trial verdict, or appellate affirmance. Understanding the litigation process enables practitioners to navigate disputes efficiently, manage client expectations, and achieve optimal outcomes.
