# Brand Architecture

Frameworks for organizing and managing brand portfolios.

---

## What is Brand Architecture

Brand architecture is the organizational structure of brands within a company's portfolio. It defines how brands relate to each other, how they're presented to the market, and how brand equity is managed across products and services.

---

## Why Brand Architecture Matters

| Benefit | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Clarity | Customers understand what brands mean and how they relate |
| Efficiency | Shared brand equity reduces marketing costs |
| Growth | Clear structure supports new product launches and acquisitions |
| Portfolio Management | Enables strategic decisions about brand investments |

---

## Brand Architecture Models

### Branded House (Monolithic)

A single master brand spans all products and services. Sub-offerings use the master brand name with descriptive modifiers.

**Examples**:
- FedEx (FedEx Express, FedEx Ground, FedEx Freight)
- Virgin (Virgin Atlantic, Virgin Mobile, Virgin Hotels)
- Google (Google Maps, Google Docs, Google Cloud)

| Benefit | Challenge |
|---------|----------|
| Maximum brand equity leverage | Risk concentration (one failure affects all) |
| Marketing efficiency | Less flexibility for diverse offerings |
| Clear customer understanding | Difficult to target distinct audiences |
| Strong corporate identity | May not suit unrelated product categories |

**Best For**: Companies with consistent value proposition across all offerings.

### House of Brands (Pluralistic)

Multiple independent brands, each with its own positioning, identity, and audience. The parent company is often invisible to consumers.

**Examples**:
- P&G (Tide, Pampers, Gillette, Olay)
- Unilever (Dove, Axe, Lipton, Ben & Jerry's)
- VF Corporation (The North Face, Vans, Timberland)

| Benefit | Challenge |
|---------|----------|
| Risk diversification | Higher marketing costs |
| Targeted positioning per brand | Less equity leverage |
| Acquisition flexibility | Portfolio complexity |
| No negative associations between brands | Potential internal competition |

**Best For**: Companies serving diverse markets with different needs.

### Endorsed Brands (Sub-brands)

Independent brands endorsed by a master brand. The endorsement provides credibility while allowing distinct positioning.

**Examples**:
- Marriott (Courtyard by Marriott, Ritz-Carlton, a Marriott International Brand)
- Nestlé (KitKat, Nescafé, endorsed by Nestlé)

| Benefit | Challenge |
|---------|----------|
| Credibility from endorsement | Potential confusion |
| Flexibility for distinct positioning | Managing endorsement visibility |
| Portfolio clarity | Complex guidelines |
| Balanced equity leverage | Endorsement may dilute sub-brand identity |

**Best For**: Companies wanting both corporate credibility and brand differentiation.

### Hybrid Architecture

Combination of models across different parts of the portfolio. Most large companies use hybrid approaches tailored to strategic needs.

**Example**:
- Coca-Cola uses branded house for core beverages (Coca-Cola, Diet Coke) but house of brands for acquisitions (Costa, Topo Chico)

## Brand Hierarchy Levels

| Level | Definition | Example |
|-------|------------|--------|
| Corporate Brand | The highest level, representing the company itself | General Electric |
| Family Brand | A brand that spans multiple related products | GE Appliances |
| Individual Brand | A standalone brand for specific product or service | GE Profile |
| Product Brand | Branding at the individual product level | GE Profile Smart Oven |

## Brand Portfolio Strategy

### Portfolio Analysis

Assess each brand across key dimensions:

| Dimension | Assessment Questions |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Role | What strategic role does this brand play? |
| Performance | Is the brand meeting financial and market goals? |
| Strategic Fit | Does the brand align with corporate strategy? |
| Growth Potential | What's the brand's future opportunity? |
| Investment Requirements | What resources does the brand need? |

### Brand Roles in Portfolio

| Role | Purpose | Characteristics |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Strategic Brand | Drive future growth | High investment, future focused |
| Power Brand | Generate current revenue | Core business, proven success |
| Fighter Brand | Compete at lower price points | Defensive positioning |
| Cash Cow Brand | Fund other initiatives | Low investment, steady returns |
| Flanker Brand | Protect core brand from competition | Segment specific |

### Portfolio Optimization Decisions

| Decision | When to Consider |
|----------|------------------|
| Invest | Brand has strong potential and strategic fit |
| Maintain | Brand performs adequately, no change needed |
| Harvest | Extract value while reducing investment |
| Divest | Brand no longer fits strategy or performs poorly |
| Consolidate | Merge overlapping or underperforming brands |
| Launch | Gap in portfolio requires new brand |

## Brand Extension Strategy

### Extension Types

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Line Extension | New variants within same category | Coca-Cola Zero |
| Category Extension | Enter new category with existing brand | Nike Golf |
| Brand Stretch | Extend into distant category | Virgin Records to Virgin Atlantic |
| Co-branding | Partner with another brand | Nike + Apple (Nike+) |

### Extension Evaluation Criteria

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Brand Fit | Does extension align with brand meaning and values? |
| Category Fit | Can brand credibly compete in new category? |
| Consumer Benefit | Does extension offer clear value to customers? |
| Strategic Rationale | Does extension support business strategy? |
| Risk Assessment | What are downside risks to core brand? |

### Extension Risks

- **Dilution**: Weakening core brand meaning
- **Cannibalization**: Taking sales from existing products
- **Failure Association**: Unsuccessful extension damaging master brand
- **Overextension**: Stretching brand beyond credible territory

---

## Architecture Decision Framework

### Key Decision Questions

1. **Leverage vs. Independence**: How much should the master brand be involved?
2. **Consistency vs. Flexibility**: How standardized should brand expression be?
3. **Efficiency vs. Targeting**: Trade-off between marketing efficiency and segment focus?
4. **Risk Distribution**: How concentrated or distributed should brand risk be?

### Architecture Selection Criteria

| Factor | Branded House Favors | House of Brands Favors |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Product Relatedness | High relatedness | Low relatedness |
| Target Audience | Same audience | Different audiences |
| Brand Equity | Strong master brand | Strong individual brands |
| Acquisition Strategy | Absorb into master | Maintain distinctiveness |
| Risk Tolerance | Higher tolerance | Lower tolerance |

### Implementation Considerations

- **Naming**: How will brands be named and connected?
- **Visual Identity**: How will brands be visually linked or distinguished?
- **Communication**: How will the relationship be explained to customers?
- **Organization**: How will brands be managed internally?
- **Governance**: How will decisions about brand use be made?
