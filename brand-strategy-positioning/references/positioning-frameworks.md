# Positioning Frameworks

Tools and methodologies for developing brand positioning.

---

## What is Brand Positioning

Brand positioning is the place your brand occupies in customers' minds relative to competitors. It's not what you do to a product—it's what you do in the mind of the prospect. Effective positioning creates a clear, distinctive, and desirable mental space for your brand.

---

## Positioning Statement Components

| Component | Definition | Questions to Answer |
|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| Target Audience | Who is this brand for? | Demographics, psychographics, needs |
| Category/Frame of Reference | What category do you compete in? | Market definition, competitive set |
| Point of Difference | What unique benefit do you offer? | Differentiation, competitive advantage |
| Reason to Believe | Why should customers believe your claim? | Evidence, proof points, credentials |

### Positioning Statement Template

"For [target audience] who [need/want], [brand] is the [category] that [point of difference] because [reason to believe]."

### Positioning Statement Examples

**Amazon (Early Days)**:
"For World Wide Web users who enjoy books, Amazon.com is a retail bookseller that provides instant access to over 1.1 million books. Unlike traditional book retailers, Amazon.com provides a combination of extraordinary convenience, low prices, and comprehensive selection."

**Volvo**:
"For upscale American families, Volvo is the family automobile that offers maximum safety."

**FedEx**:
"For businesses that need reliable, fast shipping, FedEx is the delivery service that guarantees on-time delivery because of its unmatched tracking systems and dedicated fleet."

## Positioning Strategies

### Price Positioning

| Strategy | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Premium | Higher price signaling superior quality | Rolex, Louis Vuitton |
| Value | Balanced price-quality offering | Target, Honda |
| Economy | Lowest price for budget-conscious | Walmart, Spirit Airlines |

### Quality Positioning

Emphasizing superior craftsmanship, materials, or performance. Often paired with premium pricing.

**Examples**: Mercedes-Benz, Patagonia

### Benefit Positioning

| Type | Focus | Examples |
|------|-------|----------|
| Functional Benefits | Practical advantages | Tide cleans better |
| Emotional Benefits | Feelings delivered | Disney creates magical memories |

### Use/Application Positioning

Specializing in specific use cases or occasions.

**Examples**: Gatorade for athletic performance, Nyquil for nighttime cold relief

### User Positioning

Targeting specific user types or identities.

**Examples**: GoPro for adventurers, Slack for teams

### Competitor Positioning

Positioning against or as an alternative to established competitors.

**Examples**: Avis: "We're #2, so we try harder"

### Category Positioning

| Approach | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Creating New Category | Define a new space where you're the leader | Red Bull created "energy drinks" |
| Redefining Category | Change how customers think about existing category | Apple redefined "personal computer" |

## Perceptual Mapping

### What is a Perceptual Map

A two-dimensional visualization plotting competitors on meaningful axes. Reveals market positioning and identifies white space opportunities.

### Axes Selection Criteria

- Meaningful to customers (influences purchase decisions)
- Differentiating for brands (shows clear distinctions)
- Actionable for strategy (can be leveraged or changed)

### Common Axes Pairs

| Axis 1 | Axis 2 | Best For |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Price | Quality | Value positioning |
| Traditional | Innovative | Innovation positioning |
| Serious | Playful | Personality positioning |
| Niche | Mass Market | Market scope positioning |
| Functional | Emotional | Benefit type positioning |

### Creating Perceptual Maps

1. Select two meaningful dimensions
2. Plot competitors based on customer perception research
3. Identify white space (unoccupied positions)
4. Evaluate white space for strategic attractiveness
5. Determine optimal positioning for your brand

## Brand Positioning Canvas

A structured template capturing key positioning elements:

| Section | Content |
|---------|--------|
| Target Audience | Who are you positioning for? |
| Category/Frame | What market are you in? |
| Point of Difference | What makes you unique? |
| Reason to Believe | Why should they believe you? |
| Brand Personality | What character traits define you? |

---

## Brand Pyramid (Brand Key)

Hierarchical framework building from functional to emotional:

| Level | Focus | Content |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Attributes | What the brand has | Features, facts |
| Benefits | What you get | Functional and emotional advantages |
| Values | What it means | Beliefs and principles |
| Personality | How it feels | Character and tone |
| Essence | The core idea | Fundamental truth |

---

## Golden Circle (Simon Sinek)

Circular model starting from the inside:

| Ring | Focus | Question |
|------|-------|----------|
| Why | Purpose | Why do you exist? |
| How | Process | How do you do what you do? |
| What | Product | What do you actually deliver? |

**Key Insight**: "People don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it."

## Value Proposition Canvas

### Customer Profile Side

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Customer Jobs | What are they trying to accomplish? |
| Pains | What frustrations and obstacles do they face? |
| Gains | What outcomes and benefits do they desire? |

### Value Map Side

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Products/Services | What do you offer? |
| Pain Relievers | How do you address customer pains? |
| Gain Creators | How do you enable customer gains? |

### Using the Canvas

1. Map customer jobs, pains, and gains through research
2. Document your products/services, pain relievers, and gain creators
3. Identify fit between customer needs and brand offerings
4. Highlight strongest connections for positioning

## Repositioning

### When to Reposition

- Market has fundamentally changed
- Competitive pressure requires response
- Brand has evolved beyond original positioning
- Current positioning limits growth

### Repositioning Strategies

| Approach | Description | Risk Level |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| Gradual Evolution | Slow, incremental shifts | Lower |
| Dramatic Shift | Bold repositioning | Higher |
| Segment Expansion | Addressing new audiences while maintaining core | Medium |
| Category Reframing | Changing competitive reference frame | Medium |

### Repositioning Risks

- Alienating existing customers
- Confusion during transition
- Loss of established brand equity
- Competitor response
