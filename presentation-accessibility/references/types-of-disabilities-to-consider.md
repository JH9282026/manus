## Types of Disabilities to Consider



### Visual Impairments

**Blindness**: Complete or near-complete vision loss requires screen reader compatibility, audio descriptions, and alternative text for all visual elements. Blind users navigate presentations using assistive technology that reads text aloud.

**Low Vision**: Partial vision loss affects millions of people worldwide. Low vision users benefit from high contrast, large text, scalable content, and magnification-friendly designs.

**Color Blindness**: Affecting approximately 8% of males and 0.5% of females, color blindness makes certain color combinations indistinguishable. Red-green color blindness is most common, followed by blue-yellow variants.



### Hearing Impairments

**Deafness**: Complete hearing loss requires captions, transcripts, and visual alternatives to all audio content. Sign language interpretation may be needed for live presentations.

**Hard of Hearing**: Partial hearing loss benefits from captions, clear audio quality, and the option to adjust volume. Background noise and poor audio quality create significant barriers.



### Motor and Mobility Impairments

Motor impairments affect the ability to use mice, keyboards, or touchscreens with precision. These users may rely on:

- Keyboard-only navigation
- Voice control software
- Switch devices
- Eye-tracking technology
- Head pointers or mouth sticks

Presentations must support alternative navigation methods and avoid requirements for precise mouse movements or rapid clicking.



### Cognitive Disabilities

Cognitive disabilities encompass a wide range of conditions affecting memory, attention, problem-solving, and comprehension:

**Learning Disabilities**: Dyslexia, dyscalculia, and other learning differences affect how individuals process written or numerical information.

**Attention Disorders**: ADHD and similar conditions make it difficult to focus on lengthy or complex presentations without clear structure and visual breaks.

**Memory Impairments**: Short-term memory challenges require repetition, summaries, and downloadable materials for reference.

**Intellectual Disabilities**: Varying levels of cognitive processing ability require plain language, clear visuals, and simplified concepts.



### Neurodiversity Considerations

Neurodivergent individuals, including those with autism spectrum disorder, may experience:

- Sensory sensitivities to flashing animations, bright colors, or complex patterns
- Difficulty processing figurative language or abstract concepts
- Need for predictable structure and clear transitions
- Preference for literal, direct communication

Accessible presentations accommodate these needs through predictable formats, reduced sensory stimulation, and clear communication.
