# 360° Video Shooting Techniques

On-set techniques for capturing quality 360° footage.

---

## Pre-Production Planning

### Location Scouting

**Visit with Camera**
- Bring 360° camera to scout locations
- Test capture in actual conditions
- Identify interesting viewpoints
- Check for lighting challenges

**Planning Checklist**
- [ ] Camera positions mapped
- [ ] Crew hiding spots identified
- [ ] Power/charging locations noted
- [ ] Lighting conditions assessed per time of day
- [ ] Audio challenges identified (noise, echo)
- [ ] Permits/permissions secured

### Shot Planning

**Storyboard for 360°**
- Map primary viewing direction
- Note secondary points of interest
- Plan attention guidance cues
- Indicate camera movement (if any)
- Mark subject positions relative to camera

**Information Hierarchy**
1. **Primary**: Where viewer should look first
2. **Secondary**: Supporting details
3. **Ambient**: Environmental context (full sphere)

## Camera Settings

### Resolution Settings

| Use Case | Minimum | Recommended |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Social media | 4K | 5.7K |
| Virtual tours | 5.7K | 8K |
| Professional | 5.7K | 8K+ |
| Archival | 8K | Maximum available |

Note: Higher resolution provides cropping flexibility and compensates for stitching artifacts.

### Frame Rate

| Frame Rate | Characteristics | Best For |
|------------|-----------------|----------|
| 24fps | Cinematic feel, can cause discomfort | Narrative, controlled content |
| 30fps | Standard, good balance | General purpose |
| 60fps | Smoother motion, larger files | Fast action, smooth experience |

### Exposure

**Manual Exposure Preferred**
- Balanced exposure around entire sphere
- Avoid extreme brightness differences
- Consistent across all takes

**Handling Mixed Lighting**
- ND filters for outdoor shoots
- Expose for primary subject area
- HDR modes when available (with caution)
- Consider shooting multiple exposures for bracketing

### White Balance

- Set manually for consistency
- Match across all cameras in multi-camera rigs
- Document settings for each location
- Consider mixed lighting challenges (indoor/outdoor)

## Camera Positioning

### The Fundamental Rule

Camera position = viewer position. Every placement decision determines where the audience "stands" in the scene.

### Height Considerations

| Height | Effect | Best For |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Low (2-3 ft) | Subjects appear larger, vulnerable feeling | Child/pet perspective, power dynamics |
| Eye level (5-6 ft) | Natural, comfortable | Default for most content |
| High (7+ ft) | Overview, subjects appear smaller | Establishing shots, surveillance feel |

### Positioning Strategies

**Observer Position**
- Slightly removed from action
- Documentary feel
- Comfortable viewing
- Lower engagement

**Participant Position**
- Within the scene
- Characters acknowledge camera/viewer
- Higher engagement
- More immersive

**Point-of-View**
- First-person perspective
- Character's viewpoint
- Most immersive (when done well)
- Challenging to execute

### Distance Guidelines

**Minimum Subject Distance**: 4-6 feet
- Closer causes uncomfortable fisheye distortion
- Unnatural proportions
- Stitching artifacts more visible

**Optimal Range**: 6-15 feet
- Natural proportions
- Clean stitching
- Comfortable viewing

**Far Subjects**: 15+ feet
- Less impact/presence
- Good for establishing context
- Environmental shots

## Hiding Crew and Equipment

### The Challenge

Everything is visible in 360°. Crew, equipment, lights—all captured unless hidden.

### Hiding Strategies

**Natural Obstacles**
- Behind furniture, pillars, walls
- In adjacent rooms
- Around corners
- Behind doors

**Remote Operation**
- Start recording remotely
- Monitor via smartphone/tablet
- Wireless preview systems
- Plan exit route before recording

**Timing-Based**
- Exit frame before recording starts
- Stay behind obstacles during takes
- Use pre-roll time to position

**Post-Production Removal**
- Document crew positions for rotoscoping
- Capture clean plates when possible
- Budget time for removal in post

### Equipment Hiding

**Tripod/Monopod**
- Use invisible selfie stick when possible
- Accept nadir patching for tripods
- Logo overlay in nadir common solution

**Lighting**
- Practical lights built into scene
- Embrace natural lighting
- Hidden fixtures (behind objects, in ceilings)
- 360° compatible light panels (visible as set dressing)

**Audio**
- Lavs hidden on talent
- Spatial mic positioned at camera
- Hide recorders behind obstacles

## Lighting for 360°

### The Challenge

No "off-camera" space for lighting equipment. Everything in the sphere is visible.

### Solutions

**Natural Light**
- Embrace available light
- Schedule shoots for optimal conditions
- Use reflectors (hidden from view)
- Golden hour particularly effective

**Practical Lights**
- Lamps, fixtures visible in scene
- Motivated lighting (from windows, overhead)
- LED panels dressed as set pieces
- Integrate lighting into production design

**Hidden Lights**
- Behind furniture/objects
- Recessed lighting
- Outside windows (interior shoots)
- Below frame (light from floor, hidden by camera)

**Post-Production**
- Color grading can compensate
- Balance exposure across sphere
- Targeted adjustments for specific areas

## Movement Techniques

### Static Shots

**Characteristics**
- Most comfortable for viewers
- Allows full exploration of scene
- Stable tripod/mount essential
- Good for establishing and detailed observation

**When to Use**
- Interview setups
- Tours (with cuts between positions)
- Scenes with multiple points of interest
- When viewer agency matters

### Slow Movement

**Characteristics**
- Gentle, motivated camera motion
- Smooth, consistent speed
- No acceleration or deceleration
- Cable cams, sliders, dollies

**Guidelines**
- Max speed: walking pace or slower
- Movement should feel motivated (following character, revealing space)
- Allow time for viewers to look around
- Plan movement direction relative to content

### Fast Movement

**Characteristics**
- Can cause motion sickness
- Disorienting for many viewers
- Requires viewer expectation matching

**When Acceptable**
- Thrill experiences (rollercoasters, vehicles)
- Very short duration
- When viewer expects/wants intensity
- Use sparingly

## Audio Capture On-Set

### Spatial Audio Recording

**Microphone Positioning**
- Place spatial mic at camera location
- Match visual and audio perspectives
- Secure mounting to avoid handling noise

**Recording Tips**
- Monitor levels remotely
- Record room tone/ambience (30-60 seconds)
- Capture wild sound for post flexibility
- Backup recording recommended

### Dialogue Capture

**Primary Methods**
- Lavalier mics on talent (hidden)
- Boom microphone (requires hiding operator)
- Post-sync dialogue (ADR)

**Considerations**
- Spatial mic captures ambience, may not capture clear dialogue
- Layer clean dialogue with spatial ambience in post
- Plan dialogue delivery for audibility

## Common On-Set Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Camera too close | Distortion, discomfort | Maintain 4-6 ft minimum |
| Crew visible | Breaks immersion | Remote monitoring, plan hiding |
| Inconsistent exposure | Stitching issues | Manual settings, test thoroughly |
| No spatial audio | Breaks spatial reality | Budget for spatial mic |
| Rushed setup | Technical problems | Allow adequate setup time |
| No clean plates | Difficult post removal | Capture empty scene for each setup |
| Single take | No coverage options | Multiple takes, bracketed exposure |

## Shooting Checklist

### Before Each Setup

- [ ] Camera position determined and marked
- [ ] Height and angle set
- [ ] Subject distances checked (min 4-6 ft)
- [ ] Crew hiding positions planned
- [ ] Remote monitoring connected
- [ ] Audio recording started
- [ ] Exposure/white balance set and documented
- [ ] Lenses cleaned
- [ ] Storage capacity confirmed

### Before Each Take

- [ ] Frame check (look for unwanted elements)
- [ ] Audio levels checked
- [ ] Crew in hiding positions
- [ ] Recording started remotely
- [ ] Pre-roll time allowed

### After Each Take

- [ ] Playback review on set (if possible)
- [ ] Check all angles in 360° viewer
- [ ] Verify audio quality
- [ ] Note good takes
- [ ] Document settings for continuity
