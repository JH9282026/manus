# Research Synthesis Frameworks

## Overview

Research synthesis transforms raw data into actionable insights. Frameworks provide structure for analyzing findings, identifying patterns, and communicating results to stakeholders.

---

## Affinity Diagramming

**Process**: 
1. Write each observation, quote, or data point on a sticky note
2. Spread notes on wall or digital board
3. Group related notes into clusters
4. Name each cluster with a theme
5. Group clusters into higher-level categories
6. Identify patterns and insights

**Best Practices**: Include diverse team members, let patterns emerge naturally, iterate on groupings, use different colored notes for different data sources.

**Output**: Visual map of research findings organized by themes. Reveals patterns not obvious in raw data.

## Journey Mapping

**Purpose**: Visualize user experience over time. Identifies pain points, opportunities, and emotional highs/lows.

**Components**:
- Phases: Major stages of the journey
- Actions: What user does in each phase
- Thoughts: What user is thinking
- Emotions: How user feels (often shown as line graph)
- Pain points: Frustrations and obstacles
- Opportunities: Areas for improvement

**Process**: 
1. Define scope (which journey to map)
2. Identify phases based on research
3. Populate actions, thoughts, emotions from user data
4. Highlight pain points and opportunities
5. Validate with users if possible

**Use Cases**: Understanding end-to-end experience, identifying improvement priorities, building empathy with stakeholders.

## Persona Development

**Purpose**: Create representative archetypes of user segments. Makes research findings tangible and memorable.

**Components**:
- Name and photo (makes persona feel real)
- Demographics (age, location, occupation)
- Goals and motivations (what they want to achieve)
- Behaviors and preferences (how they work)
- Pain points and frustrations (what holds them back)
- Quote (captures their perspective)

**Best Practices**: Base on research data, not assumptions. Focus on behaviors and goals, not just demographics. Create 3-5 personas max. Give each a memorable name.

**Anti-pattern**: Don't create personas before research. Don't make them too detailed (wastes time). Don't treat them as real people (they're tools).

## Jobs to Be Done Framework

**Concept**: Users 'hire' products to do a job. Understanding the job reveals what users really need.

**Job Statement Format**: 'When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].'

**Example**: 'When I'm commuting to work, I want to catch up on industry news, so I can stay informed without dedicating separate time.'

**Analysis**: 
- What job is the user trying to do?
- What are they currently 'hiring' to do this job?
- What are the limitations of current solutions?
- What would make them 'fire' current solution and 'hire' yours?

**Benefits**: Focuses on user needs rather than product features. Reveals unexpected competition. Guides feature prioritization.

## Empathy Mapping

**Purpose**: Build shared understanding of user perspective. Quick synthesis tool for workshops.

**Quadrants**:
- Says: Direct quotes from users
- Thinks: Inferred thoughts and beliefs
- Does: Observed behaviors and actions
- Feels: Emotions and attitudes

**Process**: 
1. Draw four-quadrant grid
2. Populate each quadrant with research findings
3. Identify patterns and contradictions
4. Discuss as team

**Use Cases**: Workshop activities, quick synthesis, building empathy, aligning team on user perspective.

---

## Insight Statements

**Format**: [User segment] needs [need] because [insight].

**Example**: 'Busy parents need quick meal solutions because they value family time over cooking time.'

**Characteristics of Good Insights**:
- Specific to a user segment
- Reveals underlying motivation (the 'why')
- Actionable (suggests design direction)
- Surprising or non-obvious
- Supported by research data

**Process**: Review research findings, identify patterns, articulate underlying needs, write insight statements, validate with team and users.
