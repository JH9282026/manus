# Research Methodologies

## Overview

Design research uncovers user needs, validates assumptions, and informs design decisions. Choosing the right methodology depends on your research questions, timeline, and resources.

---

## Qualitative vs Quantitative

**Qualitative Research**: Explores the 'why' behind behaviors. Methods include interviews, usability testing, field studies. Provides rich insights but smaller sample sizes.

**Quantitative Research**: Measures the 'what' and 'how much'. Methods include surveys, analytics, A/B tests. Provides statistical significance but less depth.

**Mixed Methods**: Combines both approaches. Example: Survey (quantitative) followed by interviews with interesting respondents (qualitative). Provides both breadth and depth.

---

## User Interviews

**Purpose**: Understand user needs, motivations, pain points, and mental models. Explore problem space before designing solutions.

**Structure**: 
- Introduction (5 min): Build rapport, explain purpose, get consent
- Background (10 min): Understand user context and experience
- Main questions (30 min): Deep dive into research topics
- Wrap-up (5 min): Any final thoughts, thank participant

**Question Types**:
- Open-ended: 'Tell me about your experience with...'
- Probing: 'Can you tell me more about that?'
- Specific: 'What did you do when that happened?'
- Avoid leading questions: 'Don't you think X is better?' (bad)

**Best Practices**: Listen more than talk, ask 'why' to dig deeper, embrace silence, take notes or record (with permission).

---

## Usability Testing

**Purpose**: Evaluate how easily users can complete tasks with your design. Identify usability issues before launch.

**Process**:
1. Define tasks that represent key user goals
2. Recruit representative users (5-8 per round)
3. Have users complete tasks while thinking aloud
4. Observe and take notes without helping
5. Debrief with follow-up questions
6. Analyze findings and prioritize issues

**Moderated vs Unmoderated**:
- Moderated: Researcher present, can probe and clarify, richer insights
- Unmoderated: Remote and asynchronous, faster and cheaper, less depth

**Metrics**: Task success rate, time on task, error rate, satisfaction rating. Combine with qualitative observations.

---

## Surveys

**Purpose**: Gather quantitative data from large samples. Measure attitudes, behaviors, preferences, or satisfaction.

**Question Types**:
- Multiple choice: Easy to analyze, but may miss nuance
- Rating scales: Likert scales (1-5 or 1-7) for measuring agreement or satisfaction
- Open-ended: Provides qualitative insights but harder to analyze
- Ranking: Forces prioritization among options

**Best Practices**: 
- Keep surveys short (5-10 minutes max)
- One question per item (avoid double-barreled questions)
- Use neutral language (avoid bias)
- Provide 'other' or 'prefer not to answer' options
- Pilot test before full launch

**Sample Size**: Aim for statistical significance. For 95% confidence and 5% margin of error, need ~400 responses.

---

## Field Studies

**Purpose**: Observe users in their natural environment. Understand context, workflows, and real-world constraints.

**Contextual Inquiry**: Combination of observation and interview. Watch users work, ask questions about what they're doing and why.

**Ethnographic Research**: Immersive observation over extended period. Researcher becomes part of the environment.

**Diary Studies**: Users document their experiences over time. Captures longitudinal data and in-the-moment thoughts.

**When to Use**: Understanding complex workflows, identifying unmet needs, designing for unfamiliar contexts.

---

## Card Sorting

**Purpose**: Understand how users categorize and organize information. Informs information architecture and navigation.

**Open Card Sort**: Users create their own categories. Reveals mental models.

**Closed Card Sort**: Users sort into predefined categories. Validates proposed structure.

**Process**: 
1. Create cards representing content items
2. Have users (15-30) sort cards into groups
3. Analyze results for patterns and agreement
4. Use findings to structure navigation and IA

**Tools**: OptimalSort, UserZoom, or physical cards.
