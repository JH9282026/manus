# Speech Structure and Storytelling

Frameworks and techniques for creating compelling presentation narratives that engage audiences and drive action.

---

## Presentation Structure Frameworks

### Three-Act Structure

**Act 1: Setup (25% of time)**
- Establish context and credibility
- Hook audience attention
- Preview key messages
- Create curiosity or tension

**Act 2: Confrontation (50% of time)**
- Present main content and evidence
- Build argument or narrative
- Address objections or challenges
- Develop emotional connection

**Act 3: Resolution (25% of time)**
- Synthesize key points
- Provide clear takeaways
- Call to action
- Memorable closing

### Monroe's Motivated Sequence

Five-step persuasive structure:

1. **Attention** — Grab audience focus with hook
2. **Need** — Establish problem or opportunity
3. **Satisfaction** — Present solution or approach
4. **Visualization** — Help audience see future state
5. **Action** — Clear next steps

Particularly effective for persuasive presentations and sales pitches.

### The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

Start with conclusion, then support with evidence:
- Lead with answer or recommendation
- Group supporting arguments in threes
- Organize logically (deductive or inductive)
- Use MECE principle (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)

Ideal for executive and board presentations where decision-makers want bottom line first.

## Opening Techniques

### The Surprising Statistic

Present unexpected data that challenges assumptions:
- Must be credible and verifiable
- Should relate directly to core message
- Creates cognitive dissonance that demands resolution
- Example: "By the time I finish this sentence, 3 people will have..."

### The Personal Story

Share relevant experience that establishes credibility and connection:
- Keep to 60-90 seconds maximum
- Include specific sensory details
- Show vulnerability when appropriate
- Connect explicitly to presentation topic
- Use present tense for immediacy

### The Provocative Question

Pose question that engages audience thinking:
- Rhetorical questions create internal dialogue
- Show of hands questions create participation
- "What if" questions open possibilities
- Avoid questions with obvious answers

### The Bold Statement

Make contrarian or unexpected claim:
- Must be defensible with evidence
- Creates tension that presentation resolves
- Positions speaker as thought leader
- Example: "Everything you know about X is wrong"

## Storytelling Elements

### The Hero's Journey (Applied to Business)

Adapt Joseph Campbell's framework:
- **Ordinary world** — Current state or status quo
- **Call to adventure** — Problem or opportunity emerges
- **Refusal of call** — Initial resistance or challenges
- **Meeting the mentor** — Guidance or insight (your solution)
- **Crossing threshold** — Decision to act
- **Tests and allies** — Implementation challenges
- **Reward** — Benefits and outcomes
- **Return transformed** — New state achieved

### Story Structure Components

**Conflict and resolution:**
- Every story needs tension
- Present obstacle before solution
- Show stakes (what's at risk)
- Demonstrate transformation

**Specific details:**
- Names, dates, locations create authenticity
- Sensory details (what you saw, heard, felt)
- Dialogue brings scenes to life
- Avoid generic abstractions

**Emotional arc:**
- Map emotional journey of story
- Vary emotional intensity
- Build to climax
- Resolve with satisfaction or insight

## Rhetorical Devices

### Rule of Three

Information in groups of three is memorable and satisfying:
- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- "Blood, sweat, and tears"
- Three main points in presentation
- Three supporting examples per point

### Repetition and Refrain

Repeat key phrases for emphasis and memory:
- Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I have a dream"
- Steve Jobs' "One more thing"
- Repeat at strategic intervals
- Vary context while maintaining phrase

### Metaphor and Analogy

Make complex ideas accessible through comparison:
- "The brain is like a computer" (metaphor)
- "This process is similar to how a tree grows" (analogy)
- Choose familiar reference points for audience
- Extend metaphor throughout presentation for coherence

### Contrast and Juxtaposition

Highlight differences to clarify position:
- "Not X, but Y"
- Before/after comparisons
- Problem/solution contrast
- Old way vs. new way

## Closing Techniques

### The Call to Action

Specify exactly what you want audience to do:
- Make it concrete and achievable
- Provide clear next steps
- Create urgency when appropriate
- Remove barriers to action

### The Callback

Reference opening story or hook:
- Creates satisfying narrative closure
- Demonstrates coherent structure
- Reinforces key message
- Shows preparation and intentionality

### The Memorable Quote

End with words from authority or inspiration:
- Must relate directly to core message
- Attribute properly
- Deliver with conviction
- Let it resonate (don't explain)

### The Vision of the Future

Paint picture of transformed state:
- Make it vivid and specific
- Show benefits realized
- Create emotional pull
- Connect to audience values

## Content Organization Principles

### Primacy and Recency Effects

Audiences remember best what they hear first and last:
- Place strongest content at beginning and end
- Middle content should support and develop
- Summarize key points before closing
- Avoid introducing new information in conclusion

### Cognitive Load Management

Limit information to prevent overwhelm:
- Maximum 3 main points for retention
- Break complex ideas into digestible chunks
- Use signposting ("First... Second... Finally...")
- Provide mental breaks with stories or visuals

### Logical Flow and Transitions

Connect ideas smoothly:
- Use transition phrases ("Building on that...", "This leads us to...")
- Preview upcoming content
- Summarize before moving to next section
- Create narrative thread throughout

## Adapting Structure to Time Constraints

### 5-Minute Presentation
- Single main point
- One supporting story or example
- Clear call to action
- No more than 5 slides

### 15-Minute Presentation (TED format)
- One big idea with framework
- 2-3 supporting points
- Personal story for connection
- 10-15 slides maximum
- Memorized delivery

### 30-Minute Presentation
- 3 main points
- Multiple supporting examples per point
- Audience interaction opportunities
- 20-30 slides
- Time for Q&A

### 60-Minute Presentation
- 3-5 main sections
- Deep dive into each area
- Multiple engagement techniques
- Breaks or activities
- Substantial Q&A time
