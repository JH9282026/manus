# Radio Ad Copywriting

## Introduction to Radio Ad Copywriting

Radio advertising remains one of the most powerful and cost-effective marketing channels available today. Despite the rise of digital media, podcasts, and streaming services, traditional radio reaches 92% of American adults weekly—more than television, smartphones, or computers. Radio's unique ability to reach consumers during their daily routines, particularly during commutes, makes it an invaluable medium for advertisers seeking to build brand awareness and drive action.

Radio ad copywriting is fundamentally different from writing for visual media. Without images, video, or text to rely on, radio copywriters must paint pictures entirely with words and sound. This constraint, far from being a limitation, unlocks a unique creative opportunity: the theater of the mind. When done well, radio advertising creates vivid mental imagery that can be more memorable and emotionally resonant than any visual medium.

The challenge of radio copywriting lies in capturing and holding attention using only audio elements—voice, music, sound effects, and silence. You have seconds to hook listeners, a finite amount of time to deliver your message, and no visual aids to support your words. Every syllable must earn its place, and every sound must serve a purpose.

### Why Radio Advertising Works

Radio's effectiveness as an advertising medium stems from several unique characteristics:

**Intimacy and trust:** Radio personalities become trusted companions in listeners' daily lives. When these voices recommend products or services, that trust transfers to the advertiser. This parasocial relationship is uniquely powerful in driving consumer behavior.

**Captive audiences:** Unlike other media, radio reaches people when they can't look away—while driving, working, or exercising. This captive attention is increasingly rare and valuable in our fragmented media landscape.

**Theater of the mind:** Radio engages the imagination in ways visual media cannot. A listener's mental image of a beach sunset will be more personally resonant than any stock photo could ever be.

**Frequency and reach:** Radio's relatively low production costs allow for higher frequency advertising, and research consistently shows that repetition is essential for message retention and brand recall.

**Local targeting:** Radio stations serve specific geographic areas and demographic niches, allowing advertisers to efficiently reach their target audiences.

**Immediacy:** Radio is live, current, and responsive. Ads can reference today's weather, current events, or time-sensitive promotions in ways that feel relevant and timely.

## Radio Ad Formats

### The 60-Second Spot

The 60-second radio spot is the workhorse of radio advertising. It provides enough time to tell a story, establish a scenario, present a problem and solution, and deliver a memorable call-to-action. Most branding campaigns and complex messages are best served by 60-second spots.

**Word count:** Approximately 140-170 words at conversational pace
**Best for:** Brand building, storytelling, emotional appeals, complex offerings

**Structure options:**
- Problem-solution narrative
- Testimonial format
- Character dialogue
- Announcer-driven information

**Example 60-second spot (Auto Dealership):**

*[SFX: Car engine sputtering, dying]*

**VOICE 1:** Not again...

**VOICE 2:** What's wrong?

**VOICE 1:** Third time this month my car's broken down. I should've listened to you about Anderson Auto.

**VOICE 2:** Well, it's not too late. They have over 200 certified pre-owned vehicles, all with a 100-point inspection and 3-year warranty.

**VOICE 1:** Really? I figured certified pre-owned meant expensive.

**VOICE 2:** That's the best part—prices start at just $199 a month. And they'll even give you top dollar for your trade-in. Yes, even that one.

*[SFX: Car sputtering again]*

**VOICE 1:** Okay, I'm calling them right now.

**ANNOUNCER:** Anderson Auto—your trusted partner for certified pre-owned vehicles. Visit us on Route 9 in Springfield or at AndersonAuto.com. Anderson Auto. Drive happy.

### The 30-Second Spot

The 30-second spot is radio's most common format. It requires ruthless efficiency—every word must work hard to communicate the message and drive action. There's no time for elaborate setups or complex narratives; you must get to the point immediately.

**Word count:** Approximately 70-85 words at conversational pace
**Best for:** Simple messages, reminders, promotions, calls-to-action

**Structure:** Direct and focused—typically one main message with a clear CTA

**Example 30-second spot (Restaurant):**

**ANNOUNCER:** Looking for the perfect place for date night? Bella Vista Italian Restaurant is offering a special this week only. Get a free bottle of wine with any two entrées. Our handmade pasta, wood-fired pizzas, and romantic atmosphere make every evening special. This week only—free wine with two entrées. Bella Vista on Main Street, downtown. Reservations at 555-BELLA. Bella Vista—where every meal is a celebration.

### The 15-Second Spot

The 15-second spot is pure essence. With only 35-45 words available, there's time for exactly one idea and one call-to-action. These spots work best for reinforcing messages from longer ads, simple promotions, or high-frequency campaigns where repetition does the heavy lifting.

**Word count:** Approximately 35-45 words at conversational pace
**Best for:** Sales announcements, simple offers, brand reinforcement, high-frequency campaigns

**Example 15-second spot (Retail Sale):**

**ANNOUNCER:** It's the Summer Clearance Event at Martin's Furniture! Everything in the store is 50% off—sofas, dining sets, mattresses, everything! This weekend only at Martin's Furniture on Oak Street. Fifty percent off everything!

### Live Reads and Endorsements

Live reads are ad scripts read by radio personalities in their own voice and style. They leverage the host's relationship with their audience for maximum credibility. The copy provides key points but allows for personalization.

**Word count:** Variable, typically 60-90 seconds worth of material
**Best for:** Building trust, local businesses, products requiring explanation or endorsement

**Live read script format:**

*Key points to cover:*
- *Company name: Franklin's Home Services*
- *Services: Plumbing, heating, AC, electrical*
- *Key differentiator: Same-day service, no weekend/holiday upcharges*
- *Offer: $50 off first service call for new customers*
- *Call-to-action: Call 555-HOME or visit FranklinsHome.com*
- *Tagline: "Franklin's—because your home shouldn't wait"*

*Sample talking points:*
"You know, I've used Franklin's Home Services myself, and let me tell you—when your AC goes out in July, you need someone who shows up same day. That's Franklin's. Plumbing, heating, AC, electrical—they do it all, and here's the thing: no extra charges for weekends or holidays. New customers get $50 off your first service call. 555-HOME or FranklinsHome.com. Franklin's—because your home shouldn't wait."

## Writing for the Ear

### The Fundamental Principle

Radio copywriting requires a complete shift in mindset from visual media. You're not writing for readers—you're writing for listeners. This fundamental difference affects every aspect of your craft, from word choice to sentence structure to information architecture.

**Key principles of writing for the ear:**

1. **Write the way people speak:** Conversational language, contractions, natural rhythms. Read your copy aloud—if it sounds stilted when spoken, it needs revision.

2. **Use short sentences:** Long, complex sentences lose listeners. Aim for an average of 10-15 words per sentence, with variety for rhythm.

3. **Avoid tongue-twisters:** Alliteration can be effective, but watch for awkward consonant combinations that trip up voice talent.

4. **Embrace repetition:** In print, repetition seems redundant. On radio, it's essential for retention. Repeat your key message, phone number, and company name.

5. **Front-load important information:** Listeners can't re-read a sentence they didn't understand. Put the most important words at the beginning.

6. **Use concrete, visual words:** Abstract concepts don't create mental images. "Luxury" means nothing; "butter-soft leather seats" paints a picture.

### Sound-Based Word Choice

Choose words that sound good when spoken aloud. Consider:

**Phonetic clarity:** Words that are easy to understand when heard, not just read. Avoid words that sound similar to other words or that might be confused.

*Example:* "555-4200" is clearer than "555-4020" (four-two-hundred vs. forty-twenty)

**Emotional resonance:** Words that feel a certain way when spoken. Hard consonants (k, t, p) sound energetic and urgent. Soft consonants (s, m, l) sound smooth and relaxing.

**Rhythm and flow:** The musicality of language matters on radio. Varied sentence lengths create interest; monotonous patterns put listeners to sleep.

**Example of sound-conscious writing:**

*Before:* "Our establishment offers a multiplicity of customizable options for your consideration."

*After:* "We've got options. Lots of them. And we'll help you find exactly what you need."

## Theater of the Mind

### Creating Mental Images

Radio's greatest strength is its ability to engage the imagination. Unlike television or video, where images are provided for the viewer, radio invites listeners to create their own mental pictures. These self-generated images are often more vivid and emotionally resonant than anything a camera could capture.

**Techniques for creating mental imagery:**

**Sensory language:** Don't just describe—evoke. Appeal to all five senses.

*Instead of:* "Our coffee is really good."
*Write:* "Imagine waking up to the rich aroma of freshly ground beans, that first warm sip bringing you slowly to life..."

**Specific details:** General descriptions don't create pictures. Specific details do.

*Instead of:* "A nice house in a good neighborhood."
*Write:* "A Victorian with a wraparound porch, just three blocks from downtown."

**Sound effects as setting:** Use sound effects to establish time, place, and mood without spending precious words.

*[SFX: Waves crashing, seagulls calling]* immediately transports listeners to a beach without a single word of description.

**Character voices:** Different voices create different mental images of the speakers. A gruff older voice vs. a youthful energetic voice immediately suggests different characters.

### Building Scenes and Scenarios

Effective radio ads often create mini-scenes that listeners can visualize and relate to. These scenarios provide context for your message and emotional hooks for your audience.

**Scenario-building formula:**

1. **Establish the setting:** Use sound effects and brief description to place the listener in a scene.

2. **Introduce a relatable situation:** Present a problem or desire the listener can identify with.

3. **Create emotional stakes:** Help the listener feel the frustration, desire, or hope of the situation.

4. **Provide the solution:** Introduce your product or service as the answer.

5. **Paint the improved future:** Show how life is better with your solution.

**Example (Insurance):**

*[SFX: Phone ringing]*

**WIFE:** Hello?

**HOSPITAL:** Mrs. Johnson? This is Memorial Hospital. Your husband's been in an accident.

*[SFX: Heart beating]*

**WIFE:** Is he okay? Is he...

**HOSPITAL:** He's stable, but we need to discuss his treatment. Does he have insurance?

**WIFE:** Yes. Yes, he has Premier Health. Premium coverage.

**HOSPITAL:** That's exactly what we needed to hear.

**ANNOUNCER:** In the moments that matter most, you need coverage you can count on. Premier Health Insurance—because your family's future isn't something to gamble with. Call 555-PREMIER today.

## Radio Ad Structure

### The Opening Hook

You have approximately three seconds to capture a listener's attention before they tune out or change the station. Your opening must be compelling enough to break through the ambient noise of daily life and give listeners a reason to keep listening.

**Effective opening techniques:**

**The provocative question:**
"What would you do with an extra $500 a month?"
"When was the last time you woke up without back pain?"

**The startling statement:**
"Your mattress has more bacteria than your toilet seat."
"The average American wastes $1,200 a year on unused subscriptions."

**Sound effects that create curiosity:**
*[SFX: Glass shattering, tires screeching, baby crying]*—sounds that demand attention and explanation.

**The relatable scenario:**
"You know that moment when you realize you forgot to pay the electric bill?"
"Picture this: It's 3 AM, and your basement is flooded."

**The direct address:**
"Hey, Springfield homeowners—this is important."
"Attention drivers: There's a better way to get to work."

**Avoid these weak openings:**
- Starting with the company name (no one cares yet)
- Generic greetings ("Hello, friends...")
- Slow buildups that waste precious seconds
- Industry jargon or technical terms

### The Body: Delivering Your Message

The body of your radio ad must efficiently communicate your value proposition, supporting points, and any necessary details. This is where you build interest, establish credibility, and prepare listeners for your call-to-action.

**Body structure principles:**

**One main message:** Radio ads can communicate one idea effectively. Trying to cram multiple messages dilutes everything. Decide on your single most important point and build around it.

**Benefit-focused:** Don't list features—communicate benefits. How does your product or service make the listener's life better?

**Proof and credibility:** Support your claims with evidence. Numbers, testimonials, awards, or years in business all add credibility.

**Emotional connection:** Connect your message to the listener's emotions—their desires, fears, aspirations, or frustrations.

**Example body (Lawn Care Service):**

"At GreenScape, we take lawn care off your to-do list permanently. Our expert technicians handle everything—mowing, edging, fertilizing, weed control—so you can spend your weekends doing what you actually enjoy. We've been serving Springfield families for 25 years, and we guarantee a lawn you'll be proud of. No contracts, no hassles, just a beautiful yard, week after week."

### The Call-to-Action

Every radio ad needs a clear, memorable call-to-action that tells listeners exactly what to do next. The CTA must be simple enough to remember and act on, even if the listener can't write it down.

**CTA best practices:**

**Keep phone numbers simple:** Use vanity numbers (555-FLOWERS) or easy-to-remember patterns (555-1000). Repeat the number at least twice.

**Make web addresses speakable:** Avoid hyphens, numbers, or unusual spellings. "GreenScapeLawns.com" is better than "Green-Scape-123.com."

**Include location for local businesses:** "Visit us on Main Street, across from the library" helps people find you.

**Create urgency when appropriate:** "Call today," "this weekend only," "limited time offer."

**Give them a reason to act now:** A special offer, limited availability, or time-sensitive benefit motivates immediate action.

**Example CTAs:**

*Simple:* "Visit us on Route 9, or call 555-AUTO."

*With offer:* "Call 555-DENTAL today and mention this ad for a free whitening consultation."

*Urgency:* "This sale ends Saturday, so visit Martin's Furniture this weekend. Main Street, downtown."

*Easy-to-remember:* "Just remember: 555-LAWNS. That's 555-L-A-W-N-S."

### The Tag (Sign-off)

The tag is your final impression—usually a brief sign-off that reinforces your brand name and key message. It's often a tagline, slogan, or positioning statement that encapsulates what the brand stands for.

**Effective tags:**

*Tagline:* "Anderson Auto. Drive happy."

*Positioning:* "First National Bank—your neighbors, your bank."

*Value statement:* "Quality furniture at prices that make sense."

*Call-back:* "Remember: When your home needs help, call Franklin's."

## Music and Sound Effects

### The Role of Music

Music in radio advertising serves multiple functions: it sets mood, creates emotional response, signals brand identity, and aids memorability. The right music choice can elevate a good ad to a great one; the wrong choice can undermine your entire message.

**Music functions:**

**Mood setting:** Upbeat music creates energy; soft music creates calm; urgent music creates tension.

**Demographic targeting:** Music choices signal who the ad is for. Classic rock targets different listeners than hip-hop or country.

**Brand identity:** Consistent music across campaigns builds audio brand recognition.

**Emotional trigger:** Music can evoke nostalgia, excitement, comfort, or urgency faster than words alone.

**Music considerations:**

- **Licensing:** Ensure you have proper rights to any music used. Production music libraries offer affordable, licensed options.
- **Volume balance:** Music should support, not compete with, the voice. Keep music low under voice-over.
- **Transitions:** Use music to signal transitions, build to key moments, and create a satisfying conclusion.
- **Consistency:** Use consistent music across a campaign for recognition.

### Sound Effects

Sound effects create instant mental imagery and transport listeners to specific times, places, and situations. They can establish setting in a split second, freeing your copy to focus on the message.

**Effective SFX use:**

*Setting the scene:*
- Office: keyboard typing, phones ringing, copy machine
- Home: doorbell, kids playing, TV in background
- Beach: waves, seagulls, distant laughter
- Restaurant: clinking dishes, ambient conversation

*Creating urgency:*
- Ticking clock
- Alarm bells
- Heartbeat

*Enhancing narrative:*
- Car engine (showing up, leaving)
- Cash register (sale)
- Applause (celebration)

**SFX cautions:**

- Don't overuse—too many sound effects become confusing
- Ensure SFX are recognizable—obscure sounds confuse rather than clarify
- Time them precisely—poorly timed effects disrupt the flow
- Keep them appropriate—comedic sounds in serious ads undermine credibility

## Jingles and Mnemonics

### The Power of Jingles

A great jingle is an advertising asset that appreciates over time. It creates instant brand recognition, embeds your message in memory, and can remain effective for decades. Think of "Nationwide is on your side," "I'm lovin' it," or "Like a good neighbor, State Farm is there"—these melodic signatures are worth millions in brand equity.

**What makes a jingle effective:**

1. **Simplicity:** Easy to sing, easy to remember. Complex melodies don't stick.

2. **Repetition of key information:** Brand name, phone number, or core message woven into the melody.

3. **Emotional resonance:** The melody creates a feeling that aligns with the brand.

4. **Uniqueness:** Distinctive enough to own in the listener's mind.

5. **Flexibility:** Can be adapted to different ad lengths and campaigns.

**Types of jingles:**

**Full jingle:** Complete musical advertisement that tells the brand story (often 60 seconds).

**Donut jingle:** Musical opening and closing with space for customizable voice-over in the middle.

**Tagline jingle:** Short musical signature (5-10 seconds) that signs off advertisements.

**Mnemonic device:** Very brief musical element that triggers brand recognition (Intel's five-note bong).

### Creating Memorable Audio Signatures

Even without a full jingle, you can create memorable audio elements that build brand recognition:

**Sonic logos:** Brief musical or audio identifiers (like Intel's bong or Netflix's "ta-dum").

**Consistent voice talent:** Using the same voice across all advertisements creates recognition.

**Verbal mnemonics:** Phrases that are inherently memorable without music.
- "Five dollar foot long" (Subway)
- "Save big money at Menards!"
- "Eight-hundred, five-eight-eight, two-three-hundred... Empire!"

**Memorable phone numbers:**
- 1-800-FLOWERS
- 1-800-CONTACTS
- 588-2300 (set to music: "Five-eight-eight, two-three-hundred")

## Voice Talent and Delivery

### Choosing the Right Voice

The voice delivering your message is as important as the words themselves. The right voice can make copy sing; the wrong voice can make it fall flat.

**Voice selection considerations:**

**Demographics:** Match voice characteristics to your target audience and brand identity.
- Older voices suggest experience and trustworthiness
- Younger voices suggest energy and freshness
- Regional accents can signal local authenticity

**Tone:** The voice's natural quality should match your message.
- Warm and friendly for service businesses
- Authoritative for financial or medical
- Energetic for retail and promotions
- Calm and reassuring for insurance and security

**Gender considerations:** Neither gender is universally better—choose based on your specific audience and message. Test when possible.

**Authenticity:** The voice should sound natural and believable. Overly polished "announcer" voices can sound fake to modern audiences.

### Writing for Voice Talent

Your script should support the voice talent's delivery. Consider:

**Pacing notes:** Indicate where to pause, speed up, or slow down.

**Emphasis guides:** Underline or boldface words that should be emphasized.

**Pronunciation guides:** Include phonetic spellings for unusual names or terms.

**Character descriptions:** For dialogue, describe who the characters are so talent can embody them.

**Mood directions:** Note the emotional tone you're seeking.

**Example annotated script:**

```
ANNOUNCER: (Warm, conversational—like talking to a friend)

Are you tired of waking up with back pain? (slight pause)

The Sleep Experts at Mattress World can help.

(Pick up energy) We have over 50 different mattresses—
memory foam, hybrid, innerspring—
all designed to give you the REST (emphasize) you deserve.

(Slow down, sincere) Your best night's sleep is waiting.
Visit Mattress World on Oak Street.

That's Mattress World—where YOUR comfort comes first.
(Emphasis on "your")
```

## Local vs. National Radio

### Local Radio Advertising

Local radio offers unique advantages for businesses serving specific geographic areas. The key is leveraging the local connection to create relevance and trust.

**Local radio strategies:**

**Embrace local references:** Mention local landmarks, streets, neighborhoods, and events. This signals authenticity and creates connection.

**Partner with local personalities:** Local DJs and hosts have loyal followings. Live reads from trusted personalities can be incredibly effective.

**Respond to local events:** Reference local news, weather, sports, and community happenings to feel current and connected.

**Create local urgency:** "Springfield residents only" or "Exclusive to the Valley" creates scarcity and local pride.

**Example local ad:**

"Hey, Springfield! Are you tired of driving all the way to Riverside for quality furniture? Now there's a better option. Martin's Furniture just opened on Oak Street, right across from Lincoln Park. We've got sofas, dining sets, bedroom furniture—all at prices that'll make your neighbor jealous. Stop by this Saturday for our grand opening—free coffee, donuts, and 20% off everything. Martin's Furniture on Oak Street. Finally, quality furniture right here in Springfield."

### National Radio Advertising

National radio campaigns face the challenge of connecting with diverse audiences across different markets. The key is finding universal themes while allowing for local customization.

**National radio strategies:**

**Universal themes:** Focus on emotions and experiences that transcend geography—family, success, comfort, value.

**Flexible templates:** Create "donut" spots with consistent openings and closings but customizable local dealer or location information.

**Consistent brand voice:** Even as local details change, maintain consistent tone, music, and core messaging.

**Multiple creative versions:** Create variations that resonate with different demographics or regions.

## Daypart Considerations

Radio audiences vary significantly by time of day. Understanding dayparts helps you craft copy that resonates with who's listening when.

### Morning Drive (6 AM - 10 AM)

**Audience:** Commuters heading to work, parents doing school drop-off
**Mood:** Rushing, need-to-know orientation, preparing for the day
**Best for:** Quick-service breakfast, gas stations, news-relevant products, professional services

**Copy approach:** Energetic, efficient, to-the-point. Respect that listeners are multitasking.

### Midday (10 AM - 3 PM)

**Audience:** At-work listeners, stay-at-home parents, retirees
**Mood:** More relaxed, background listening
**Best for:** Lunch restaurants, retail, home services, financial products

**Copy approach:** Can be slightly longer-form, more conversational. Listeners have more attention available.

### Afternoon Drive (3 PM - 7 PM)

**Audience:** Commuters heading home, parents picking up kids
**Mood:** Tired but engaged, thinking about evening and weekend
**Best for:** Restaurants, entertainment, retail sales, grocery

**Copy approach:** Energetic, promotional. People are thinking about what to do next.

### Evening (7 PM - Midnight)

**Audience:** Home listeners, often background to other activities
**Mood:** Relaxed, entertainment-focused
**Best for:** Entertainment, restaurants, weekend events, dating services

**Copy approach:** More creative, storytelling-friendly. Audiences are more receptive to longer-form content.

### Overnight (Midnight - 6 AM)

**Audience:** Night workers, insomniacs, early risers
**Mood:** Quiet, often seeking companionship
**Best for:** 24-hour services, specialty products, direct response

**Copy approach:** Intimate, conversational. This is often when direct-response advertising performs best.

## Different Radio Formats

### Music Format Considerations

Different radio formats attract different audiences and create different contexts for your advertising.

**Country:** Family-focused, often rural or suburban, value-conscious. Authentic, down-to-earth copy works well.

**Top 40/Contemporary Hits:** Young, trend-conscious, urban and suburban. Energetic, current-sounding copy.

**Classic Rock:** Adults 35-54, often male-skewing. Nostalgic references and rock-oriented production can resonate.

**Urban/Hip-Hop:** Young, diverse, culture-conscious. Contemporary language and production essential.

**Adult Contemporary:** Adults 25-54, slightly female-skewing. Warm, lifestyle-focused copy.

**News/Talk:** Educated, engaged, news-focused. More detail and information can work; listeners are patient and attentive.

**Sports Radio:** Male-skewing, competitive, engaged. Energy, statistics, and sports metaphors resonate.

**NPR/Public Radio:** Highly educated, affluent, values-driven. Thoughtful, substantive copy; avoid hard-sell approaches.

### Adapting Copy for Format

**Example—same product, different formats:**

*Country format (pickup truck):*
"There's nothing like hitting the back roads with a truck you can count on. The new Ford F-150—built for the long haul, just like you. Test drive at Valley Ford."

*Top 40 format (same truck):*
"Upgrade your ride. The all-new Ford F-150 with Apple CarPlay, built-in Wi-Fi, and power that doesn't quit. Valley Ford—where deals happen."

*Sports radio (same truck):*
"When you need to haul the whole tailgate party, you need the F-150. Best-in-class towing, best-in-class payload. Get yours at Valley Ford."

## Testing and Optimization

### Measuring Radio Ad Effectiveness

Radio advertising measurement has historically been challenging, but several methods can help you understand what's working:

**Direct response tracking:**
- Unique phone numbers or extensions per ad
- Unique URLs or landing pages
- Specific offer codes or mentions

**Lift studies:**
- Website traffic increases during/after campaigns
- Search query increases for brand terms
- Foot traffic increases (measured via mobile data)

**Surveys and attribution:**
- "How did you hear about us?" surveys
- Brand awareness tracking
- Purchase intent studies

**A/B testing approaches:**
- Test different creative in different markets
- Test different dayparts
- Test different stations within a market
- Test different offers or CTAs

### Optimizing Over Time

**What to test:**

- **Creative approach:** Humor vs. straight-forward, story vs. announcer, etc.
- **Voice talent:** Different voices may resonate differently
- **Music:** With music vs. without, different styles
- **Offer:** Different promotions or CTAs
- **Length:** 30 vs. 60 seconds
- **Daypart:** Morning vs. afternoon, weekday vs. weekend
- **Frequency:** How many impressions before diminishing returns

**Optimization best practices:**

1. Change one variable at a time when possible
2. Run tests long enough to reach statistical significance
3. Account for external factors (weather, news events, seasonality)
4. Track trends over time, not just individual campaigns
5. Document learnings for future campaigns

## Production Considerations

### Working with Studios and Producers

**Pre-production:**
- Provide clear, finalized scripts
- Include pronunciation guides and emphasis notes
- Share reference spots you like
- Communicate brand voice and tone
- Discuss music and SFX preferences

**During the session:**
- Be present (or available by phone) for direction
- Get multiple takes with different approaches
- Ask for variations you might need later
- Trust the professionals—they know audio

**Post-production:**
- Review drafts promptly and provide specific feedback
- Ensure all versions needed (different lengths, different endings)
- Get properly formatted files for station submission
- Request stems/elements for future use

### Technical Requirements

**Standard radio ad formats:**
- MP3 (320 kbps) or WAV
- Sample rate: 44.1 kHz
- Bit depth: 16-bit
- Mono or stereo depending on station requirements

**Timing considerations:**
- 60-second spots: exactly 59-60 seconds
- 30-second spots: exactly 29-30 seconds
- 15-second spots: exactly 14-15 seconds
- Include slight cushion for station bumpers

**Volume standards:**
- Follow broadcast standards (varies by market/station)
- Avoid excessive compression or loudness
- Don't exceed peak levels

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Copy Mistakes

1. **Cramming too much information:** Trying to say everything results in saying nothing effectively.

2. **Starting with the company name:** No one cares about your name until they care about what you offer.

3. **Using print language:** Formal, complex sentences don't work when spoken aloud.

4. **Forgetting the CTA:** Always tell listeners what to do next.

5. **Making the phone number too complicated:** Unrememberable numbers are useless.

6. **Lack of specificity:** Vague claims don't create mental images or credibility.

7. **No emotional hook:** Purely rational appeals don't move people to action.

8. **Inconsistent brand voice:** Ads should sound like they come from the same company.

9. **Ignoring the medium's strengths:** Not using sound effects, music, or theater of the mind.

10. **Testing in print, not out loud:** Copy that reads well may not sound well.

### Production Mistakes

1. **Wrong voice for the brand:** Voice talent mismatch undermines credibility.

2. **Poor audio quality:** Bad recording reflects poorly on the brand.

3. **Music overpowering voice:** If listeners can't hear the words, nothing else matters.

4. **Inconsistent volume:** Jarring shifts in loudness are unprofessional.

5. **Rush delivery:** Speaking too fast sacrifices comprehension for words.

## Best Practices Summary

### The 15 Commandments of Radio Copywriting

1. **Capture attention in the first three seconds** with a hook that makes listeners want to hear more.

2. **Write for the ear, not the eye**—use conversational language that sounds natural when spoken.

3. **Focus on one main message**—trying to communicate multiple ideas dilutes everything.

4. **Paint pictures with words**—engage the theater of the mind with vivid, sensory language.

5. **Lead with benefits**, not features—show listeners how their lives improve.

6. **Use sound strategically**—music and effects should enhance, not distract.

7. **Repeat key information**—brand name, phone number, website should appear multiple times.

8. **Keep phone numbers simple**—vanity numbers or easy patterns only.

9. **Create urgency when appropriate**—give listeners reasons to act now.

10. **End with a clear call-to-action**—tell listeners exactly what to do.

11. **Match voice to brand**—the right talent makes copy sing.

12. **Read everything aloud**—if it doesn't sound good spoken, rewrite it.

13. **Time your scripts accurately**—don't write 75 words for a 30-second spot.

14. **Consider the context**—daypart, format, and local relevance matter.

15. **Test and optimize**—measure results and improve continuously.

## Radio Ad Templates

### Direct Response Template (60 seconds)

```
[HOOK - 5 seconds]
(Provocative question or startling statement)
```

## Conclusion

Radio advertising, in its second century, remains a vibrant and effective medium for reaching audiences. The fundamentals of radio copywriting—capturing attention, creating mental imagery, delivering clear messages, and driving action—are timeless principles that transcend technological change.

The best radio copywriters understand that they're not just writing words; they're creating experiences. Every second of airtime is an opportunity to transport listeners, connect emotionally, and motivate behavior. When you master the craft of writing for the ear—using the unique capabilities of sound, music, and the theater of the mind—you unlock creative possibilities that no visual medium can match.

Whether you're creating a 15-second retail spot or a 60-second brand anthem, remember: someone is going to hear this while living their life—driving to work, cooking dinner, waiting in traffic. Your job is to break through that daily routine, capture a moment of attention, and leave a lasting impression. Do that well, and radio will continue to deliver results for generations to come.

The microphone is on. What will you say?
