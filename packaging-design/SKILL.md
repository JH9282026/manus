# Packaging Design


## Overview

This section provides an overview of the skill.

Packaging design is a specialized discipline that combines structural engineering, graphic design, and marketing strategy to create containers and wrappings that protect products, communicate brand identity, and influence purchasing decisions. This comprehensive guide covers everything from fundamental concepts to production processes.

---

## 1. Packaging Design Fundamentals

### What is Packaging Design?

Packaging design encompasses the creation of the exterior container or wrapping for a product. It involves both structural design (the physical form and construction) and surface graphics (visual elements including branding, imagery, and information). Effective packaging serves as a silent salesperson, communicating product benefits and brand values at the critical moment of purchase.

### Importance of Packaging Design

**Brand Identity**: Packaging is often the first physical touchpoint between a consumer and a brand. It communicates brand personality through colors, typography, imagery, and materials. Consistent packaging across product lines builds brand recognition and loyalty. Iconic packaging—such as Coca-Cola's contour bottle or Tiffany's blue box—becomes inseparable from the brand itself.

**Product Protection**: The primary functional purpose of packaging is protecting contents during shipping, handling, and storage. This includes protection from physical damage, moisture, light, temperature changes, contamination, and tampering. Protection requirements vary dramatically by product type, from fragile electronics to perishable foods.

**Shelf Appeal**: In retail environments, products have seconds to capture consumer attention. Effective packaging stands out through distinctive shapes, colors, and graphics while communicating key product benefits. Understanding planogram placement and competitive context is essential for designing packages that perform in real-world retail settings.

**Consumer Experience**: Modern packaging design extends beyond purchase to the entire usage journey. This includes ease of opening, storage convenience, portion control, resealability, and disposal. Premium brands increasingly focus on the "unboxing experience" as a touchpoint for exceeding expectations and generating social media sharing.

### Packaging Design Principles

**Functionality**: Above all, packaging must work. This means protecting the product, fitting efficiently on shelves and in shipping containers, being easy to open and use, and meeting all practical requirements. Form should follow function—aesthetic choices should enhance rather than compromise usability.

**Aesthetics**: Visual appeal drives purchasing decisions and brand perception. Aesthetic considerations include color harmony, typography hierarchy, image quality, white space, and overall composition. The visual design should align with brand positioning—luxury brands require sophisticated elegance while budget brands may emphasize value through bold, straightforward graphics.

**Sustainability**: Environmental responsibility is now a core packaging requirement. Sustainable packaging minimizes material usage, uses recyclable or biodegradable materials, reduces carbon footprint in production and transportation, and communicates environmental benefits to increasingly eco-conscious consumers.

**Cost-Effectiveness**: Packaging must balance quality with economic viability. This involves optimizing material usage, considering production efficiency at scale, minimizing shipping costs through weight and dimensional optimization, and ensuring packaging costs align with product margins and brand positioning.

**Brand Consistency**: Packaging must integrate seamlessly with broader brand identity. This requires consistent use of logos, colors, typography, imagery style, and messaging tone across all products and touchpoints. Brand guidelines should include specific packaging applications and adaptation rules.

## 2. Types of Packaging

### Boxes

**Folding Cartons**: Lightweight paperboard boxes that ship flat and are assembled at the point of packing. Common for cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, food products, and consumer goods. Available in various styles including straight tuck, reverse tuck, auto-bottom, and display boxes.

**Rigid Boxes**: Premium boxes made from thick chipboard wrapped in printed paper or specialty materials. Also called set-up boxes, they don't fold flat and convey luxury quality. Common for high-end electronics, jewelry, cosmetics, and gift packaging.

**Corrugated Boxes**: Durable boxes with fluted corrugated medium between paperboard liners. The industry standard for shipping and e-commerce. Available in single-wall, double-wall, and triple-wall constructions with varying flute sizes (A, B, C, E, F) for different strength and cushioning requirements.

**Mailer Boxes**: Self-locking corrugated boxes designed for e-commerce fulfillment. Feature tuck closures or magnetic closures, often with custom printing inside and out. Designed to create a branded unboxing experience for direct-to-consumer shipping.

### Bags

**Paper Bags**: Versatile packaging for retail, food service, and grocery applications. Available in kraft (brown), white, or custom-printed varieties. Styles include flat bags, SOS (self-opening sack) bags, and shopping bags with handles.

**Plastic Bags**: Lightweight, moisture-resistant packaging in various forms including poly bags, ziplock bags, and retail shopping bags. Common materials include LDPE, HDPE, and PP. Increasingly replaced by sustainable alternatives due to environmental concerns.

**Pouches**: Flexible packaging gaining market share from rigid containers. Stand-up pouches (SUPs) feature a bottom gusset allowing upright display. Common for snacks, pet food, beverages, and personal care products. Can include spouts, zippers, and other functional features.

**Stand-Up Pouches**: A specific pouch type with a bottom gusset that allows the package to stand upright on shelves. Combines the lightweight benefits of flexible packaging with the shelf presence of rigid containers. Highly customizable with various closure types, shapes, and finishes.

### Bottles and Jars

**Glass Containers**: Premium option offering excellent product visibility, chemical inertness, and recyclability. Available in clear, amber, cobalt, and other colors. Common for beverages, food products, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals. Limitations include weight, fragility, and higher shipping costs.

**Plastic Bottles and Jars**: Lightweight, durable, and cost-effective containers in various shapes and sizes. Material selection depends on product compatibility, barrier requirements, and sustainability goals. Common materials include PET (clear, recyclable), HDPE (opaque, chemical-resistant), and PP (higher temperature tolerance).

**Metal Containers**: Primarily aluminum and steel for beverages, food preservation, and specialty applications. Offer excellent barrier properties, durability, and recyclability. Metal closures (caps, lids) are also common across container types.

### Cans

**Aluminum Cans**: The standard for beverages, offering lightweight construction, infinite recyclability, and excellent graphics reproduction. Two-piece cans (body drawn from single piece) are most common. Specialty shapes and sizes available for differentiation.

**Steel Cans**: Traditional choice for preserved foods, offering strength and extended shelf life. Three-piece construction (body, top, bottom) is standard. Also used for aerosols, paints, and industrial products.

**Tin Cans**: Actually steel coated with tin for corrosion resistance. Often used for decorative containers, specialty foods, and gift packaging. Offer a premium, nostalgic aesthetic.

### Additional Packaging Types

**Tubes**: Squeezable containers for pastes, gels, and viscous products. Options include plastic squeeze tubes and metal collapsible tubes. Common for toothpaste, cosmetics, ointments, and food products like tomato paste.

**Blister Packs**: Thermoformed plastic cavities sealed to a printed card backing. Common for pharmaceuticals, electronics, hardware, and toys. Provides product visibility, tamper evidence, and theft deterrence.

**Shrink Wrap**: Plastic film that shrinks tightly around products when heat is applied. Used for bundling multiple products, tamper evidence, and protection. Available in printed versions for promotional packaging.

**Labels and Stickers**: While technically not packaging, labels are essential components. Includes pressure-sensitive labels, shrink sleeves, wrap-around labels, and hang tags. Often the primary branding surface on bottles, jars, and other containers.

## 3. Packaging Materials

### Cardboard and Paperboard

**Kraft Paperboard**: Strong, unbleached paperboard with natural brown color. Conveys eco-friendliness and authenticity. Common for shopping bags, folding cartons, and corrugated boxes. Available in various weights measured in points or GSM.

**Chipboard**: Gray recycled paperboard used for cereal boxes, shoe boxes, and other cartons. Often wrapped or laminated with printed paper for visual appeal. Cost-effective for high-volume applications.

**Corrugated Board**: Multi-layer construction with fluted medium between flat liners. Strength varies by flute size: A-flute (largest, best cushioning), B-flute (good stacking strength), C-flute (all-purpose), E-flute (excellent print surface), F-flute (thin, retail-ready packaging).

### Plastic

**PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)**: Clear, strong, and widely recyclable. The standard for water bottles, beverage containers, and clear food packaging. Offers excellent barrier properties and is food-safe.

**HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)**: Opaque, chemical-resistant plastic for milk jugs, detergent bottles, and industrial containers. Good moisture barrier and impact resistance. Recyclable and commonly available in recycled form.

**LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene)**: Flexible plastic for squeeze bottles, bags, and wraps. Lower density than HDPE with different physical properties. Common for flexible packaging applications.

**PP (Polypropylene)**: Versatile plastic with higher temperature tolerance than PE. Used for food containers, caps, closures, and hinged packaging. Good chemical resistance and relatively recyclable.

**PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)**: Clear, rigid plastic once common for blister packs and clamshells. Declining usage due to environmental concerns and recycling challenges. Being replaced by PET and other materials in many applications.

### Glass

**Clear Glass**: Offers maximum product visibility for beverages, food, and cosmetics. Available in various shapes, sizes, and neck finishes. Weight is a consideration for shipping costs and sustainability.

**Colored Glass**: Amber, cobalt, green, and other colors provide UV protection and aesthetic differentiation. Amber glass is standard for beer and some pharmaceuticals. Cobalt blue creates premium associations for cosmetics and specialty products.

**Frosted Glass**: Sandblasted or acid-etched finish creating an elegant, diffused appearance. Popular for premium cosmetics, fragrances, and spirits. Can be achieved through various manufacturing techniques with different effects.

### Metal

**Aluminum**: Lightweight, corrosion-resistant, and infinitely recyclable. Used for beverage cans, aerosol containers, caps, and foil packaging. Excellent barrier properties for oxygen and light.

**Steel**: Strong and cost-effective for food cans, paint cans, and industrial containers. Often coated with tin or other materials for corrosion resistance. Fully recyclable with established recycling infrastructure.

**Tin**: Actually tin-plated steel in most applications. Provides corrosion resistance and can be decoratively printed. Popular for specialty food containers, gift tins, and decorative packaging.

### Sustainable Materials

**Recycled Materials**: Post-consumer recycled (PCR) content reduces virgin material usage. Available for paper, plastic, and metal packaging. Percentage claims require certification and supply chain verification.

**Biodegradable Materials**: Materials that decompose naturally through biological processes. Includes paper, certain bioplastics (PLA, PHA), and plant-based materials. Decomposition rates and conditions vary significantly.

**Compostable Materials**: Materials that break down in composting conditions to become nutrient-rich soil. Requires specific conditions (industrial vs. home composting) and certification. Often made from plant-based materials like corn starch, sugarcane, or bamboo.

## 4. Structural Design

### Dielines and Templates

**What are Dielines?**: Dielines are technical drawings that serve as templates for packaging construction. They show all cut lines, fold lines, and glue areas in a flat, two-dimensional format. Dielines are essential for accurate production and are used to align graphics with structural elements.

**Creating Dielines**: Dielines are typically created in Adobe Illustrator using precise measurements. Key elements include cut lines (solid), fold lines (dashed or dotted), glue tabs, and bleed areas. Standard conventions use specific colors—typically magenta for cut lines and green for fold lines.

**Dieline Software**: While Adobe Illustrator is standard, specialized software like ArtiosCAD, KASEMAKE, and EngView offer advanced structural design features including 3D visualization, automatic parameter adjustment, and direct connection to cutting equipment.

### 3D Mockups

**Creating Mockups**: 3D mockups bring flat designs to life, allowing visualization before production. Methods include digital rendering, physical prototypes, and augmented reality previews. Mockups help identify design issues and facilitate client presentations.

**Mockup Software**: Digital mockups can be created using packaging-specific software (ArtiosCAD, Esko Studio), 3D software (Cinema 4D, Blender), or mockup generator tools. Many designers use Photoshop smart object templates for quick visualization.

**Realistic Visualization**: Effective mockups include proper lighting, shadows, reflections, and contextual settings. Environmental renders showing products on shelves or in use scenarios help stakeholders understand real-world impact.

### Structural Considerations

**Product Fit**: Packaging should accommodate the product with minimal excess space while providing adequate protection. Internal dimensions must account for product variations, inserts, and cushioning materials.

**Protection**: Structural design must protect against anticipated hazards including drops, compression, vibration, and environmental factors. Drop testing standards (ISTA, ASTM) guide protection requirements for different product types.

**Assembly**: Production efficiency requires packaging that can be quickly and reliably assembled. Automated filling and closing requirements influence structural choices. Easy manual assembly is important for hand-packed products.

**Shipping**: Packaging must stack efficiently in shipping containers and pallets. Standard pallet footprints (40" x 48" in North America) influence package dimensioning. Cube optimization reduces shipping costs and environmental impact.

### Packaging Engineering

**Strength**: Edge crush test (ECT) and burst strength ratings indicate corrugated box performance. Box compression test (BCT) measures stacking strength. Material selection and structural design combine to meet strength requirements.

**Durability**: Packaging must maintain integrity throughout the supply chain. Humidity resistance, temperature tolerance, and repeated handling endurance are key considerations. Testing validates real-world performance.

**Stackability**: Warehouse efficiency requires stackable packaging. Box compression strength must exceed anticipated stack loads with appropriate safety factors. Structural features like interlocking corners improve stack stability.

## 5. Label Design

### Label Types

**Wrap-Around Labels**: Labels that encircle cylindrical containers, meeting or overlapping at a seam. Common for bottles, cans, and jars. Can be pressure-sensitive, glue-applied, or shrink sleeve.

**Front and Back Labels**: Separate labels for product face and back panel. Allows different information hierarchy and design treatment. Standard for bottles, jars, and many food products.

**Hang Tags**: Labels attached by string, cord, or plastic fasteners. Common for apparel, gifts, and specialty products. Offer additional branding surface and can include pricing or promotional information.

**Stickers**: Pressure-sensitive labels for various applications including sealing, branding, and promotional use. Can be applied to primary packaging, shipping boxes, or directly to products.

### Label Information

**Product Name**: Primary identification typically receiving the most prominent visual treatment. Should be clearly legible and memorable.

**Brand Identity**: Logo, brand name, and visual elements establishing brand ownership. Placement and prominence reflect brand strategy.

**Ingredients and Nutrition Facts**: Required for food and many consumer products. Format and content governed by regulations (FDA, EU). Must be legible at required minimum type sizes.

**Barcodes**: UPC, EAN, or QR codes for inventory tracking and retail checkout. Require specific size, quiet zones, and print quality. Placement should not interfere with primary graphics.

**Regulatory Information**: Required warnings, certifications, country of origin, manufacturer information, and other legally mandated content. Varies by product category and market.

### Label Design Principles

**Hierarchy**: Visual organization prioritizing information by importance. Primary elements (brand, product name) receive dominant treatment. Secondary information follows in logical reading order.

**Readability**: Text must be legible at intended viewing distance and conditions. Font selection, size, contrast, and spacing all affect readability. Minimum type sizes are often legally mandated.

**Brand Consistency**: Labels should align with overall brand identity and other packaging elements. Consistent color palette, typography, and imagery style reinforce brand recognition.

**Visual Appeal**: Engaging graphics, imagery, and color draw consumer attention. Balance between aesthetic impact and informational clarity. Photography, illustration, and pattern can enhance appeal.

### Label Materials and Finishes

**Paper Labels**: Cost-effective option suitable for many applications. Available in matte, semi-gloss, and high-gloss finishes. May require lamination for moisture resistance.

**Vinyl Labels**: Durable, moisture-resistant option for demanding applications. Common for outdoor products, chemicals, and industrial use. Higher cost than paper but superior durability.

**Clear Labels**: Transparent labels creating a "no-label" look. Allow container or product to show through. Available in various materials including PET, polypropylene, and vinyl.

**Metallic Labels**: Labels with metallic paper, foil, or metallic ink effects. Create premium appearance for spirits, cosmetics, and specialty products. Options range from subtle effects to high-impact mirror finishes.

**Finish Options**: Matte finishes offer sophistication and reduced glare. Gloss finishes maximize color vibrancy and visual impact. Spot varnish and spot UV create selective contrasts between matte and gloss areas.

## 6. Packaging Design Process

### Research and Discovery

**Market Research**: Understanding the competitive landscape, market trends, and retail environment. Includes shelf audits, competitive analysis, and trend research. Informs differentiation strategy and design direction.

**Competitor Analysis**: Documenting competitor packaging including structure, graphics, materials, and positioning. Identifies category conventions and opportunities for differentiation. Analysis should include direct competitors and adjacent categories.

**Target Audience**: Defining consumer demographics, psychographics, and purchasing behavior. Understanding how target consumers shop, what influences their decisions, and what values resonate with them. Persona development helps focus design decisions.

### Concept Development

**Sketching**: Rapid ideation through hand sketching explores structural and graphic possibilities. Low-fidelity exploration allows quick iteration. Sketches communicate ideas before investing in detailed development.

**Ideation**: Brainstorming sessions generate diverse concepts without premature judgment. Techniques include mind mapping, word association, and collaborative workshops. Quantity of ideas enables quality of final selections.

**Mood Boards**: Visual collages establishing aesthetic direction including color, typography, imagery, and texture. Align team and client on design intent before detailed execution. Can be physical or digital.

### Design Development

**Refining Concepts**: Selected concepts are developed with increasing detail and specificity. Multiple rounds of refinement narrow options and optimize solutions. Feedback integration guides evolution.

**Creating Dielines**: Structural templates are developed with precise specifications. Dielines integrate with graphic design development. Production requirements influence structural decisions.

**Surface Graphics**: Visual design is applied to structural templates. Multiple design variations explore color, typography, and imagery options. Design must work within structural constraints and production capabilities.

### Prototyping

**Physical Prototypes**: Actual samples produced through various methods including plotter cutting, 3D printing, and short-run production. Prototypes reveal issues not apparent in digital visualization.

**Testing**: Prototypes undergo evaluation including consumer testing, shelf testing, and functional testing. Testing validates design assumptions and identifies improvement opportunities.

**Refinement**: Prototype feedback drives design refinement. Multiple prototype iterations may be required. Final prototype serves as production reference.

### Production Preparation

**Print-Ready Files**: Final files prepared according to production specifications. Includes proper color mode, resolution, bleeds, and file format. Press-ready PDFs with proper color management are standard.

**Specifications**: Detailed documentation of all production requirements including materials, dimensions, printing methods, and finishing techniques. Specifications ensure accurate quotes and consistent production.

**Vendor Coordination**: Communication with manufacturers including quoting, sampling, and production scheduling. Clear specifications and communication prevent costly errors. Relationship building enables better outcomes.

## 7. Packaging Design Considerations

### Shelf Appeal and Visibility

**Standing Out**: Differentiation through distinctive structure, color, or graphics. Breaking category conventions can create impact but requires careful execution. Balance between standing out and fitting category expectations.

**Color Psychology**: Colors communicate meaning and evoke emotions. Category conventions often dictate base colors (green for organic, blue for cleaning products). Strategic color choices support brand positioning and product messaging.

**Typography**: Type choices communicate brand personality and ensure information clarity. Display typography creates visual impact. Body text must meet legibility requirements. Consistent type hierarchy guides reader attention.

**Imagery**: Photography, illustration, and graphics communicate product attributes and brand personality. Food photography creates appetite appeal. Lifestyle imagery establishes brand context. Abstract graphics can create distinctive brand identity.

### Brand Consistency

**Logo Placement**: Consistent logo positioning across product lines builds recognition. Primary display panels typically feature logos prominently. Secondary panels may include smaller logo treatments.

**Color Palette**: Brand colors applied consistently across all packaging creates visual coherence. Color system should accommodate product line differentiation while maintaining brand unity.

**Typography**: Brand fonts used consistently reinforce identity. Primary typeface for headlines, secondary typeface for body text. Typography guidelines ensure consistent application.

**Brand Voice**: Copywriting tone reflects brand personality. Product descriptions, taglines, and instructional text should sound like the brand. Voice guidelines ensure consistent verbal expression.

### Regulatory Compliance

**FDA Regulations**: United States food, drug, and cosmetic packaging requires specific labeling including ingredient lists, nutrition facts, net contents, and manufacturer information. Specific requirements vary by product category.

**EU Regulations**: European Union packaging must comply with directives covering materials, food contact, labeling, and environmental impact. Requires different information than US regulations including metric measurements and country-specific languages.

**Labeling Requirements**: Minimum type sizes, required statements, warning labels, and certification marks vary by product and market. Legal review essential for regulated products.

**Safety Warnings**: Products with potential hazards require appropriate warnings. Standardized symbols (choking hazards, sharp edges) and text warnings protect consumers and reduce liability.

### Sustainability and Eco-Friendly Packaging

**Recyclable Packaging**: Materials accepted by local recycling programs. Clear recycling instructions help consumers dispose properly. Design for recyclability including material simplification and label removal.

**Biodegradable Materials**: Materials that decompose naturally in reasonable timeframes. Certification (OK Compost, BPI) validates claims. Consider end-of-life scenarios in design decisions.

**Minimal Packaging**: Right-sized packaging reduces material usage and shipping costs. Eliminating unnecessary packaging components reduces environmental impact. Balance protection requirements with minimization goals.

**Sustainable Materials**: Materials with lower environmental impact including recycled content, renewable resources, and responsibly sourced materials. Certifications (FSC, SFI) validate sustainable sourcing.

### Unboxing Experience

**First Impressions**: Initial encounter with packaging sets expectations. Exterior condition, visual appeal, and quality cues influence perception before opening.

**Reveal**: The opening sequence can create anticipation and delight. Tissue paper, ribbon, and sequential reveals build drama. Premium brands invest in memorable reveal experiences.

**Tactile Experience**: Material textures and structural interactions engage touch. Soft-touch coatings, embossing, and quality materials create physical pleasure. Handle and heft communicate quality.

**Shareability**: Instagram-worthy packaging generates social media exposure. Photogenic design, surprise elements, and brand moments encourage sharing. User-generated content extends marketing reach.

## 8. Packaging Production

### Printing Methods

**Offset Printing**: Traditional lithographic printing offering excellent quality and cost-effectiveness for medium to large runs. Four-color process (CMYK) plus spot colors available. Standard for folding cartons and labels.

**Digital Printing**: Direct-to-substrate printing ideal for short runs, variable data, and rapid prototyping. Quality increasingly comparable to offset. Cost-effective for quantities under several thousand units.

**Flexography**: Rotary printing using flexible relief plates. Standard for flexible packaging, corrugated boxes, and labels. High-speed, high-volume process with improving quality capabilities.

**Screen Printing**: Stencil-based printing for specialty effects including heavy ink deposits, metallics, and textured inks. Common for glass, plastic containers, and specialty applications. Limited to simpler graphics.

### Finishing Techniques

**Lamination**: Protective film applied over printed surface. Matte lamination creates sophisticated feel; gloss lamination maximizes color vibrancy. Soft-touch lamination adds velvety texture.

**Coating**: Liquid coating applied by press or separate machine. Options include aqueous, UV, and specialty coatings. Can be applied overall or in spot patterns.

**Embossing and Debossing**: Raised (embossing) or recessed (debossing) dimensional effects created through pressure and dies. Creates tactile interest and premium perception. Can be registered to graphics or blind (unprinted).

**Foil Stamping**: Metallic or holographic foil transferred through heat and pressure. Creates eye-catching reflective effects. Hot foil traditional method; cold foil integrated with printing for efficiency.

**Die Cutting**: Custom shapes cut using steel rule dies. Essential for box construction but also used for distinctive shapes, windows, and structural features. Laser cutting available for prototypes and short runs.

### Quality Control

**Color Accuracy**: Press proofs, color targets, and densitometer readings ensure color consistency. Match proofs at press approval. Spectrophotometer measurements verify color accuracy objectively.

**Registration**: Alignment between printing units and with cutting/folding. Tight registration essential for multi-color graphics. Registration marks enable verification during production.

**Defects**: Visual inspection and automated systems identify print defects, material flaws, and construction errors. Acceptable quality levels (AQL) define inspection sampling and tolerance limits.

### Working with Manufacturers

**Quotes**: Obtaining competitive quotes requires complete specifications. Request quotes from multiple vendors for comparison. Understand what's included and excluded in pricing.

**Samples**: Pre-production samples validate design and specifications. Request samples before committing to production runs. Sample approval documents protect against disputes.

**Production Runs**: Minimum order quantities (MOQs) vary by process and vendor. Lead times depend on complexity and vendor capacity. Production scheduling coordinates with product launch timelines.

**Timelines**: Realistic scheduling accounts for design completion, sampling, revisions, production, and shipping. Buffer time prevents launch delays. Communication with all stakeholders ensures alignment.

## Conclusion

Packaging design represents a unique intersection of engineering, graphic design, marketing, and sustainability. Success requires understanding materials and production processes, creating compelling visual designs, meeting regulatory requirements, and delivering functional protection—all while managing costs and environmental impact.

The best packaging designers combine technical knowledge with creative vision, always keeping the end consumer experience at the center of their work. From concept sketches to production specifications, every decision contributes to packaging that protects products, represents brands, and delights consumers.

As sustainability concerns grow and e-commerce continues to transform retail, packaging design continues to evolve. Designers who stay current with materials innovation, production technologies, and consumer expectations will create packaging that meets the demands of today's marketplace while preparing for tomorrow's challenges.
