# Job Evaluation

Comprehensive guide to job evaluation methodologies, job leveling, job architecture, and internal equity frameworks.

---

## Purpose of Job Evaluation

Job evaluation is a systematic process for establishing the relative value of specific roles within an organization. It focuses on the importance of the role itself, not the individual occupying it.

### Objectives

**Internal Equity:**
- Ensure fair, consistent pay for similar work
- Create transparent career progression
- Reduce pay disparities and perceived unfairness
- Support defensible compensation decisions

**Foundation for Compensation:**
- Establish job hierarchy and levels
- Create salary bands aligned with job value
- Enable accurate market benchmarking
- Support pay-for-performance differentiation

**Organizational Clarity:**
- Define roles, responsibilities, and scope
- Clarify reporting relationships and decision-making authority
- Support workforce planning and org design
- Enable career pathing and development

## Job Evaluation Methods

### 1. Ranking (Paired Comparison)

**Type:** Qualitative, non-quantitative

**Process:**
1. List all jobs in organization
2. Select single factor for comparison (e.g., overall importance, required education, complexity)
3. Compare each job to every other job in pairs
4. Rank jobs from most to least valuable based on comparisons
5. Create hierarchy of positions

**Example:**
Comparing three roles based on "overall importance":
- CEO vs. VP Sales: CEO more important
- CEO vs. Software Engineer: CEO more important  
- VP Sales vs. Software Engineer: VP Sales more important
- **Ranking:** 1. CEO, 2. VP Sales, 3. Software Engineer

**Advantages:**
- Simple and fast to implement
- Easy to understand
- Low cost, no specialized expertise required
- Good for small organizations

**Disadvantages:**
- Highly subjective
- Doesn't consider multiple factors
- Difficult to defend or explain
- Becomes unwieldy with many jobs (n² comparisons)
- Prone to bias

**Best For:**
- Small organizations (<50 employees)
- Simple organizational structures
- Quick initial job hierarchy
- Organizations with limited resources

### 2. Point Factor Method

**Type:** Quantitative, analytical

**Process:**
1. **Identify Compensable Factors:** Select factors that determine job value
   - Common factors: Knowledge/Skills, Responsibility, Complexity, Working Conditions, Decision-Making Impact
2. **Define Factor Levels:** Create scale for each factor (e.g., 1-5 or 1-10)
3. **Assign Point Values:** Determine maximum points for each factor based on importance
4. **Evaluate Jobs:** Score each job on each factor
5. **Calculate Total Points:** Sum points across all factors for each job
6. **Create Job Hierarchy:** Rank jobs by total points
7. **Group into Grades:** Cluster similar point totals into job grades

**Example Point Factor Framework:**

| Factor | Weight | Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 | Level 4 | Level 5 |
|--------|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| **Knowledge/Skills** | 40% | 40 pts | 80 pts | 120 pts | 160 pts | 200 pts |
| **Responsibility** | 30% | 30 pts | 60 pts | 90 pts | 120 pts | 150 pts |
| **Complexity** | 20% | 20 pts | 40 pts | 60 pts | 80 pts | 100 pts |
| **Working Conditions** | 10% | 10 pts | 20 pts | 30 pts | 40 pts | 50 pts |
| **Total Possible** | 100% | 100 pts | 200 pts | 300 pts | 400 pts | 500 pts |

**Example Job Evaluation:**

*Software Engineer II:*
- Knowledge/Skills: Level 3 (120 pts) - Bachelor's degree + 3-5 years experience
- Responsibility: Level 2 (60 pts) - Responsible for features, not products
- Complexity: Level 3 (60 pts) - Complex technical problems
- Working Conditions: Level 1 (10 pts) - Standard office environment
- **Total: 250 points**

*Engineering Manager:*
- Knowledge/Skills: Level 4 (160 pts) - Technical expertise + management skills
- Responsibility: Level 4 (120 pts) - Manages team, budget, roadmap
- Complexity: Level 4 (80 pts) - Strategic and technical decisions
- Working Conditions: Level 1 (10 pts) - Standard office environment
- **Total: 370 points**

**Advantages:**
- Objective and defensible
- Considers multiple dimensions of job value
- Transparent and systematic
- Supports pay equity analysis
- Scalable to large organizations
- Legally defensible

**Disadvantages:**
- Time-intensive to set up
- Requires expertise and training
- Can be complex to maintain
- May feel bureaucratic

**Best For:**
- Medium to large organizations (100+ employees)
- Organizations needing rigor and defensibility
- Regulated industries
- Organizations with pay equity concerns

### 3. Factor Comparison Method

**Type:** Quantitative, analytical

**Process:**
1. **Select Key Jobs:** Choose 10-20 benchmark jobs that are well-understood and fairly paid
2. **Identify Factors:** Select compensable factors (typically 5-7)
   - Common: Knowledge, Mental Effort, Physical Effort, Responsibility, Working Conditions
3. **Rank Benchmark Jobs:** Rank benchmark jobs on each factor separately
4. **Allocate Current Pay:** Distribute each benchmark job's current salary across factors
5. **Create Comparison Scale:** Use benchmark jobs as reference points for each factor
6. **Evaluate Other Jobs:** Compare all other jobs to benchmark jobs on each factor
7. **Assign Value:** Determine monetary value for each job based on factor comparisons

**Example:**

*Benchmark Jobs with Current Salaries:*
- Administrative Assistant: $40,000
- Software Engineer: $120,000  
- Engineering Manager: $150,000

*Factor Allocation for Software Engineer ($120,000):*
- Knowledge: $50,000
- Mental Effort: $40,000
- Responsibility: $20,000
- Working Conditions: $10,000

*Evaluating Product Manager (new role):*
- Knowledge: Between Software Engineer ($50K) and Eng Manager ($60K) = $55K
- Mental Effort: Similar to Eng Manager = $45K
- Responsibility: Between Software Engineer ($20K) and Eng Manager ($40K) = $30K  
- Working Conditions: Same as Software Engineer = $10K
- **Total Estimated Value: $140,000**

**Advantages:**
- Considers multiple factors
- Directly links to monetary value
- Uses real jobs as benchmarks
- Comprehensive assessment

**Disadvantages:**
- Very complex and time-consuming
- Requires significant expertise
- Difficult to explain to employees
- Assumes current benchmark pay is correct
- Hard to maintain as jobs evolve

**Best For:**
- Large enterprises with complex job structures
- Organizations with stable, well-defined roles
- When high precision is required
- Organizations with dedicated compensation teams

### 4. Job Grading (Classification)

**Type:** Qualitative, whole-job

**Process:**
1. **Define Job Grades:** Create grade descriptions with criteria
   - Example: Grade 1 (Entry-level), Grade 2 (Experienced), Grade 3 (Advanced), Grade 4 (Expert), Grade 5 (Leadership)
2. **Establish Grade Criteria:** Define characteristics of each grade
   - Education, experience, scope, decision-making, supervision, impact
3. **Evaluate Jobs:** Match each job to appropriate grade based on overall fit
4. **Assign to Grades:** Place jobs in grades
5. **Create Salary Ranges:** Establish salary band for each grade

**Example Job Grade Framework:**

**Grade 1: Entry-Level Individual Contributor**
- Education: Bachelor's degree or equivalent
- Experience: 0-2 years
- Scope: Individual tasks, well-defined responsibilities
- Decision-Making: Limited, follows established procedures
- Supervision: Works under close supervision
- Impact: Affects own work quality
- Examples: Junior Software Engineer, Marketing Coordinator, Sales Development Rep

**Grade 2: Experienced Individual Contributor**  
- Education: Bachelor's degree + relevant experience
- Experience: 2-5 years
- Scope: Projects, some ambiguity
- Decision-Making: Moderate, some independent judgment
- Supervision: Works independently with periodic oversight
- Impact: Affects team outcomes
- Examples: Software Engineer, Marketing Specialist, Account Executive

**Grade 3: Senior Individual Contributor**
- Education: Bachelor's degree + significant experience or advanced degree
- Experience: 5-10 years
- Scope: Complex projects, high ambiguity
- Decision-Making: Significant, expert judgment
- Supervision: Fully independent, may mentor others
- Impact: Affects department or product outcomes
- Examples: Senior Software Engineer, Senior Product Manager, Sales Manager

**Grade 4: Staff/Principal Individual Contributor or Manager**
- Education: Advanced degree or equivalent experience
- Experience: 10+ years
- Scope: Strategic initiatives, organization-wide impact
- Decision-Making: High, sets direction for others
- Supervision: Leads others (formally or informally)
- Impact: Affects company outcomes
- Examples: Staff Engineer, Director of Marketing, Regional Sales Director

**Grade 5: Executive Leadership**
- Education: Advanced degree + extensive experience
- Experience: 15+ years with leadership track record
- Scope: Company strategy, vision, culture
- Decision-Making: Highest level, shapes company direction
- Supervision: Leads large teams or functions
- Impact: Affects entire organization and market position
- Examples: VP Engineering, CMO, CRO, CFO, CEO

**Advantages:**
- Simple and easy to understand
- Clear career progression
- Fast to implement
- Works well for traditional hierarchies
- Easy to communicate to employees

**Disadvantages:**
- Less precise than point factor methods
- Subjective judgment in classification
- May not fit fluid or overlapping roles
- Can create rigid structures
- Difficult for cross-functional comparisons

**Best For:**
- Organizations with clearly defined roles and levels
- Traditional hierarchical structures
- Public sector and government
- Organizations wanting simplicity and clarity

## Job Architecture and Leveling

### Job Families

Group similar roles into families for consistent career paths and benchmarking.

**Common Job Families:**
- **Engineering:** Software Engineer, Data Engineer, DevOps Engineer, QA Engineer
- **Product:** Product Manager, Product Designer, UX Researcher
- **Sales:** Sales Development Rep, Account Executive, Account Manager, Sales Engineer
- **Marketing:** Marketing Manager, Content Marketer, Demand Gen, Brand Manager
- **Customer Success:** Customer Success Manager, Support Engineer, Implementation Specialist
- **Operations:** Operations Manager, Business Analyst, Program Manager
- **Finance:** Financial Analyst, Accountant, Controller, CFO
- **HR:** Recruiter, HR Business Partner, Compensation Analyst, HRBP
- **Legal:** Paralegal, Corporate Counsel, General Counsel
- **Executive:** CEO, CFO, CTO, COO, CMO, CPO

**Benefits of Job Families:**
- Consistent career progression within function
- Easier benchmarking (compare like roles)
- Clear skill development paths
- Supports internal mobility
- Simplifies compensation structure

### Job Levels

Define levels within each job family.

**Individual Contributor (IC) Levels:**

**IC1 - Junior/Entry-Level:**
- 0-2 years experience
- Executes well-defined tasks
- Learns fundamentals
- Close supervision
- Examples: Junior Software Engineer, Marketing Coordinator

**IC2 - Mid-Level:**
- 2-5 years experience  
- Owns projects independently
- Developing expertise
- Periodic oversight
- Examples: Software Engineer, Product Manager

**IC3 - Senior:**
- 5-10 years experience
- Owns complex projects or features
- Deep expertise in domain
- Mentors junior team members
- Examples: Senior Software Engineer, Senior Product Manager

**IC4 - Staff/Lead:**
- 10+ years experience
- Owns critical initiatives across teams
- Recognized expert, thought leader
- Influences technical/strategic direction
- Examples: Staff Engineer, Lead Product Manager

**IC5 - Principal/Distinguished:**
- 15+ years experience
- Owns company-wide initiatives
- Industry-recognized expert
- Sets technical/product strategy
- Examples: Principal Engineer, Distinguished Designer

**IC6 - Fellow/Chief:**
- 20+ years experience
- Shapes industry, not just company
- Rare, reserved for exceptional individual contributors
- Examples: Engineering Fellow, Chief Scientist

**Management (M) Levels:**

**M1 - Manager:**
- Manages 3-8 individual contributors
- Tactical execution and team development
- Examples: Engineering Manager, Marketing Manager

**M2 - Senior Manager:**
- Manages 8-15 people or multiple teams
- Strategic planning for function
- Examples: Senior Engineering Manager, Senior Marketing Manager

**M3 - Director:**
- Manages 15-50 people or multiple managers
- Owns functional strategy and roadmap
- Examples: Director of Engineering, Director of Sales

**M4 - Senior Director/VP:**
- Manages 50-200 people or multiple directors
- Owns department strategy, budget, and outcomes
- Examples: VP Engineering, VP Sales, VP Marketing

**M5 - SVP/EVP:**
- Manages 200+ people or multiple VPs
- Owns business unit or major function
- Examples: SVP Product & Engineering, EVP Sales

**M6 - C-Suite:**
- Manages entire function or company
- Sets company strategy and vision
- Examples: CTO, CMO, CFO, COO, CEO

### Level Definitions

Create detailed level definitions for each job family.

**Example: Software Engineering Levels**

**Software Engineer I (IC1):**
- **Experience:** 0-2 years
- **Scope:** Well-defined tasks and small features
- **Technical Skills:** Proficient in 1-2 programming languages, learning best practices
- **Autonomy:** Works under close supervision, frequent check-ins
- **Impact:** Affects own work quality and learning
- **Collaboration:** Participates in team activities, asks questions
- **Typical Projects:** Bug fixes, small features, test coverage

**Software Engineer II (IC2):**
- **Experience:** 2-5 years
- **Scope:** Features and small projects
- **Technical Skills:** Proficient in multiple languages, understands system architecture
- **Autonomy:** Works independently with periodic oversight
- **Impact:** Affects team velocity and product quality
- **Collaboration:** Contributes to design discussions, reviews peers' code
- **Typical Projects:** Full features, performance improvements, technical debt reduction

**Senior Software Engineer (IC3):**
- **Experience:** 5-10 years
- **Scope:** Complex features, cross-team projects
- **Technical Skills:** Expert in domain, strong system design skills
- **Autonomy:** Fully independent, sets own priorities
- **Impact:** Affects product outcomes and team technical direction
- **Collaboration:** Leads design discussions, mentors junior engineers
- **Typical Projects:** Major features, architecture improvements, technical leadership

**Staff Software Engineer (IC4):**
- **Experience:** 10+ years
- **Scope:** Critical initiatives spanning multiple teams
- **Technical Skills:** Deep expertise, recognized technical leader
- **Autonomy:** Defines own scope, identifies high-impact opportunities
- **Impact:** Affects engineering org and company technical strategy
- **Collaboration:** Influences technical direction, mentors senior engineers
- **Typical Projects:** Platform initiatives, architectural overhauls, technical strategy

**Principal Software Engineer (IC5):**
- **Experience:** 15+ years
- **Scope:** Company-wide technical initiatives
- **Technical Skills:** Industry-recognized expert
- **Autonomy:** Sets technical vision and strategy
- **Impact:** Affects company success and industry standards
- **Collaboration:** Advises executives, represents company externally
- **Typical Projects:** Technical vision, industry standards, major platform shifts

## Implementation Process

### Step 1: Choose Evaluation Method

Select method based on:
- Organization size and complexity
- Resources available (time, expertise, budget)
- Need for precision and defensibility
- Organizational culture (bureaucratic vs. flexible)

**Recommendation:**
- **Small (<100 employees):** Job Grading (simple, fast)
- **Medium (100-500):** Point Factor (balance of rigor and simplicity)
- **Large (500+):** Point Factor or Factor Comparison (precision, defensibility)

### Step 2: Gather Job Information

**Job Analysis:**
- Collect current job descriptions
- Interview job holders and managers
- Observe work being performed
- Review organizational charts

**Document:**
- Job title and family
- Key responsibilities and deliverables
- Required knowledge, skills, abilities
- Decision-making authority and scope
- Reporting relationships
- Working conditions

### Step 3: Conduct Job Evaluation

**Assemble Evaluation Committee:**
- HR/Compensation lead
- Representatives from different functions
- Senior leadership
- Diverse perspectives (avoid bias)

**Evaluate Jobs:**
- Use chosen method to evaluate each job
- Document rationale for decisions
- Ensure consistency across similar roles
- Calibrate across evaluators

**Review and Validate:**
- Check for internal consistency
- Compare to market benchmarks
- Identify outliers and investigate
- Adjust as needed

### Step 4: Create Job Hierarchy and Grades

**Rank Jobs:**
- Order jobs by evaluation score/points
- Identify natural clusters or breakpoints

**Define Job Grades:**
- Group similar-value jobs into grades
- Typical: 8-15 grades for most organizations
- Ensure meaningful differentiation between grades

**Assign Jobs to Grades:**
- Place each job in appropriate grade
- Ensure consistency within grades
- Document grade assignments

### Step 5: Link to Compensation

**Establish Salary Ranges:**
- Benchmark each grade to market data
- Create salary range for each grade (min, midpoint, max)
- Ensure ranges overlap slightly (typically 10-20%)

**Example:**
- **Grade 3:** $80K - $100K - $120K
- **Grade 4:** $100K - $125K - $150K  
- **Grade 5:** $125K - $156K - $187K

**Map Employees:**
- Place current employees in new structure
- Identify employees below minimum (need adjustment)
- Identify employees above maximum (red-circled, freeze until range catches up)

### Step 6: Communicate and Implement

**Communication Plan:**
- Explain why job evaluation was conducted
- Describe methodology and process
- Share job grades and salary ranges
- Provide resources for questions

**Individual Conversations:**
- Managers discuss new grades with employees
- Explain how job was evaluated
- Address concerns and questions
- Discuss career progression opportunities

**Implementation:**
- Effective date for new structure
- Make necessary compensation adjustments
- Update HRIS and systems
- Train managers on new structure

### Step 7: Maintain and Update

**Regular Reviews:**
- Annual review of job descriptions
- Re-evaluate jobs when responsibilities change significantly
- Add new jobs as created
- Retire obsolete jobs

**Market Benchmarking:**
- Annual benchmarking to ensure ranges remain competitive
- Adjust salary ranges as market moves

**Governance:**
- Clear process for job evaluation requests
- Approval workflow for new jobs or re-evaluations
- Documentation and audit trail

## Best Practices

### 1. Focus on the Job, Not the Person
- Evaluate role requirements, not current incumbent's skills
- Separate job evaluation from performance evaluation
- Avoid "evaluating up" for valued employees

### 2. Ensure Consistency
- Use same criteria and process for all jobs
- Calibrate across evaluators
- Document rationale for decisions
- Regular audits for consistency

### 3. Involve Stakeholders
- Include diverse perspectives in evaluation committee
- Gather input from job holders and managers
- Communicate transparently throughout process
- Address concerns and questions

### 4. Keep It Simple
- Don't overcomplicate the system
- Use clear, understandable criteria
- Avoid too many factors or levels
- Make it easy to explain to employees

### 5. Link to Market Data
- Validate internal hierarchy against market benchmarks
- Ensure job grades align with market pay levels
- Use market data to set salary ranges
- Regular benchmarking to stay current

### 6. Support Career Development
- Clear progression paths within job families
- Transparent criteria for advancement
- Development resources for skill-building
- Regular career conversations

### 7. Maintain Flexibility
- Allow for exceptions when justified
- Review and update as organization evolves
- Don't let structure become rigid bureaucracy
- Balance consistency with business needs

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Resistance to Change

**Problem:** Employees or managers resist new job structure

**Solutions:**
- Involve stakeholders early in process
- Communicate benefits clearly (fairness, transparency, career paths)
- Grandfather current employees (no pay cuts)
- Provide transition period
- Address concerns individually

### Challenge 2: Grade Inflation

**Problem:** Pressure to place jobs in higher grades than warranted

**Solutions:**
- Stick to objective criteria
- Calibrate across organization
- Separate job evaluation from compensation decisions
- Leadership support for consistent standards
- Document rationale for grade assignments

### Challenge 3: Overlapping Roles

**Problem:** Roles with fluid or overlapping responsibilities hard to evaluate

**Solutions:**
- Focus on core, consistent responsibilities
- Use multiple evaluators for perspective
- Consider creating broader job families
- Allow for some flexibility in structure
- Regular re-evaluation as roles evolve

### Challenge 4: Maintaining Over Time

**Problem:** Job evaluation system becomes outdated or neglected

**Solutions:**
- Assign clear ownership (HR/Compensation team)
- Establish regular review cadence (annual)
- Create simple process for updates
- Integrate with other HR processes (hiring, promotions)
- Track and report on system health

### Challenge 5: Balancing Internal Equity and Market Competitiveness

**Problem:** Market pay for some roles doesn't align with internal job evaluation

**Solutions:**
- Use market data to validate and adjust job grades
- Allow for market premiums on critical roles
- Communicate rationale for exceptions
- Regular benchmarking to stay current
- Consider total rewards, not just base salary
