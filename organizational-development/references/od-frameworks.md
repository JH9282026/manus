# OD Frameworks

Comprehensive guide to organizational development frameworks and models for diagnosing organizational challenges and designing effective interventions.

---

## McKinsey 7-S Model Deep Dive

The McKinsey 7-S Model is one of the most widely used frameworks for analyzing organizational effectiveness through the lens of seven interconnected elements.

### The Seven Elements in Detail

**Hard Elements (Easier to Define and Change):**

**1. Strategy:**
- **Definition:** Plan for allocating resources to achieve competitive advantage
- **Key Questions:**
  - What is our competitive strategy?
  - How do we differentiate from competitors?
  - What are our strategic priorities?
  - How do we allocate resources?
  - Is strategy clearly communicated and understood?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Clear, documented strategy
- Alignment between stated strategy and resource allocation
- Strategy understood at all levels
- Regular strategy review and adaptation
- Competitive positioning clarity

**2. Structure:**
- **Definition:** How the organization is organized (hierarchy, divisions, departments)
- **Key Questions:**
  - How is work divided and coordinated?
  - What is the reporting structure?
  - How centralized or decentralized are we?
  - Does structure support strategy execution?
  - Are roles and responsibilities clear?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Organization chart clarity
- Appropriate span of control
- Clear reporting relationships
- Minimal organizational layers
- Structure aligned with strategy

**3. Systems:**
- **Definition:** Processes and procedures that govern daily operations
- **Key Questions:**
  - What are our core business processes?
  - How do we make decisions?
  - What systems support our work (IT, HR, finance)?
  - Are systems efficient and effective?
  - Do systems enable or hinder performance?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Documented, efficient processes
- Effective IT and operational systems
- Clear decision-making processes
- Performance management systems
- Knowledge management and information flow

**Soft Elements (Harder to Define and Change):**

**4. Shared Values (Core of the Model):**
- **Definition:** Core beliefs and norms that guide behavior and decision-making
- **Key Questions:**
  - What are our core values?
  - Are values clearly articulated and understood?
  - Do behaviors align with stated values?
  - What gets rewarded and punished?
  - What is our organizational culture?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Clear, compelling values statements
- Values demonstrated in leadership behavior
- Values integrated into decisions and actions
- Strong cultural identity
- Employee alignment with values

**5. Skills:**
- **Definition:** Distinctive capabilities and competencies of the organization
- **Key Questions:**
  - What are we really good at?
  - What capabilities differentiate us?
  - Do we have skills needed for our strategy?
  - How do we develop organizational capabilities?
  - Where are our skill gaps?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Clear competency frameworks
- Distinctive organizational capabilities
- Skills aligned with strategy
- Effective talent development
- Competitive advantage from capabilities

**6. Staff:**
- **Definition:** Human resources and how they are developed, trained, and motivated
- **Key Questions:**
  - Do we have the right people in the right roles?
  - How do we attract, develop, and retain talent?
  - What is our approach to performance management?
  - How diverse and inclusive is our workforce?
  - Are employees engaged and motivated?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Strong talent acquisition and retention
- Effective performance management
- Clear career development paths
- High employee engagement
- Diversity and inclusion

**7. Style:**
- **Definition:** Leadership approach and management style
- **Key Questions:**
  - How do leaders behave and make decisions?
  - What is the management style (directive, participative, etc.)?
  - How do leaders spend their time?
  - What behaviors do leaders model?
  - Is leadership style consistent with culture and values?

**Diagnostic Indicators:**
- Consistent leadership behaviors
- Leadership style aligned with culture
- Visible, accessible leadership
- Effective communication from leaders
- Leaders as role models for values

### Using the 7-S Model for Diagnosis

**Step 1: Assess Each Element**
- Evaluate current state of each of the seven elements
- Use surveys, interviews, focus groups, and observation
- Gather both quantitative and qualitative data
- Involve diverse stakeholders in assessment

**Step 2: Analyze Alignment**
- Examine how well the seven elements align with each other
- Identify areas of strong alignment and misalignment
- Assess whether all elements support the strategy
- Determine if shared values are at the center

**Step 3: Identify Gaps**
- Compare current state to desired state for each element
- Prioritize gaps based on impact on performance
- Understand root causes of misalignment
- Identify interdependencies between elements

**Step 4: Design Interventions**
- Develop action plans to address gaps and improve alignment
- Ensure interventions consider all seven elements
- Sequence changes appropriately (often start with shared values)
- Plan for change management and communication

**Step 5: Monitor Progress**
- Track improvements in each element over time
- Reassess alignment regularly
- Adjust interventions based on results
- Maintain focus on continuous improvement

### 7-S Model Application Examples

**Merger & Acquisition Integration:**
- Assess 7-S elements for both organizations
- Identify cultural and operational differences
- Design integration plan addressing all elements
- Monitor alignment during integration process

**Strategic Transformation:**
- Start with new strategy definition
- Assess how other six elements must change to support strategy
- Redesign structure, systems, and processes
- Develop new skills and capabilities
- Shift culture and leadership style

**Performance Improvement:**
- Diagnose root causes of performance issues using 7-S
- Identify which elements are misaligned or underperforming
- Focus interventions on highest-impact areas
- Ensure changes in one element are supported by others

## Jay Galbraith's Star Model Deep Dive

The Star Model provides a comprehensive framework for organizational design, focusing on how design choices influence employee behavior.

### The Five Points of the Star

**1. Strategy (Top Point):**

**Components:**
- **Mission:** Why the organization exists
- **Vision:** Where the organization is going
- **Goals:** What the organization aims to achieve
- **Values:** Principles guiding decisions and behavior
- **Strategic Direction:** How the organization will compete and succeed

**Design Implications:**
- Strategy determines what capabilities are needed
- Influences all other design decisions
- Must be clear and well-communicated
- Should drive resource allocation

**2. Structure (Left Point):**

**Design Dimensions:**

**Specialization:**
- How work is divided into roles and units
- Functional, product, market, or geographic specialization
- Depth vs. breadth of expertise

**Shape:**
- Span of control (number of direct reports)
- Number of organizational layers
- Tall (narrow spans) vs. flat (wide spans) structures

**Distribution of Power:**
- Centralized vs. decentralized decision-making
- Vertical (hierarchical) vs. lateral (cross-functional) power
- Authority and accountability distribution

**Departmentalization:**
- **Functional:** Organized by function (Engineering, Sales, Marketing)
- **Product:** Organized by product line or service
- **Market:** Organized by customer segment or industry
- **Geographic:** Organized by region or location
- **Hybrid:** Combination of multiple organizing principles

**3. Processes (Right Point):**

**Process Types:**

**Vertical Processes:**
- Business planning and budgeting
- Performance management
- Talent management and succession planning
- Resource allocation

**Horizontal Processes:**
- Product development
- Order fulfillment
- Customer service
- Supply chain management

**Design Considerations:**
- Information flow and communication
- Decision-making processes and authority
- Coordination mechanisms
- Integration across organizational boundaries
- Technology enablement

**4. Rewards (Bottom Left Point):**

**Reward System Components:**

**Compensation:**
- Base salary and pay structure
- Variable pay (bonuses, commissions)
- Equity and long-term incentives
- Benefits and perks

**Performance Management:**
- Goal setting and alignment
- Performance evaluation criteria
- Feedback and development
- Consequences for performance (rewards and accountability)

**Recognition:**
- Formal recognition programs
- Informal appreciation and praise
- Career advancement opportunities
- Non-monetary rewards

**Design Principles:**
- Align rewards with desired behaviors and outcomes
- Ensure fairness and transparency
- Balance individual and team rewards
- Link to strategy and organizational goals

**5. People (Bottom Right Point):**

**HR Policies and Practices:**

**Talent Acquisition:**
- Recruiting strategy and employer brand
- Selection criteria and processes
- Onboarding and integration

**Talent Development:**
- Learning and development programs
- Leadership development
- Career pathing and succession planning
- Performance coaching

**Talent Management:**
- Workforce planning
- High-potential identification and development
- Retention strategies
- Diversity and inclusion

**Organizational Capabilities:**
- Core competencies required for strategy
- Capability building initiatives
- Knowledge management
- Culture and values

### Using the Star Model for Organizational Design

**Design Process:**

**1. Start with Strategy:**
- Clarify strategic direction and priorities
- Identify critical capabilities needed
- Define success metrics

**2. Design Structure:**
- Determine organizing principle (function, product, market, geography)
- Define reporting relationships and hierarchy
- Establish spans of control and layers
- Clarify roles and responsibilities

**3. Design Processes:**
- Map critical business processes
- Define decision rights and governance
- Establish coordination mechanisms
- Enable with technology and systems

**4. Design Rewards:**
- Align incentives with strategy and desired behaviors
- Define performance metrics and goals
- Create recognition and career development programs
- Ensure fairness and competitiveness

**5. Design People Practices:**
- Define talent requirements and competencies
- Develop recruiting and development strategies
- Build organizational capabilities
- Foster desired culture

**6. Ensure Alignment:**
- Check that all five points support strategy
- Identify and resolve conflicts between elements
- Test design against scenarios and challenges
- Iterate and refine design

### Star Model Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive view of organizational design
- Clear linkage between strategy and design choices
- Emphasis on alignment across elements
- Practical and actionable framework
- Widely used and validated

**Limitations:**
- Does not explicitly address culture (though implied in people and values)
- Less emphasis on external environment and stakeholders
- Can be complex to apply in practice
- Requires significant data and analysis
- May not capture all organizational dynamics

## Weisbord's Six Box Model Deep Dive

A diagnostic framework for quickly assessing organizational health and identifying areas for intervention.

### The Six Boxes in Detail

**1. Purpose:**
- **Focus:** Organizational mission, vision, and goals
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - What business are we in?
  - Is our purpose clear to everyone?
  - Do people agree on our goals?
  - Is there clarity about priorities?
  - How well do we balance multiple stakeholder needs?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Clarity and communication of purpose
- Alignment on goals across organization
- Connection between purpose and daily work
- Stakeholder agreement on direction

**2. Structure:**
- **Focus:** How work is divided and coordinated
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - How is work organized?
  - Is the structure appropriate for our purpose?
  - Are roles and responsibilities clear?
  - How well do different parts coordinate?
  - Are there gaps or overlaps in structure?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Clarity of organizational structure
- Appropriate division of labor
- Effective coordination mechanisms
- Minimal duplication or gaps

**3. Relationships:**
- **Focus:** How people and units interact and manage conflict
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - How well do people work together?
  - How are conflicts managed?
  - What is the quality of communication?
  - Are there silos or collaboration barriers?
  - How is trust and respect among teams?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Quality of working relationships
- Effective conflict resolution
- Cross-functional collaboration
- Trust and psychological safety
- Communication effectiveness

**4. Rewards:**
- **Focus:** Incentives and consequences for performance
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - What gets rewarded (formally and informally)?
  - Are rewards aligned with goals and desired behaviors?
  - Do people feel fairly compensated and recognized?
  - What happens when performance is poor?
  - Are there consequences for not meeting expectations?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Alignment of rewards with goals
- Fairness and transparency of rewards
- Balance of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation
- Accountability for performance

**5. Leadership:**
- **Focus:** How leaders balance and integrate all other boxes
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - Do leaders provide clear direction?
  - How well do leaders manage conflicts and trade-offs?
  - Are leaders accessible and supportive?
  - Do leaders model desired behaviors?
  - How effective is leadership communication?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Leadership clarity and direction
- Effective balancing of competing priorities
- Leadership accessibility and support
- Role modeling of values and behaviors
- Communication effectiveness

**6. Helpful Mechanisms:**
- **Focus:** Coordinating technologies, systems, and processes
- **Diagnostic Questions:**
  - What systems and processes support our work?
  - Are our technologies adequate and effective?
  - How well do our systems integrate?
  - Are there bureaucratic barriers or inefficiencies?
  - Do we have the right tools and resources?

**Assessment Indicators:**
- Adequacy of systems and technology
- Process efficiency and effectiveness
- Integration across systems
- Minimal bureaucracy
- Appropriate tools and resources

### Using the Six Box Model for Diagnosis

**Diagnostic Process:**

**Step 1: Start with Purpose**
- Always begin diagnosis with purpose/mission
- Ensure clarity and alignment on organizational direction
- Use purpose as anchor for assessing other boxes

**Step 2: Assess Each Box**
- Gather data through surveys, interviews, observation
- Ask diagnostic questions for each box
- Identify strengths and weaknesses
- Look for patterns and themes

**Step 3: Examine Relationships Between Boxes**
- How do boxes interact and influence each other?
- Are there conflicts or misalignments between boxes?
- Which boxes are most problematic?
- What are root causes vs. symptoms?

**Step 4: Prioritize Interventions**
- Focus on boxes with biggest impact on performance
- Address root causes, not just symptoms
- Consider interdependencies between boxes
- Sequence interventions appropriately

**Step 5: Design Action Plans**
- Develop specific interventions for priority areas
- Assign ownership and accountability
- Set timelines and milestones
- Plan for monitoring and adjustment

### Six Box Model Application Examples

**Rapid Organizational Assessment:**
- Use Six Box Model for quick diagnostic (1-2 weeks)
- Conduct interviews with key stakeholders
- Identify top 3-5 issues across boxes
- Develop action plan for highest-priority issues

**Team Effectiveness:**
- Apply Six Box Model at team level
- Assess team purpose, structure, relationships, rewards, leadership, mechanisms
- Facilitate team discussion of findings
- Co-create team improvement plan

**Change Readiness:**
- Use Six Box Model to assess readiness for change
- Identify which boxes will be most affected by change
- Determine where resistance may emerge
- Design change management plan addressing all boxes

## Additional OD Frameworks

### Mintzberg's 5Ps of Strategy

Five perspectives for understanding and creating business strategy:

**1. Plan:**
- Consciously intended course of action
- Formal, documented strategy
- Deliberate and proactive

**2. Ploy:**
- Specific maneuver to outwit competitors
- Tactical and competitive
- Short-term and situational

**3. Pattern:**
- Consistency in behavior over time
- Emergent strategy from actions
- May be unintended or unconscious

**4. Position:**
- Locating organization in its environment
- Competitive positioning
- Market and industry context

**5. Perspective:**
- Organization's fundamental way of doing things
- Shared mindset and culture
- Ingrained and difficult to change

**Application:**
- Use all five perspectives to understand strategy holistically
- Recognize that strategy is both deliberate (plan) and emergent (pattern)
- Consider both external positioning and internal perspective
- Balance planning with flexibility and adaptation

### Burke-Litwin Model of Organizational Performance and Change

Causal model showing how organizational elements influence performance:

**Transformational Factors (Driven by External Environment):**
- Leadership
- Mission and Strategy
- Organizational Culture

**Transactional Factors (Day-to-Day Operations):**
- Structure
- Management Practices
- Systems (Policies and Procedures)
- Work Unit Climate
- Task Requirements and Individual Skills
- Individual Needs and Values
- Motivation

**Outcomes:**
- Individual Performance
- Organizational Performance

**Key Insights:**
- Transformational change requires addressing transformational factors
- Transactional factors drive incremental improvement
- External environment influences transformational factors
- All factors ultimately impact individual and organizational performance

**Application:**
- Diagnose whether change is transformational or transactional
- Focus interventions on appropriate factors
- Understand causal relationships between factors
- Design comprehensive change initiatives

### ADKAR Model for Individual Change

Framework for managing individual change (often used alongside organizational OD):

**Five Elements:**

**1. Awareness:** Of the need for change
**2. Desire:** To participate and support the change
**3. Knowledge:** Of how to change
**4. Ability:** To implement required skills and behaviors
**5. Reinforcement:** To sustain the change

**Application:**
- Assess individuals' readiness at each ADKAR stage
- Design interventions to move people through stages
- Recognize that change happens sequentially (can't skip stages)
- Address barriers at each stage
- Use for change management planning and execution
